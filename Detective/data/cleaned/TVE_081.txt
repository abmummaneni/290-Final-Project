--- BACKGROUND AND CONTEXT ---

"Murder on the Orient Express" is a feature-length episode of ITV's Agatha
Christie's Poirot, first broadcast in December 2010. It is directed by Philip
Martin and stars David Suchet as Hercule Poirot. It is based on Christie's
1934 novel of the same name — one of the most famous mystery novels ever written,
whose central twist is known even by many people who have never read it, and
which represents one of Christie's most audacious structural achievements: a
solution that is simultaneously the least probable outcome in conventional
mystery terms and the only outcome that satisfies the specific moral logic
the novel has been building.

The Suchet adaptation is the third major screen version of the novel, following
Sidney Lumet's celebrated 1974 theatrical film (with Albert Finney as Poirot
and an all-star cast) and a 2001 television film. It is widely regarded as the
most faithful to Christie's text and the most thoughtful in its treatment of
the moral question at the novel's heart — the question of whether Poirot,
having discovered a truth that the law would handle one way and justice might
handle another, is obligated to report everything he knows.

The episode is notable for Suchet's performance, which is the most emotionally
complex he gives in the entire series: a Poirot who is genuinely troubled by
what he finds, who experiences a real moral crisis, and who makes a choice
at the end that costs him something.

--- THE RATCHETT MURDER ---

Hercule Poirot is aboard the Orient Express, traveling from Istanbul to Calais.
The train is unusually full for the season — every compartment in the first-class
sleeping car occupied — and among the passengers is a man named Ratchett, an
elderly American who approaches Poirot on the first evening and asks for his
professional protection. He has been receiving threatening letters, he says.
He fears for his life.

Poirot declines. Something about Ratchett — something in the man's face, in
the quality of his eyes, in the specific feeling Poirot's instinct generates —
tells him that this is not a man he wishes to help. He does not know why, yet.
But he does not want Ratchett's protection.

The following morning, Ratchett is found dead in his locked compartment. He
has been stabbed — many times, with what appears to be multiple weapons of
different sizes, creating a pattern of wounds that is medically puzzling. The
Orient Express is halted in a snowdrift somewhere in Yugoslavia, unable to
move. There is no outside communication and no possibility of calling in
official authorities. M. Bouc, the director of the Compagnie Internationale
des Wagons-Lits and an old friend of Poirot's, asks him to investigate.

The murderer is among the passengers of that car. There is no other possibility.

--- RATCHETT'S IDENTITY: CASSETTI ---

Poirot's investigation of the dead man quickly establishes that Ratchett was
not who he claimed to be. His real name was Cassetti — an American gangster
who, some years earlier, kidnapped and murdered a small girl named Daisy
Armstrong. The Armstrong kidnapping — modeled closely on the real Lindbergh
kidnapping of 1932 — was one of the most notorious crimes in American history:
a wealthy family's child taken for ransom, the ransom paid, the child found
dead anyway. The consequences of the Armstrong case spread far beyond the
immediate family: Daisy's mother died in premature childbirth brought on by
the shock; her father, Colonel Armstrong, killed himself; a household servant
who was falsely suspected committed suicide; and the family was effectively
destroyed.

Cassetti was caught but escaped American justice — he had money and lawyers
and used both to avoid conviction. He changed his identity, left America, and
has been living in Europe as Ratchett. He has been living freely, comfortably,
apparently without consequence, for years.

Poirot now understands why his instinct refused to help Ratchett: the specific
quality of evil he recognized in the man's face was real.

--- THE PASSENGERS ---

The first-class sleeping car of the Orient Express contains thirteen other
passengers besides Poirot and Ratchett. All of them are suspects. All of them
had access to the corridor. All of them have alibis of varying quality. And
all of them have connections — which the investigation gradually reveals —
to the Armstrong family and its tragedy.

The passengers include:

Colonel Arbuthnot — a retired British officer, apparently accompanying Mary
Debenham, a prim English governess.

Mary Debenham — calm, controlled, and slightly too controlled, in Poirot's
assessment.

Hector MacQueen — Ratchett's personal secretary, a young American who worked
for him closely and who has specific knowledge of his history.

Edward Henry Masterman — Ratchett's manservant, phlegmatic and British.

Princess Dragomiroff — an elderly Russian noblewoman of enormous presence and
authority.

Hildegarde Schmidt — Princess Dragomiroff's German maid.

Count and Countess Andrenyi — a Hungarian nobleman and his wife, elegant and
apparently unconnected to anything.

Greta Ohlsson — a Swedish missionary, gentle and kind.

Antonio Foscarelli — an ebullient Italian-American car salesman.

Cyrus Hardman — an American private detective.

Mrs. Hubbard — a loud, overwhelming American woman who has the compartment
next to Ratchett's and who had various alarming experiences during the night.

Dr. Constantine — a Greek physician who assists Poirot with the medical aspects
of the investigation.

--- THE INVESTIGATION: CLUES AND COMPLICATIONS ---

The physical evidence of the murder is carefully presented:

The wounds — the multiple stab wounds of different depths and angles — are
medically inconsistent with a single attacker. Either the wounds were inflicted
by multiple people, or by a single ambidextrous attacker, or the depth
variations represent something else.

A handkerchief with the initial H was found near the body.

A button from a Wagon-Lits conductor's uniform was found.

A scarlet kimono was seen by Mrs. Hubbard.

A pipe cleaner was found in Ratchett's compartment that belonged to Colonel
Arbuthnot.

A charred piece of paper with the words "member little Daisy Armstrong" was
found in Ratchett's compartment — a fragment that survived burning and that
connects Ratchett definitively to Cassetti.

The watch found in Ratchett's pocket was stopped at 1:15 a.m. — but this,
Poirot determines, was arranged: the watch was stopped deliberately to
misdirect the time of death.

The timing of various passengers' movements through the night — heard by others,
confirmed or denied by their own accounts — creates a puzzle of remarkable
complexity.

--- THE CONNECTIONS REVEALED ---

As Poirot interviews each passenger, the Armstrong connection is established
one by one. This revelation — that almost every passenger in the car has some
specific link to the Armstrong family tragedy — is the episode's great mounting
horror and its great mounting moral logic.

Mary Debenham was Daisy Armstrong's governess. Colonel Arbuthnot was Colonel
Armstrong's closest friend and fellow officer. Hildegarde Schmidt was the
Armstrong cook. Greta Ohlsson was Daisy's Swedish nurse. Antonio Foscarelli
was the Armstrong chauffeur. Cyrus Hardman was in love with a woman connected
to the Armstrong household. MacQueen's father was the district attorney who
handled Cassetti's prosecution. Mrs. Hubbard is revealed as Linda Arden —
Daisy's grandmother, one of America's most celebrated actresses, who has
been using a disguise and assumed identity for the journey. Countess Andrenyi
is the sister of Sonia Armstrong — Daisy's mother.

Twelve people. Twelve people whose lives were destroyed or permanently damaged
by Cassetti's crime and by the American justice system's failure to punish him.
Twelve people who booked every available berth in the first-class car on this
specific train. Twelve people who agreed, together, to kill the man who had
killed and gone free.

--- THE SOLUTION ---

The solution to Murder on the Orient Express is known to virtually everyone
who has heard of the novel, but it retains its power regardless:

All twelve passengers killed Ratchett. Each of them stabbed him once — hence
the multiple wounds of varying depths and angles, some inflicted by people
more physically capable than others, some uncertain and some decisive. The
wounds that seemed medically impossible from a single attacker were in fact
delivered by twelve different hands.

The clues scattered through the investigation — the handkerchief, the button,
the kimono, the pipe cleaner — were placed deliberately by the conspirators
to muddy the investigation with false leads pointing to different individuals.
The stopped watch was arranged to confuse the time of death. The alibis were
designed to corroborate each other without being provable. The entire scene
was constructed by twelve intelligent, motivated people who had planned their
action carefully and who had agreed to share the act and the moral responsibility
equally.

The specific person who actually killed Cassetti — who is guilty in the direct
legal sense — is all of them, equally. Or, in the moral sense, none of them
more than any other.

--- POIROT'S MORAL CRISIS ---

Poirot's discovery of the truth places him in a position unlike any other in
the series: he knows who killed Ratchett, and he knows why, and the "why"
does not fit easily into any category he usually works with.

Cassetti was a murderer who escaped justice through money and legal manipulation.
He killed a child and destroyed a family and lived freely for years. The twelve
people on the Orient Express were his victims in the broadest sense — people
whose lives he had ruined — and they exacted a justice that the legal system
had refused to provide.

Poirot is not a man who believes in vigilante justice. He believes in the law.
He has dedicated his life to the specific proposition that human society requires
law and that the law must be upheld even when it fails. And yet what he is
looking at is twelve people who did something comprehensible, who did it for
reasons that any human being would understand, who gave Cassetti more
consideration in their planning than Cassetti gave Daisy Armstrong.

The Suchet adaptation handles this crisis with unusual directness and emotional
depth. Poirot does not find the solution comforting. He finds it genuinely
disturbing — not the crimes of the twelve but the specific position it places
him in. He is the detective. He has found the answer. What does he do with it?

--- THE CHOICE ---

Poirot presents two possible solutions to M. Bouc and Dr. Constantine. The
first is a fictional alternative — a single unknown intruder who boarded the
train, killed Ratchett, and left, and whose existence cannot be proved but also
cannot be disproved. The second is the truth: all twelve passengers, acting
together, with full knowledge of what they were doing and why.

He leaves the choice to M. Bouc. Bouc chooses the first solution — the
impossible outsider. They will tell the Yugoslav police this story. The twelve
passengers will go free.

Poirot, in the Suchet version, does not endorse this choice with equanimity.
He accepts it — he steps back and allows it to happen — but the specific cost
to him is visible. He has, for the first time in the series, allowed a known
truth to be suppressed in the service of a justice that the law would not
recognize. Whether he has done the right thing is not answered. The film ends
without resolution of that question, and this is its most honest quality.

--- THE EMOTIONAL AND MORAL WEIGHT ---

What the Suchet "Murder on the Orient Express" does that earlier versions do
not is take the moral question entirely seriously. The 1974 Lumet film treats
the revelation with a certain theatrical relish — the assembled suspects, the
brilliant detective, the extraordinary solution. The Suchet version treats it
as a genuine moral crisis for the detective at its center.

This Poirot has spent his career believing in order and in the law. This case
gives him a situation where order and justice point in opposite directions,
where the law would punish people whose act most human beings would find
understandable if not excusable, and where suppressing the truth might be the
most human thing available to him. The cost of that choice — what it takes
from him to make it — is the episode's final and most important statement.

--- NOTES ON THE EPISODE ---

The 2010 "Murder on the Orient Express" is among the finest Suchet Poirot
productions and a genuine landmark in the series. Philip Martin's direction
is restrained and precise, the cast is excellent, and Suchet's performance —
particularly in the scenes where Poirot confronts his own moral situation —
is perhaps his finest in the entire run. The episode handles one of the most
famous mystery plots in the world with complete confidence, finding fresh
dramatic and emotional territory in a story that most of its audience already
knows. The specific decision to take Poirot's moral dilemma seriously, rather
than treating it as the epilogue to a clever puzzle, is what makes this
adaptation definitive.
