THE STORY: ONE STEP BEHIND


The Midsummer Party

The episode opens with three young people — a small group of friends celebrating
Midsummer's Eve, the Swedish midsommar, the most important public holiday in the
Swedish calendar, a night of dancing, drinking, and festivity. They are in costume,
in the woods outside Ystad, having their own private celebration away from the formal
parties. They are young, they are happy, and they are killed. All three of them, in
the woods, on Midsummer's Eve, for no reason that is initially apparent.

The specific quality of this opening — the radical contrast between the festivity of the
night and the violence that intrudes on it, the youth and innocence of the victims, and
the complete absence of any obvious motive — establishes the episode's particular
emotional register from its first minutes. This is not a crime of passion or of greed
or of specific targeted grievance. This is something else, and the something else is
worse.

Wallander does not investigate these deaths immediately. He is not on duty when they
occur, and by the time the bodies are found and the investigation begins, the trail is
already partially cold. This delay — being one step behind from the start — is the
episode's title and its central frustration.


Svedberg

One of the episode's most painful and distinctive elements is that the investigation
of the Midsummer murders is connected to the investigation of a second, apparently
unrelated death: that of Svedberg, a detective on the Ystad force. Svedberg — one of
Wallander's colleagues, a man he has worked alongside for years — is found shot dead
in his apartment.

The discovery of Svedberg's body is one of the episode's gut-punch moments. Svedberg
was not a headline character — not a major protagonist in his own right — but his death
is handled with the full weight of what it means when a colleague dies. Wallander's
reaction to it is not the professional calculation of an investigator adding a case to
his workload; it is the grief of a man who has lost someone he worked alongside for a
long time, in a context where working alongside someone creates a specific kind of bond
even in the absence of deep personal friendship.

The investigation into Svedberg's death runs alongside the investigation into the
Midsummer murders, and the two threads are connected in ways that gradually become clear.


Svedberg's Secret Life

As the investigation of Svedberg's death proceeds, it reveals aspects of his private
life that his colleagues — including Wallander — did not know. Svedberg had a hidden
personal life: a relationship with a woman, a set of private habits and connections,
a version of himself that he kept entirely separate from his professional identity.

This is handled by the episode and by Mankell's novel with considerable sensitivity.
The revelation of a colleague's private life is a specific kind of exposure — posthumous,
without the person's consent, in the full light of a murder investigation. Wallander's
response to learning things about Svedberg that Svedberg had chosen not to share is
complex: there is loss in it, and there is the specific discomfort of realizing that
someone you thought you knew was living at a significant distance from the version
of themselves they presented.

The specific nature of Svedberg's secret — the woman he was involved with, the
relationship they had, and how that relationship connects to the Midsummer murders —
is the episode's central investigative thread.

THE MIDSUMMER VICTIMS


Who They Were and Why It Matters

The three young people killed on Midsummer's Eve — identified as they are investigated —
turn out to have specific connections to each other and to the wider world of the
investigation. They were not random victims. They were chosen. Understanding why they
were chosen is the key to understanding who chose them.

Their youth is emphasized throughout — these were people at the beginning of their lives,
with futures ahead of them, whose deaths are therefore particularly weighted. Mankell
consistently uses this quality of youth and potential in his victims to ensure that the
investigation carries genuine moral urgency. The deaths are not simply puzzles to be
solved; they are losses to be mourned.


The Connection to Svedberg

The connection between the three young people and Svedberg — the murdered detective —
is what Wallander must establish. The link, when found, reveals that Svedberg's secret
life and the Midsummer murders are not parallel tracks but the same track: the person
who killed the young people and the person who killed Svedberg are the same person,
and the reason Svedberg died was connected to his specific knowledge of — or proximity
to — the events that produced the Midsummer killings.

THE KILLER


A Specific Individual, Not a Conspiracy

Unlike Firewall, which involves an organized network, One Step Behind's crime is the
work of a single person — someone with a specific obsessive psychology and a specific
grievance or attachment that produced escalating violence. The killer is not a
professional criminal or an ideological operator; they are someone who was close to
the situation, whose proximity gave them both knowledge and access, and whose specific
emotional relationship to the events surrounding the Midsummer party produced the
decision to kill.

Identifying this person requires Wallander to understand the emotional and psychological
architecture of the relationships among the victims and those around them — to find
the person for whom the specific combination of people on Midsummer's Eve represented
an intolerable situation.


The Obsessive Quality

The episode is particularly effective at conveying the specific quality of the killer's
psychology — the obsessive, consuming nature of whatever drove them to the woods on
Midsummer's Eve and to Svedberg's apartment afterward. This is not someone who kills
easily or without emotional cost; their murders are the product of a specific internal
pressure that the investigation helps Wallander understand even as he is hunting the
person who created it.

The gap between the killer's inner experience — the world as they experienced it, the
logic of what they did from the inside — and the external reality of three young people
murdered in festival costumes in the Swedish woods is one of the episode's most
disturbing elements.

WALLANDER'S PERSONAL STATE


The Diagnosis

One Step Behind is the episode in which Wallander's medical situation becomes more
explicit. He is not well — beyond the ordinary wear of his demanding life and his
poor self-care habits, there are specific cognitive symptoms that are concerning. His
increasing forgetfulness, his moments of disorientation, his difficulty sustaining
certain kinds of attention, are observed both by himself and by those around him.

The shadow of dementia — Mankell's long-prepared arc for Wallander, the same thing
he is watching happen to his father — is present here without being explicitly confirmed.
Wallander does not seek clarity about his situation. He pushes through, as he always
does, because stopping is not a thing he can do while a case is open.


The Cost of Being One Step Behind

The episode's title describes both the literal investigation — Wallander starts late,
misses things, arrives at scenes after the critical moments have already passed — and
something more interior: the specific feeling of a man who is losing the sharpness
that defined him, who senses that he is behind in ways he has not been before, who
cannot always identify whether a failure of insight is the case's complexity or his
own diminishment.

Branagh conveys this with the specific restraint the role requires: not self-pity, not
dramatic anguish, but a quiet, stubborn awareness of something that he cannot yet name
and will not yet surrender to.


The Landscape

Philip Martin's direction in One Step Behind is particularly notable for its use of the
Swedish summer landscape — the long days, the pale golden light that never quite fades
into full dark at this latitude, the specific atmosphere of Midsummer in Scandinavia.
The contrast between the beauty of the environment and the darkness of what happened
in it on the night of the killings is sustained throughout the episode with considerable
visual intelligence.

RESOLUTION

Wallander identifies the killer — assembles the picture from Svedberg's secret life, the
connections among the Midsummer victims, and the specific psychology that produced the
violence. The confrontation is managed with the physical danger and emotional weight that
the discovery of a colleague's killer requires.

The resolution is not triumphant. It is the resolution of a case that began with three
young people in festival costumes and a colleague shot in his apartment — there is nothing
in its conclusion that undoes those losses. The killer is found. The investigation is
complete. Wallander goes home.

The final images — Wallander in the Swedish light, the flat landscape, the enormous sky —
are as beautiful and as melancholy as everything that preceded them.
