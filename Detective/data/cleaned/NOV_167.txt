DETAILED SPOILER SYNOPSIS

The novel opens when Virginia Fablon hires private investigator Lew Archer to look into her fiancé, Francis Martel, a mysterious Frenchman who has appeared at a California tennis club with apparently unlimited money and no clear past. Virginia is engaged to Martel but her former boyfriend, Peter Jamieson, has become obsessed with proving that Martel is a fraud — and Peter has recently been found dead, apparently a suicide by drowning, though Archer quickly suspects otherwise.

Archer begins investigating Martel and quickly discovers that "Francis Martel" is not who he claims to be. The man is actually Roy Fablon — Virginia's own father, who disappeared years earlier after embezzling money and faking his death. Roy Fablon had reinvented himself with a new identity, new accent, and laundered money (the "black money" of the title), and had unknowingly re-entered his own daughter's life while she was engaged to him. Virginia had been too young to remember her father clearly, and the disguise — combined with the passage of time — was sufficient to fool her.

The source of Martel/Fablon's money is gradually exposed as the proceeds of drug trafficking run through a network connected to Latin America. The trail leads Archer into the world of Mexican criminals and corrupt financiers. A key figure is Tish Macready, the wife of a local professor, who has her own hidden connections to the money and the past.

Peter Jamieson's death, initially ruled a suicide, is revealed to be murder. Peter had discovered the truth about Martel's identity and the source of his money, making him a liability. He was killed to keep him silent before he could expose the scheme to Virginia or the authorities.

As Archer peels back the layers, the full tragedy of the Fablon family history comes into focus. Roy Fablon originally fled not just from embezzlement charges but from a web of guilt, failed relationships, and moral compromise that stretched back many years. His return in disguise — unconsciously courting his own daughter — is presented less as a deliberate act of evil than as the grotesque culmination of a life lived in denial and self-deception.

The climax brings together the strands of past crime, reinvented identity, and corrupting money. Roy Fablon/Francis Martel is exposed and confronted. The criminal network behind the drug money is dismantled, at least partially. Virginia is spared the worst of the revelation — that she was engaged to her own father — through a combination of circumstances and Archer's careful handling of the truth.

The novel ends, as most Archer novels do, with a melancholy sense of damage done and only partially repaired. Archer has solved the case but the human wreckage — a dead young man, a father who erased himself, a daughter robbed of both a father and a fiancé — cannot be undone.

THEMES AND NOTES

The title operates on multiple levels: the literal laundered drug money, the moral blackness of lives built on lies, and the corrupting legacy passed between generations. Black Money is considered one of Macdonald's tighter later entries, notable for its near-Oedipal horror at its center and for Archer's characteristic compassion toward even the most compromised characters.

The novel engages Macdonald's recurring preoccupations: the sins of fathers visited on children, the impossibility of truly escaping one's past, Southern California as a landscape of reinvention and denial, and the detective as a figure who unearths buried family secrets with almost therapeutic intent.
