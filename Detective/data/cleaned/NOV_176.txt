Nemesis has Miss Marple investigating, at long distance and years after the fact, the murder of a young woman named Verity Hunt and the wrongful conviction of Jason Rafiel’s son Michael, all orchestrated by Verity’s adoptive aunt, Clotilde Bradbury‑Scott, out of obsessive “mother‑love.” The story runs from Rafiel’s posthumous request and a mysterious garden‑tour coach trip, through an old headmistress’s death and the discovery of Verity’s real grave, to a final night‑time confrontation where Miss Marple exposes Clotilde as a serial murderer who then drinks her own poisoned milk.
Rafiel’s Will and the Mysterious Mission

Some time after the events of A Caribbean Mystery, Miss Marple reads in the paper that millionaire Jason Rafiel has died. Shortly afterward, she is summoned to his solicitors, Broadribb & Schuster, and told that Rafiel left her a bequest of £20,000 on one condition: she must undertake an unspecified “task” connected to a crime, using the qualities he once admired in her and the nickname he gave her, “Nemesis.”

He gives no crime, no names, no instructions—only the promise that further steps have been arranged and that she will know what to do when the time comes. Miss Marple, tempted by the money but also intrigued and flattered by Rafiel’s faith in her, accepts despite having no idea where to begin.

A few days later a second letter from Rafiel (to be sent after his death) arrives, telling her she will soon receive an all‑expenses‑paid ticket on a tour of “Famous Houses and Gardens of Britain,” and hinting there may be danger and that he has provided a “guardian angel.”
The Coach Tour and Miss Temple’s Story

Miss Marple joins the coach tour with a mixed group of about fifteen strangers, including:

    Miss Elizabeth Temple, a retired headmistress.

    Miss Cooke and Miss Barrow, a seemingly fussy, inseparable pair.

    Several couples and individuals who appear to be ordinary tourists.

On the first part of the trip, Miss Marple quietly observes her fellow passengers, convinced that at least some of them are connected to Rafiel’s mystery. She remembers that before any letters arrived she had been approached in St Mary Mead by a woman calling herself Miss Bartlett, companion to a Mrs Hastings, who tried a little too hard to get into Miss Marple’s life as a gardener; Miss Marple now connects that odd encounter with the “guardian angel” hint.

The first real piece of the puzzle comes from Miss Temple. Recognizing Miss Marple as a sympathetic listener, Miss Temple tells her about a former pupil, Verity Hunt, a gentle, artistic girl who had once been in her school. Years earlier, Verity had become engaged to Michael Rafiel, Jason Rafiel’s wild, disreputable son; the match had been opposed by everyone around them.

Verity was living with three middle‑aged sisters in the countryside—the Glynne/Bradbury‑Scott sisters: Lavinia, Clotilde, and Anthea—who were related to her and effectively brought her up. Clotilde, the dominant, charismatic sister, adored Verity with an intense, possessive love and was violently opposed to her marriage to Michael Rafiel. At the same time, Michael was known locally as a “bad lot”—a womanizer with a violent temper—so both sides of Verity’s life were arrayed against the engagement.

Then tragedy struck. Verity vanished and a young woman’s battered body was later found and identified as hers; around the same time a rough local girl, Nora Broad, was also believed murdered. Michael Rafiel was arrested, convicted of murder, and imprisoned, largely on circumstantial evidence and his bad reputation.

Miss Temple, however, has always doubted Michael’s guilt. Recently she was consulted in prison by Professor Wanstead, a criminal psychologist asked to assess Michael; Wanstead told her he believed Michael, though a thoroughly unpleasant young man, was not a murderer.

Miss Marple now has the outline: Jason Rafiel’s son is in prison for murder; Rafiel likely believed in his innocence and is enlisting her posthumously to set things right.
Miss Temple’s “Accident” and the House with Three Sisters

During the tour, Miss Temple is injured in what appears at first to be an accident: a large boulder is pushed or dislodged from a hillside above a path, crashing down onto the track just as she walks below. She survives long enough to be hospitalized but later dies of her injuries.

Before she dies, Miss Temple manages to see Miss Marple one more time and tries to say something important, but her words are fragmented and not fully understandable—she hints at something about Verity and the country house but dies before she can clarify.

Shortly afterward, Miss Marple receives private arrangements (made by Rafiel before his death) to leave the tour and go to stay with Lavinia Glynne and her two sisters, Clotilde and Anthea Bradbury‑Scott, at their old manor house, the “house with three spinsters” Miss Temple had described.

Lavinia, flustered and hospitable, is the nominal owner of the house; Anthea is vague and mild; Clotilde is the formidable central figure—cultured, strong‑willed, and self‑sufficient, with a powerful personality that dominates the household. Clotilde had practically raised Verity after Verity’s parents died; among the three sisters, she is the one who truly loved Verity as a daughter and still keeps her room almost as a shrine.

Miss Marple realizes she has been placed exactly where Rafiel wanted her: inside the household that once contained Verity, in the same village where Michael Rafiel was accused.
The Village, Nora Broad, and Verity’s Past

Living in the village and talking with locals, Miss Marple pieces together the backstory. Years earlier, two young women were at the center of scandal:

    Verity Hunt, the refined, gentle ward of the three sisters, engaged to Michael Rafiel.

    Nora Broad, a coarse village girl with a reputation for loose morals, whom Michael had also known.

Verity had wanted to marry Michael despite Clotilde’s intense opposition; Clotilde believed Michael would ruin Verity’s life and may also have harboured deeper, unspoken reasons for her opposition. Around the time Verity disappeared, a girl’s body was found and identified—largely on clothing and circumstantial grounds—as Verity’s, while Nora Broad was assumed to have been killed elsewhere; Michael was thought to have killed one or both.

Professor Wanstead’s assessment of Michael—spoiled, violent in temper, but not psychopathic or murderous—has convinced Jason Rafiel that his son has been wrongly imprisoned. Rafiel’s posthumous mission to Miss Marple is to find the real killer, which must lie in Verity’s circle rather than in Michael.

Miss Marple studies the Bradbury‑Scott household closely:

    Clotilde is capable, fiercely self‑reliant, and physically strong, with a cold ruthlessness beneath her cultured exterior.

    Lavinia and Anthea orbit around her, dependent and a little afraid.

    Clotilde speaks of Verity with a mixture of adoration and bitterness, as if Verity’s wish to marry Michael were a betrayal.

Miss Marple’s deep “village” experience tells her that Clotilde’s love for Verity was not normal auntly affection but a possessive, almost obsessive attachment: Clotilde wanted Verity to belong to her, not to any man.
Miss Cooke, Miss Barrow, and the Hidden Protection

Miss Cooke and Miss Barrow, who had been on the coach tour, leave the tour early and also appear at the Bradbury‑Scott house, ostensibly by chance. They present themselves as slightly ridiculous spinster travellers, but Miss Marple recognizes them—especially Miss Cooke—as the woman who tried to insert herself into her life earlier under the name “Miss Bartlett.”

Their behaviour is odd: they are watchful, reserved, and seem more interested in Miss Marple’s safety than in sightseeing. As the days pass, Miss Marple deduces that these two are the “guardian angels” Rafiel promised: private protection, likely ex‑nurses or ex‑police, hired to shadow her in case the unknown murderer decides to strike again.

When Miss Marple visits the grounds and old structures of the property, she begins to suspect that Verity’s body is not where everyone thinks it is. There is an abandoned greenhouse on the estate, now overgrown; Miss Marple feels it might conceal something and quietly encourages a search.
Discovery of Verity’s Real Body

Eventually, in the derelict greenhouse, human remains are found: a concealed body that, by clothing and circumstances, can be identified as the real Verity Hunt. This proves that the body identified years earlier as Verity was in fact Nora Broad’s body, misidentified.

The implications are devastating:

    Verity was murdered on the Bradbury‑Scott property and buried there.

    Nora Broad, killed elsewhere, was dressed or otherwise arranged to be taken for Verity.

    The deliberate misidentification directed suspicion onto Michael Rafiel, who had connections with both young women.

Miss Marple now knows that the murderer must be someone with intimate access to Verity, the house, and the local girls—someone who loved Verity, hated the idea of her marriage, and was ruthless enough to kill another girl and sacrifice her body as a decoy. All of that points straight at Clotilde Bradbury‑Scott.

Miss Marple also reinterprets Miss Temple’s death: the “accident” with the boulder looks increasingly like a deliberate attempt to silence a headmistress who was beginning to connect Verity’s story with Michael’s innocence and Rafiel’s suspicions.
Clotilde’s Motive and Past Murders

Miss Marple mentally reconstructs the sequence:

    Clotilde adored Verity as a daughter and, in effect, as a substitute for all other emotional ties; Verity’s desire to marry Michael threatened to take her away.

    Unable to bear losing Verity, Clotilde killed her—probably in a sudden, violent confrontation—then hid her body in the greenhouse.

    To divert suspicion and destroy Michael Rafiel, whom she blamed for “stealing” Verity, Clotilde murdered Nora Broad, a girl with whom Michael had also been involved.

    Clotilde then ensured Nora’s body would be taken as Verity’s—through clothing, jewellery, and manipulation of identification—so that everyone would believe Verity dead and Michael guilty.

Jason Rafiel, years later, came to suspect the truth through Professor Wanstead’s report and fragments of information, but he was too ill and too distant to act himself. He therefore designed this elaborate posthumous plan: bring Miss Marple into the orbit of Verity’s past, confident that her “village” sense and moral ruthlessness would make her his instrument of Nemesis.

Clotilde, realizing that Miss Temple and now Miss Marple are following dangerous lines of thought, has already pushed one boulder; she is fully capable of killing again to protect her secret.
The Poisoned Milk and Midnight Confrontation

After the discovery of the body, tensions in the house rise. Miss Cooke and Miss Barrow visit the Bradbury‑Scott home for coffee; during the conversation, Miss Cooke comments that coffee would keep Miss Marple awake at night. Clotilde warmly insists on sending Miss Marple a glass of warm milk at bedtime instead.​

Miss Marple accepts the tray but does not drink the milk, suspecting that Clotilde may try to eliminate her. Before Miss Cooke and Miss Barrow leave, they secretly slip a whistle into Miss Marple’s hand—a signal they will respond to if she is in danger.​

At three in the morning, Clotilde comes silently into Miss Marple’s bedroom, expecting to find her asleep or dead from the milk. Miss Marple switches on the light and calmly tells her she did not drink it, knowing it was poisoned.​

Miss Marple then lays out the full accusation:

    Clotilde killed Verity, whom she loved too passionately to let go.

    She buried Verity in the greenhouse.

    To frame Michael, she murdered Nora Broad and arranged for her body to be misidentified as Verity’s.

    She later killed Miss Temple by pushing the boulder when she realized Miss Temple knew or suspected the truth.

Facing exposure and realizing that her entire motive—her obsessive, quasi‑maternal love for Verity—has been understood and morally condemned, Clotilde moves toward Miss Marple, evidently intending to kill her as well. At that moment Miss Marple blows the whistle.

Miss Cooke and Miss Barrow, who have been nearby precisely for this contingency, rush in; Clotilde, seeing that she is trapped, picks up the glass of milk—the same poisoned milk meant for Miss Marple—and drinks it herself. She collapses and dies, choosing suicide rather than arrest and trial.
Aftermath: Michael, Rafiel’s Bequest, and Nemesis

With Clotilde’s death and the discovery of Verity’s real body, the case against Michael Rafiel can be re‑examined. The evidence now shows that:

    The corpse used to convict him was not Verity but Nora Broad.

    The real Verity was murdered at the Bradbury‑Scott house by someone else.

    Miss Temple’s “accident” was another murder in the same chain.

Professor Wanstead’s professional assessment, combined with Miss Marple’s investigation, paves the way for Michael’s conviction to be questioned, and Miss Marple has fulfilled Jason Rafiel’s last request by bringing justice—Nemesis—to the person truly responsible.

Miss Marple is awarded the £20,000 Rafiel promised, money she can now use to help friends and relations; more importantly, she has vindicated Rafiel’s faith in her as a “gentle lamb” with a lion’s heart when justice is at stake. The story closes with Miss Marple reflecting that she and Rafiel shared a certain ruthlessness in pursuit of justice, and that through his posthumous game she has acted as his chosen instrument of Nemesis against Clotilde Bradbury‑Scott’s tragic, possessive crimes.
