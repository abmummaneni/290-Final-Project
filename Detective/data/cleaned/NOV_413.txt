--- BACKGROUND AND SETTING ---

"The Great Shelby Holmes," published in 2017, is the first book in Elizabeth
Eulberg's middle-grade mystery series reimagining Sherlock Holmes and John
Watson as contemporary children in New York City's Harlem neighborhood. The
premise is a direct gender-swap and age-down of the classic characters: Shelby
Holmes is a nine-year-old girl detective of extraordinary observational and
deductive ability, and John Watson is an eleven-year-old boy, newly arrived in
the neighborhood, who becomes her friend, chronicler, and partner.

The book is aimed at middle-grade readers (roughly ages 8–12) and is warm,
funny, and genuinely clever. Eulberg constructs the Holmesian elements with
care: Shelby's deductions are fair and logical, her methods are clearly derived
from the original canon, and the pleasure of watching someone see more than
everyone else around them is fully present.

--- JOHN WATSON ARRIVES IN HARLEM ---

John Watson is eleven years old. His father is a doctor who has recently
relocated the family to Harlem, and John is starting fresh at a new school
without friends or familiarity. He is athletic, friendly, and — though he
wouldn't describe himself this way — a good observer of people.

John's family has moved into a brownstone apartment building. Their downstairs
neighbor is a small, intense nine-year-old named Shelby Holmes, who immediately
makes it clear that she knows a remarkable amount about John from a ten-second
observation: the state of his shoes, his posture, the calluses on his hand,
and a dozen other details add up, for Shelby, to a complete sketch of who he
is and where he has come from.

John is simultaneously startled and fascinated. Shelby is unlike anyone he has
ever met. She is also, it quickly becomes clear, socially oblivious in ways
that make her difficult but never unkind — she is not malicious, just operating
on a completely different frequency from most people her age.

--- SHELBY'S CHARACTER AND METHOD ---

Shelby is established as a genuine child detective — not in the adventure-story
sense but in the observational, deductive sense. She notices everything. She
processes what she notices into conclusions that other people could reach if
they paid attention, but almost no one pays Shelby's quality of attention.

She is also established as having the characteristic Holmesian limitations:
she is not good at ordinary social interaction, she has a very small number
of genuine friends (essentially none before John), and she has the detective's
focus that makes conventional childhood activities feel like noise. She is
not miserable — she is genuinely comfortable in her own mind — but she is
genuinely solitary.

Shelby's mother is present and warm, if occasionally exasperated. Shelby has
a dog, Watson — yes, a dog named Watson — who is established as a comic
presence throughout the series.

--- THE MISSING DOG CASE ---

The main mystery of the novel involves a dog — specifically, a prize-winning
show dog named Sir Arthur Conan Doyle (a joke that Eulberg extends throughout
the book with the kind of affectionate self-awareness that characterizes the
series). The dog belongs to a woman in the neighborhood, Mrs. Jones, and it
has gone missing.

Mrs. Jones is distraught. The dog is valuable — both financially and personally —
and his disappearance is suspicious given the timing before a major dog show
at which he was expected to compete.

Shelby is hired (for a modest fee, naturally) to find the dog. John, who has
nothing better to do and who is increasingly drawn into Shelby's orbit, agrees
to help. This is effectively the establishment of their partnership.

--- THE INVESTIGATION ---

Shelby's investigation of the missing dog proceeds through the neighborhood
with characteristic Holmesian thoroughness. She interviews Mrs. Jones, examines
the location from which the dog disappeared, identifies the specific traces left
by whoever took him, and begins to map the possible motives.

Dog theft in the context of dog shows involves a particular set of motivations:
eliminating a competitor, acquiring a valuable animal for profit, or spite.
Shelby examines each possibility with the focused pleasure of a child who finds
puzzles genuinely fun.

John functions as a Watson in the classic sense: he asks the questions the
reader is asking, he occasionally provides a practical perspective that Shelby
has overlooked, and he documents the investigation in the notebook that will
become the basis of his accounts of Shelby's cases throughout the series.

The investigation takes them through various neighborhood locations and
introduces them to various characters — some of whom are directly relevant
to the case and some of whom are the texture of Harlem community life that
Eulberg renders with real warmth.

--- THE GUILTY PARTY ---

The dog was taken by a competitor in the show world — someone whose own animal
was unlikely to win against Sir Arthur Conan Doyle and who decided that
removing the competition was the most reliable strategy. The specific person
responsible is identified through Shelby's deductions: a combination of physical
evidence from the crime scene, behavioral observation of the suspects, and
a logical process of elimination.

The solution is entirely fair by middle-grade mystery standards: the clues
are present in the narrative, and readers who pay Shelby-level attention will
find them. The guilty party is not a violent criminal but someone whose pettiness
and competitive anxiety led them to do something genuinely wrong.

--- THE RECOVERY AND RESOLUTION ---

Sir Arthur Conan Doyle is found and returned to Mrs. Jones. The guilty party
is confronted with what Shelby has discovered and appropriate consequences
follow — not dramatic courtroom consequences but the more proportionate
consequences of a neighborhood case handled by a nine-year-old detective.

The dog show proceeds. The outcome is appropriately satisfying.

--- THE PARTNERSHIP ESTABLISHED ---

By the novel's end, John and Shelby's partnership is established as the series'
central dynamic. John has found, unexpectedly, exactly the kind of friendship
he didn't know he needed: someone who sees the world completely differently
from everyone else and who, in the process of working with her, makes John see
it differently too. Shelby has found, unexpectedly, something she did not know
she needed either: someone who pays attention to her not as a phenomenon or
a prodigy but as a person.

The balance of their relationship — Shelby's deductive brilliance, John's
social intelligence and steady good nature, their genuine mutual respect across
the age gap — is the series' great pleasure, and Eulberg establishes it fully
in this first book.

--- NOTES ON THE SERIES ---

"The Great Shelby Holmes" is notable for the specificity of its Harlem setting
(the neighborhood is drawn with warmth and detail), the genuine quality of its
mystery construction (the clues are fair, the solution is logical), and the
pleasure of the Holmes-Watson dynamic transposed to a contemporary child-world
without losing what makes it work. Eulberg is clearly fond of the original
canon and deploys its elements with appreciation rather than mere imitation.
