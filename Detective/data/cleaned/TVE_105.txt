--- BACKGROUND AND CONTEXT ---

"Line of Duty" Series 2 was broadcast on BBC Two in February–March 2014.
It consists of six episodes directed by Peter Travis and stars Adrian Dunbar
as Superintendent Ted Hastings, Vicky McClure as Detective Sergeant Kate Fleming,
and Martin Compston as Detective Sergeant Steve Arnott, with Keeley Hawes
joining as the series' central subject, Detective Inspector Lindsay Denton.

Series 2 follows directly from the template established in Series 1 but
significantly expands the show's ambition: the investigation is more complex,
the moral territory is more uncertain, and the specific question of institutional
corruption within the police is developed with greater sophistication. Where
Series 1's Tony Gates was a genuinely corrupt officer whose specific wrongdoing
was the case's subject, Series 2's Lindsay Denton is a more ambiguous figure —
someone whose guilt or innocence is sustained as a genuine question through
much of the series, and who the viewer is never entirely certain about until
the evidence is fully assembled.

The series is also notable for introducing the figure of "Tommy Hunter" and
the organised crime network that will become increasingly significant to the
show's overarching mythology in later series.

--- THE INCITING INCIDENT: THE AMBUSH ---

Series 2 opens with a catastrophic event: a police convoy transporting a
protected witness is ambushed. The attack is devastating — officers are killed,
the protected witness is killed, and the convoy is destroyed with a precision
and foreknowledge that immediately suggests an inside job. Someone with knowledge
of the convoy's route, timing, and security arrangements provided that information
to the people who carried out the ambush.

The only survivor of senior rank from the convoy is Detective Inspector Lindsay
Denton, who is found with minor injuries at the scene. She is the immediate
suspect for the leak: she had knowledge of the convoy details, she survived
when others did not, and the specific circumstances of her survival raise
immediate questions.

AC-12 is assigned to investigate whether the ambush was facilitated by police
corruption, and Denton is at the center of that investigation from the first
episode.

--- LINDSAY DENTON ---

Keeley Hawes's Denton is one of the finest performances in the series and
one of the most complex character creations in British crime drama of the
decade. She is a detective of genuine capability and genuine frustration: a
woman who has been working in the police force for years and who has experienced
the specific forms of institutional obstruction and gender-based undermining
that women in senior police positions encountered in the period. She is not
warm, but she is not without feeling. She is a woman who has learned to protect
herself in specific ways from a professional environment that has not always
treated her fairly.

Whether she is guilty of facilitating the ambush is Series 2's central and
sustained question. Hawes plays Denton with extraordinary ambiguity: her
responses to interrogation, her specific behavior under investigation, and
her specific choices through the series are all consistent with both guilt
and innocence, which is the performance's most significant achievement. The
viewer cannot be certain, and neither, for much of the series, can AC-12.

--- THE INVESTIGATION STRUCTURE ---

AC-12's investigation of Denton proceeds through the show's characteristic
mechanisms: surveillance, financial analysis, interview sequences, and the
specific management of informants and witnesses within the police world. The
formal interview sequences — the established signature of the "Line of Duty"
format — are deployed with the same precision and tension as in Series 1, and
Hawes's performance in these sequences is the series' dramatic centerpiece.

The specific techniques that the interview sequences use — the specific legal
and procedural frameworks, the specific strategies of the interviewing officers,
and the specific responses of the subject — are rendered with the accuracy that
Mercurio's research consistently provides. The interview room is once again
the most important location in the series, and the sustained duration of the
interview sequences — which run longer than virtually any other British drama
would hold them — creates a specific kind of pressure that is unique to this show.

--- ARNOTT'S SPECIFIC ROLE ---

Steve Arnott's investigation in Series 2 involves him in a direct personal
engagement with Lindsay Denton that has complications beyond the professional.
A specific interaction between Arnott and Denton — in the context of the
investigation — creates a personal dimension to his involvement that has both
professional and human consequences. The specific nature of this interaction,
and how it affects both Arnott's investigation and his own standing within
AC-12, is one of the series' most significant secondary threads.

His management of this complication — and its eventual impact on the investigation
and on his relationship with Hastings and Fleming — is characteristic of the
Arnott arc across the series: a man of genuine integrity who occasionally makes
decisions that complicate his professional position, and who faces the specific
consequences of those decisions within an institution that does not easily
forgive lapses.

--- TOMMY HUNTER AND THE ORGANISED CRIME NETWORK ---

Series 2 introduces Tommy Hunter, a significant organised crime figure whose
specific relationship to police corruption is central to the investigation's
wider context. Hunter's network is connected to the ambush — the attack on
the convoy was conducted on behalf of or in the service of interests connected
to him — and his specific relationship to individual officers within the police
is one of the mechanisms through which the corruption that AC-12 is investigating
operates.

Hunter's introduction in Series 2 establishes a thread that will run through
subsequent series: the specific organised crime network with which corrupt police
officers are connected, and the specific figure at the centre of that network.
The mythology of "H" — the high-ranking corrupt officer whose identity is
the overarching mystery of later series — has its conceptual roots in what
Series 2 establishes about the relationship between organised crime and police
corruption.

Hunter's specific relationship with certain officers — and the specific history
of how that relationship was formed and what obligations it generated — is
part of what the investigation must establish to understand the full picture
of how the ambush was possible.

--- KATE FLEMING'S INVESTIGATION ---

Kate Fleming's role in Series 2 involves a different kind of undercover work
from Series 1. She is deployed in contexts that require her specific capabilities —
her ability to build relationships and extract information — in the investigation
of the ambush and its surrounding network. Her specific interactions with
certain figures connected to the case, and the specific evidence she gathers
through these interactions, are significant contributions to the investigation's
development.

The Fleming-Hastings-Arnott team dynamic — now more established than in Series
1, with the specific history of the Gates investigation as shared background —
operates with the efficiency and the specific trust of a team that has been
through difficult territory together. The specific ways in which their collective
expertise and their individual qualities are deployed in Series 2 reflect the
show's developing understanding of what makes this specific team effective.

--- THE INVESTIGATION'S DEVELOPMENT ---

The AC-12 investigation proceeds across six episodes with the sustained procedural
attention that defines the show. The specific mechanics of establishing whether
Denton was the source of the convoy information — the financial analysis, the
communications records, the specific witness accounts from the convoy's survivors
and the ambush's perpetrators — are assembled with the accuracy and the attention
to institutional detail that has been the show's consistent standard.

Key developments include:

The establishment of whether specific financial transactions in Denton's history
— unexplained deposits, movements of money that don't fit her salary — constitute
evidence of payment for the convoy information.

The investigation of Denton's specific relationships within the police — who
she knew, who she was connected to, and what specific individuals might have
had access to and motive to use the convoy information she possessed.

The specific evidence from the ambush itself — forensic, testimonial, and
the specific intelligence gathered about who carried it out and who directed them.

The Tommy Hunter connection — how the ambush connects to his network and what
specific individuals within the police were the conduit between Hunter's
organisation and the convoy information.

--- THE TRUTH ABOUT DENTON ---

Series 2's central dramatic question — is Denton guilty? — is resolved in the
final episodes with the complexity that the series has sustained throughout.

The full truth involves a specific combination of factors: Denton's specific
culpability is real but is not what the investigation initially assumed, and
the specific nature of her involvement — what she did, why she did it, and
what she did not do — is more nuanced than simple corruption in the Gates mode.

She was manipulated. She was in possession of information that she used or
allowed to be used in ways that contributed to the ambush, but the specific
circumstances of how this happened — the specific pressure she was under, the
specific relationship that was exploited to extract the information — are
established through the investigation in ways that complicate the simple
narrative of a corrupt officer.

This does not mean she is innocent. The investigation establishes her specific
culpability within the full picture of what happened. But it establishes it
in a way that is more morally complex than straightforward corruption, and
Hawes's performance throughout has been preparing the viewer for this complexity.

--- HASTINGS AND INSTITUTIONAL INTEGRITY ---

Ted Hastings's role in Series 2 develops his character significantly beyond
the Series 1 establishment. His specific management of the investigation —
the specific decisions he makes about how to pursue Denton, what pressure
to apply and when, and how to handle the complications that arise — reveals
more of who he is and what he believes about the job of AC-12.

His specific conviction that the police cannot police themselves without genuine
independence in the anti-corruption function — that the specific mandate of
AC-12 is not simply procedural but moral — is given its most direct expression
in Series 2. The specific ways in which institutional pressure attempts to
limit or redirect the investigation, and Hastings's specific resistance to
that pressure, are central to his character arc in this series.

--- RESOLUTION ---

The investigation of the ambush and Lindsay Denton's role in it is resolved
across the final episodes. The full picture — Denton's specific culpability,
the Tommy Hunter network's involvement, the specific corrupt officers whose
conduct enabled the ambush — is established through the accumulated investigation.

Denton faces the specific legal consequences of what the investigation establishes
about her conduct. The Tommy Hunter thread — and the wider question of the
organised crime network with police connections that the ambush has exposed —
is addressed to the degree that Series 2 can address it, with the understanding
that the full resolution of that thread will require subsequent series.

--- THE SERIES' PLACE IN THE LINE OF DUTY ARC ---

Series 2 establishes the show's overarching mythology more explicitly than
Series 1 did. The Tommy Hunter network, and the specific suggestion of a highly
placed corrupt officer (or officers) within the police whose identity is not
yet established, sets up the long-form question that will drive the show through
six series. Series 2 plants specific seeds — specific characters, specific
connections, specific unanswered questions — that the later series will develop.

The show's ambition is visible in Series 2 in a way that Series 1, necessarily
more focused on establishing itself, could not fully achieve: "Line of Duty"
is not simply a series of individual investigations but a sustained project
of examining institutional corruption across multiple connected investigations,
and Series 2 is the first episode in which that project's full scope becomes
visible.

--- NOTES ON THE SERIES ---

Series 2 of "Line of Duty" is a significant advance on an already strong debut.
Keeley Hawes's Lindsay Denton is one of the finest guest-star performances
in British television drama and one of the most carefully constructed morally
ambiguous figures in the crime drama of the period. The expansion of the show's
institutional mythology — the Tommy Hunter network, the suggestion of a highly
placed corrupt figure — gives the series an ambition that makes it more than
the sum of its individual investigations. And the interview sequences, fully
mature in their technique by this second series, remain unmatched in British
television for their specific kind of sustained, procedurally grounded dramatic
tension.
