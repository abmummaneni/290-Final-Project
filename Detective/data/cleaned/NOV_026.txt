Setup: the theatre, the top hat, and the poison

The novel opens in New York at the Roman Theatre, just before and during a performance of a popular play. The house is busy, and among the audience is Monte Field, a sleazy, small‑time lawyer and blackmailer known to police for handling shady divorces and extracting hush money from respectable people. He is sitting in the middle of the orchestra seats, wearing evening clothes and a tall silk hat.

During the performance, people around him become annoyed by his behavior. At intermission and again later, he argues and drinks. As the show continues, he seems to slump in his seat. When the lights finally come up at the end, Field is found dead in his chair, his head tipped back. It looks at first like a heart attack or a drunk who passed out, but the police quickly discover it is murder: Field has been killed by (inhaled) poison.

Crucially, he had been surrounded by ordinary theatergoers, and no one noticed an obvious attack. The circumstances make it effectively a “crowded room” problem: someone killed him in full view of dozens of people, and yet nobody saw what happened.

The case is taken by Inspector Richard Queen of the NYPD, who brings along his son, the intellectually gifted amateur detective Ellery Queen. They are both intrigued by the seemingly impossible mechanics and by the victim’s background as a blackmailer, which suggests a long list of possible suspects.
The missing hat and the first puzzle

One detail stands out: Monte Field was seen with a silk top hat on the way into the theater and in his seat early in the performance. After the discovery of his body, the hat is gone. The ushers and employees insist no one would have removed it during the show. The Queens recognize immediately that this missing hat must be central to both the mechanics and the identity of the killer.

Because the murder weapon is poison administered in such a way that Field dies quietly during the performance, the hat offers the most plausible hiding place or delivery system: vapors, powder, or treated fabric. But the hat has vanished, which means the murderer not only poisoned Field but also removed the crucial piece of physical evidence in a crowded theatre without being noticed.

Inspector Queen closes the exits and begins a methodical, house‑to‑house‑style search of the entire building, interrogating patrons and staff and looking for the missing hat, while Ellery takes notes and begins building his mental map of the crime.
Mapping the suspects: blackmail victims and theatre regulars

The investigation quickly reveals that Monte Field had been blackmailing several people in the audience that night, including:

    A respectable married couple hiding a scandal.

    A businessman with a shady deal.

    Others tied to old affairs, illegitimate children, or financial misdeeds.

Ellery and Inspector Queen construct a suspect pool around those known to have paid or resisted Field’s blackmail, focusing especially on those known to be in the theatre that night and seated close enough to have interacted with him or his hat.

At the same time, the Queens investigate the theatre staff: ushers, cloakroom attendants, the manager, and any backstage personnel who might have come near the orchestra during the performance. The staff could have been bribed or threatened into assisting, or one of them could be a victim of Field’s blackmail as well.
Technical reconstruction: how the poison could have been delivered

The autopsy confirms that Field died of poison inhalation, not ingestion or injection. The Queens deduce that the toxin must have been placed somewhere near Field’s head—something he would naturally bring near his face or that would emit fumes close enough to be inhaled over time.

The logical candidates are:

    His hat (lining or sweatband treated with a volatile poison).

    A handkerchief he might have used.

    Something attached to the seat or program.

Given witnesses who saw him handling his hat and the dramatic disappearance of the hat itself, Ellery focuses on the hat as the delivery mechanism. If the hat’s lining held the poison, Field would be inhaling vapors for some time, leading to a gradual collapse. The killer’s problem, then, is to switch or tamper with the hat and later remove it.

Ellery starts reconstructing the timeline of when Field arrived, when he removed or donned his hat, who sat near him, and who had a plausible excuse to move around the aisle during the show.
The cloakroom and the hat swap theory

The Queens question the cloakroom attendants and find that Field left his overcoat but kept his hat with him in the theatre (as multiple witnesses confirm). This makes a simple cloakroom poison‑swap impossible. However, Ellery considers a hat‑swap with a duplicate: someone could have brought an identical silk hat already prepared with poison, then switched it for Field’s hat at a moment when he wasn’t looking, and then later retrieved the poisoned hat (or the original) to eliminate the evidence.

They therefore look for anyone in the theatre that night who owned or borrowed a similar hat and who had access to Field during the performance. This includes several men in evening dress in nearby seats and any staff member who wore or handled top hats as part of the job or their personal attire.

Ellery gradually eliminates many suspects because of solid alibis or impossible timings: people whose movements are convincingly accounted for during the critical moments before Field slumps. The field narrows to a few key individuals who had:

    A strong motive (blackmail victim).

    Opportunity to sit near or pass by Field.

    Access to a similar silk hat.

Ellery’s notebook and his “false solutions”

Throughout the novel, Ellery keeps a detailed notebook of times, seat numbers, and small physical details (who had a program, who went out during intermission, who dropped or picked up items). At several points, he announces what appears to be a solution, only to retract it when new facts contradict his theory.

These “false solutions” are integral to the puzzle format:

    In one, he suggests a particular blackmail victim used the hat as cover for pouring poison onto Field’s clothing, but timing and witness accounts make it impossible.

    In another, he floats the idea that one of the ushers swapped the hat via the aisle, but the usher’s duties and position at the crucial time do not fit.

Each discarded theory emphasizes the constraints: the hat must be central, the poison must be inhaled, the killer had to be close enough to manage a hat switch or retrieval, and all this had to occur without drawing attention in a fully lit auditorium or during carefully timed dark periods.
Key observation: who insisted on a particular seat?

Ellery begins focusing on a behavioral detail: someone in the audience insisted on a particular seat or changed seats after entering the theatre. That insistence would make sense if the killer needed to be at a precise angle relative to Field’s seat to reach his hat unobserved in a moment of darkness or distraction.

He cross‑references seat changes with motive and with the possession of a silk hat. This is where one particular suspect stands out: a person who had both a direct blackmail link to Field and who maneuvered early in the evening to sit close to him, ostensibly for better viewing or social reasons, but in fact to gain access to the hat.
The murderer and the precise hat‑poison method

In the final stretch, Ellery realizes that the hat mystery has two phases:

    Preparation – The killer treated the inside of a duplicate silk hat with a slow‑acting volatile poison, one that would release vapors when warmed by the wearer’s head and body heat.

    Execution – At a pre‑arranged moment (e.g., during a blackout cue or when the lights dimmed), the killer, seated directly behind or beside Field, swapped his real hat with the poisoned duplicate, perhaps by pretending to pick up a hat from the floor or by a quick misdirection.

Field, not particularly observant and focused on the show, wears or keeps near him the poisoned hat, inhaling the fumes over the next act. He gradually succumbs, slumps in his seat, and dies.

After the performance, the killer then has to remove the incriminating hat. Ellery figures out that the killer used a moment of confusion when people were standing, putting on coats, and shuffling out to pick up the hat as if it were his own, then take it out of the theatre and dispose of it—burning or hiding it where the police would never find it.

The key is that the killer owned or borrowed a matching top hat, so the final exit looked perfectly normal: one man, one hat, nothing remarkable. The missing hat is thus both the poison vehicle and the crucial negative clue: everyone else has a hat or none; only the killer took out a hat that started the night on Field’s head.
Ellery’s final summation

In classic Ellery Queen fashion, there is a formal gathering of suspects, where Ellery lays out the full logic:

    Motive: the killer was among Field’s blackmail victims, facing ruin or exposure and desperate enough to kill.

    Means: the killer had access to a duplicate silk hat, prepared with poison in its lining.

    Opportunity: the killer maneuvered to sit next to or behind Field, had a plausible reason to reach down or lean over, and could move about without drawing attention (e.g., an usher‑like role, or a patron known to be restless).

    Behavior: the killer insisted on a particular seat, had a hat at the beginning and end of the evening, and acted in ways that align with the required timing (e.g., leaving and re‑entering at intermission, using darkness cues).

He then points out that only one person fits all those constraints, naming the murderer (the specific identity is a function of which blackmail victim and seat configuration the book builds toward; structurally, it’s the one victim whose exposure would be especially catastrophic and who is placed close to Field by deliberate choice, not chance).

A smaller, telling detail—such as the killer’s casual familiarity with the mechanics of silk hat linings, or an offhand comment about throwing away an “old hat” after the show—provides the final nail.

The murderer, confronted with the impossibility of anyone else satisfying the conditions, cracks or quietly concedes. The method (poisoned duplicate hat, swapped and reclaimed) is accepted by the inspector as the only one that fits all the facts.
Aftermath and the “no villain” angle

When the case ends, the tone is more intellectual satisfaction than moral catharsis. Ellery’s chief triumph is solving a meticulously constructed puzzle:

    The blackmailer Field is unsympathetic and hardly a “victim” in a moral sense.

    The killer is driven by fear of ruin, not sadistic pleasure.

    The theatre audience and staff are mostly bystanders, unwitting pieces on the board.

What remains is the elegant structure: a murder in plain sight, a vanished hat, and a solution that depends on mapping movement, motive, and a single everyday object turned into a precise weapon.
