NOV_441 — All the Devils Are Here
Louise Penny, 2020
Full Plot Synopsis (Inspector Gamache #16)

SETTING THE STAGE: PARIS


The Gamache Family Gathers

The novel takes place entirely in Paris, a deliberate departure from the series' usual
setting of Three Pines, Quebec. Armand Gamache, Chief Superintendent of the Sûreté du
Québec, and his wife Reine-Marie have traveled to Paris for the birth of their first
grandchild — the child of their son Daniel and his wife Roslyn. Also in Paris is their
daughter Annie, her husband Jean-Guy Beauvoir (Gamache's former second-in-command and now
son-in-law), and their young son Honoré. It is a rare and joyful gathering of the full
family in one of the world's great cities.

Also in Paris is Armand's godfather: Stephen Horowitz, a legendary, enormously wealthy, and
deeply private financier in his nineties, who has long been one of the most powerful and
secretive figures in international finance. Stephen is beloved by Armand, who regards him
as a second father.


Stephen Is Attacked

On the family's first evening together, Stephen is struck by a car outside his apartment
building near the Eiffel Tower and left critically injured in the street. The hit appears
intentional. He is rushed to hospital in a coma, and Armand and Jean-Guy immediately begin
to investigate what happened and why someone would want to kill a ninety-three-year-old man.

In Stephen's apartment, they find that someone has already been through it — someone was
looking for something. What that something is becomes the engine of the entire plot.

THE CONSPIRACY UNFOLDS


GHS Engineering

Armand and Jean-Guy discover that Stephen had recently made an unusual investment: he had
bought a significant stake in GHS Engineering, a massive Paris-based conglomerate with
divisions spanning construction, defense, infrastructure, and technology. The company is
headed by the Plessner family and is deeply enmeshed with European and global institutions.
Stephen, whose investment instincts over decades have been impeccable and always ethically
motivated, had been disturbed by something he discovered within GHS.

That discovery, it becomes clear, is the reason someone tried to kill him.


The Document and the Whistleblower

Stephen had obtained internal documents suggesting that GHS Engineering was knowingly
constructing defective infrastructure — bridges, buildings, dams — in developing nations,
using substandard materials while billing for premium-grade ones. The scale of the fraud is
enormous, affecting projects across Asia, Africa, and South America. Beyond fraud, the
implications are catastrophic: structures are being built to fail, and people will die when
they do.

A GHS insider — a young analyst named Plessner's protégé — had been compiling evidence.
That person is found dead during the investigation, the death staged to look like suicide.
The documents Stephen secured are hidden, and finding them before GHS can retrieve and
destroy them becomes the central mission.


Sébastion Lacroix and the French Angle

Armand's investigation leads him into the world of Paris high finance and its entanglement
with corrupt corporate power. He is assisted by Sébastion Lacroix, a French magistrate,
and operates on French soil without official jurisdiction, relying on his relationships,
his instincts, and Jean-Guy's resourcefulness. The Paris police are largely kept at arm's
length — some out of honest jurisdictional propriety, others because GHS has reach into law
enforcement and government.

Jean-Guy takes on significant personal risk, working undercover threads while also managing
his anxiety about Armand's safety and his own growing understanding of the stakes involved.

CHARACTER THREADS


Annie and Jean-Guy

Annie Gamache-Beauvoir's presence in Paris adds domestic grounding and emotional weight to
the thriller plot. Her relationship with Jean-Guy is warm and strong, a settled partnership,
but the novel puts both of them in increasing danger as the conspiracy closes in. Jean-Guy's
devotion to Armand as both a mentor and now family member is tested as events escalate.


Claude and the Grandchildren

Daniel's wife Roslyn gives birth during the novel, and the arrival of the grandchild serves
as a heartbeat of normalcy and hope running through the darkness of the investigation.
Armand's ability to move between his role as a devoted grandfather and a tenacious investigator
is characteristic of Penny's portrayal of him — he is a man for whom love and duty are not
in opposition but are expressions of the same thing.


Stephen as a Figure of Hidden Depth

As Armand investigates, he learns things about Stephen Horowitz he never knew. Stephen's
wealth was never simply inherited or casually accumulated — it was built with intention, and
some of that intention was the ability to do exactly what he is doing now: expose wrongdoing
at the highest levels. Stephen had been positioning himself for years to be in a place where
he could act when it mattered. His attack was not random. He was targeted because he had
already begun to move against GHS.

THE RESOLUTION


Exposing GHS

Armand and Jean-Guy secure the documents Stephen had hidden. The evidence is sufficient to
expose GHS's systematic fraud and the deliberate use of defective materials in large-scale
infrastructure projects. The scale of the corruption — and the number of powerful European
institutions and individuals implicated — means that the exposure carries global consequences.

The conspirators within GHS who ordered Stephen's killing and the murder of the internal
whistleblower are identified and brought to account. Several key figures within the company's
leadership face arrest.


Stephen Survives

Stephen recovers from his injuries — slowly, imperfectly, at ninety-three — but he survives.
His reunion with Armand is one of the novel's most emotionally resonant scenes. The depth of
their bond, spanning Armand's entire life, is fully articulated for perhaps the first time in
the series.


The Title's Meaning

The title is drawn from Shakespeare's The Tempest: "Hell is empty, and all the devils are
here." It refers to the novel's central argument — that the worst evils are not supernatural
or faraway, but embedded in respectable institutions, wearing the faces of businessmen and
financiers, operating through contracts and investments. The devils of the novel are entirely
human, and they wear suits.


A New Direction

The novel ends with Armand and Jean-Guy's relationship — professional and familial — deepened
by shared danger. Jean-Guy's role in the Sûreté and in Armand's life is further cemented.
The family returns, changed, from Paris. And the series signals that the corrupt networks
exposed here will have ongoing consequences in subsequent books.
