Easter dinner and the setup for murder

Fred and Sheila Merton are a very wealthy, deeply unpleasant couple living in an upscale neighborhood in upstate New York. They have three adult children—Catherine, Dan, and Jenna—who all resent Fred’s cruelty and control, yet depend on their parents’ money and future inheritance. On Easter Sunday, the whole family gathers at the Merton mansion for dinner, along with Irena, the longtime housekeeper and former nanny who still works for the family, and later their aunt Audrey Stancik, Fred’s sister, who lives nearby.

The dinner is tense from the start. Fred is openly abusive: he belittles Dan about losing his place in the family company, needles Jenna about her free‑spirited lifestyle and dependence on parental funds, and subtly controls and humiliates Sheila. Catherine, the eldest and “favorite,” has her own money as a doctor but has always assumed she will inherit the family home and feels entitled to it. During the meal, Fred makes two bombshell announcements: first, he has already sold his company instead of passing it to Dan as planned; second, he and Sheila intend to sell the beloved family house because they believe none of the children deserves it. The revelations devastate Dan, who has built his future around the company, and enrage Catherine, who sees the house as her birthright. Jenna, already treated as the irresponsible youngest child, seethes as well, especially because her financial lifeline from Fred is clearly at risk.

The dinner ends badly, with sharp arguments and everyone leaving in anger—except Jenna, who stays behind after the others go. It’s later revealed that, in this private conversation, Fred tells Jenna that he also plans to change his will so that half of his fortune will go to his sister Audrey. This means there will be far less money for any of the three siblings, and it’s a direct threat to Jenna’s lifestyle and security. The scene plants every major motive: each child has a strong financial and emotional reason to hate Fred and to want him out of the way.
The bodies are found and suspicion falls on the children

A few days after Easter, Irena comes to the Merton house to clean, as usual. When no one answers, she lets herself in and discovers a gruesome scene: Sheila lies dead, killed in what appears to be a relatively quick, “kinder” way (strangling), while Fred has been stabbed repeatedly in a clearly rage‑filled attack. The house has been staged to look like a robbery—drawers pulled open, items scattered—but detectives immediately suspect the break‑in is fake. The brutality of Fred’s injuries suggests personal hatred rather than a random burglar, and nothing important seems truly taken.

The police quickly zero in on the three children as prime suspects. Catherine is outwardly composed and respectable but was furious about losing the house. Dan, the “screw‑up” who has never actually done anything truly wrong, is jobless after Fred spitefully sold the company, leaving Dan deeply in debt and humiliated. Jenna is the bohemian artist who largely lives off parental support and stands to lose the ongoing payments and the inheritance she has been counting on. All three were at Easter dinner, all had big financial stakes, and all had reasons to be angry after that night.

Detectives also look at Irena, who is fiercely loyal to the children and has cleaned up some evidence: she is found to have washed the murder weapon, which complicates things. Audrey, meanwhile, becomes a loud, volatile presence. She claims Fred had told her he was about to change his will so that she would inherit half his wealth, arguing that she deserves it after keeping a family secret for decades—namely, that Fred murdered their abusive father years before. Audrey insists that one of the children must have killed Fred and Sheila to prevent this will change and vows to find and turn in the killer if the police don’t.
The will, the illegitimate child, and everyone’s lies

The lawyer, Walter, eventually gathers the siblings to read the will. To their shock, there has been no change: Fred died before he could alter it, so his estate—around $26 million—is to be split four ways among Catherine, Dan, Jenna, and an illegitimate child no one knew about. Irena and Audrey are each left a substantial fixed amount (around a million dollars each), but not the half of the estate Audrey expected. Audrey is furious, convinced that the murder prevented Fred from signing the new will that would have given her far more. The revelation that Fred had an affair and an unknown child further deepens the family’s dysfunction and suspicions.

Walter reveals that the illegitimate child is Rose Cutter, a woman connected to Dan through a shady business deal that involved fraud. Rose is called in and learns that Fred was her father and that she’s a beneficiary. She’s angry at having been lied to about her parentage and now also faces legal trouble when the police bring up her role in the fraudulent deal with Dan. Rose herself becomes another possible motive source and another person hurt by Fred’s secrecy and cruelty.

The police keep pulling on threads of everyone’s alibis, and lies start to unravel. Dan’s wife, Lisa, lies for him, telling the police he was home all night, when in fact he went out for a drive. Neighbors contradict Dan’s story, noting his car leaving at odd hours, and detectives conclude Dan has no solid alibi and is under immense financial pressure. Jenna’s boyfriend, Jake, also lies initially, claiming he stayed with her the night of the murders, but it later comes out that he actually left and went home, leaving Jenna alone and free to move around. Catherine, despite projecting the image of the stable, dutiful daughter, is also hiding things: we eventually learn that after leaving Easter dinner, she later went back to the house and discovered her parents already dead before anyone else knew, then left without calling the police, terrified and unsure what to do.

As the investigation continues, Audrey pushes her own line of inquiry, focusing on the money she lost. She’s convinced one of the kids killed their parents to stop Fred changing the will and to secure their inheritance. The detectives keep circling between Dan—with his resentment over the company, debts, and movements that night—and Jenna, who had reason to be furious about both her financial dependence and Fred’s plan to give half his fortune to Audrey, and whose alibi has completely fallen apart.
The truth about Jenna and the murders

In the final reveal, the narrative shows what really happened on Easter night. After dinner, when the others left, Jenna stayed and fought with her father about money. Hearing that he would give half his wealth to Audrey and threaten her support pushed her over the edge. Jenna, who had long been treated as the flaky, irresponsible youngest child, made a cold, calculated decision: she would kill her parents. Her appearance as the free‑spirited artist had masked a capacity for anger and ruthless planning that no one suspected.

Once she decided, Jenna acted methodically. She went to Dan’s house and took items from his garage—coveralls and tools—so that any trace evidence might make it look as if Dan were involved. She then went to Irena’s place and took Irena’s car, further muddying the trail by using someone else’s vehicle. Armed with weapons from the Merton home and dressed in gear that could be tied back to Dan, she returned to the house and murdered her parents: Sheila relatively quickly, and Fred in a frenzy of stabbing that expressed her pent‑up rage. Afterward, she ransacked the house to create the illusion of a robbery gone wrong.

Jenna didn’t stop at staging the scene. She carefully gathered the bloody clothes, tools, and other incriminating items into a bag and took them to a construction site at a friend’s farm. There, knowing that concrete was about to be poured for a new floor, she hid the evidence so it would be entombed and essentially impossible to find later. This level of premeditation shows just how deliberate and unemotional she was about killing her parents and framing others.

Later, as Audrey’s private digging begins to edge closer to the truth, Jenna goes even further to protect herself. She poisons Audrey by putting antifreeze in her iced tea, intending to stop her aunt’s investigation permanently. In doing so, she reveals almost no remorse: her primary concern is preserving her freedom and access to the money she feels entitled to.
Aftermath and the novel’s tone

By the end, it becomes clear that nearly everyone in this family has lied, concealed motives, or tried to manipulate the situation, but Jenna is the actual killer, responsible not only for the brutal double homicide but also for trying to murder Audrey. The detectives’ early sense that the crime scene was staged and that the killer had a close, emotional connection to Fred proves correct. The elaborate suspicion cast on Dan, Catherine’s secrecy about finding the bodies, Rose’s anger, Irena’s cleanup, and Audrey’s bitterness all serve to cloud the truth until the final chapters.

The title, Not a Happy Family, proves apt: the Mertons are a study in wealth, entitlement, resentment, and buried secrets, where love is transactional and nearly everyone is thinking about money even in the wake of murder. The novel closes with the case solved but leaves a sour taste—no cathartic justice, just the exposure of how far someone from a privileged, “respectable” family will go when threatened with losing power and security.
