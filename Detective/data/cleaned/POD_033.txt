FULL PODCAST SYNOPSIS — SPOILERS INCLUDED

--- BACKGROUND AND CONTEXT ---

"Bone Valley" is an investigative podcast hosted and produced by Gilbert King,
a Pulitzer Prize-winning author (his book "Devil in the Grove" won the 2013
Pulitzer for general non-fiction), released in 2022 through Lava for Good
Podcast Network. The podcast covers the case of Leo Schofield, a Florida man
convicted in 1989 of the murder of his wife Michelle Schofield and who has
maintained his innocence ever since.

The case is one of the most troubling wrongful conviction cases in recent
American legal history: there is substantial evidence suggesting that a serial
killer named Jeremy Scott — who is serving life in prison for other murders —
committed the crime for which Schofield was convicted. The podcast's
investigation of this evidence, and of the specific failures of the Florida
justice system that have kept Schofield imprisoned despite this evidence,
is one of the most rigorous pieces of true crime journalism in the podcast era.

--- MICHELLE SCHOFIELD'S MURDER ---

Michelle Schofield was twenty years old when she was murdered in February 1987
in Polk County, Florida — a rural area in the center of the state known as the
"bone valley" of the title, so named for the phosphate mining operations that
have scarred the landscape and that have historically made the area economically
dependent on extractive industry.

Michelle's body was found near a rural road. She had been stabbed to death.
Her husband Leo Schofield, also twenty years old at the time, was a young man
who worked at a fast-food restaurant. He and Michelle had a young baby.

--- LEO SCHOFIELD'S CONVICTION ---

Leo Schofield was arrested and charged with Michelle's murder. The case against
him was circumstantial — there was no physical evidence directly linking him
to the killing. The prosecution's case relied primarily on:

The statistical fact that a woman is most likely to be murdered by an intimate
partner, making the husband the natural first suspect.

Testimony about the state of the Schofields' marriage — evidence of arguments,
financial stress, and marital friction that was presented as establishing
motive.

Inconsistencies in Leo's account of the night Michelle disappeared.

Leo was convicted in 1989 and sentenced to life in prison. He has maintained
his innocence throughout his imprisonment — through decades of appeals, through
changing legal representation, and through the emergence of evidence that
points to a different perpetrator.

--- JEREMY SCOTT ---

Jeremy Scott is a Florida man who is currently serving life in prison for
the murder of another young woman in circumstances remarkably similar to
Michelle Schofield's death. Scott killed his victim by the same method —
stabbing — in the same general area of Florida, in a case that followed
patterns consistent with serial predation.

More significantly: Jeremy Scott has confessed to killing Michelle Schofield.
He told people — including a former girlfriend whose account is central to
the podcast's evidence — that he killed her. The confession was not made in
circumstances where Scott was under immediate legal pressure to confess; it
was a disclosure made in the context of a relationship, one of several
disclosures Scott made about violent acts.

The physical evidence from Michelle's murder is also consistent with Scott's
methodology in his confirmed murder: the specific nature of the wounds, the
location of the body, and the manner in which she was left.

--- THE FAILURE OF THE FLORIDA COURTS ---

The case's most disturbing dimension is what has happened in the legal system
after Scott's confession and the other evidence pointing away from Schofield
became known. The Florida courts have repeatedly refused to grant Schofield
a new trial or to take sufficient account of the new evidence.

The specific legal mechanisms available to challenge a conviction in Florida —
post-conviction motions, appeals, requests for review based on newly discovered
evidence — have been used by Schofield's advocates and have been consistently
denied or dismissed. The courts have found various procedural or substantive
grounds to decline the relief that the evidence seems to demand.

The podcast examines the specific decisions in each proceeding with Gilbert
King's characteristic rigor. He is particularly interested in the gap between
what the evidence shows and what the legal system has been willing to do with it —
the specific ways that procedural constraints, institutional inertia, and the
difficulty of overturning convictions in American law operate to keep a possibly
innocent man in prison.

--- GILBERT KING'S INVESTIGATION ---

King's investigation draws on:

The legal record: the original trial transcripts, the post-conviction
proceedings, the appeals court decisions.

Interviews with Leo Schofield, who speaks at length about his experience of
the trial, his imprisonment, and his understanding of what happened to Michelle.

Interviews with Scott's former girlfriend and others who heard Scott's
disclosures about Michelle's murder.

Interviews with lawyers, investigators, and advocates who have worked on
Schofield's behalf.

Physical evidence analysis from the original case and from Scott's confirmed
murder, examining the similarities.

--- LEO SCHOFIELD IN PRISON ---

Leo Schofield has now spent more than thirty-five years in prison for a crime
he says he did not commit. The podcast captures his situation with considerable
humanity: a man who married young, lost his wife violently, lost his child's
childhood to his imprisonment, and has spent his adult life fighting to prove
what he knows to be true.

His psychological state — his maintenance of dignity and purpose through decades
of imprisonment, his continued engagement with the legal process despite its
consistent failure to help him — is one of the most affecting portraits in
any true crime podcast.

--- THE PODCAST'S IMPACT ---

"Bone Valley" generated significant public attention to Schofield's case and
renewed pressure on Florida authorities to review the conviction. The podcast
was downloaded millions of times and produced substantial coverage in national
media.

In 2023 — after the podcast's main run — a Florida judge granted Leo Schofield
a new evidentiary hearing, citing the newly presented evidence about Jeremy
Scott. The hearing represented a significant development in a case that had
been stuck in the courts for decades, though it did not immediately result in
Schofield's release.

As of the most recent information available: the proceedings are ongoing.
Schofield has not been exonerated or released, but the case has been reopened
in a way that the podcast directly contributed to.

--- THE "BONE VALLEY" CONTEXT ---

King uses the specific landscape and history of Polk County, Florida as a
contextual frame throughout the podcast. The phosphate mining industry that
shaped the area — the economic dependence, the environmental damage, the
specific culture of a rural community built around extraction — is rendered
as relevant to the case's social world: the specific poverty, the specific
institutions (the police, the courts), and the specific community dynamics
that shaped how Michelle Schofield's murder was investigated and how Leo
Schofield's conviction was obtained and has been maintained.

--- SUMMARY ---

"Bone Valley" is one of the most important true crime podcasts of recent years —
not for entertainment value but for its rigorous documentation of what appears
to be a serious miscarriage of justice. Gilbert King's Pulitzer-level
investigative standards applied to the Schofield case produce a portrait of
a man very likely convicted of a crime he did not commit, imprisoned for
decades while the probable actual killer is in prison for other crimes,
and failed repeatedly by the legal system that should have corrected the error.
The resolution is partial and ongoing: the case has been reopened, but justice
has not yet been fully served.
