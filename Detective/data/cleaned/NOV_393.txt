Career of Evil by Robert Galbraith (J.K. Rowling) is the third novel in the Cormoran Strike series and centers on a deranged killer who stalks Strike and his assistant Robin Ellacott with a deeply personal vendetta. The story is told partly through the killer’s twisted inner monologue, which runs alongside Strike and Robin’s investigation. Below is a long, spoiler‑filled, beat‑by‑beat plot synopsis told in chronological order, with motives, clues, and the final resolution clearly laid out.
The severed leg arrives

The novel opens with a brief, chilling chapter from the killer’s perspective: he has just murdered a woman and is pleased with himself. The tone is cold, meticulous, and narcissistic, hinting that this is only one of several planned killings.
Back in the shared office of the Cormoran Strike Investigation Agency, Robin Ellacott takes delivery of a brown package addressed to her. Inside she finds a severed right leg packed in salt, wrapped in a child’s pink‑and‑white sock. The sock triggers a horrifying realization for Cormoran Strike: he has seen that exact design before, and it belongs to a girl named Kelsey Platt, who Strike believes he once failed to save.

Strike immediately calls the police, including DI Eric Wardle, and the leg is sent for forensic examination. Robin is traumatized but insists on staying involved, while Strike is shaken by the personal nature of the attack; he is certain that someone is targeting him, and that Robin has been chosen as his proxy.
The killer’s taunt and the three suspects

Later, the killer sends Strike a letter containing a harmless‑seeming rhyme that ends with a terrifying line: “I’m going to do to you what you did to me.” The rhyme also lists three names: Ray Leger, Kevin “Brockie” Brockbank, and Donald Laing.
Strike recognizes all three as men he has encountered in his past, each of whom bears a grudge against him.

    Ray Leger is a fireman who seduced Strike’s late mother, Leda, and then rejected her. Strike had a violent confrontation with him years ago, but Leger has since moved on and appears to have cleaned up his life.

    Kevin Brockbank is a former soldier Strike helped imprison for abusing his wife; he was dishonorably discharged and remains a violent, misogynistic man.

    Donald Laing is a former soldier Strike investigated for domestic abuse years earlier; Strike was instrumental in getting him convicted, and Laing swore revenge.

Strike and Robin, along with DI Eric Wardle, try to track down all three men, but initially nothing fits neatly. Robin is horrified to realize that one of the victims the killer mentions is the same Kelsey Platt Strike has quietly haunted by: a teenage sex worker who died in suspicious circumstances years before.
The killer’s POV and the prostitutes’ murders

Interspersed with the main narrative are italicized chapters in the killer’s voice, offering a warped, self‑justifying account of his crimes. These sections reveal that he has already murdered at least three young women working in the sex trade, each of whom he associates with Strike in some way.
He carefully stages the scenes, sometimes leaving behind small, symbolic clues—like lingerie or items subtly connected to Strike’s past—that make him feel clever and powerful. The killer’s main obsession is that Strike once ruined his life, and he wants to make Strike experience the same fear, helplessness, and violation he believes he himself endured.​

The first victim whose body is discovered is Brit “Bry” Morgan, a prostitute whose severed leg is the one Robin receives. The police later learn that two other women have been murdered in similar ways, and the pattern suggests a serial killer is at work.
Strike’s guilt and his three “enemies”

The novel gradually reveals more backstory about Strike’s involvement with Kelsey Platt. Years earlier, when he was a teenager, he had a brief, bittersweet relationship with Kelsey, a girl who was exploited by others. When she died in a fire, Strike blamed himself for not being able to protect her.
The killer’s allusions to Kelsey deepen Strike’s sense of guilt and make him feel personally responsible for the new murders. At the same time, he is forced to confront his own morality and the consequences of his past work; he knows that each of the three men he put away genuinely wanted to hurt him.​

Strike and Robin begin to investigate the three suspects more closely, but the killer uses their investigation to his advantage. He manipulates events so that suspicion shifts toward one of them, creating red herrings and distractions.
Robin’s investigation and near‑death attack

Under police pressure, Strike reluctantly tells Robin to stay home and work from her flat, fearing for her safety. This strains Robin’s relationship with her fiancé Matthew Cunliffe, who has long disapproved of her job with Strike. Robin and Matthew reconcile briefly, but Robin chafes at being sidelined.
She pushes Strike to let her return to active work, and he agrees—assigning her what he thinks is the safer line of inquiry: interviewing the girlfriend of Kevin Brockbank, Alyssa. Brockbank is already under suspicion because of his violent history and his known hatred of Strike.

On the way to speak to Alyssa, Robin is suddenly attacked by a man in a car park. He stabs her in the arm, and she fights him off using Krav Maga‑style self‑defense. She does not see his face clearly, but she later realizes that the attacker wore a distinctive silk tie.
The attack devastates Strike, who feels he has endangered her again. He tells Robin she must leave the case entirely and even suggests she quit the agency altogether, believing that the killer will focus on her as Strike’s “weak point.”
Missteps and tension with Brockbank

Meanwhile, another gruesome package arrives at Strike’s office: this time it contains a severed toe that matches the same woman as the leg—Brit Morgan. The leak of this detail to the press damages the agency’s reputation and increases pressure on Strike and Robin.
Robin, still refusing to back down, decides to warn Alyssa that Brockbank may be a pedophile targeting children. She does this without telling Strike, breaking into Alyssa’s home and confronting her. Alyssa initially disbelieves Robin, but Alyssa’s teenage daughter later confirms that Brockbank has sexually abused her.

When Brockbank arrives home and finds Robin there, he threatens her; he is then driven off by a neighbor, Shanker, who brandishes a knife. Brockbank flees and goes on the run, which makes the police and the media even more certain he is the killer. Brockbank’s disappearance also reflects poorly on Strike’s agency, as it seems they have incited a fugitive.

In a furious confrontation, Strike blames Robin for the chaos and fires her on the spot, believing he needs to push her out of harm’s way. He later tries to apologize, but Matthew deletes the call before Robin sees it, deepening the rift between her and Strike.
Strike’s private investigation and the fake alibi

While the police focus on Brockbank, Strike privately pursues his own leads. He realizes that one of the three suspects—Donald Laing—has been living under a different identity as “Ray Williams,” allegedly a disabled firefighter who can barely walk and therefore could not possibly be the killer.​
Strike discovers that Laing has been using a dead man’s identity (the actual Ray Williams, a firefighter who died) and borrowing his disability pension. Strike learns that Laing’s supposed “crippled” state is largely a performance: he fakes his immobility to keep a low profile and collect the benefits.​

Strike tracks Laing to a small flat he uses intermittently. When he breaks in, he finds a horrifying sight: a refrigerator containing women’s body parts, including the rest of Brit Morgan’s remains.
Laing arrives unexpectedly and attacks Strike, but Strike manages to overpower him in a brutal fight. He then alerts the police, turning Laing over as the killer.
The killer’s identity and motives fully revealed

Donald Laing is confirmed as the murderer of the three prostitutes and the sender of the severed leg and toe. His motive is revenge against Cormoran Strike for ruining his life years earlier. He sees himself as a victim of Strike’s investigations and wants to make Strike feel helpless and terrified, just as he believes he once did.
The killer’s POV chapters are revealed to be Laing’s own “confession”‑style narrative, written in the first person. These sections show how he has meticulously planned his crimes, studied Strike’s past, and used the three‑name clue to manipulate Strike into re‑examining his own history.

Laing’s choice of victims is also tied to his warped sense of justice: he kills women he associates with Strike’s past failures and moral guilt, including Brit Morgan, who he links to Kelsey Platt. The grisly packages are meant not only to horrify Robin and Strike, but to drag them into the same psychological torment he feels.
The Brockbank red herring

Kevin Brockbank, the violent ex‑soldier, is ultimately cleared as the serial killer. He is indeed a dangerous man—abusive, misogynistic, and capable of assault—but he is not the organized, symbolic killer striking women in the sex trade.
The police eventually arrest Brockbank for other crimes (including his abuse of Alyssa and likely his pedophilia), but the murder investigation focused on Laing. The misdirection around Brockbank serves as a major red herring, heightening the tension and misleading both the police and the reader.
Robin’s personal crisis and wedding day

While the case is being solved, Robin’s relationship with Matthew spins further out of control. Matthew’s reaction to the attack, his jealousy of Strike, and his patronizing attitude toward her career push Robin to question their engagement.
Nevertheless, the wedding day arrives. Robin, still formally working for Strike despite having been fired, reconciles with Matthew and decides to go through with the marriage.

At the same time, Strike has just captured Laing and is physically battered from the confrontation. He convinces Shanker to drive him to the small Yorkshire town of Masham, where Robin is getting married. He arrives late, bruised and disheveled, stumbling into the back of the church just as Robin is about to say “I do.”
When Robin sees him, she smiles and says her vows anyway, choosing to stay with Matthew. The moment underscores the emotional distance between her and Strike, even as their professional bond remains intact.
Aftermath and unresolved tensions

Laing is convicted for the murders and the sending of the body parts, and the case is officially closed. Strike’s name is cleared from any suspicion, and the agency survives the scandal, though its reputation is dented.
For Robin, the experience forces her to confront her own desires and the limits of her relationship with Matthew. She has proven herself as an investigator, surviving an attack and helping to expose Brockbank’s crimes, but she has also chosen security and convention over the more volatile, unspoken connection she has with Strike.

Strike, meanwhile, is left both physically and emotionally scarred. He has saved lives, unmasked the killer, and preserved Robin’s safety in the end—but he has also lost her, at least romantically, in the process. The novel closes with the two of them still working together professionally, but their personal futures remain uncertain and tinged with regret.
