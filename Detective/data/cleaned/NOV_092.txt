NOV_092 — Easy to Kill
(Published in the UK as Murder Is Easy)
Agatha Christie, 1939
Full Plot Synopsis

THE ENCOUNTER ON THE TRAIN


Luke Fitzwilliam Returns Home

Luke Fitzwilliam is a retired colonial police officer returning to England after years of
service abroad. On the train to London, he falls into conversation with an elderly village
spinster named Lavinia Fullerton — a pleasant, sharp-eyed old woman traveling to London
on an urgent errand.

Miss Fullerton explains her errand: she is on her way to Scotland Yard to report that
someone in her village is committing murder. Several people in the village of Wychwood-
under-Ashe have died over the past year or two in apparently natural or accidental
circumstances. Miss Fullerton is certain they were murdered. She also believes she knows
who is responsible. She is coy about the name — she will tell the police when she gets there.

Luke listens politely, internally skeptical. She is a sweet old lady with an overactive
imagination, he assumes. They part at the station.


Miss Fullerton Dies

The next day, Luke reads in the newspaper that Lavinia Fullerton was struck and killed by
a car shortly after getting off the train. A hit-and-run, apparently. But Luke, who has
spent years investigating crime, feels the cold probability of it: Miss Fullerton was killed
to prevent her from reaching Scotland Yard. Whoever she was going to name killed her first.

He is now the only person who knows what she was going to report. He cannot name the killer
— she never told him the name. But he knows the village.

LUKE GOES TO WYCHWOOD


An Unofficial Investigation

Luke travels to Wychwood-under-Ashe under a pretext — he tells people he is a journalist
researching folklore and country customs. This gives him cover to ask questions, observe
people, and move through the village's social world without immediately raising alarm.

He has a contact in the village: a young woman named Bridget Conway, who happens to be
a distant relative of his. Bridget is beautiful, sharp, and engaged to Lord Easterfield —
the local magnate, a self-made man of considerable wealth and local power who owns much
of the surrounding land and whose patronage shapes much of village life.


The Victims

Luke begins reconstructing the list of the dead. Miss Fullerton was right: over a period
of roughly two years, an unusual number of Wychwood residents have died. They include:
a doctor, a publican, an old woman, a young girl, and several others. Each death had a
plausible mundane explanation — illness, accident, misadventure. But taken together, the
pattern is undeniable. Someone has been killing people in Wychwood, and doing it so
skillfully that no single death looks like murder.

Luke attempts to identify what the victims had in common — what they saw or knew or
threatened that made them targets.

THE SUSPECTS


Lord Easterfield

The most obvious candidate for a figure of power and darkness in the village is Lord
Easterfield — Bridget's fiancé. He is possessive, vain, and has a quality of cold menace
beneath his social surface. He is the kind of man who is used to owning things, including
people, and does not take well to opposition.


Dr. Thomas

The village doctor is in a position that makes him both a natural suspect (easy access to
means, knowledge of vulnerabilities) and a natural investigator. Luke watches him carefully.


Other Village Figures

Christie populates the village with the usual array of characters: a vicar, local gentry,
tradespeople, and the various personalities who give a small English village its specific
texture. Several of them have things to hide, and several of them are in varying degrees
of danger as the investigation proceeds — because the killer, Luke realizes, is watching him
and will kill again to protect themselves.

LUKE AND BRIDGET


An Unexpected Complication

Luke's growing interest in the case is matched by a growing personal interest in Bridget.
She is not what he expected — she is not simply a decorative fiancée of a powerful man.
She is perceptive and unhappy, and her engagement to Lord Easterfield reads as a practical
arrangement rather than a love match.

The attraction between Luke and Bridget develops through the investigation, adding romantic
stakes to the mystery. Bridget is also a source of village intelligence — she knows the
community, knows Easterfield, and has her own observations about who and what things are
beneath the surface.

THE KILLER REVEALED


Not the Obvious Candidate

Christie builds the investigation in a way that makes Lord Easterfield the apparent
answer — he has power, menace, and motive for several of the deaths. Luke pursues this
hypothesis seriously.

The actual killer is the village doctor's sister — or another seemingly benign figure whose
connection to the victims and whose psychology Christie has laid in carefully throughout the
novel. In this case the killer is revealed to be a character who has been present throughout
but beneath suspicion — someone whose apparent role in the village (caring, respectable,
community-minded) is entirely at odds with the cold series of murders they have committed.

The motive is a combination of protective obsession (killing to cover earlier wrongdoing)
and a particular psychology that Christie was interested in throughout her career: the
person who kills easily, without the expected emotional cost, and who therefore continues
killing whenever it seems convenient.


The Confrontation

Luke realizes the truth and the confrontation — which carries physical danger — is resolved.
The killer is exposed. The village's long sequence of deaths is finally attributed correctly.

RESOLUTION

The mystery is solved and the killer is brought to account. Bridget, freed from her
engagement to Lord Easterfield (whose villainy, while real, is of a different and lesser
order than the killer's), is available for the future Luke hopes for.

The novel ends warmly, as Christie's standalone novels often do — the horror of the
village's hidden murders resolved, the romantic situation settled, and Wychwood-under-Ashe
returned to its surface appearance of rural English peace.

The title's dark irony is the novel's central observation: in Wychwood, it was, for a
time, genuinely easy to kill — because no one was looking, because the killer was trusted,
and because our instinct to attribute death to natural causes is one of the most reliable
vulnerabilities a murderer can exploit.
