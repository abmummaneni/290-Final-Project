--- BACKGROUND AND SETTING ---

"Tale of the Witch Doll" is a mystery novel by Mildred Wirt Benson, published
in 1939 under her pen name "Don Palmer" in the Penny Parker Mystery Series.
Mildred Wirt Benson is best known as the original author of the Nancy Drew
series (under the pseudonym Carolyn Keene), and the Penny Parker books reflect
her characteristic style: a plucky, resourceful teenage girl protagonist, a
mystery with gothic or supernatural surface elements concealing a rational
criminal plot, and a narrative that moves quickly through escalating dangers.

Penny Parker is the daughter of Anthony Parker, the editor of the Riverview
Star newspaper. She is bright, brave, occasionally impulsive, and
enthusiastically curious. Throughout the series she involves herself in
investigations that often begin as stories for her father's paper and end with
real criminals exposed.

The novel is set in and around Riverview, a fictional Midwestern town, with
the action centered on an old house with a reputation for strange occurrences,
the mysterious figure of a reputed witch, and a sinister doll that appears to
carry a curse.

--- PENNY AND THE OLD HOUSE ---

The story opens with Penny Parker and her friend Louise becoming curious about
an old, dilapidated house in the Riverview area — a property with a local
reputation for being haunted or cursed. The house is associated with an eccentric
elderly woman, rumored in the neighborhood to be a witch, who lives in near
isolation and is viewed with a mixture of fear and mockery by the townspeople.

Penny, journalistic instincts engaged, persuades herself that there is a story
here. She and Louise approach the house and make contact with the old woman,
who proves to be not a witch in any meaningful sense but a solitary, somewhat
frightening figure with unusual habits — she keeps strange objects in her home,
including dolls made in a peculiar manner, and she speaks in a way that feeds
the local legends about her.

Among the objects in the old woman's possession is the "witch doll" of the
title: a doll made in an old style, dressed in dark colors, and presented with
a kind of ritualistic deliberateness. Local legend holds that the doll has
the power to bring misfortune to those it is directed against.

--- THE STRANGE EVENTS BEGIN ---

Shortly after Penny and Louise's initial contact with the old woman and the
doll, a series of alarming events begins. People connected to the house, or
who have come into contact with the doll, begin to experience misfortunes:
accidents, illness, theft, or financial reversal. The community's superstitious
interpretation is that the doll's curse is active.

Penny is skeptical. She suspects that someone is engineering these "curses" to
achieve a practical, non-supernatural goal — using the community's fear of the
old woman and the doll as cover for real criminal activity. Her father, the
newspaper editor, is interested too, both from a journalistic and protective
standpoint.

The misfortunes escalate. The old woman herself is threatened or victimized in
ways that suggest she is not the author of the trouble but one of its targets.
Property disappears. Threats are made. There are break-ins and disturbances at
the old house.

--- THE CRIMINAL SCHEME BENEATH THE CURSE ---

As Penny investigates, the shape of the real crime emerges. The witch doll and
the old woman's reputation are being cynically exploited by a small group of
criminals who have identified an opportunity in the neighborhood's credulity.

The specific scheme involves one of the following kinds of fraud or criminal
activity common in Benson's plots: the exploitation of a vulnerable person
(the old woman herself) in order to gain access to her property or possessions;
a fraudulent scheme that uses the cover of local superstition to frighten
potential witnesses or victims into silence or compliance; or the concealment
of stolen goods or other criminal material in the old house, relying on the
community's reluctance to approach a "cursed" or "haunted" property.

The criminals — one or two adults, possibly with a local connection that makes
them trusted members of the community — have been using the doll and the old
woman's strange behavior as both a tool of intimidation and a distraction from
their actual activities.

--- PENNY IN DANGER ---

True to the genre conventions of the series, Penny's investigation leads her
into direct personal danger. She discovers things she should not know, and the
criminals become aware that the newspaper editor's daughter is asking too many
questions.

There is at least one scene in which Penny is threatened, trapped, or faces a
dangerous confrontation — a standard element of the Penny Parker books. Her
courage and quick thinking, combined eventually with timely help from her father,
his newspaper contacts, or the police, see her through the crisis.

The old woman, meanwhile, is revealed in fuller light as the story progresses.
She is not malevolent — she is, in fact, a victim. Her eccentricities, which fed
the local legends, are the product of a long, unhappy history. The "witch" is
simply an old woman who has been isolated and frightened and is now being
actively exploited by the criminals who recognized that her reputation as a witch
made her perfect cover.

--- THE RESOLUTION ---

The criminal scheme is fully exposed through Penny's investigation. The
individuals responsible — who have been using the witch-doll legend as cover
for their activities — are identified and turned over to the police or
otherwise brought to justice.

The old woman is vindicated in the community's eyes, or at least partially
rehabilitated — the source of the actual danger is shown to be not her but
the criminal element that exploited her and the neighborhood's superstition.
The doll itself is shown to be simply an object: old, strange, associated with
old folk practices, but entirely without supernatural power. The "curse" was
manufactured malice.

Penny returns to her father with the story — a genuine news scoop — and the
Riverview Star publishes the account of the criminal scheme behind the witch
doll legend.

--- NOTES ON THE SERIES AND NOVEL ---

The Penny Parker series (1939–1947) is one of Mildred Wirt Benson's most
interesting non-Nancy Drew achievements. Penny is in some ways a livelier,
more independent character than the Nancy Drew of the 1930s editions — she is
specifically connected to journalism, which gives her investigations a
professional pretext, and she is allowed more humor and self-awareness.

"Tale of the Witch Doll" is representative of the series' approach to the
supernatural: always providing a rational explanation while sustaining the
gothic atmosphere long enough to create genuine unease. The old woman, the doll,
and the cursed house are taken seriously as sources of mood and menace, even
as the novel works steadily toward their demystification. The criminal scheme,
when fully revealed, is grounded in recognizable human motives — greed,
exploitation of the vulnerable, calculated use of community fears — rather than
anything genuinely uncanny.
