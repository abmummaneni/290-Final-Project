FULL EPISODE SYNOPSIS — SPOILERS INCLUDED
================================================================================

--- BACKGROUND AND CONTEXT ---

"The Way Through the Woods" is a Season 8 episode of Inspector Morse, first
broadcast on ITV on November 27, 1995. It is directed by John Madden and is
based on Colin Dexter's 1992 novel of the same name, which won the Crime
Writers' Association Gold Dagger Award — the most prestigious prize in British
crime fiction — for that year. The novel is widely regarded as one of Dexter's
finest, and the television adaptation is considered among the best of the later
Morse episodes.

The episode has a distinctive structure: it begins with an already-cold case —
a woman who disappeared a year earlier — and adds a new body and new evidence
that reopen the old investigation. This double timeline, and the specific
mystery of why events are connected across a year's gap, gives the episode
an unusual depth.

--- THE DISAPPEARANCE OF KARIN ERIKSSON ---

A year before the episode's present day, a young Swedish woman named Karin
Eriksson disappeared while hitchhiking through the Oxfordshire countryside.
She was a student traveler, carrying a backpack, and she was last seen somewhere
on the road between Oxford and Woodstock. She was never found. The investigation
at the time — not led by Morse — concluded with no clear resolution.

Her disappearance left an open file and a Swedish family waiting for news.

--- THE POEM ---

In the episode's present day, a poem appears — submitted anonymously to a
national newspaper competition. The poem is extraordinary: it describes, in
literary and oblique terms, what appears to be the experience of a woman in
the Oxfordshire woods, in circumstances that suggest she was killed there.
The poem contains specific details — about the location, about the season,
about the specific sensory details of the place — that indicate the author
has direct knowledge of what happened to Karin Eriksson.

Morse, who has a deep love of poetry and who possesses the specific literary
sensitivity to read the poem for what it actually contains, identifies it as
a coded account of a murder. He begins to investigate: who wrote the poem,
and what do they know?

The investigation of the poem's authorship — following the submission through
the newspaper competition's records, identifying the source — is the episode's
initial investigative thread.

--- THE BODY IN THE WOODS ---

While the poem investigation is developing, a body is found in Wytham Woods —
the large wooded area northwest of Oxford that is the episode's central
geographic location. The body is skeletal, has been there for approximately
a year, and is eventually identified as Karin Eriksson.

Her murder — the murder that the poem describes — is now confirmed. Morse's
investigation expands from the poetry question to the full murder investigation,
but the poem remains central: whoever wrote it knows what happened, either
because they witnessed it, discovered the body, or committed the murder.

--- THE INVESTIGATION ---

Morse investigates in his characteristic fashion — simultaneously through
the practical evidence (the body, the scene in Wytham Woods, the physical
evidence of how Karin died) and through the more intellectual avenue of the
poem and its author.

Lewis's practical investigation of Karin's last known movements, the people
she encountered while hitchhiking, and the specific road she was on when
she disappeared provides the contextual evidence. Several people in Oxfordshire
gave her lifts or saw her in the days before her disappearance, and their
accounts are carefully assembled.

The poem's authorship investigation leads Morse into the world of local
literary and academic life — the Oxford-area community of people who write
seriously or who participate in poetry competitions. The specific community
to which the anonymous poet belongs is established through the newspaper
competition's administration.

--- THE LOVE TRIANGLE ---

The investigation eventually reveals a specific romantic and sexual triangle
involving people in the Oxfordshire area whose relationships with each other —
and with Karin Eriksson — provide the context for her death.

Karin, while hitchhiking, was picked up by someone connected to this community.
Her presence in the area — her specific interaction with specific people —
intersected with an existing situation of jealousy, desire, and conflict that
had been building for some time.

The specific nature of the triangle and who was involved, and how Karin's
arrival precipitated or was caught up in its violence, is established through
the double investigation of the old case and the new evidence provided by
the poem and the body.

--- MORSE'S PERSONAL INVESTMENT ---

The episode has a specific personal resonance for Morse, who is drawn to the
case partly through the poem — literature being one of the channels through
which Morse connects to the world most directly — and partly through a specific
personal interaction with someone connected to the case.

A woman — attractive, intelligent, connected to the poetry world — appears
in the investigation and generates in Morse the specific combination of
intellectual admiration and emotional investment that he rarely allows himself
to fully experience. His personal feeling for this woman, and the specific
complication that the investigation introduces into that feeling, gives the
episode an emotional weight that is characteristic of the best Morse entries.

Whether the personal feeling is requited, and what the investigation ultimately
reveals about the woman and her connection to the case, are among the episode's
most affecting threads.

--- THE POEM DECODED ---

The full decoding of the poem — what it actually describes, what each image
corresponds to in the physical reality of Karin's murder — is one of the
episode's great pleasures. Morse's literary reading of the poem, identifying
the specific details that could only be known to someone with direct knowledge,
is presented with real attention to the poem's language and to the specific
gap between what the poem says aesthetically and what it describes literally.

The poet wrote the poem because they could not not write it — because the
experience of what they witnessed or knew demanded expression, and the
oblique literary form allowed them to express it in a way that they thought
would be too indirect to constitute evidence. They underestimated Morse's
capacity to read.

--- THE KILLER IDENTIFIED ---

The specific person responsible for Karin's death is identified through the
combined investigation of the body, the witnesses, the love triangle, and
the poem's authorship. The killing was connected to the specific emotional
situation of the triangle and to the specific moment at which Karin's presence
became relevant to it.

The revelation is delivered with the particular bittersweet quality that
characterizes the best Morse endings: the solution is intellectually satisfying,
but the human cost — what was lost, what was destroyed by the events — is
not diminished by the fact of its resolution.

--- RESOLUTION ---

The killer is identified and the evidence presented to the authorities. Karin
Eriksson's family has their answer. The year-old disappearance is solved.

Morse closes the case carrying the specific melancholy that comes from knowing
that a poem — written to process the inexpressible — was what finally brought
justice to a young woman who disappeared on a road in Oxfordshire and was
found a year later in the woods she had been resting in.

--- NOTES ON THE EPISODE ---

"The Way Through the Woods" is one of the finest Morse episodes — John Madden's
direction is confident and atmospheric, the Wytham Woods setting is beautifully
used, and the poem conceit is handled with genuine literary intelligence.
The episode trusts the viewer to find the poetry itself interesting rather
than simply using it as a plot device. Dexter's novel is one of his most
carefully constructed, and the adaptation preserves both the puzzle quality
and the emotional richness of the source. John Thaw's performance — particularly
in the scenes involving the woman connected to the case — is among his most
subdued and most effective in the series.
