FULL SYNOPSIS — SPOILERS INCLUDED

NOTE: This synopsis covers both Season 1 and Season 2 of Money Heist (Spanish:
La Casa de Papel), which together constitute a single complete story — the
Royal Mint of Spain heist. In the original Spanish broadcast, this was a single
continuous series. Netflix split it into two seasons for international release
and added Seasons 3–5 as a separate continuation. TVE_125 and TVE_126 are
therefore combined here into one complete synopsis covering the full original
heist from beginning to end.

--- BACKGROUND AND CONTEXT ---

"Money Heist" (Spanish: La Casa de Papel, literally "The House of Paper") is
a Spanish crime drama created by Álex Pina, first broadcast on Antena 3 in
Spain in May 2017 and subsequently acquired by Netflix, which gave it
international distribution and a global audience it had not initially anticipated.
It became the most-watched non-English language series in Netflix history and
generated both a global cultural phenomenon and a continuation series.

The original story — the Royal Mint heist — is a robbery thriller of considerable
ingenuity and considerable emotional investment. It is structured as a heist
with a specific set of rules: the Professor (the mastermind) has planned every
detail, has anticipated every contingency, and has constructed a plan whose
specific cleverness is the show's primary intellectual pleasure. The execution
of the plan — and the ways in which reality departs from the plan, requiring
improvisation, sacrifice, and difficult choices — drives the drama.

The show is narrated in retrospect by Tokyo — one of the robbers — which
establishes from the beginning that she survived whatever happened, while
leaving the fate of the others open.

--- THE PROFESSOR AND HIS PLAN ---

The Professor (Álvaro Morte) is the architect of the heist. He is a man whose
entire life has been directed toward this plan: he spent years designing it,
selecting his team, preparing for every contingency. His specific genius is
the combination of meticulous preparation and specific knowledge — of the Mint's
layout, its personnel, its systems, and the specific legal and institutional
mechanisms that govern how hostage situations are managed.

The plan's central ingenuity: the robbers take hostages and lock themselves
inside the Royal Mint of Spain, which is the institution that prints Spain's
currency. They are not stealing money that exists — they are printing it.
The longer they stay inside, the more money they print and the more they have
to leave with. Their goal is not a quick smash-and-grab but a sustained siege
that lasts long enough to print and bag the maximum possible sum.

The Professor operates entirely from outside. He is never inside the Mint.
His specific role is to manage the negotiation with the police from the outside —
specifically, to manipulate Inspector Raquel Murillo (the lead police negotiator)
in ways that keep the siege going long enough for the team inside to finish
the job.

--- THE TEAM ---

Each member of the team is named after a city — a rule the Professor established,
to prevent personal attachments from forming around real names. The team members
are:

Tokyo (Úrsula Corberó) — the narrator, a young woman with a volatile personality
and a history of criminal activity, who is the most emotionally impulsive
member of the team. Her specific qualities — courage, recklessness, and genuine
emotional investment in the people around her — make her both valuable and
dangerous.

Berlin (Pedro Alonso) — the team leader inside the Mint, the Professor's
brother. He is intelligent, theatrical, and possessed of a specific authoritarian
streak that becomes one of the team's most significant internal tensions. He
also has a terminal illness — a degenerative condition that means he has nothing
to lose — which shapes his specific approach to every decision.

Rio (Miguel Herrán) — a young hacker, Tokyo's romantic partner, who is responsible
for the team's technical operations. His relationship with Tokyo is one of the
series' central emotional threads.

Moscow (Paco Tous) — an older man, a former miner, whose specific physical
skills (tunneling) are essential to the plan and whose specific gentleness
makes him one of the series' most affecting figures.

Denver (Jaime Lorente) — Moscow's son, a young man whose emotional volatility
is managed by his father's steadying influence. His specific relationship with
one of the hostages becomes one of the series' most significant developments.

Nairobi (Alba Flores) — the team member responsible for managing the printing
operation, a woman of competence and warmth whose specific relationship to the
other team members develops across the series.

Oslo and Helsinki — two cousins who serve as the team's muscle, less individually
characterised than the others but essential to the operation's physical management.

--- THE HOSTAGES ---

The Mint's employees and a school group of tourists who were visiting on the
day of the raid are the hostages — approximately seventy people locked inside
with the team. Their specific management — keeping them controlled without
unnecessary harm, managing the specific vulnerabilities and the specific
personalities among them — is one of the team's most sustained challenges.

Among the hostages, several individuals are given sustained development:

Alicia Sierra would join later — Arturo Román (Enrique Arce) is the Mint's
head of security and the hostage most actively working against the team from
within. His specific management of the hostage group — the ways he attempts
to generate resistance and disclose information to the police — creates sustained
internal tension.

Mónica Gaztambide (Esther Acebo) — a Mint employee who is Arturo's mistress
and who is pregnant. Her specific situation creates both vulnerability and
the emotional connection with Denver that becomes one of the story's most
significant threads. Denver, ordered by Berlin to kill her, cannot do it —
and his decision not to, and the specific relationship that develops between
them, is one of the series' warmest and most affecting elements.

--- INSPECTOR RAQUEL MURILLO ---

Inspector Raquel Murillo (Itziar Ituño) leads the police negotiation from
the outside. She is a capable, experienced negotiator dealing with an
extraordinary situation — a robbery whose specific mechanics (they're printing
the money, not stealing it) she and her team struggle to understand and counter.

She is also, without knowing it, being systematically manipulated by the
Professor, who has inserted himself into her life under a false identity in
the weeks before the heist. Their relationship — the specific romantic connection
they develop, which the Professor cultivated as part of his operational plan —
is one of the series' central emotional and moral complications.

The Professor genuinely falls in love with Raquel during the manipulation.
Whether his feelings for her can be separated from the manipulation that produced
them, and what Raquel does when she discovers the truth, are among the most
carefully handled elements of the first and second seasons.

The dynamic between the Professor (feeding information and disinformation to
the negotiation from outside, while conducting a romantic relationship with
its lead officer) and Raquel (conducting the negotiation while unknowingly
providing intelligence to its target) is the story's most sustained irony and
its most emotionally complex relationship.

--- THE SIEGE: INTERNAL TENSIONS ---

The siege generates sustained internal tension among the team. Berlin's specific
authoritarianism — his willingness to use violence against hostages as a
management tool, his specific contempt for team members whose emotional responses
he finds weak — creates conflicts with Tokyo, with Denver, and with others
whose approach to the hostages is less coldly instrumental.

The specific incident in which Berlin orders Denver to kill Mónica, and Denver's
refusal, is the series' most significant internal fracture: it establishes
that the team's internal cohesion cannot be maintained through pure authority
when members are asked to do things they find intolerable.

Berlin's illness — progressive, eventually fatal — gives his specific behaviour
a quality that is simultaneously comprehensible and disturbing. He is a man
who has decided, in the face of his death, to live exactly as he chooses, and
his choices are not always ones that serve the team or the hostages.

Moscow is mortally wounded during the siege — shot in an operational incident
that is among the series' most painful moments. His specific death, and Denver's
response to the loss of his father, is one of the first and second seasons'
most significant emotional events.

--- THE PROFESSOR AND RAQUEL: THE REVELATION ---

Raquel discovers that the man she has been romantically involved with is the
Professor — the mastermind of the heist she is trying to end. The specific
moment of discovery, and her response to it, is the series' most significant
personal turning point.

She is not simply betrayed in the conventional romantic sense. She is a police
officer who has been operationally deceived by the primary subject of her
investigation — who has provided intelligence to the person she was trying to
catch while believing she was in a genuine relationship. The specific professional
and personal dimensions of this betrayal are both real and both handled.

Her ultimate response — the choice she makes about what to do with what she
knows and who she decides to be in relation to the Professor — is one of the
series' most significant and most debated elements. She chooses the Professor.
She chooses to protect him rather than deliver him to the investigation she
has been leading.

This choice is not presented as simply romantic: it is a choice about what
she believes about what has happened, about who the Professor is and what the
heist is, and about what her own position in the institutional world she has
been serving actually means to her. Her specific disillusionment with the
institutional framework — the specific things she learns about how the
investigation has been conducted and what her colleagues have done — is part
of what makes her choice possible.

--- THE ESCAPE ---

The heist's operational conclusion involves the team's escape from the Mint
— one of the plan's most critical phases, requiring the specific tunnel that
Moscow's skills were needed to dig and the specific timing that the Professor's
management of the outside situation must deliver.

Berlin stays behind. His illness is at a stage where he will not survive the
escape regardless, and his specific choice — to remain in the Mint and ensure
the escape while the rest of the team leaves — is his valediction. He faces
the police alone, buying time for his team, and dies in the Mint.

His death is handled with the ambivalence he has generated throughout: he is
not heroic in any simple sense, and the show does not present it as a redemption.
He is who he is, and his final act is entirely consistent with his character —
a man who chose to die on his own terms, doing the one thing left to do.

The team escapes through the tunnel. The money — printed across the length of
the siege — is recovered. The Professor, with Raquel's specific assistance,
manages the final phase of the operation.

--- RESOLUTION ---

The heist is completed. The team escapes with the money. No hostages are killed
(with the specific exception of events that are resolved within the siege itself).
The police investigation does not produce the captures it was trying to achieve.

The final image — the team dispersed, the money in their possession, the
Professor and Raquel together in the aftermath — is the show's first conclusion:
the original story complete, a heist that succeeded, a plan that worked through
the specific combination of genius preparation and genuine human improvisation
when the plan encountered reality.

Tokyo's narration closes the story with the specific quality of someone who
has survived something extraordinary and who understands that it changed
everything.

--- NOTES ON THE SERIES ---

Money Heist Seasons 1 and 2 constitute one of the most entertaining extended
heist narratives in television history. The Professor's plan — its specific
ingenuity, its sustained cleverness, and the specific pleasures of watching
someone who has genuinely thought everything through — is constructed with
real craft. The character development of the team members, and the specific
relationships that develop under siege conditions, gives the thriller framework
genuine emotional investment. The Professor-Raquel dynamic is the series' finest
achievement: a relationship that is simultaneously a manipulation and a genuine
love story, and whose specific moral dimensions are handled with more seriousness
than the thriller genre usually demands.
