FULL EPISODE SYNOPSIS — SPOILERS INCLUDED
================================================================================

--- BACKGROUND AND CONTEXT ---

"Now You See Him" is a Season 5 episode of Columbo, airing February 29, 1976.
It stars Jack Cassidy in his third and final appearance as a Columbo villain —
his last role before his death in December 1976. Cassidy plays Jerome Brindle,
a famous stage magician, and the episode uses the world of stage illusion with
genuine creativity: a magician's alibi for murder depends on the same principle
as his stage act — that the audience sees what he wants them to see, not what
is actually happening.

The episode is directed by Harvey Hart and is considered one of the strongest
entries of the later classic era, with Cassidy's performance described by many
critics as his finest of the three.

--- THE KILLER, THE VICTIM, AND THE MOTIVE ---

Jerome Brindle is "The Great Santini," a celebrated stage magician who performs
nightly at a supper club in Los Angeles. He is polished, theatrical, and — in
his private self — deeply dangerous. His real name is not Brindle, and his
real past is not what his public biography claims. Before he became The Great
Santini, he was a German national involved with the Nazi SS during World War II.

Dollman is a club owner who knows Brindle's real identity and his wartime past.
He has been blackmailing Brindle for some time, extracting regular payments
in exchange for silence. The blackmail has been financially burdensome and
psychologically intolerable. Brindle decides to kill Dollman.

--- THE PERFECT ALIBI: THE STAGE TRICK ---

The murder is Brindle's masterpiece — a scheme as carefully constructed as
any stage illusion. During his nightly performance, Brindle has a specific
segment in which he enters a locked cabinet, apparently visible to the audience,
and then reappears elsewhere. This illusion involves a dummy or stand-in that
makes it appear Brindle is in the cabinet while he is actually elsewhere.

Brindle uses this window — the time during which the audience believes he is
in the cabinet — to leave the club, travel to where Dollman is, kill him,
and return before the illusion concludes. His alibi is perfect: hundreds of
people in the audience saw him (or what they believed was him) on stage at
the time of the murder. The magician's trick that he performs every night is
simultaneously the murder's alibi.

--- COLUMBO ENTERS ---

Columbo investigates Dollman's death and quickly identifies that the obvious
explanation — robbery, random violence, enemies from his criminal associations —
is incomplete. The manner of the death, or the specific circumstances around
it, directs Columbo's attention toward Brindle.

Meeting Brindle at the supper club, Columbo is genuinely delighted by the magic
act. His apparent childlike fascination with the tricks is partly authentic —
Columbo does find Brindle impressive — and partly strategic. He watches the
act carefully. He is interested not just in the spectacle but in the mechanics:
how are the illusions achieved? What is actually happening while the audience
sees something else?

This is the episode's central theme: the parallel between stage illusion and
criminal alibi. Both depend on directing attention away from what is actually
happening. Columbo, who always sees what is actually happening, is the natural
enemy of both.

--- INVESTIGATING THE TRICK ---

The core of Columbo's investigation is establishing how the cabinet illusion
works — specifically whether it contains a mechanism that would have allowed
Brindle to leave the stage during the performance. This requires getting access
to the cabinet and to Brindle's assistant, who knows how the trick is done.

Brindle's assistant — a young woman who has worked with him and who understands
the mechanics of the act — is a key witness. Her knowledge of the illusion's
mechanism is something Brindle cannot allow Columbo to fully access.

Columbo also examines the timing: the specific duration of the cabinet segment
in the act, what the travel time would be between the club and where Dollman
was killed, and whether the window available was sufficient. The logistics of
the scheme — which Brindle calculated carefully — are the terrain on which
Columbo works.

--- BRINDLE'S NAZI PAST ---

The revelation of Brindle's wartime history is an important element of the
episode's moral texture. Brindle is not simply a murderer defending a lifestyle
but a man with a genuinely dark past who has been living under a constructed
identity for decades. His killing of Dollman is the latest in a long series
of acts in which he has put his own survival above any other consideration.

The Nazi history also affects how Columbo — and the viewer — relates to Brindle.
Dollman's blackmail, while illegal, was extracting payment from someone who
had committed crimes of a different order entirely. The episode does not use
this to excuse Dollman's murder, but it gives the moral landscape a complexity
that straightforward killer/victim scenarios lack.

--- THE TRAP ---

Columbo engineers the exposure through the mechanics of the illusion itself.
Having established how the trick works, he creates a situation in which Brindle's
specific access to and use of the cabinet during the performance can be proved —
either through a witness whose testimony about what they actually saw (versus
what they were supposed to see) establishes that the illusion was running during
the murder window, or through physical evidence that Brindle left during his
departure and return.

The magician's alibi — which depended on the audience seeing a performance rather
than reality — is dismantled by someone who, unlike most of the audience, was
looking at what was actually there.

--- RESOLUTION ---

Jerome Brindle is exposed. His identity — both his false name and his wartime
history — is part of the final reckoning. He faces arrest not only for Dollman's
murder but as the full picture of who he is becomes available to the relevant
authorities.

Cassidy's final scene in his last role is one of the most memorable in the
series: the magician who spent his life making people see what wasn't there,
confronted by the one man who always saw what was.

--- NOTES ON THE EPISODE ---

"Now You See Him" is thematically one of the richest Columbo episodes, using
stage magic as a sustained metaphor for the construction of criminal deception.
The episode earns its premise — the trick genuinely works as an alibi mechanism
and the investigation of it genuinely requires understanding how the illusion
functions. Jack Cassidy's third and final performance as a Columbo villain has
an added elegiac quality in retrospect: knowing it was his last, the viewer
sees something in Brindle that the two earlier roles didn't have — a kind of
exhaustion beneath the showmanship, the cost of a life built on performances
and evasions.
