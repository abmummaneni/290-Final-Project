--- BACKGROUND AND CONTEXT ---

"The Origin of Evil," published in 1951, is the tenth novel in the Ellery Queen
series and belongs to what is sometimes called the "Hollywood period" of the
Queen canon — a sequence of novels set in Los Angeles during Ellery's sojourn
there as a screenwriter. The Hollywood novels have a specific character: the
closed-community institutional setting of the early Queen novels (hospitals,
colleges, theatres) is replaced by the specific social world of the film
industry and the specific residential world of mid-century Los Angeles, and
the psychological complexity of the later Queen period is more fully developed
than in the early nationality novels.

Ellery is in Hollywood, attempting to write a screenplay with the specific
mixture of commercial engagement and artistic frustration that the assignment
entails, when he is drawn into a case that begins with a dead dog and a girl
at his door.

The novel is set in Wrightsville — the fictional New England town that Queen
used as the setting for several of their most psychologically ambitious novels,
including "Calamity Town" (1942). Wrightsville is Queen's equivalent of
Christie's St. Mary Mead: a specific community whose specific social world,
recurring characters, and accumulated history give it a weight and texture that
the one-off settings of the early novels could not achieve.

--- THE INCITING EVENT: LAUREL HILL ---

A young woman named Laurel Hill comes to Ellery's Hollywood door in a state
of genuine distress. She tells him a story that is bizarre enough to attract
his attention immediately: her father, Leander Hill, received a gift — a dead
dog, delivered to him in a box. Shortly after receiving this gift, Leander
Hill collapsed and died of what was certified as a heart attack.

Laurel believes the dead dog killed her father. Not literally — she is not
suggesting poison or anything of the kind — but that the dead dog was a
deliberate communication designed to terrify him, and that the terror of it
brought on the fatal heart attack. She believes the dead dog was sent by someone
who knew that her father had a specific guilty secret, and that the guilty
secret, confronted in the specific form of the dead dog, was what killed him.

This is an unusual kind of murder: not a physical killing but a psychological
one, if Laurel's theory is correct. The investigation of what the dead dog
meant to Leander Hill, and why it might have killed him, is Ellery's initial
investigative problem.

--- THE SECOND GIFT: ROGER PRIAM ---

Ellery's investigation quickly establishes that the dead dog delivered to
Leander Hill was not the only strange gift that arrived in the relevant social
circle. Roger Priam — Leander Hill's business partner, a difficult and reclusive
man who lives in an extraordinarily designed house in the Hollywood Hills —
has also received a disturbing package: a dead cat, delivered in circumstances
similar to the dog.

Priam is not dead. Unlike Hill, he has survived the receipt of his mysterious
gift — or it has not yet had the effect on him that the dog had on Hill. But
he is frightened, in the specific way of someone who understands what the gift
means, and his specific response to Ellery's investigation — the evasiveness,
the specific things he will and will not say — tells Ellery that Priam knows
the source of the gifts and knows what they represent.

Roger Priam is a physically imposing man who has been confined to a wheelchair
— not by illness but by a specific choice, or a specific psychological response
to something that happened to him, whose nature is itself one of the
investigation's sustained questions. He conducts his life from the wheelchair
with a specific quality of chosen immobility that is one of the novel's more
distinctive characterisations.

--- THE SHARED SECRET: WRIGHTSVILLE AND THE PAST ---

The investigation moves to Wrightsville — where the shared history of Leander
Hill and Roger Priam, and the specific event in their past that the gifts are
referencing, must be found and understood.

Hill and Priam were business partners with a shared history that reaches back
before their commercial association to a specific event in their younger lives.
The gifts — the dead dog and the dead cat — are references to this event:
specific animals that were connected to a specific moment whose significance
to both men is what made the gifts so devastating.

The specific event in the shared past is a death — a death that occurred under
circumstances that implicated Hill and Priam in specific ways. Whether their
involvement in that old death was criminal, morally culpable but not illegal,
or something more ambiguous is one of the novel's sustained questions. What
is clear is that the event created a specific shared guilt whose weight both
men have been carrying for years, and that someone who knows about the event
is using that knowledge in a campaign designed to kill them.

The gifts are not random horror. They are specific symbols whose meaning would
be immediately legible to anyone who knew about the old event, and whose receipt
by both Hill and Priam in sequence constitutes a message: I know what you did,
and you are going to die for it.

--- THE WRIGHTSVILLE INVESTIGATION ---

Ellery's investigation in Wrightsville involves the specific community characters
that the Queen novels set in the town have developed: the recurring figures of
Wrightsville life whose specific knowledge of local history and local gossip
provides the investigative raw material for cases set there.

The reconstruction of the old death — what happened, who was involved, and what
specifically Hill and Priam did in connection with it — is the investigation's
primary historical task. The specific records of the Wrightsville community,
the memories of specific residents who were present or connected to the events,
and the specific physical evidence that has survived from the relevant period
are all part of what Ellery assembles.

The question of who is sending the gifts — who has the specific knowledge of
the old event and the specific motivation to use it in a campaign of psychological
assassination — is the investigation's primary present-day task.

--- THE KILLER: DELIA PRIAM ---

The person sending the gifts and conducting the campaign of terror that killed
Leander Hill and was designed to kill Roger Priam is Delia Priam — Roger
Priam's wife.

Delia's specific motivation is rooted in her knowledge of the old death — what
Hill and Priam did in connection with it — and in the specific personal consequences
that their actions had for her. The old event was not simply a shared guilt
between two business partners: it involved a woman — a woman connected to
Delia's own history and her own losses — whose fate was determined by the
specific choices Hill and Priam made.

Delia has lived for years alongside Roger Priam with the specific knowledge
of what he did, and her specific response to that knowledge — sustained, patient,
and deliberate — is the campaign of symbolic gifts that killed Hill and was
designed to kill Priam next.

Her specific use of the animals as symbols — the dead dog and cat whose connection
to the old event both men would immediately understand — reflects both her
specific knowledge of the event and her specific psychological relationship
to the people she is punishing. She is not trying to kill them quickly or
cleanly: she is trying to make them understand, in the moment before death,
that they are being killed by someone who knows exactly what they did and exactly
what it was worth.

Priam's confinement to the wheelchair — his chosen immobility — is itself a
consequence of the psychological weight of the old guilt: a man who stopped
moving through the world after a specific event made it impossible for him to
do so without carrying something he did not know how to carry. Delia's campaign
is directed at the specific wound his guilt represents.

Ellery identifies Delia through the specific reconstruction of who had both
the knowledge of the old event and the specific access to the symbols — the
dead animals — that the gifts required, and through the specific psychological
analysis of the person whose specific position in the situation the gifts
express. Only someone who was intimately connected to both the old event and
to its specific human cost would have chosen these particular symbols, and
only someone in Delia's position had that specific combination of knowledge
and motivation.

--- RESOLUTION ---

Delia Priam is identified and confronted. The old death — its specific
circumstances, the specific things Hill and Priam did in connection with it,
and the specific human cost of those actions — is fully established. Roger
Priam faces both the legal consequences of the investigation's findings and
the specific personal reckoning that Delia's campaign was designed to force.

The novel's title — "The Origin of Evil" — refers both to the old event and
to the specific philosophical question the case raises: where does evil originate?
In the original act? In the response to it? In the specific conditions that
make people capable of both?

--- NOTES ON THE NOVEL ---

"The Origin of Evil" is a strong entry in the Hollywood/Wrightsville period
of the Queen canon. The psychological murder — terror as a weapon — is one of
the more unusual central devices in the Golden Age tradition, and the novel
uses it with real seriousness. The Priam household and its specific dynamics —
the wheelchair, the designed house, the marriage whose specific condition is
concealed for much of the novel — are rendered with the character depth that
the later Queen novels increasingly prioritised. The Wrightsville setting
provides the historical weight that the investigation requires.
