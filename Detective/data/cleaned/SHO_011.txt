The Adventure of the Priory School is a classic Holmes kidnapping‑turned‑murder case with a country‑house scandal at its core.​
Disappearance from the Priory School

The story begins when Dr. Thorneycroft Huxtable, pompous and respectable founder of the Priory School in northern England, appears in Baker Street and collapses dramatically at Holmes’s feet. He begs Holmes to help with a crisis: ten‑year‑old Lord Saltire, son of the immensely wealthy and influential Duke of Holdernesse, has vanished from the school overnight. The boy’s window was found open, the bed empty, and footprints outside are impossible because of the hard ground; since other boys were awake in the corridor, Huxtable believes Lord Saltire must have climbed down the ivy. At the same time, a German master, Heidegger, is also missing, along with his bicycle, suggesting he went after the boy and disappeared as well.

The Duke has already offered a large reward for any information about his son and an additional reward for the kidnapper’s identity. When Holmes and Watson travel north to Mackleton, they meet the Duke at Holdernesse Hall. The Duke swears he has received no ransom note and insists his estranged wife is not behind the disappearance. The local police have arrested a group of gypsies after finding the boy’s school cap in their possession, but the gypsies claim they simply found it on the moor. Holmes is unconvinced that the gypsies are responsible; he believes the cap and the absence of road sightings point to a cross‑country escape over the moor, not along the obvious road.
Tracks on the moor and the Fighting Cock

Holmes and Watson go out onto the moor to look for physical traces. At first they find bicycle tracks, but the tyre marks don’t match Heidegger’s machine; one tyre bears a distinctive patch. Oddly, the only clear marks on the ground everywhere are many cow hoof‑prints, yet they never see any cows. As they search further, they eventually come across Heidegger’s genuine bicycle tracks, which lead them to the master’s body on the moor: he’s clearly been killed, not merely injured. This raises the stakes from a simple abduction to a kidnapping plus murder.

Following the trail, Holmes and Watson arrive at the Fighting Cock Inn, run by Reuben Hayes. Hayes looks startled and uneasy when Holmes says he wants to visit Holdernesse Hall with news of the Duke’s son, and his entire manner suggests he is hiding something. Holmes notes again the abundance of “cow” hoof‑marks outside and grows suspicious of their odd pattern—trot, canter, and gallop—movements no actual cow would make. Sneaking into Hayes’s stable, Holmes inspects a horse’s hooves and finds evidence of recent shoeing with old shoes and new nails, clearly done to fake cow tracks. At the nearby blacksmith’s, he and Watson are aggressively driven away when they start asking questions, confirming that the so‑called cows are in fact horses shod with cow‑hoof‑shaped shoes from the Duke’s own collection of antique horse shoes.
James Wilder, the inn, and Holmes’s demand

Soon after, Holmes and Watson hide near the road and see a cyclist ride toward the inn from the direction of Holdernesse Hall. The rider is James Wilder, the Duke’s personal secretary, visibly agitated. He goes into the Fighting Cock; a trap (carriage) then leaves the yard headed toward Chesterfield, and later another, unidentified visitor arrives under cover of gathering darkness. Holmes examines Wilder’s bicycle tyres and recognizes them as the same make as the first set of tracks seen on the moor, with the same patched tyre. This ties Wilder directly to the mysterious cross‑country movements.

The next morning, Holmes and Watson go to Holdernesse Hall. The Duke is sick at heart after hearing of Heidegger’s death and the continuing absence of his son. Holmes, however, announces bluntly that he has solved the case and demands a cheque for £6,000, saying he has earned the reward. He tells the Duke that Lord Saltire is at the Fighting Cock Inn and that, astonishingly, the Duke himself is implicated in the affair, though not as its mastermind. Under pressure, the Duke confesses that James Wilder is his illegitimate elder son, a secret he has kept hidden to avoid scandal.

Wilder, embittered by his illegitimacy and furious that the legitimate younger son, Lord Saltire, would inherit the title and fortune, had concocted a scheme: he persuaded Hayes to abduct Lord Saltire from the Priory School and hide him at the inn. Wilder’s aim was to force his father to change his will in Wilder’s favor, relying on the Duke’s terror of public scandal to keep him from going to the police. Heidegger, having seen the boy climb down or otherwise leave, set off after him on his bicycle; Hayes encountered him on the moor and killed him, turning what Wilder imagined as a non‑fatal pressure tactic into a capital crime.
Motive, scandal, and Holmes’s moral compromise

When Wilder learned of Heidegger’s murder, he panicked and confessed everything to the Duke. Desperate to avoid the exposure of his secret elder son and the resulting scandal, the Duke made a morally disastrous choice: he agreed to keep quiet, allow Lord Saltire to remain hidden at the inn for several days, and give Hayes time to escape, even though this meant condoning kidnapping and aiding a murderer’s flight. Holmes judges this decision harshly, telling the Duke he has placed himself in a very serious legal position by condoning a felony and assisting a killer’s escape. Nonetheless, as a private consulting detective, Holmes is not legally bound to report everything; he chooses to balance strict justice with mercy and the boy’s welfare.

Acting on Holmes’s information, the authorities catch Hayes, who will face the gallows for Heidegger’s murder. Lord Saltire is safely retrieved from the Fighting Cock and brought home. The Duke, shamed and chastened, writes to his estranged wife, asking her to reconcile now that the main source of tension between them—James Wilder’s hidden presence and the resulting strain—is being removed. Wilder, whose plotting set the entire chain of events in motion, is packed off to Australia to seek his fortune, effectively exiled but not prosecuted, another compromise between exposure and quiet resolution.

Holmes also finally explains the “cow” tracks: Hayes and his accomplices used horses shod with special cow‑hoof‑shaped shoes from the Duke’s collection to lay false trails across the moor and confuse pursuers into thinking only cattle had passed that way. This clever trick, combined with Wilder’s insider position at Holdernesse Hall and the Duke’s fear of scandal, allowed the kidnapping plot to run far longer than it should have—until Holmes followed the tracks no one else really saw and forced the truth into the open.
