The Kims in the semi‑basement and the first “opportunity”

Kim Ki‑woo lives with his parents Kim Ki‑taek and Chung‑sook, and his sister Ki‑jung, in a cramped semi‑basement apartment in a poor Seoul neighborhood. They fold pizza boxes for money, chase free Wi‑Fi from the street, and watch drunk men urinate outside their window.

One day, Ki‑woo’s college‑bound friend Min‑hyuk visits with a proposition. He is going abroad and wants Ki‑woo to take over his job tutoring English to the daughter of a very rich family, the Parks. He gives Ki‑woo a scholar’s rock as a good‑luck gift and suggests Ki‑woo forge university documents to impress the Park mother. Though not in college, Ki‑woo agrees and Ki‑jung forges convincing certificates.
The Kims infiltrate the Park household

Ki‑woo goes to the Parks’ modern hillside mansion, designed by a famous architect and now owned by tech executive Mr Park Dong‑ik, his anxious wife Yeon‑kyo, teenage daughter Da‑hye, and young son Da‑song. The housekeeper Moon‑gwang, a holdover from the previous owner, manages the home.

Yeon‑kyo is naïve and impressed by Ki‑woo’s performance. He is hired as Da‑hye’s tutor. Soon he flirts with Da‑hye, beginning a secret relationship, and sees a chance to bring his family into the house.

Step by step, the Kims infiltrate:

    Ki‑jung as “Jessica”
    Ki‑woo suggests an “art therapist” for Da‑song, framing the boy’s erratic behavior as trauma from seeing a “ghost” years earlier. He recommends “Jessica,” actually Ki‑jung, who improvises art‑therapy jargon and quickly convinces Yeon‑kyo. She is hired at a high salary and begins manipulating Da‑song’s behavior to justify her importance.

    Framing the chauffeur
    The Kims next target the Park chauffeur. Ki‑jung leaves her underwear in the car; Ki‑woo plants the idea that the driver has been using the car for sex with young women. Mr Park, worried about scandal and contamination of his pristine car, fires the driver. Ki‑taek later appears as a “highly recommended” replacement, posed as a respectable, experienced chauffeur. He is hired.

    Removing Moon‑gwang
    Finally, they must remove the loyal housekeeper, Moon‑gwang. The Kims exploit her peach allergy: Ki‑jung secretly dusts slices of peach fuzz on Moon‑gwang, causing coughing fits and hives, and then engineers a scene where Yeon‑kyo sees Moon‑gwang coughing into a tissue with red smear (actually hot sauce), suggesting tuberculosis. Horrified by the idea of disease in the home, Yeon‑kyo dismisses Moon‑gwang.

Chung‑sook is brought in as the “perfect” replacement housekeeper, with the Kims hiding that they’re related. Over time, all four Kims are employed by the Parks in different roles, each maintaining separate fabricated identities.
The Parks go camping; the Kims “move in”

The Park family leaves for a camping trip for Da‑song’s birthday. With the house empty, the Kims treat it as their own: they eat expensive food, drink the Park’s liquor, lounge in the living room, and fantasize about living there permanently. Ki‑taek, drunk, talks about how the rich are “nice because they’re rich,” and Ki‑jung mocks Yeon‑kyo’s gullibility.

A heavy rainstorm begins; they watch it from the Park’s huge windows as something beautiful, unaware that this same rain will devastate their own neighborhood.
Moon‑gwang returns and the hidden bunker is revealed

Late that night, the doorbell rings. It is Moon‑gwang, drenched, carrying food and asking to be let in because she “forgot something” in the house. Reluctantly, Chung‑sook allows her in. Moon‑gwang goes straight to the basement, pushes aside shelves, and reveals a hidden bunker door built by the architect as an emergency shelter.

Inside the bunker, they find Moon‑gwang’s husband Geun‑sae, who has been secretly living there for years, hiding from loan sharks and surviving by stealthily taking food from the house. He is the “ghost” Da‑song saw in the past when Geun‑sae came up at night.

Moon‑gwang begs Chung‑sook to allow Geun‑sae to keep living there in exchange for monthly payments. But while they argue, Ki‑taek, Ki‑woo, and Ki‑jung slip down the stairs and are overheard. They tumble into view, revealing themselves as a family.

Realizing the Kims have been posing under false identities, Moon‑gwang grabs her phone and records video of them calling each other “Dad,” “Mom,” etc. She threatens to send the video to Yeon‑kyo unless the Kims submit and help her. The power dynamic flips: the Kims, who have been parasitic on the Park home, are themselves blackmailed by another hidden “parasite” family.

A violent struggle follows. The Kims subdue Moon‑gwang and Geun‑sae, eventually kicking them down the bunker stairs and tying them up, leaving Moon‑gwang with a serious head injury.
The Parks abruptly return; the basement chaos

Yeon‑kyo calls: the camping trip is cancelled due to the storm; the Parks will be home in eight minutes. Panic. The Kims scramble to clean the house, hide evidence of their occupation, and shove the injured Moon‑gwang and Geun‑sae back into the bunker.

As the Parks return, the Kims hide under tables and furniture:

    Ki‑taek, Ki‑woo, and Ki‑jung end up under the coffee table and in hidden spaces in the living room.

    Chung‑sook pretends she has been faithfully maintaining the house.

Mr and Mrs Park settle on the sofa above the hiding Kims, talking casually about their employees’ smell—especially Ki‑taek’s “subway smell”—and having a sexual moment. Ki‑taek overhears their disdain and disgust, internalizing the humiliation.

Later that night, the Kims manage to slip out of the house one by one in the rain.
The flood and the collapse of the Kims’ illusions

The rain that looked romantic from the Park home has flooded the Kim neighborhood. Sewage backs up into their semi‑basement; toilets spew brown water, and the family wades through filthy floodwater to salvage what little they can, including the scholar’s rock. They shelter in a crowded gym with other displaced poor families.

Ki‑taek, lying on the gym floor, tells Ki‑woo that the best plan is to have no plan—if you don’t plan, you can’t fail. It’s a crucial emotional turn: his hope is eroding.

Meanwhile, Yeon‑kyo, whose camping trip is ruined, decides to throw an impromptu garden birthday party for Da‑song the next day. She calls the Kims, expecting them to show up cheerful and ready to work, unaware that their home was destroyed the previous night.
The birthday party and the eruption of violence

The Kims return to the Park house in borrowed or donated clothes, hiding their exhaustion. Mr Park asks Ki‑taek to play “Indian” with Da‑song at the party, promising extra pay. Ki‑taek forces smiles but is increasingly resentful, especially after overhearing Mr Park again talk about his distaste for Ki‑taek’s smell.

Overcome by grief over Moon‑gwang’s death (her head injury from the fall has now killed her), Geun‑sae escapes from the bunker. He emerges during the party, still bleeding, with a heavy rock and a kitchen knife.

He ambushes Ki‑woo in the basement stairwell, smashing the scholar’s rock into Ki‑woo’s head, leaving him gravely injured. He then staggers outside into the garden party and stabs Ki‑jung in the chest, in front of guests.

Da‑song sees Geun‑sae—the “ghost” from his childhood—and has a seizure. Chaos erupts. Chung‑sook fights with Geun‑sae and kills him by impaling him with a barbecue skewer.

As Ki‑taek kneels over dying Ki‑jung, trying to stop her bleeding, Mr Park shouts at him to drive Da‑song to the hospital, treating Ki‑jung’s mortal wound as secondary to his son’s seizure. Mr Park tosses Ki‑taek the car keys. They land under Geun‑sae’s body.

Mr Park, trying to retrieve the keys, recoils in disgust from Geun‑sae’s smell, pinching his nose. Ki‑taek sees this—Mr Park’s reflexive revulsion at the “poor man’s smell,” applied equally to Geun‑sae and, symbolically, to Ki‑taek himself. The years of slights crystallize in that moment.

In a sudden, rage‑filled impulse, Ki‑taek picks up Geun‑sae’s knife and stabs Mr Park to death, in front of the horrified guests. Then he flees.
Aftermath: fractured families, no neat justice

Weeks later, Ki‑woo awakens from a coma, having survived severe brain trauma from the rock blow. He and his mother Chung‑sook are tried for fraud and related charges but receive probation; their crimes are seen as con jobs, not murders. Ki‑jung has died from her stab wound. Ki‑taek has vanished; the authorities believe he is on the run.

Publicly, Geun‑sae is written off as a random deranged homeless man who suddenly attacked the party. The media never uncover the full story of the bunker or the infiltration. The Parks’ house is sold to a foreign (German) family, who move in and live much as the Parks did, oblivious to the hidden history beneath them.

Ki‑woo, back in a semi‑basement with his mother, notices that at night a pattern of flickering lights from the former Park house appears in Morse code. Decoding it, he realizes it is a message from Ki‑taek, who never escaped: after killing Mr Park, Ki‑taek slipped back into the house and now lives in the bunker, just as Geun‑sae once did, hiding from police. He survives by sneaking upstairs for food when he can, burying Moon‑gwang in the garden, and sending daily Morse messages via the house lights, hoping Ki‑woo will see them.

Ki‑woo writes a letter to his father, voicing a fantasy plan: he will work, study, become rich, and one day buy the house outright. Then, on a sunny day, Ki‑taek will simply walk up the stairs from the bunker into the yard, free and reunited with his son and wife. We see this fantasy play out on screen: Ki‑woo in a suit, the family embracing in the garden.

But the film’s last shot returns to reality: Ki‑woo still in the dim semi‑basement, finishing his letter. The plan is almost certainly impossible; Bong Joon‑ho has said this cut back is his “surefire kill”—the confirmation that the dream of upward mobility is effectively dead.

No one is a pure victim or villain:

    The Kims deceive, exploit, and commit violence, yet are ground down by structural poverty.

    The Parks aren’t cartoonishly evil; they’re thoughtlessly insulated, their obliviousness and class prejudice culminating in Mr Park’s fatal disgust.

    Moon‑gwang and Geun‑sae are parasitic in one sense, yet also marginalized refugees from the same system.

The film ends with everyone trapped in different kinds of basements—literal and metaphorical—and with the sense that any “victim” is also someone else’s parasite, and vice versa.
