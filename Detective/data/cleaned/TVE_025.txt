TVE_025 | Twin Peaks: The Complete Laura Palmer Case | Season 1 & Early Season 2
Creator: Mark Frost & David Lynch | Various Directors
================================================================================
FULL STORY SYNOPSIS — SPOILERS INCLUDED
================================================================================

NOTE: This synopsis covers the complete Laura Palmer murder investigation
from the Twin Peaks pilot through the killer's revelation in Season 2,
Episode 7 ("Lonely Souls," directed by David Lynch, aired November 1990).
This constitutes the single self-contained case arc. The remaining episodes
of Season 2 follow a different storyline and are not covered here.

--- BACKGROUND AND CONTEXT ---

"Twin Peaks" was created by Mark Frost and David Lynch and first broadcast
on ABC in April 1990. The series is one of the most influential works in
American television history — it essentially created the prestige drama form,
demonstrated that network television could sustain complex serialized
storytelling, and introduced a visual and tonal language that influenced
everything that followed. Its creator David Lynch brought the surrealist,
dream-logic sensibility of his film work to a medium that had not previously
seen anything like it.

The investigation of Laura Palmer's murder is the spine on which everything
in the first season and the early second season hangs. Twin Peaks is not simply
a murder mystery — it is a mystery in which the investigation of a crime
gradually reveals the dark underside of a community that appeared, on the
surface, to be a paradise of normalcy. The show's central argument is that
evil is not exotic or external but domestic and intimate, hiding in the most
familiar places.

--- THE TOWN OF TWIN PEAKS ---

Twin Peaks is a small logging town in the Pacific Northwest, Washington State,
near the Canadian border. Population approximately 51,201. It is surrounded
by Douglas firs, mountains, and the Black Lodge — a mythic location in the
mythology of the show's supernatural layer. The town is introduced through its
surface: the Double R Diner, the Great Northern Hotel, the sawmill, the Sheriff's
station. Everyone knows everyone. It is, on the surface, the most ordinary
American small town imaginable.

The central characters who form the town's social world include:

Sheriff Harry S. Truman — the decent, solid, slightly bewildered local law
officer who becomes the primary official investigator.

Deputy Andy Brennan — gentle and prone to tears, whose emotional openness
is played first for comedy and then for genuine feeling.

Lucy Moran — the Sheriff's station receptionist, precise and somewhat literal.

Dr. Lawrence Jacoby — the town psychiatrist, eccentric, who was treating
Laura Palmer.

Donna Hayward — Laura's best friend, a high school girl devastated by the loss.

James Hurley — Laura's secret boyfriend, a brooding young man with a motorcycle.

Bobby Briggs — Laura's official boyfriend, superficially more conventional.

Ben and Jerry Horne — brothers who own much of the town's commerce, including
the Great Northern Hotel.

Audrey Horne — Ben's teenage daughter, clever and restless.

Pete and Josie Packard, Catherine Martell — the complicated triangle around
the Packard Sawmill.

Leland Palmer — Laura's father, a lawyer, a grieving parent.

Sarah Palmer — Laura's mother, a fragile and visionary woman.

Bobby's parents: Major Garland Briggs, a military intelligence officer with
connections to Project Blue Book and the supernatural mythology of the show.

Leo Johnson, Jacques Renault, and various denizens of the world across the
border, connected to the One-Eyed Jacks casino and to the darker life Laura
was living.

--- THE DISCOVERY: LAURA PALMER IS DEAD ---

The pilot episode opens on a gray February morning. Laura Palmer's body is
found wrapped in plastic on the bank of the lake at the edge of town. She is
beautiful. She is seventeen years old. She is dead.

Laura Palmer was the homecoming queen, the most beloved girl in Twin Peaks —
the girl who visited elderly residents and tutored children after school, who
was kind to everyone, who seemed to embody everything good about the town.
Her death is a catastrophic rupture in the community's self-image.

The FBI is called in — the body was found near state lines and the case
warrants federal involvement. Special Agent Dale Cooper is assigned.

--- DALE COOPER ---

Special Agent Dale Cooper of the FBI arrives in Twin Peaks and immediately
becomes one of American television's most beloved characters. He is:

Enthusiastic about coffee, pie, and the Douglas firs with a genuine openness
to sensory pleasure that is slightly incongruous in a murder investigator.

A man who records all his observations into a tape recorder as audio memos
addressed to his unseen assistant "Diane."

Intellectually formidable in ways that do not look like conventional detective
intelligence — his methods include intuition, dream interpretation, and
eventually direct consultation with supernatural entities.

Deeply decent, genuinely caring, and possessed of a specific moral clarity
that the show tests severely.

Cooper's relationship with Twin Peaks — his growing love for the town and its
people, his specific attraction to its coffee and cherry pie, his developing
friendship with Sheriff Truman — is as central to the show as the investigation
itself.

--- LAURA'S SECRET LIFE ---

The investigation of Laura's death proceeds on two levels simultaneously.
On the surface: Laura was a beloved homecoming queen. Beneath: Laura was living
a completely hidden second life.

She was a cocaine addict. She was involved with the sex trade across the
Canadian border — specifically with One-Eyed Jacks, a casino and brothel run
by Ben Horne with his partner Jean Renault. She had multiple secret relationships
including James Hurley. She had been, for years, the victim of sexual abuse
by a person she identified in her diary only as "BOB."

Laura's diary — obtained by Cooper through various means, including a secret
copy kept by Dr. Jacoby — is the primary documentary record of her hidden life.
It is a record of someone trying to survive an unbearable private horror through
dissociation and the various self-destructive behaviors that her double life
involved.

The portrait of Laura Palmer that emerges through the investigation is one of
the most complex and affecting in American television: a young woman who
maintained the appearance of a perfect life while suffering something that
nobody around her saw or addressed. The community's grief over the loss of
their beloved Laura is complicated, as the investigation progresses, by the
community's forced acknowledgment that they did not really know her — that
the Laura they loved was partly a performance by someone in desperate need of
help.

--- BOB ---

The supernatural element of Twin Peaks enters through "BOB" — a name Laura
wrote in her diary to identify the source of her abuse. BOB appears to Cooper
in dreams: a long-haired man with wild eyes who appears in visions and who
seems to represent something more than an ordinary human perpetrator. Cooper's
dream of BOB — in which he appears in the Red Room with the Little Man From
Another Place — is the pilot episode's most famous sequence.

BOB is both a real person and a supernatural entity in the show's mythology.
He is a malevolent spirit who inhabits human hosts, and it is through his
human host that he has been abusing and ultimately killing.

--- THE RED ROOM AND COOPER'S DREAMS ---

Cooper's investigative method is not entirely conventional. He receives
information through dreams — the Red Room dreams, in which he encounters
versions of Laura Palmer and a dwarf who speaks in reversed speech, and which
contain encoded information about the case. Lynch's direction of these sequences
is among the most distinctive in television history: the red curtains, the
black-and-white zigzag floor, the reversed speech, the dancing dwarf — an
entirely coherent visual and auditory language that is both dreamlike and
precisely structured.

The dreams provide Cooper with genuine investigative information: names, images,
partial revelations. They are not simply atmospheric but functional — Cooper
learns things in the Red Room that direct his waking investigation.

--- SEASON 1: THE INVESTIGATION EXPANDS ---

The seven episodes of Season 1 develop the investigation while introducing
an extraordinary number of subplots — the soap opera of Twin Peaks, which
involves the sawmill, the Horne family, various romantic entanglements, and
the supernatural mythology of the Black Lodge — that are both genuinely
interesting in themselves and structurally relevant to the central mystery.

Key developments in Season 1:

The identification of Flesh World magazine and the connection between Laura's
hidden life and the cross-border sex trade.

The investigation of One-Eyed Jacks and the Renault brothers (Jacques, Jean,
and Bernard) as people connected to Laura's world.

The discovery that Ronette Pulaski, another young woman, survived the same
night that Laura was killed — she was found wandering on the railroad bridge
in a catatonic state. Ronette's memories of that night, recovered gradually,
provide direct evidence of the killer's identity that the investigators are
trying to access.

Donna and James's amateur investigation of Laura's life, which leads them
into the same dark territory the police investigation is uncovering.

The MIKE and BOB mythology: Cooper learns through a vision that BOB is a
supernatural entity who was once partnered with MIKE (another spirit) and who
feeds on fear and suffering. MIKE, now inhabiting a one-armed man named Philip
Gerard, has been searching for BOB.

The increasingly disturbing behavior of Leland Palmer — Laura's father — who
is overtaken by apparent grief to the point of erratic, sometimes violent behavior.

--- THE SUSPECT: LEO JOHNSON ---

For much of Season 1, Leo Johnson — a local truck driver with a history of
violence and a connection to the drug trade — is the primary suspect. He is
genuinely connected to Laura's world (he supplied drugs, he moved in the same
criminal orbit), and his violent character makes him a plausible candidate.

Leo is eventually shot and left in a coma — by his wife Shelley's other lover,
Bobby Briggs — and is incapacitated but not resolved as the killer.

--- SEASON 2 BEGINS: BOB KILLS AGAIN ---

Season 2 opens with the murder of Maddy Ferguson — Laura's identical cousin,
who has come to Twin Peaks to stay with the Palmers during their grief. Maddy's
arrival creates a disturbing doubling: she looks exactly like Laura, which
affects different characters in different ways.

Maddy's murder, when it occurs in Episode 7 ("Lonely Souls"), is one of the
most disturbing sequences in American television — shot in real time, in the
Palmer living room, by David Lynch himself. The scene is extraordinary in its
sustained horror and in what it reveals: the killer's identity is shown directly,
and it changes everything.

--- THE REVELATION: LELAND PALMER IS BOB'S HOST ---

The killer of Laura Palmer — and now of Maddy Ferguson — is Leland Palmer.
Laura's father.

Leland is BOB's host. The supernatural entity BOB has been inhabiting Leland
since Leland was a child — Leland remembers an old man who came to his
grandfather's place when he was young, who told him to "let him in," and who
entered him. Leland has been, for much of his adult life, a man periodically
possessed by a malevolent spirit — sometimes aware of what he was doing,
sometimes not, sometimes a man in genuine anguish about what was happening
to him and unable to stop it.

Laura knew. She wrote in her diary about "BOB" — she knew the name her abuser
gave himself, she knew that what was being done to her was connected to something
her father could not entirely control, and she was both the daughter of Leland
and the victim of BOB inhabiting Leland. The double identity of her abuser —
her loving father and the monster inside him — is the most unbearable truth
in the show.

The abuse began when Laura was a child. It continued for years. It escalated.
On the night of her death, BOB/Leland killed her when she refused to be fully
possessed herself — BOB wanted to enter Laura, and she refused. The killing
was both punishment and an attempt to prevent exposure.

Ronette Pulaski was with Laura that night. The evidence from Ronette's recovered
memories confirms the sequence of events.

--- LELAND'S DEATH AND THE FINAL CONFESSION ---

Leland is arrested after the murder of Maddy. In the Sheriff's station holding
cell, the BOB possession becomes visible — the entity reveals itself to Cooper.
Leland, briefly himself again in the period before death, understands what has
happened. He weeps for what was done to his daughter — not entirely the monster,
not entirely innocent, a man who was both perpetrator and victim in a way the
show refuses to simplify.

Leland dies in the holding cell. BOB departs.

Cooper, Harry, and the others are left with the truth: Laura Palmer was killed
by her own father, acting as the vessel of a malevolent supernatural entity
that had been living in him since his childhood. The investigation that began
with a girl wrapped in plastic on the lakeshore ends with a confession made
to a dying man by something that wore his face.

--- WHAT THE CASE REVEALS ABOUT TWIN PEAKS ---

The resolution of the Laura Palmer case is devastating specifically because of
what it says about the town and the investigation. The evil was not external —
it was not a stranger, a criminal from across the border, a monster from outside.
It was in the Palmer house. It was at the breakfast table. It was in the most
ordinary and intimate space of a family home.

The community of Twin Peaks spent the entire investigation looking outward —
at Leo Johnson, at the Renault brothers, at One-Eyed Jacks — because the
alternative was to look at what was in front of them. Laura's suffering was
invisible precisely because she suffered at the hands of someone who was beloved
and trusted. Her double life — the drugs, the sex trade, the secret relationships —
were the survival strategies of a person who had no other way to bear what was
happening to her at home.

The show's lasting moral statement is about the invisibility of domestic
violence and childhood sexual abuse in communities that organize their self-
image around normalcy and togetherness. The homecoming queen, wrapped in
plastic, is an image of what a community produces when it refuses to see
what is happening in its own houses.

--- DALE COOPER'S POSITION ---

The investigation of Laura's murder is solved. The killer was identified,
confessed, and died. Cooper accomplished what he was sent to Twin Peaks to do.

But in the show's mythology, the story does not end cleanly for Cooper. BOB
is still present in the world, and the Black Lodge mythology — established
through the investigation — will demand further reckoning. The second half
of Season 2 and the 2017 continuation "Twin Peaks: The Return" follow what
happens to Cooper in the aftermath of the case. But those threads are beyond
the scope of the Laura Palmer investigation, which is complete.

--- NOTES ON THE SHOW ---

Twin Peaks Season 1 and early Season 2 constitute one of the great sustained
achievements in American television. The combination of genuine mystery
construction — the Laura Palmer case is fairly clued, in retrospect, and the
solution is both surprising and inevitable — with Lynch's specific visual and
tonal language created something that had no predecessors and many successors.
The show's treatment of Laura Palmer herself — not simply as a victim but as
a fully realized person whose suffering was real and whose hidden life was a
response to real abuse — is one of television drama's most serious and
consequential achievements.
