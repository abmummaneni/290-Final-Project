--- BACKGROUND AND CONTEXT ---

"A Caribbean Mystery" is a BBC Miss Marple adaptation first broadcast in
December 1989, directed by Christopher Petit. It stars Joan Hickson as Miss
Marple and is based on Christie's 1964 novel of the same name. This is one
of the few Marple stories set entirely outside England — Miss Marple is on
holiday at a resort hotel in the Caribbean, a setting that Christie renders
with the specific pleasure of someone who genuinely enjoyed such places, and
which gives the episode a warmth and visual richness that distinguishes it
from the English village and country house settings of most of the series.

The episode also introduces Jason Rafiel, the wealthy and acerbic businessman
who will posthumously commission Miss Marple in "Nemesis" — making this episode
the origin of one of the Marple series' most important relationships.

--- MISS MARPLE ON HOLIDAY ---

Miss Marple has been sent to the Golden Palm Hotel on a Caribbean island by
her nephew Raymond and his wife Joan, who felt she needed a holiday in the sun.
She is settled comfortably in the hotel, enjoying the warmth, the flowers,
and the parade of interesting people that a resort hotel provides.

Her fellow guests include:

Major Palgrave — a garrulous, somewhat boring retired military man who
buttonholes other guests and tells them interminable stories about his past
adventures.

Jason Rafiel — an extremely wealthy, physically disabled (and consequently
rather bad-tempered) man who is accompanied by his nurse Esther Walters and
his manservant Jackson. He is sharp, skeptical, and privately rather more
interesting than his gruff surface suggests.

Tim and Molly Kendal — the young couple who manage the hotel, warm and
apparently happily married.

Lucky and Edward Hillingdon — a handsome couple whose relationship has a
specific undercurrent of tension.

Colonel and Evelyn Hillingdon — an older couple connected to the Lucasta set.

Gregory and Lucky Dyson — another couple at the hotel.

Victoria Johnson — a local hotel employee.

--- MAJOR PALGRAVE'S STORY AND DEATH ---

Major Palgrave corners Miss Marple one afternoon and tells her one of his
stories — this one about a murderer. He says he once knew a man who had
murdered his wife and got away with it, and he produces a photograph from
his wallet to show her. As he is about to show her the photograph, something
over Miss Marple's shoulder catches his attention and he quickly puts the
photo away, changing the subject.

Miss Marple is curious about what he saw — what made him abruptly stop —
but the moment passes.

The next morning, Major Palgrave is found dead in his room. The cause of
death is attributed to a heart attack — he was known to have heart problems
and took medication for the condition. The hotel doctor certifies the death
as natural, and the island's authorities accept this. Most guests accept it.

Miss Marple does not. The timing — the day after Palgrave appeared to be
about to show her a photograph of a murderer — is too significant to ignore.
She is also troubled by something about the medication found in his room.

--- THE INVESTIGATION ---

Miss Marple begins her characteristic quiet investigation, talking to hotel
guests and staff with the appearance of a sociable elderly tourist and the
reality of a precise observer of human behavior. She attempts to find
Palgrave's photograph — which was in his wallet, which has now disappeared —
and to establish what he saw over her shoulder in the moment he stopped his story.

Her analysis of the moment: Palgrave was about to show her a photograph of
a murderer. He looked up and saw someone. He put the photo away. The person
he saw must be the murderer in the photograph — or the person connected to
the murderer — someone whose presence at the hotel told Palgrave that he
was in danger if he continued.

This means the murderer is among the hotel guests.

--- JASON RAFIEL ---

Miss Marple's relationship with Jason Rafiel develops through the investigation.
He is dismissive of her initially — an elderly tourist, obviously — but
becomes increasingly interested as he observes her intelligence. His own
specific knowledge of the hotel guests and his financial resources make him
a valuable if unconventional ally.

Rafiel's physical limitations — he cannot move without assistance — mean
that his contribution to the investigation is informational rather than
practical, but he pays close attention and his observations complement
Miss Marple's in important ways.

--- FURTHER DEATHS ---

The investigation becomes more urgent when additional deaths occur at the
hotel. A local girl — Victoria Johnson, who may have seen or known something
about Palgrave's death — is found dead. And another guest dies under suspicious
circumstances.

The pattern of deaths confirms what Miss Marple suspected: there is a murderer
at the Golden Palm Hotel, someone for whom Major Palgrave's recognition was
a death sentence, and who has been systematically eliminating anyone who might
expose them.

--- THE PHOTOGRAPH AND THE KILLER ---

The photograph Palgrave was going to show Miss Marple is eventually traced.
It showed a man. The man in the photograph is one of the male hotel guests —
a man whom Palgrave had encountered in a previous context where the man's
role in the death of a wife was visible to those who knew what to look for.

The killer is Gregory Dyson. His wife Lucky — apparently the devoted, attractive
companion of a successful man — is in fact his second wife. His first wife
died in circumstances that looked like accident but were not. Lucky herself
is in danger, though she does not know it: she is the next wife, and the
pattern of Gregory Dyson's behavior suggests that when a wife becomes
inconvenient, she dies.

Palgrave recognized Dyson from the photograph he carried — a photograph
of a man who had murdered his first wife and whose face Palgrave had connected
to that crime through some prior knowledge. When he looked up and saw Dyson
standing nearby as he was about to show Miss Marple the photograph, he
understood immediately that he was in danger.

Dyson killed Palgrave by substituting his heart medication — replacing his
normal medication with something that would cause a fatal cardiac event,
timed to occur in the night. This is the "murder by medication" that Miss
Marple identified: something about the specific pills found in Palgrave's
room was wrong, not consistent with the medication he was known to take.

--- RESOLUTION ---

Miss Marple exposes Gregory Dyson through the accumulated evidence — the
medication substitution, the identification through Palgrave's photograph,
the connection to the deaths of Victoria Johnson and the other guest, and
Dyson's history with his previous wife.

Jason Rafiel's resources and connections are used to ensure that the island's
authorities take the evidence seriously. Dyson is arrested.

Lucky Dyson survives — barely, in some versions — and is forced to confront
the truth about the man she married.

Miss Marple departs the Golden Palm Hotel, her holiday somewhat more eventful
than intended. Rafiel watches her go with the specific appreciation of a man
who has encountered, unexpectedly, someone worth knowing.

--- NOTES ON THE EPISODE ---

"A Caribbean Mystery" is one of the warmest of the BBC Marple adaptations —
the setting suits both the character and the story, and Hickson's enjoyment
of the holiday atmosphere is visible and genuine. The introduction of Jason
Rafiel, whose posthumous commission in "Nemesis" gives the character such
significance in the series, is handled with particular care: the relationship
between these two very different people — one elderly and apparently powerless,
one wealthy and physically limited — is drawn with real affection.
