NOV_003 — A Study in Scarlet
Arthur Conan Doyle, 1887
Full Plot Synopsis

PART ONE: THE MYSTERY IN LONDON


A Doctor Returns from War

Dr. John H. Watson, recently invalided home from the Second Anglo-Afghan War after being
wounded at the Battle of Maiwand, finds himself alone in London with limited means and no
fixed address. Through a mutual acquaintance, he is introduced to Sherlock Holmes, who is
also seeking someone to share lodgings. The two men meet in a laboratory at St. Bartholomew's
Hospital, where Holmes has just developed a chemical test for detecting bloodstains — his
first appearance in the entire canon. Holmes immediately deduces from Watson's appearance
that he has been in Afghanistan, startling the doctor. They agree to share rooms at 221B
Baker Street, and Watson begins to observe his peculiar new flatmate: a man of extreme
habits, extraordinary knowledge in narrow fields, and an inexplicable occupation as a
"consulting detective."


The Body at Lauriston Gardens

Scotland Yard detectives Gregson and Lestrade summon Holmes to a puzzling crime scene at
3 Lauriston Gardens, an empty house in Brixton. A man has been found dead in an empty room,
with no visible wounds and no apparent cause of death. On the floor, written in blood, is
the word RACHE. The police assume it is an incomplete attempt to write "Rachel." Holmes
examines the scene with minute precision — the body, the floor, the dust, the walls — and
draws a series of conclusions that astonish Watson and baffle the detectives: the dead man
was tall, middle-aged, had a ruddy complexion, and was in the company of someone else who
left footprints. Holmes determines RACHE is the German word for "revenge," not an unfinished
name. He also notices traces of a woman's wedding ring at the scene.

The victim is identified as Enoch Drebber, an American from Cleveland, Ohio, traveling with
a secretary named Joseph Stangerson.


A Second Murder

Before Holmes can fully unravel the first death, a second body is discovered: Joseph
Stangerson has been stabbed in a city hotel. On the wall beside him, again written in blood,
is the word RACHE. Holmes is not surprised. He tells Watson and the police that the murders
are connected and that he already knows who the killer is — he simply needs to confirm one
final detail and arrange for the man's capture.

Holmes places an advertisement in the papers, posing as someone who has found a wedding ring.
A man comes to collect it at Baker Street and Holmes has him arrested on the spot. The man
is Jefferson Hope, an American cab driver who has been working the London streets and who,
Holmes deduced, was one and the same person present at both murders.


The Method Explained

Holmes explains to Watson and the Scotland Yard detectives how he traced Hope: by reading
the physical evidence at both crime scenes — the footprints, the height suggested by the
stride, the tobacco ash, the style of the writing — to build a precise profile of the killer.
He had then scoured London's cab trade until he found the man who matched. Jefferson Hope
does not deny the murders. He is calm, almost serene, and asks only to tell his story before
he dies — for he has an aortic aneurism and knows he has only days left to live. He dies of
it in his prison cell before he can stand trial. But he tells his story first.

PART TWO: THE BACKSTORY IN UTAH


The Desert and the Rescue

The novel's second half travels back in time and across the Atlantic to the American West in
the 1840s. John Ferrier and a young girl named Lucy are the sole survivors of a wagon party
that has become lost and dying in the Utah desert. They are found and rescued by a large
column of Mormon settlers moving westward under the leadership of Brigham Young. Ferrier and
the child are taken in and saved — but only on the condition that they adopt the Mormon faith.
Ferrier agrees. He and Lucy (whom he adopts as his daughter) settle with the Mormon community
in the Salt Lake valley and build a prosperous life over the following years.


A Community with Iron Rules

Doyle portrays the Mormon theocracy of this period as oppressive and coercive, particularly
regarding the practice of polygamy. The Church's leadership, through a secret enforcement body
called the Avenging Angels (also known as the Danites), enforces absolute compliance with
doctrinal law. Men who have accumulated wealth or standing are expected to offer their
daughters in marriage to prominent elders. Dissent or departure is not tolerated. This is the
dark world in which Lucy Ferrier grows up.


Jefferson Hope and Lucy

When Lucy is a young woman, she falls in love with Jefferson Hope, a vigorous and independent
young frontiersman and silver miner who passes through the territory. The two become engaged.
Hope leaves temporarily to pursue a mining venture, intending to return and marry Lucy and
take her and her father away from the Mormon settlement before the Church can force another
match on her.

But while Hope is absent, the Mormon leadership moves. Brigham Young himself visits John
Ferrier and delivers an ultimatum: Lucy must be promised in marriage to one of two men —
Enoch Drebber or Joseph Stangerson, both sons of powerful elders — within a month.


Flight and Tragedy

Ferrier sends a secret message to Hope, who returns and helps Lucy and her father flee the
settlement by night. They escape into the mountains, pursued by the Avenging Angels. Ferrier
is caught and killed. Hope and Lucy become separated from each other. Lucy is taken back to
the settlement and forced to marry Enoch Drebber. She falls into a swift, grief-stricken
decline and dies within a month of the forced wedding.

Hope manages to recover her body long enough to take a keepsake — her wedding ring — which
he carries for the rest of his life. It is this ring that Holmes later uses as bait to trap him.


A Life Given Over to Vengeance

Jefferson Hope spends years in pursuit of Drebber and Stangerson. He tracks them across
America, but they are wealthy and move often, always staying just ahead of him. Eventually
they emigrate to Europe, and Hope follows. In London, he takes work as a cab driver because
it allows him to move freely through the city while watching his prey. When the moment finally
comes, he acts.

He forces Drebber to choose between two pills — one harmless, one containing a swift-acting
poison — leaving the choice to fate, or to God. Drebber takes the poison pill and dies.
Hope intended the same fate-driven method for Stangerson, but Stangerson attacked him first,
and Hope stabbed him in self-defense. The word RACHE written in blood was Hope's deliberate
message — a declaration that these deaths were acts of revenge, not random violence.

RESOLUTION

Jefferson Hope dies of his aneurism before trial, satisfied that he kept his promise to Lucy.
Holmes reflects on the case as a triumph of observation and deduction over the blunt methods
of official police work. Watson, newly fascinated by his companion, decides to write up the
case — setting in motion the entire literary relationship that will define the Holmes canon.
The novel ends with Holmes mildly irritated that Gregson and Lestrade received public credit
for the arrest, though Watson notes that Holmes seems indifferent to fame itself, interested
only in the problem.
