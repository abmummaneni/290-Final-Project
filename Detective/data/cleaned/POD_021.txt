--- BACKGROUND AND CONTEXT ---

"The Teacher's Pet" is an investigative journalism podcast produced by The
Australian newspaper and hosted by journalist Hedley Thomas, released in May
2018. It is one of the most consequential true crime podcasts ever made: the
series is directly credited with reviving public and prosecutorial interest
in a cold case that had been dormant for decades, and with ultimately driving
the criminal conviction of the man responsible.

The case concerns Lynette Dawson, a young mother who disappeared from her home
in the Bayview area of Sydney's Northern Beaches in January 1982. Her husband
Chris Dawson, a former professional rugby league player turned PE teacher,
reported her missing but did not report it to police for weeks. He told
acquaintances she had "gone away." Within weeks of her disappearance, a
teenage girl — one of his former students — had moved into the family home
to look after his two young daughters.

Lynette was never seen again. No body was found for decades. Chris Dawson
was never charged. The case was investigated periodically but the matter
was repeatedly closed without prosecution. For thirty-six years, Lynette's
family waited for justice.

--- THE DAWSON FAMILY AND ITS WORLD ---

The podcast opens by establishing the world of Sydney's Northern Beaches
in the late 1970s and early 1980s: a tight, prosperous, sports-obsessed
community where the Dawson brothers — Chris and Paul, both professional rugby
league players — were local celebrities. Chris taught physical education at
Cromer High School and coached swimming. He was popular, good-looking, and
accustomed to the specific social authority that a charismatic male PE teacher
held in that era and that environment.

Lynette was his wife — a nurse, a devoted mother to their two daughters
Shanelle and Sherryn, a woman described by family and friends as warm,
capable, and entirely without enemies. She was not unhappy in any way that
anyone who knew her could identify. She had no known reason to leave her
children, her home, or her life.

--- THE STUDENT: JC ---

Chris Dawson became obsessively involved with one of his students — a girl
referred to in the podcast as "JC" to protect her privacy, as she was a minor
at the time the relationship began. JC was perhaps fifteen or sixteen when
the relationship started. Chris was in his thirties, married, with children.

The relationship was, by any current or contemporary standard, a serious
abuse of a position of trust and authority. In the specific culture of the
Northern Beaches in the late 1970s, it was whispered about but not reported
or formally addressed. JC was, in the parlance of the time, his "girlfriend."

By the time Lynette disappeared in January 1982, JC had been involved with
Chris for some time. Almost immediately after Lynette's disappearance, JC
moved into the Dawson family home. Chris told people he and Lynette had
separated. He introduced JC to his daughters as a kind of surrogate mother.

--- THE DISAPPEARANCE ---

The specific circumstances of Lynette's disappearance are as follows, according
to Chris Dawson's various accounts (which changed over time in details):

In January 1982, Lynette left the family home. She took no significant personal
belongings, no money, no identification. She left her children behind. She
never contacted her parents, her sisters, her friends, or her children again.
She never attempted to access any financial accounts. She never appeared on
any government records. She never sought medical attention, renewed a license,
or surfaced in any way in the thirty-six years between her disappearance and
the resolution of the case.

Chris claimed she had gone to a religious cult or commune. He made vague
references to her having "wanted to get away." He did not report her missing
to police for some weeks. When he eventually did, the investigation was brief
and inadequate.

--- PREVIOUS INVESTIGATIONS AND FAILURES ---

The podcast documents the previous investigations into Lynette's disappearance,
which represent a series of institutional failures:

The original 1982 police inquiry was minimal. The investigating officers
accepted Chris's account too readily, failed to pursue obvious lines of
inquiry, and closed the matter quickly.

A coronial inquest in the 1990s found that Lynette was "likely dead" but
did not formally determine cause of death or implicate anyone.

Further investigations in the 2000s and early 2010s, prompted by continuing
pressure from Lynette's family — particularly her sister Helena — generated
media attention but no prosecution. The NSW Police, at various times, either
lacked sufficient evidence or lacked the will to bring charges against Chris
Dawson, who by this point had remarried and was living in Queensland.

The specific failures of each investigation are documented by Thomas with
particular attention to what was known at the time and what was not followed up.

--- HEDLEY THOMAS'S INVESTIGATION ---

Thomas's podcast investigation draws on newly conducted interviews with a
wide range of people connected to the case:

Lynette's family members — her sisters and parents who spent decades seeking
answers — provide the emotional core of the podcast. Their accounts of Lynette
as a person and of the specific circumstances around her disappearance are
central to the series.

Former students and colleagues from Cromer High School provide accounts of
Chris Dawson's behavior, the culture of the school, and specifically the
relationship between teachers and students that allowed the Dawson-JC
relationship to develop without institutional response.

JC herself — now an adult — speaks to Thomas at length. Her account of the
relationship, of what she observed in the Dawson household, and of specific
things Chris told her and did in the period around Lynette's disappearance
are among the most significant evidence in the podcast.

Former acquaintances of the Dawsons provide accounts of Chris's behavior
in the period before and after the disappearance — his attitude toward
Lynette, what he said about her, and specific incidents that retrospectively
look very different from how they were understood at the time.

--- JC'S TESTIMONY ---

JC's account is the podcast's most significant single contribution to the
public record. She describes:

The specific nature of her relationship with Chris from its beginning when
she was a schoolgirl — the grooming, the specific ways he managed her attachment,
and the explicit nature of the relationship that developed.

Conversations she had with Chris in which he spoke about Lynette in ways
that were alarming in retrospect: expressions of rage, specific things he said
about wanting Lynette gone, and a statement she gives considerable weight to
in which Chris allegedly told her directly that he had "got rid of" Lynette.

Her experience of moving into the family home and what that was like — looking
after two young girls in the house from which their mother had vanished, in
a relationship with the man who had taken her from her school.

The eventual collapse of her own relationship with Chris and the specific
circumstances of how she came to understand what she had been involved in.

--- THE BROADER COMMUNITY PICTURE ---

The podcast also establishes the broader social picture of how a man in
Chris Dawson's position was able to conduct an affair with a student without
institutional consequence, and was able to report his wife as a missing person
without serious investigation. Thomas is specific about the role of the
Northern Beaches sporting culture — the specific immunity from accountability
that athletic celebrity provided — and about the gender dynamics of the era:
Lynette's disappearance was not taken seriously partly because the assumption
was that women sometimes simply left difficult marriages.

Thomas also examines the role of the Church of Christ community in the story —
the religious connections of various people in the Dawson circle and the
specific ways those connections were relevant to what people knew and what
they said or did not say.

--- THE PODCAST'S IMPACT ---

"The Teacher's Pet" was downloaded more than thirty million times in its
first year, an extraordinary figure that made it Australia's most successful
podcast to that point. More significantly:

The podcast generated an overwhelming public response, including new witnesses
coming forward with information they had held for decades.

NSW Police publicly announced a renewed investigation shortly after the
podcast's release.

Chris Dawson was arrested in December 2018 — approximately seven months
after the podcast launched — and charged with Lynette's murder.

--- THE TRIAL AND CONVICTION ---

Chris Dawson was extradited to New South Wales and stood trial in 2022.
The trial was held before a judge alone (no jury) at Dawson's request. The
case was built on circumstantial evidence — no body had been found and no
physical evidence of the killing survived — but the circumstantial case was
extensive and compelling:

Chris's multiple inconsistent accounts of Lynette's departure. His failure
to report her missing. His immediate installation of JC in the family home.
JC's testimony about what Chris had told her. The testimony of others about
his behavior and statements. The complete absence of any trace of Lynette
after January 1982.

In August 2022, Justice Ian Harrison found Chris Dawson guilty of Lynette's
murder beyond reasonable doubt. He was sentenced to twenty-four years in
prison with a non-parole period of eighteen years.

--- LYNETTE'S REMAINS ---

At the time of conviction, Lynette's remains had still not been formally
located, though excavations of properties connected to Chris Dawson were
conducted at various points. The conviction was secured without physical
remains — based entirely on the weight of the circumstantial evidence.

--- HELENA AND FAMILY ---

Lynette's sister Helena, who spent decades driving the campaign for justice
and who was the family's most publicly visible advocate, lived to see the
conviction. The emotional resolution for Lynette's family — incomplete
in the absence of remains but real in the fact of the verdict — is one of
the podcast's most affecting threads.

--- SUMMARY ---

"The Teacher's Pet" is one of the clearest examples of investigative journalism
through the podcast format producing direct real-world consequence. Thomas's
investigation did not simply document a case but actively changed its trajectory.
The full arc — the 1982 disappearance, the decades of institutional failure,
the podcast investigation, the renewed police investigation, the arrest, and
the 2022 conviction — is one of the most complete and consequential stories
in true crime podcast history.
