Setup: the Club and a new case

The novel returns to Coopers Chase, a luxury retirement village in Kent, where the Thursday Murder Club—Elizabeth, Joyce, Ibrahim, and Ron—meet weekly to pore over old case files and, increasingly, real crimes. Elizabeth is a sharp former British intelligence (MI5) operative, Joyce a warm ex‑nurse who narrates parts of the story in her chatty diary style, Ibrahim a meticulous retired psychiatrist, and Ron a blunt former trade‑union firebrand. Their comfortable routine is disrupted when Elizabeth receives an unsettling letter from someone using the name “Marcus Carmichael,” a man whose death she once faked in an operation years ago and who, in reality, never existed as a real person. When she goes to the meeting, she discovers the sender is actually her ex‑husband, Douglas Middlemiss, a charming but unreliable MI5 agent she once worked with and later divorced.

Douglas arrives at Coopers Chase under the protection of a young, somewhat green handler named Poppy. He confides to Elizabeth that he is in serious danger: while infiltrating the home of a criminal financial middleman, Martin Lomax, he stole around £20 million in diamonds being held on behalf of international gangsters and drug lords. Douglas never told MI5 he had taken them and now various violent criminals, including an American mobster and a Colombian cartel figure, want their diamonds back. If they are not returned, Martin, and by extension Douglas, will be killed. Douglas is hiding out in Coopers Chase until he can escape abroad, and he asks Elizabeth to help protect him and manage the threat. Sensing a new puzzle, Elizabeth pulls in the rest of the Thursday Murder Club, while keeping Douglas’s presence semi‑secret.
Ibrahim’s attack and the local crime thread

Parallel to this espionage‑laden plot, a more grounded crime thread begins with Ibrahim. While running a simple errand in town, he is attacked by a teenage thug, Ryan Baird, who shoves him to the ground, steals his phone, and leaves him badly injured. The incident deeply shakes Ibrahim’s confidence, leaving him anxious, withdrawn, and wary of going out, and it becomes a personal crusade for the others to secure justice for him. Local police friends DCI Chris Hudson and PC Donna De Freitas suspect Ryan is involved and know he works for a small‑time but vicious local drug dealer, Connie Johnson, but they lack evidence strong enough to charge him.

Elizabeth, unwilling to let formal justice fail Ibrahim, hatches a scheme. She asks her helpful, quietly dangerous friend Bogdan—a Polish handyman with a talent for getting things done—to obtain some cocaine. Working from Elizabeth’s plan, Ron poses as a plumber sent by the housing authority to Ryan’s house, and while “inspecting” the property he plants the drugs and Ibrahim’s stolen credit card in the cistern of Ryan’s toilet. An anonymous tip to Chris and Donna leads to a search, the drugs and card are found, and Ryan is arrested. This vigilante‑style operation shows the Club’s moral flexibility: they will frame someone who truly did harm Ibrahim in order to ensure he doesn’t walk away unpunished.
Diamonds, assassins, and the first deaths

Back at Coopers Chase, Douglas and Poppy settle into a safe house flat. Elizabeth begins building a perimeter of protection, quietly observing suspicious comings and goings around the retirement village, and pressuring Douglas to be honest about what he’s done and where the diamonds are. He admits he did steal them but claims he has hidden them in a way only Elizabeth will be able to work out later. Elizabeth, wary of being manipulated by him again, insists on trading favors: she wants information on Ryan Baird’s background, which Poppy and Douglas eventually provide, helping the Club in their quest against Ryan.

Before long, the danger Douglas warned about materializes. An intruder breaks into the flat at Coopers Chase, clearly intending to kill Douglas. In a tense confrontation, the nervous but determined Poppy shoots the would‑be assassin dead. MI5 quickly swoops in to manage the fallout: senior agents Sue Reardon and Lance James arrive to sanitize the crime scene, take statements, and reassert official control over Douglas. The Club offers Poppy emotional support, but the incident proves that the criminals hunting the diamonds know at least roughly where Douglas is hiding and are closing in.

Douglas is eventually moved with Poppy to another safe location. Soon after, shocking news arrives: Douglas and Poppy have apparently been murdered in a shooting at a safe flat in Hove. Their deaths are presented as a tragic but unsurprising outcome—Douglas, the reckless thief, finally pays the price; Poppy, the rookie handler, was caught in the crossfire. Elizabeth is notified through MI5, and the official version is that a hit team found them and executed them both. For the Thursday Murder Club, this solidifies the stakes: what began as a favor to an old colleague has transformed into a full‑blown murder investigation.
Untangling spies, gangsters, and false leads

The question now is who killed Douglas and Poppy and where the diamonds are. The Club fans out across several fronts. Elizabeth visits Martin Lomax, the money man, who is terrified: the diamonds belonged to extremely dangerous people, and they will kill him if he cannot produce them. He admits he did send someone to Coopers Chase to put pressure on Douglas, but he insists he was not behind the Hove shootings, claiming the international gangster Andrade is the real threat. Meanwhile, MI5’s Lance tracks Andrade’s movements and confirms he is traveling to the UK, increasing the pressure on everyone.

Elizabeth also scrutinizes the internal MI5 players. Sue and Lance are ostensibly allies, but Elizabeth has lived a lifetime in espionage and trusts no one entirely. She notices inconsistencies in the story around the Hove shootings and in how the evidence and timelines are presented. Simultaneously, Joyce’s diary entries and the group’s conversations reveal little clues: a note slipped into Joyce’s pocket about a woman named Siobhan, connections to Poppy’s family, and odd behaviors around some of the MI5 agents. While the reader is presented with an intricate web of suspects—Martin Lomax, foreign gangsters, Ryan’s boss Connie Johnson, and shadowy figures from MI5—Elizabeth begins to suspect that the truth is less convoluted and more personal.

In the background, the local police storylines continue. Chris and Donna work to build a case against Connie Johnson, hoping to take down her drug operation, while also exploring their own personal lives and relationships. Their investigation eventually intersects with the diamond plot when Connie’s criminal network is used as a cover or intermediary in the negotiations over the stolen jewels. The Thursday Murder Club, Chris and Donna, MI5 (Sue and Lance), Connie, Martin, and the foreign crime bosses are all drawn into a spiraling convergence, orchestrated in large part by Elizabeth’s behind‑the‑scenes planning.
The climactic meeting and the real killer

Elizabeth decides the only way to end the stand‑off is to flush everyone into the open. She engineers a high‑stakes meeting where multiple parties will gather under controlled conditions. Martin Lomax, desperate to save his skin, is told that Andrade will get his diamonds back. Andrade is led to believe his property will be returned. Connie Johnson is drawn in under the impression that she will be exchanging diamonds for cash on behalf of a supposed London gangster (in reality, a role linked through Ron and the Club). Bogdan and Lance are positioned as armed “bodyguards” in the meeting room to keep things from spiraling out of control, while Chris and Donna wait outside to make arrests.

When the meeting takes place, tensions spike immediately. Connie produces only two diamonds, because Elizabeth has hidden the rest as leverage. Andrade realizes he has been tricked and that the full amount is not present. Furious, he turns his gun on Martin and shoots him. Connie, acting on instinct for self‑preservation, then shoots Andrade before he can kill her as well. With both men down, Connie attempts to flee, but Chris and Donna rush in from outside and arrest her on the spot, along with Ryan, who has been serving as her driver. The violent confrontation removes several dangerous players from the board and provides the police with the opportunity to wrap up their drug case, but it still leaves the core mystery—who killed Douglas and Poppy, and where the diamonds are—only partially solved.

After the meeting, Elizabeth and Joyce find themselves in a vehicle with Sue, ostensibly being driven back under MI5 protection. Elizabeth has used the chaos and information from the day to re‑evaluate everything they thought they knew. She now believes that the elaborate story involving Poppy’s mother Siobhan, suggested by a note Joyce received, was a deliberate misdirection. The note, she reasons, could not realistically have come from Poppy at the time they believed; instead, it must have been planted by someone who wanted them to look at Siobhan and away from the real culprit.

In the car, Elizabeth confronts Sue directly. She lays out the logic: Poppy and Douglas are both genuinely dead—there were no faked deaths this time—and the person who benefited most from their deaths and had access to all the necessary information was Sue. Elizabeth deduces that Sue was romantically involved with Douglas. Together, Douglas and Sue stole the diamonds, planning to escape and sell them for themselves, but Douglas intended to double‑cross her. When Sue realized his betrayal, she killed him and Poppy and then continued to insert herself into the investigation, steering suspicion toward other suspects while working alongside Elizabeth to “solve” the case. Now that Elizabeth has pieced it together, the façade crumbles. Sue admits her involvement in both the theft and the murders, exposing the heart of the “man who died twice” mystery: Douglas, who should have died once in an earlier faked operation, truly died this time at the hands of someone he loved and betrayed.
Resolution, diamonds, and the Club’s aftermath

With Sue unmasked, the institutional side of the story is tied off: MI5 can quietly deal with one of their own turning rogue, the foreign criminals have been either killed or neutralized, Connie and Ryan are in custody, and Martin Lomax is dead. What remains is the matter of the missing diamonds. Before his death, Douglas had left a cryptic message for Elizabeth suggesting that she already “knew” where the diamonds were, implying that the hiding place was something obvious once she thought about it. Elizabeth and Joyce eventually trace this clue to a train station locker Douglas had used, where they first find only an empty chip bag. However, using an ultraviolet light, Elizabeth reveals a message on the bag that confirms the diamonds are hidden elsewhere and that this locker was only an extra layer of security. Following Douglas’s trail through these clues, Elizabeth recovers the stones and quietly decides their fate.

True to the series’ tone, the ending balances justice with mischief. The diamonds are not simply handed back to the criminal underworld, nor are they entirely surrendered to the government; instead, Elizabeth ensures they are used in ways that align with her own sense of morality and with the Club’s interests, including a measure of financial security for people she cares about. Ibrahim gradually begins to reclaim his confidence with the support of his friends, facing his trauma step by step. Chris and Donna’s professional and personal lives continue to evolve, hinting at further adventures. Joyce remains delighted by the excitement and by the handsome men who drift in and out of their orbit, and Ron relishes having another story of derring‑do under his belt.

By the close, the Thursday Murder Club has once again solved a set of intertwined crimes involving multiple murders, high‑level corruption, and dangerous criminals, all while navigating the everyday realities of aging, friendship, and loneliness. The title, The Man Who Died Twice, refers both to the earlier fictional death of “Marcus Carmichael” that Elizabeth helped stage and to Douglas’s real death in the present, encapsulating the book’s blend of spycraft, deception, and second chances that run out.
