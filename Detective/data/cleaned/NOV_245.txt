DETAILED SPOILER SYNOPSIS

Journey Under the Midnight Sun (Byakuyakō, literally "Whitewalk" or "Walking in the White Night") is widely considered Higashino's masterpiece and one of the greatest crime novels of postwar Japanese fiction. It spans nearly twenty years of investigation and character study, following two children from the night of a murder in 1973 through their adult lives in 1992. The novel is structurally unconventional: it is told almost entirely from the outside, through the perspectives of peripheral characters, investigators, and witnesses — the two central figures, Ryoji and Yukiho, are almost never seen from the inside. The reader gradually assembles their story through what other people observe and report.

THE INCITING CRIME: 1973

A pawnshop owner named Kirihara is found murdered in an abandoned building in Osaka. He has been strangled. The primary suspect is Kirihara's wife's lover, a man named Nio Masaharu. Nio is questioned but no charges are filed and the case goes cold. Detective Sasagaki becomes personally obsessed with the case, believing the official conclusion is wrong.

Two children are present in the background of this crime: Kiriahara's son, Ryoji (age approximately 11), and Nio's daughter, Yukiho (age approximately 10). Their connection to each other — and to the murder — will only become clear much later.

THE NOVEL'S STRUCTURE

The narrative proceeds in chapters covering different years, each focusing on a different set of peripheral characters who come into contact with either Ryoji or Yukiho. Ryoji and Yukiho grow up separately in apparent poverty and social difficulty, yet both achieve remarkable things — Yukiho in particular becomes a successful, beautiful, and socially elevated woman, moving through Tokyo's high society. Ryoji operates in shadows, involved in criminal enterprises.

What emerges gradually, through accumulation of episodes, is that Ryoji and Yukiho are secretly connected and have been working in tandem throughout their entire adult lives. Yukiho's ascent through society has been engineered and protected by Ryoji, who operates as her hidden instrument — removing obstacles, silencing threats, and enabling her advancement while remaining invisible. They are never seen together. Their coordination is entirely covert.

THE ACTUAL EVENTS OF 1973 (Revealed Gradually)

The murder of Kirihara was not committed by Nio Masaharu. It was committed by the two children.

On the night of the murder, Kirihara had been sexually abusing Yukiho — he had been molesting her for some time. Ryoji witnessed this. In the confrontation, Kirihara was killed. The two children, age 10 and 11, covered up the crime and allowed suspicion to fall on Nio (Yukiho's father, the legitimate suspect), who died by suicide before he could be fully exonerated.

The children made a pact that night, the terms of which drive the entire subsequent narrative: they would never be together openly, would never acknowledge each other, but Ryoji would protect Yukiho and enable her to live the life that had been stolen from her by abuse and poverty. They would walk under the midnight sun — in the perpetual light of a Nordic summer, where the sun never sets and there are no shadows, no darkness to hide in — except they walk in darkness while appearing to be in light. The title is ironic: they cannot actually walk in the midnight sun; they can only simulate it.

THE CRIMES OF THE ADULT YEARS

Over the subsequent decades, Ryoji commits multiple murders to protect Yukiho and advance her interests. Individuals who pose threats to Yukiho — who know something compromising, who threaten to expose her past, who stand between her and advancement — are killed. Yukiho herself may be directly responsible for at least one death (her first husband dies under circumstances that benefit her, and her involvement is strongly implied).

The peripheral characters whose stories comprise the novel's chapters are often victims, potential victims, or people who get close to the truth. The reader watches them approach the hidden story and then watches them be deflected or destroyed.

DETECTIVE SASAGAKI

Sasagaki, the detective who investigated the 1973 Kirihara murder, never closes the case. He returns periodically across the decades, following leads, watching Yukiho's career from a distance, unable to make anything stick. He becomes a kind of haunting presence in Yukiho's life — she knows he watches her. His investigation represents the possibility of justice that is perpetually deferred.

THE CONCLUSION

The novel's ending is deliberately ambiguous and withholding. By the final pages, the reader knows everything — the original murder, the pact, the subsequent crimes, the entire architecture of Ryoji and Yukiho's secret collaboration. But justice, in the conventional sense, does not arrive.

Ryoji's fate is sealed: he dies, either killed or through circumstances connected to the final unraveling of their arrangement. The manner and exact circumstances are characteristically indirect. Yukiho's fate is left unresolved in the most unsettling sense — she survives, but the reader understands that the price of her survival is the entirety of her humanity. She has never had a real relationship, never existed openly, lived her whole adult life as a performance.

Sasagaki, now old and retired, does not achieve a prosecution. Whether he reaches some form of private understanding of the truth is suggested but not confirmed.

THEMES AND NOTES

Byakuyakō is a novel about the long consequences of childhood trauma and the impossibility of escaping the past. The abuse Yukiho suffered, and the murder that resulted, set in motion a chain of events that destroys dozens of lives over two decades. The novel asks whether survival by these means is truly survival at all.

The structural choice — never entering the protagonists' interiority — creates a sustained and deeply uncomfortable effect: the reader desires to understand Ryoji and Yukiho but is kept at the same distance as the peripheral characters who can only observe them from outside. This is a formal enactment of the novel's theme: these two people are fundamentally unknowable because they have made themselves unknowable in order to survive.

The title's Japanese original, Byakuyakō (白夜行), uses the concept of the "white night" (byakuya — the phenomenon of polar summer with continuous daylight) — a place where there are no shadows in which to hide. The tragic irony is that the characters spend their entire lives in darkness precisely because they were denied the chance to live openly. The English title, Journey Under the Midnight Sun, captures the same paradox.

The novel was adapted as a Japanese television drama in 2006 (TBS, starring Takuya Kimura and Ryoko Shinohara) and as a Korean film, White Night (2009). The drama is considered particularly successful.
