--- BACKGROUND AND CONTEXT ---

"Unforgotten" Series 3 was broadcast on ITV in July–August 2018. It consists
of six episodes and follows the series' established format while significantly
developing both the central investigation's moral complexity and the personal
lives of DCI Cassie Stuart and DS Sunny Khan. Series 3 is notable for being
the darkest and most morally demanding of the three series to that point — the
original crime is more intimate and more implicating, the suspects are in some
ways more sympathetic than those of previous series, and the specific cost to
the investigative team is more directly personal.

The series also deepens the portrayal of Cassie Stuart in ways that Series 1
and 2 did not quite reach: her specific personal situation, and the specific
weight that the series of cold case investigations has accumulated in her, are
given more space in Series 3 than previously.

--- THE COLD CASE: PETE CARR ---

A body is discovered in a river — the remains of Pete Carr, a young man who
disappeared in 1990. The identification of his remains opens an investigation
into his death, which the physical evidence establishes was a killing — he
was murdered — and concealed for nearly thirty years.

Pete Carr was, the investigation establishes, a troubled young man in 1990:
someone who was on the edges of stability, who had specific vulnerabilities,
and who moved through a specific world of temporary accommodation, social
services, and the particular kind of institutional care that young people
without family support systems received in 1990. He was not, in the conventional
sense, a person whose disappearance would have been aggressively investigated —
a young man with no fixed address and no powerful advocates whose absence from
the world was noted but not urgently pursued.

--- THE FOUR SUSPECTS ---

The four people identified as connected to Pete Carr and to the circumstances
of his death in 1990 are among the most carefully drawn in the series:

DS Sal Brady (Nicola Walker's partner in the investigation) — wait, the suspects
are civilian figures, not police. The four suspects in Series 3 are:

James Hollis (Neil Morrissey) — a family man and businessman whose present
life is entirely ordered and entirely ordinary, and whose specific connection
to Pete Carr is buried in a youth he does not revisit.

Hayley Reid (Clare Calbraith) — a woman whose present life includes a family
and a community role, whose specific history with Pete Carr is connected to
a specific period of her youth and the specific circumstances in which she
encountered him.

Tim Finch (Mark Bonnar returning in a different role) — a man whose specific
profession and specific present life are built on foundations that the investigation
will test.

Chris Lowe (Kevin McNally) — an older man whose specific history in the social
services world of 1990 is the most directly connected to the institutional
dimension of Pete Carr's story.

--- THE 1990 WORLD ---

The reconstruction of Pete Carr's life and death requires understanding the
specific world of 1990 — specifically the world of young people without family
support in that period: the council provision, the temporary accommodation,
the specific institutional culture of social services and the specific ways in
which young people in care were managed, or failed to be managed, in that
period.

The show is attentive to the specific conditions of institutional care in 1990:
the specific failures of a system that was underfunded, understaffed, and
operating with specific assumptions about young people's needs and capabilities
that subsequent decades have revised. Pete Carr's specific vulnerability was
a function of a specific time and a specific institutional failure, and the
investigation must understand that context to understand what happened to him.

--- THE INTIMATE SCALE OF THE CRIME ---

One of Series 3's most distinctive qualities is the intimate scale of the
original crime. Where Series 1's Jimmy Sullivan was killed in circumstances
involving violence with multiple actors, and Series 2's David Walker in
circumstances with a specific social context, Pete Carr's death is smaller
and more personal: a killing that arose from a specific situation among specific
people in specific circumstances that were not large or dramatic but simply
human and terrible.

The specific intimacy of what happened — the specific people involved, the
specific place, and the specific dynamics of a situation that escalated in
a way that nobody involved fully intended or predicted — is one of Series 3's
most carefully handled elements. The show is interested in how ordinary people
come to do terrible things in ordinary circumstances, and Pete Carr's case
is its clearest expression of that interest.

--- CASSIE'S PERSONAL ARC ---

Series 3 gives Cassie Stuart her most sustained personal development in the
series. Her relationship with her partner Martin, and the specific decisions
she is navigating about her personal life, are given meaningful space alongside
the investigation. The specific weight that three series of cold case
investigations have accumulated in her — the specific way that sustained
engagement with decades-old deaths and the people who caused them affects
someone with her specific sensitivity and specific commitment — is present
in Series 3 in ways that previous series have suggested but not fully explored.

Nicola Walker's performance in Series 3 is perhaps her finest in the role:
a woman whose professional commitment and personal warmth are both real, and
whose specific management of what the work costs her is rendered with a
specificity and an honesty that gives the series its most fully realised
central character portrait.

--- SUNNY'S SITUATION ---

DS Sunny Khan's personal situation is also given more space in Series 3 than
previously. The specific complications of his personal life — his family
situation, his specific relationship with someone connected to his world outside
work — are developed in ways that begin to establish him as a fully three-
dimensional figure rather than primarily Cassie's reliable professional partner.

Sanjeev Bhaskar's performance, always assured, deepens in Series 3 as the
character is given more to carry.

--- THE TRUTH OF PETE CARR'S DEATH ---

The investigation assembles the full picture of what happened to Pete Carr.
The truth is both smaller and more affecting than a large-scale conspiracy:
Tim Finch bears the most direct responsibility for Pete's death — it was Tim's
specific act of violence in a specific moment of confrontation at a specific
location that caused Pete's death. The other suspects — James Hollis, Hayley
Reid, and Chris Lowe — were present or connected to the events in varying
degrees, some helping to manage the aftermath and the concealment across the
thirty years since. The specific culpability of each — who struck the blow,
who was present and failed to summon help, who participated in the concealment —
is the investigation's most morally demanding distinction to establish. The distribution of culpability among the four suspects —
the specific degrees of directness and complicity, the specific choices each
of them made both then and since — is the investigation's most morally complex
element.

--- RESOLUTION ---

The full picture of Pete Carr's death is established and the appropriate legal
processes engaged. The specific people responsible face the consequences of
the truth's emergence.

Series 3's ending carries a specific development that will be significant going
forward: an event that affects Cassie directly and that changes the series'
landscape beyond the immediate case. The specific nature of this event — and
what it means for the show's central character — is the conclusion that Series
3 is building toward from its first episode.

--- NOTES ON THE SERIES ---

Unforgotten Series 3 is the most mature and most emotionally demanding entry
in the series to that point. Its cold case is the most intimate — the smallest
in scale, the most ordinary in its human circumstances, and for those reasons
the most unsettling. The suspect ensemble, while less starry than Series 1's
casting, is among the series' most effective: the specific distribution of
guilt and complicity, and the specific ways in which the investigation affects
each of the four, is handled with the moral precision that Unforgotten consistently
achieves. And Nicola Walker's Cassie Stuart reaches her fullest and finest
expression in this series.
