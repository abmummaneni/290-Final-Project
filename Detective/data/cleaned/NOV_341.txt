NOV_341 — Confessions (告白)
Kanae Minato, 2008 (English translation 2014)
Full Plot Synopsis

STRUCTURE NOTE

The novel is told in six first-person monologues, each from a different narrator. Together
they tell a complete story, but each narrator is unreliable in their own way, and each
reveals things the previous narrators withheld or misunderstood. The effect is cumulative
and deeply disorienting — what seems certain in one chapter is complicated or shattered in
the next.

CHAPTER ONE: THE TEACHER'S CONFESSION


Yuko Moriguchi's Address to Her Class

Middle school teacher Yuko Moriguchi addresses her homeroom class on her last day before
resigning. Her four-year-old daughter Manami drowned in the school's swimming pool. It was
ruled an accident. The teacher tells her students — calmly, without raising her voice, in
the horrifying register of someone who has nothing left to lose — that it was not an accident.
Two students in this very classroom murdered her daughter.

She does not name them to the police or the school authorities. She names them to their
classmates: Student A and Student B. Both are thirteen. Under Japanese law at the time,
children under fourteen cannot be criminally prosecuted.

Since the law cannot touch them, she has devised her own punishment. She has contaminated
the milk cartons of both students with HIV-positive blood — blood obtained from her late
husband, who died of AIDS. She does not know if they will contract the virus. She does not
particularly care whether they do. She wants them to live with the fear.

She leaves. The chapter ends.

The effect is one of the most devastating opening chapters in contemporary crime fiction.

CHAPTER TWO: THE SAINT


A Classmate's Account

The second chapter is narrated by a classmate — a girl — who provides an account of what
happened to the two boys after Moriguchi's revelation. Student B, identified as Naoki
Shimomura, became consumed by terror and germophobia after learning about the milk. He
began to unravel. His behavior at school became extreme. His relationship with his mother —
overly close, dysfunctional — became the lens through which his deterioration was experienced
and, by his mother, enabled.

Student A, identified as Shuya Watanabe, showed almost no outward reaction. This made him
more frightening to his classmates, not less.

This chapter also begins to reveal more about the killing of Manami — why it happened, what
the boys were thinking, and what role each played.


━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━


CHAPTER THREE: THE MOTHER


Naoki's Mother Speaks

Naoki's mother narrates this chapter. She is a woman of profound, suffocating devotion to
her son — devotion that has curdled into something damaging for both of them. She refused
to believe her son was capable of killing anyone. She constructed elaborate alternative
explanations. She isolated him from the world's judgment and from consequences.

As Naoki descended into paranoia and self-destruction — eventually committing acts of
serious violence — his mother remained committed to her mythology of his innocence. The
chapter is devastating because it reveals how maternal love, uncorrected by honesty, can
become a mechanism of harm. Her son's psychological state by the end of this chapter is
catastrophic.

CHAPTER FOUR: THE CLASSMATE


Naoki's Own Voice

Naoki himself narrates this chapter. His account of events — his own psychology, his
relationship with Shuya, his role in Manami's death — reveals new dimensions of what
happened. He was involved in the killing, but his agency in it was not what has been
assumed. He was manipulated by Shuya, who understood him and used that understanding.

Naoki is not sympathetic exactly, but he is pitiable: a weak, frightened boy who was led
into something monstrous by a more intelligent and colder person. His subsequent
psychological breakdown was genuine. The milk incident — whether or not he actually
contracted HIV, which is left uncertain — broke what was left of him.

His account also reveals something important about Shuya: Shuya's motivation for killing
Manami was specifically designed to get the attention of Yuko Moriguchi, whom he admired
and wanted to notice him. The murder was a form of communication — a bid for recognition
from a teacher he idealized. The horror of this is that the act was committed for the
most banal of emotional reasons: the desire to be seen.

CHAPTER FIVE: THE TEACHER'S NEW LIFE


A New Teacher Arrives

The class has a new homeroom teacher, Tokura, a young man full of naive optimism about
reaching troubled students. He becomes fixated on Shuya — convinced that with the right
approach, he can rehabilitate him, bring him back from whatever damage has been done. Tokura's
chapter is partly about the failure of well-intentioned pedagogical optimism when confronted
with a fundamentally different kind of person.

Shuya allows Tokura to believe he is making progress. He is not. He is using the teacher's
attentiveness for his own purposes. By this point in the novel, Shuya is a fully drawn
figure: brilliant, emotionally detached from normal human empathy, and completely focused
on his own needs and projects.

This chapter also advances what Shuya has been doing since Moriguchi's departure: planning.


━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━


CHAPTER SIX: SHUYA'S CONFESSION


The Boy Speaks

Shuya narrates the final chapter. His voice is chillingly lucid. He explains his version
of everything — his childhood, his mother (who was a scientist and who abandoned the family
to pursue her career, whose absence shaped him), his killing of Manami, his manipulation
of Naoki, and what he has been doing since.

Shuya had been building a device — a bomb, or something analogous — intending to create an
event large enough to force the world, and specifically his absent mother, to acknowledge
him. He placed the device at the school. He intended mass casualties.


Moriguchi's Counter-Move

The final twist is Moriguchi's. She has been watching Shuya this entire time. She is not
done with him. While Shuya was building his device and placing it at the school, Moriguchi
was constructing her own counter-plan.

She reveals to Shuya — at the moment of maximum impact — that she already found and
disarmed his device. It did not go off. Whatever he built, it is gone. And in its place,
she detonated something else: a bomb placed at the location where his mother now lives and
works.

The final lines are Moriguchi's: "Now your rehabilitation begins." The implication — whether
the bomb actually detonated, whether Shuya's mother is alive or dead, whether Moriguchi has
truly done this or is simply breaking him psychologically — is deliberately unresolved.

THEMES AND RESONANCE

The novel operates as a sustained examination of revenge, its logic and its limits. Each
character who enacts punishment — Moriguchi, the boys against each other, society against
its own children — generates more damage than justice. The Japanese educational system,
juvenile justice laws, parental abandonment, the way children fall through institutional
gaps: all of it is indicted without the novel ever becoming polemical.

Minato does not resolve the moral questions she raises. She presents them fully and leaves
them in the reader's hands, which is precisely what makes the book so enduring and so
disturbing.
