--- BACKGROUND AND CONTEXT ---

"In the Dark" is an investigative journalism podcast produced by APM Reports
and hosted by Madeleine Baran, one of the most rigorous and highly regarded
investigative journalists in American public media. Season 1 examined the case
of Jacob Wetterling, a Minnesota boy abducted in 1989 whose disappearance
drove the creation of the national sex offender registry. Season 2, released
in 2018, covered the case of Curtis Flowers, a Black man from Winona,
Mississippi, who was tried six times for the same 1996 murders and became
the subject of a landmark United States Supreme Court decision.

Season 3, released in 2022–2023, returns to the Curtis Flowers case with new
investigation and new developments, covering what happened after the Supreme
Court's 2019 ruling and examining the ongoing questions about Flowers's guilt
or innocence with the same methodical standard of evidence that characterized
Season 2.

This synopsis covers the Curtis Flowers case comprehensively across both
the Season 2 and Season 3 investigation, as the two seasons together tell
the complete story.

--- THE TARDY FURNITURE STORE MURDERS ---

On July 16, 1996, four people were found shot to death at Tardy Furniture
store in Winona, Mississippi. The victims were:

Bertha Tardy, the store's owner, a white woman in her fifty-nine.
Carmen Rigby, a white employee.
Robert Golden, a Black employee.
Derrick Stewart, a Black employee.

The store had been robbed. The victims were each shot at close range, execution
style, with a .380 caliber pistol. The crime was brutal and left Winona —
a small town of approximately five thousand people — deeply traumatized.

--- CURTIS FLOWERS ---

Curtis Flowers was a twenty-six-year-old Black man from Winona who had briefly
worked at Tardy Furniture some weeks before the murders, before being let go
by Bertha Tardy. He was arrested and charged with the four murders.

The primary investigator on the case was John Johnson, a special investigator
with the Mississippi Attorney General's office, who would become a central
and controversial figure in the podcast's investigation. The prosecutor was
Doug Evans, the District Attorney for the Fifth Circuit Court District of
Mississippi, who would personally prosecute Flowers in all six of his trials.

--- SIX TRIALS ---

Curtis Flowers was tried six times for the same murders. This extraordinary
legal history unfolded as follows:

TRIAL 1 (1997): Conviction. Sentenced to death. Overturned on appeal due to
prosecutorial misconduct.

TRIAL 2 (1999): Conviction. Sentenced to death. Overturned on appeal due to
prosecutorial misconduct.

TRIAL 3 (2004): Conviction. Sentenced to death. The Mississippi Supreme Court
overturned this conviction as well, finding multiple instances of prosecutorial
misconduct including improper questioning of witnesses.

TRIAL 4 (2007): Mistrial. The jury could not reach a unanimous verdict,
reportedly hung 11-1 for conviction.

TRIAL 5 (2008): Mistrial. Again hung, this time reportedly 9-3 for acquittal.

TRIAL 6 (2010): Conviction. Sentenced to death.

--- THE EVIDENCE ---

The podcast examines the evidence against Flowers with extraordinary rigor.
In the original Season 2, Baran's team spent years reviewing court records,
trial transcripts, investigative files, and conducting hundreds of interviews
in Winona. What they found was deeply troubling:

The physical evidence was thin. There was no DNA evidence linking Flowers to
the crime. The gun was never found. There were no eyewitnesses to the murders.

The case against Flowers relied primarily on: testimony from informants who
said Flowers made incriminating statements; circumstantial evidence about his
movements; and evidence about a small amount of money that could have connected
him to the robbery, which was of very limited value.

The investigation found that John Johnson, the lead investigator, had conducted
his investigation in a way that was not neutral: he had specifically cultivated
witnesses willing to testify against Flowers and had treated potentially
exculpatory information differently.

--- DOUG EVANS AND JURY SELECTION ---

Season 2's most significant investigation concerned the jury selection in
Flowers's trials, specifically the systematic exclusion of Black jurors by
prosecutor Doug Evans. This was the center of the Supreme Court case.

The "In the Dark" team built a database of all jury strikes by Evans across
all of his capital cases over his career. The statistical analysis was
extraordinary: Evans struck Black jurors at significantly higher rates than
white jurors. In the Flowers trials specifically, he struck almost every
Black prospective juror — using peremptory challenges to keep the jury white.

This evidence, combined with specific documented instances of Evans's stated
reasons for striking Black jurors being pretextual (he accepted white jurors
with the same characteristics he claimed disqualified Black ones), formed the
basis of the Supreme Court appeal.

--- THE SUPREME COURT DECISION ---

In June 2019, the United States Supreme Court ruled 7-2 in Flowers v. Mississippi
that Curtis Flowers's Sixth Trial conviction must be overturned. Justice Brett
Kavanaugh, writing for the majority, cited Evans's systematic exclusion of
Black jurors as a violation of the Equal Protection Clause under Batson v. Kentucky.

The decision specifically referenced the "In the Dark" team's statistical
analysis of Evans's jury selection patterns as evidence supporting the finding
of discriminatory intent.

The ruling overturned Flowers's conviction and death sentence and sent the
case back to the Mississippi courts.

--- SEASON 3: AFTER THE SUPREME COURT ---

Season 3 picks up after the Supreme Court decision. Curtis Flowers had been
released from death row to await further proceedings. The key questions Season 3
investigates are:

What happens to the case now? Will Evans retry Flowers a seventh time, or
will the charges be dropped?

What does the continued investigation reveal about the original crime? Are
there new leads? New evidence? Does the investigation that convicted Flowers
look even more problematic under further scrutiny?

What happened to the witnesses? Several of the key witnesses whose testimony
was used to convict Flowers had, in the years since the trials, recanted or
significantly changed their accounts. Season 3 examines these recantations
in detail.

--- THE WITNESSES REEXAMINED ---

Season 3's most significant contribution is its systematic reexamination of
the witnesses whose testimony convicted Flowers. The podcast finds:

Several witnesses who testified that Flowers made incriminating statements
to them have since said their testimony was false — that they were pressured
by investigators, that they received benefits in exchange for their cooperation,
or that they simply lied.

The specific circumstances of these witnesses' original testimony — what was
offered to them, what pressure was applied, what the relationship between
investigator John Johnson and these witnesses looked like — is documented
in detail.

The cumulative effect of the recantations, combined with the original evidence's
thinness, raises serious questions about whether there is any reliable evidence
that Flowers committed the murders at all.

--- CHARGES DROPPED ---

In September 2020, District Attorney Doug Evans announced that he would not
retry Curtis Flowers for the seventh time. The charges against Flowers were
dismissed.

Curtis Flowers was released. He had spent approximately twenty-three years
in prison, much of it on death row, across six trials for the same four murders.

--- THE UNRESOLVED MURDER CASE ---

The Tardy Furniture murders are officially unsolved. With the charges against
Flowers dismissed, there is no active prosecution of anyone for the four deaths.
The families of the victims have not received definitive answers about who
killed their family members.

Season 3 examines what the evidence actually shows about the case — beyond
the official narrative that Flowers was guilty — and whether the investigation
that focused exclusively on Flowers from the beginning missed or suppressed
other leads.

--- JOHN JOHNSON AND DOUG EVANS ---

Season 3 investigates both figures in considerable detail. John Johnson, the
lead investigator, died in 2020 before Season 3 was complete. Doug Evans did
not seek re-election in 2019 and left office.

The podcast's investigation of both figures documents a pattern of conduct —
in the Flowers case and in other cases Evans prosecuted in the Fifth Circuit
District — that raises serious questions about the justice system in that part
of Mississippi.

--- SUMMARY ---

"In the Dark Season 3" completes one of the most important investigative
journalism projects in the history of audio journalism. Together, Seasons 2
and 3 document: a man tried six times for the same crime, convicted through
racially discriminatory jury selection and unreliable witness testimony, his
conviction overturned by the Supreme Court following the podcast's investigation,
and his eventual release after the charges were dropped. The Tardy Furniture
murders remain officially unsolved. The podcast's achievement is to have
demonstrated, through exhaustive and methodologically rigorous investigation,
that the state of Mississippi spent twenty-three years prosecuting a man for
murders it could not reliably prove he committed.
