TVE_006 | Columbo: Troubled Waters | Director: Ben Gazzara | TV Episode
================================================================================
FULL EPISODE SYNOPSIS — SPOILERS INCLUDED
================================================================================

--- BACKGROUND AND CONTEXT ---

"Troubled Waters" is a Season 4 episode of Columbo, airing February 9, 1975.
It is directed by Ben Gazzara — the actor, who took the director's chair for
this episode — and is notable for its setting: the entire episode takes place
aboard a luxury ocean cruise ship sailing between Los Angeles and Mexico. This
closed-world setting, where the pool of suspects is confined to the ship's
passenger list and crew, gives the episode a particularly Golden Age mystery
feel, almost in the tradition of Agatha Christie's "Evil Under the Sun."

The episode stars Robert Vaughn as Hayden Danziger, one of the more calculating
and cold-blooded of all Columbo killers. Also notable is the presence of Dean
Stockwell in a supporting role.

--- THE KILLER, THE VICTIM, AND THE MOTIVE ---

Hayden Danziger is a successful and powerful man — the episode establishes him
as someone accustomed to getting what he wants through a combination of wealth
and ruthlessness. He is on the cruise with his wife, Sylvia. Also on board is
Lloyd Harrington, a musician and singer performing as entertainment on the ship,
who has a history with Sylvia — specifically, a previous romantic relationship
that Danziger knows about and has not forgiven.

The specific threat that Harrington poses to Danziger is not simply the past
relationship: it is the possibility that Harrington and Sylvia might rekindle
their connection on the cruise, or that Harrington has information that could
damage Danziger in some way. In some readings of the episode, Danziger's motive
is primarily jealousy; in others it is more coldly preventive — he is eliminating
a threat before it materializes.

Danziger kills Harrington aboard the ship. The murder is committed in a way
that Danziger has planned to exploit the ship's confined geography: Harrington
is shot, and the body or the circumstances are arranged to suggest a different
explanation — an argument with another passenger, an accident, or a random act.

--- COLUMBO ON THE SHIP ---

Columbo is on the cruise himself — he is traveling with his wife, Mrs. Columbo
(who is, as always, never seen on screen but frequently referenced). He is
on vacation. When Harrington's death occurs, Columbo is drawn into the
investigation as the only available detective — not officially assigned, but
effectively the most qualified person on the ship to investigate.

This setup allows for a slightly different dynamic than usual: Columbo is not
operating with the full resources of the LAPD behind him. He is working in a
closed world with limited forensic support, relying almost entirely on his
observational intelligence and his specific knowledge of human behavior.

The confined setting of the ship is both a limitation and an asset. There are
only so many people on board. Any movement or activity on the night of the
murder is, in principle, observable by someone. The killer's alibi depends on
the ship's specific geography and the specific limitations of visibility in
different areas.

--- DANZIGER'S ALIBI AND BEHAVIOR ---

Danziger's alibi for the time of Harrington's death is constructed around his
presence in a specific part of the ship where other passengers can place him.
He plays the role of concerned fellow passenger — someone who knew Harrington
marginally and is appropriately troubled by the death but not specifically
connected to it.

His behavior with Columbo is characteristic of the Columbo-killer dynamic:
initially dismissive or pleasant-dismissive, becoming gradually more controlled
and tense as Columbo's visits continue and his questions become more pointed.
Vaughn plays Danziger as a man of significant personal force — not charming in
the Cassidy manner but cold and self-assured — and his management of Columbo's
attention is more aggressive than many killers: he pushes back, he suggests
Columbo is wasting time, he implies that a detective who knows what he's doing
would be looking elsewhere.

Columbo, of course, is undeterred by this. The pushback is itself data.

--- INVESTIGATING THE SHIP ---

Columbo's investigation moves through the ship's specific spaces: the decks,
the cabins, the entertainment areas, the crew's quarters. He talks to the
entertainers, the crew members, the other passengers. He establishes the
geography of who could have seen what from where on the night of the murder.

The specific evidence that develops against Danziger involves the sequence of
events on the night — where he claims to have been, what certain witnesses saw,
and the specific physical evidence from the area where Harrington was killed.
Something about the physical evidence — the angle of the shot, a discarded
object, the position of a witness — tells Columbo something about the killer
that is inconsistent with Danziger's account.

Sylvia Danziger's behavior also contributes to the investigation. She is
frightened — not of the murder investigation but of her husband. The specific
dynamics of the Danziger marriage, visible to Columbo's observational eye,
tell him something about the emotional reality of the situation that the
official account does not.

--- THE FINAL CONFRONTATION ---

The episode builds toward a confrontation between Columbo and Danziger in which
Columbo presents the accumulated evidence: the specific things he has found
or established over the course of the voyage that, together, make Danziger's
account untenable.

Danziger's final response — whether he tries to bluster, to negotiate, or to
accept the situation — is characteristic of Vaughn's cold portrayal: this is
not a man who will dissolve into confession but one who recognizes that a
specific game is over.

--- RESOLUTION ---

Danziger is exposed and faces arrest when the ship reaches port. The confined
world of the cruise — which he thought would work in his favor as a controlled
environment for the murder and its concealment — proved instead to be the
mechanism of his exposure: in a closed world, Columbo has nowhere to go but
closer to the truth.

--- NOTES ON THE EPISODE ---

"Troubled Waters" is one of the most atmospheric Columbo episodes, using the
cruise ship setting with genuine skill. The ocean, the enclosed social world,
the sense of a community cut off from ordinary reality — all contribute to
an episode that feels genuinely different from the Los Angeles-based entries
in the series. Ben Gazzara's direction makes excellent use of the ship's visual
possibilities. Robert Vaughn's Danziger is one of the series' more cold-blooded
killers — the absence of charm makes him more threatening and makes Columbo's
exposure of him feel proportionally more satisfying.
