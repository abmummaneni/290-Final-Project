--- BACKGROUND AND CONTEXT ---

"Mare of Easttown" is an HBO limited series of seven episodes, first broadcast
from April to May 2021. It is written by Brad Ingelsby and directed by Craig
Zobel, and stars Kate Winslet as Mare Sheehan — a detective in Easttown,
Pennsylvania, a fictional working-class suburb of Philadelphia — alongside
Julianne Nicholson, Jean Smart, Evan Peters, and a large ensemble cast.

The series was one of the most successful prestige dramas of 2021, generating
enormous audience and critical attention, and winning multiple Emmy Awards
including Outstanding Limited Series, Outstanding Lead Actress (Winslet), and
Outstanding Supporting Actress (Nicholson). It is widely regarded as one of
the finest American limited series of its decade and as the most accomplished
entry in the specific subgenre of the working-class community murder mystery
that the success of shows like Broadchurch established.

What distinguishes "Mare of Easttown" from similar shows is the specificity
of its setting — the particular culture, social dynamics, and economic realities
of a specific kind of small Pennsylvania community — and the specific quality
of its central character, whose personal life is as fully rendered as her
professional investigation.

--- EASTTOWN ---

Easttown is a specific kind of place: a working-class suburb of Philadelphia
whose residents have mostly grown up there, whose social world is tight and
self-referential, and whose specific economic circumstances — the post-industrial
decline of the Philadelphia region, the specific pressures of communities where
the economy has changed faster than the social structures — give it a particular
character.

Everyone in Easttown knows everyone else. There is no privacy in any real sense.
The specific social dynamics of this kind of place — where personal history
is always present, where old grievances and old connections shape current
events, and where the distinction between the public and the private is almost
impossible to maintain — are rendered with the accuracy and the love of someone
who knows this specific world.

--- MARE SHEEHAN ---

Mare Sheehan is a detective — specifically, the Easttown detective, in the
sense of someone who has been doing this job in this place for her entire
career and whose specific knowledge of the community is both her greatest
investigative asset and the source of her most significant complications.

She is managing a specific set of personal difficulties: her marriage is over —
she and her ex-husband Frank are co-parenting in the specific awkward proximity
of people who live in a small community; her son Kevin died by suicide two
years earlier, and the specific grief of that loss is present in her constantly;
she is raising her grandson Drew, Kevin's child, in specific circumstances
involving Drew's mother Carrie, who is in recovery from addiction and fighting
for custody; and she is carrying the specific professional shame of a cold
case — the disappearance of Katie Bailey a year earlier — that she has not
been able to solve.

Kate Winslet's Mare is one of the finest television performances of the decade.
She inhabits the character with total commitment to the specific physicality,
the specific accent, the specific emotional register of a woman from this place
and this background — someone whose toughness is the specific toughness of
someone who has had a lot to be tough about, and whose warmth breaks through
the toughness in specific unguarded moments.

--- THE MURDER OF ERIN MCMENAMIN ---

Erin McMenamin — a nineteen-year-old single mother from Easttown's margins —
is found dead. She has been shot, her body left near a creek in the woods.
Erin was a young woman whose specific life circumstances — teenage motherhood,
a difficult home situation, a social world that was not always kind to her —
made her vulnerable in specific ways that the investigation must understand.

Her death is connected to the specific social world of Easttown in ways that
require the investigation to navigate the same community that Mare has known
all her life. The suspects — as the investigation develops — are people she
knows. Their possible guilt is not abstract: it is personal, in the specific
way that investigating crime in a place where you know everyone is always
personal.

--- DETECTIVE COLIN ZABEL ---

Detective Colin Zabel (Evan Peters) — a younger detective from a neighbouring
county who is assigned to assist the investigation — becomes Mare's partner.
He is less experienced than Mare, eager, and possessed of a specific combination
of genuine capability and specific blind spots. His arc across the series is
one of the most carefully managed in the show: a character developed with real
specificity and real care, whose specific fate is one of the series' most
shocking and most devastating developments.

His relationship with Mare — the specific dynamics of a temporary partnership
between a very experienced detective and a much less experienced one — develops
with warmth and with real professional respect on both sides.

--- THE DISAPPEARANCE OF KATIE BAILEY ---

Alongside the Erin McMenamin investigation, the cold case of Katie Bailey —
a young woman who disappeared a year earlier, whose disappearance Mare was
unable to solve — develops in ways that connect to the murder investigation.
Katie is eventually found alive, held captive, as part of a larger situation
connected to the criminal world of the area.

The specific person responsible for Katie's captivity — and for the captivity
of other young women — is established through the investigation and represents
a criminal dimension of the Easttown world that is separate from the murder
investigation while intersecting with it in specific ways.

--- THE INVESTIGATION AND ITS SUSPECTS ---

The investigation of Erin's murder moves through the Easttown community with
the sustained attention that the seven-episode format makes possible. The
suspects are multiple and are given real development:

Dylan Hinchey — Erin's ex-boyfriend and the putative father of her son DJ.
His specific relationship with Erin and the specific events of the night of
her death are established through the investigation.

Deacon Mark Burton — a local priest who was in contact with Erin before her
death and whose specific movements and specific conduct require explanation.

Kenny McMenamin — Erin's father, whose response to his daughter's murder
creates its own criminal complications.

Various members of the Easttown community whose specific connections to Erin —
financial, romantic, or circumstantial — require investigation.

The series is carefully fair in its presentation of the suspects: each of them
is given genuine reasons to be suspicious and genuine reasons for alternative
explanations, and the sustained management of ambiguity — who could have done
it, who seems most likely, who is eventually cleared — is one of the show's
most accomplished narrative achievements.

--- MARE'S PERSONAL LIFE ---

Running through the investigation is a portrait of Mare's personal life whose
depth and specificity are one of the series' most significant achievements.
Her relationship with her mother Helen (Jean Smart) — a woman of considerable
acerbic warmth who lives with Mare and who has her own specific views on
everything in Mare's life — is one of the series' great pleasures.

Her relationship with Richard Ryan (Guy Pearce) — a visiting academic with
whom she develops a tentative romantic connection — is handled with the
appropriate tentativeness of someone who has been through enough to be
specifically cautious about hope.

The Drew custody situation — Carrie's fight for her son, and Mare's specific
attachment to the boy who is both her grandson and the only piece of Kevin
she has left — is the series' most painful personal thread. The specific
question of whether Mare is holding onto Drew for Drew's sake or for her own
is asked honestly and answered honestly.

--- THE KILLER: JOHN ROSS ---

The killer of Erin McMenamin is John Ross (Joe Tippett) — the husband of
Mare's closest friend Lori (Julianne Nicholson) and the man Mare has known
for most of her life.

The specific truth: Erin's baby DJ was not Dylan Hinchey's son. DJ was John
Ross's son. John Ross had a sexual relationship with Erin — a relationship
whose specific nature, given the age gap and the power dynamics, was an
exploitation. When this relationship and DJ's paternity were at risk of exposure,
John killed Erin to prevent it.

Lori Ross — Mare's best friend, someone she has known since childhood — knew
about the relationship and has been protecting her husband. Her specific
position — knowing what her husband did, and what Erin was to him, and choosing
her marriage and her family over the truth — is one of the most painful
elements of the resolution.

The scene in which Mare confronts Lori with the full truth — and the specific
quality of what that confrontation costs both of them, two women who have
loved each other for decades facing the specific destruction of one relationship
by the truth of another — is the series' finest single scene and one of the
finest scenes in recent American television drama. Winslet and Nicholson are
both extraordinary.

--- RESOLUTION ---

John Ross is arrested. Lori's complicity faces its legal and personal consequences.
The investigation of Katie Bailey's disappearance is separately resolved.

Mare continues. Easttown continues. The specific weight of what the investigation
found — in her community, among people she loved — is present in the final
sequences without being dramatised. She has solved the case. She has lost
something irreplaceable in the process.

The series ends with Mare climbing the ladder to the attic she has been unable
to enter since Kevin's death — finally doing the thing she has been avoiding,
finally going up. It is a specific act of readiness to be in the present rather
than the past, rendered in the show's characteristic register: quiet, specific,
and entirely earned by everything that preceded it.

--- NOTES ON THE SERIES ---

"Mare of Easttown" is one of the finest American limited series of the decade.
Its specific achievement is the combination of a genuinely constructed mystery —
the fair-play plotting of the murder investigation, whose solution is both
surprising and retrospectively entirely supported — with a character portrait
of extraordinary depth. Kate Winslet's Mare is the finest performance of her
career. The Easttown setting is rendered with the specific love of someone who
knows this world and wants to honour it. And the series' final twenty minutes —
the confrontation with Lori, the resolution of Drew's custody, the attic ladder —
constitute as satisfying an ending as American crime drama has produced in
this period.
