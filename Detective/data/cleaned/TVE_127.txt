--- BACKGROUND AND CONTEXT ---

"The Lying Detective" is the second episode of Series 4 of the BBC's "Sherlock,"
first broadcast on January 8, 2017. It is directed by Nick Hurran and written
by Steven Moffat, and stars Benedict Cumberbatch as Sherlock Holmes and Martin
Freeman as Dr. John Watson, with Toby Jones delivering one of the series' most
disturbing guest performances as Culverton Smith. The episode is based loosely
on Arthur Conan Doyle's "The Adventure of the Dying Detective" — a story in
which Holmes feigns serious illness to expose a killer — but substantially
reimagines and expands the source material into something considerably more
ambitious and more psychologically dark.

The episode is set in the aftermath of Mary Watson's death — which occurred
in the Series 4 premiere, "The Six Thatchers" — and deals with the specific
psychological states that her death has produced in both Holmes and Watson.
It is one of the most emotionally serious episodes in the series' run, carrying
the weight of a major character loss and exploring both Holmes's drug use and
Watson's grief with unusual directness.

--- THE AFTERMATH OF MARY'S DEATH ---

Mary Watson died at the end of "The Six Thatchers" — shot while protecting
Sherlock. Her death has separated Holmes and Watson: Watson blames Holmes for
Mary's death, holds him responsible for the circumstances that led to it, and
has broken off contact. Holmes, for his part, is in a state of psychological
crisis — using drugs at a level that goes beyond recreational and into self-
destruction — and Watson has not intervened because he cannot bring himself
to care enough about Holmes's state to intervene.

The specific quality of Watson's grief — the frozen, flat, unable-to-feel
state that severe loss sometimes produces — is present from the episode's
opening. He is seeing a therapist. He is going through the motions of existing.
He has a series of conversations with an imagined Mary — her voice in his head,
a presence that the series renders visually as Mary herself appearing to Watson —
that constitute both a form of processing and a form of denial.

--- CULVERTON SMITH ---

Culverton Smith (Toby Jones) is a celebrity philanthropist — a famous figure
of public generosity and good works whose specific position in British public
life gives him extraordinary access to hospitals, charitable institutions, and
the specific vulnerable people whose trust he systematically exploits. He is
fabulously wealthy, publicly beloved, and privately a serial killer.

He kills people. Not in a dramatic or structured way — he is not a ritual killer
with a specific signature. He simply kills, when he can, in circumstances where
he can manage it and escape accountability. He tells people — specific people,
in specific controlled contexts — that he has done this, and the specific
relief of admission is part of what he needs. He told his own daughter, once.
He has managed to keep this knowledge from becoming consequential through a
combination of wealth, social position, and the specific vulnerability of the
people he targets.

Toby Jones plays Smith with something worse than menace: a specific jovial
warmth, a specific public-facing charm that is entirely convincing as the
surface of a genuinely beloved public figure and that makes the private reality
behind it more horrifying rather than less. He is one of the series' finest
villains precisely because he is not theatrical.

His daughter Faith — who comes to Baker Street with the specific information
that her father told her something terrible before administering a drug to
make her forget — is the episode's entry point. She gives Sherlock a half-
remembered account of what her father said. This begins the investigation.

--- HOLMES'S SELF-DESTRUCTION ---

Holmes is using drugs heavily when the episode begins. His specific state —
physically deteriorating, psychologically disturbed, deliberately not managing
his condition — is the result of both the specific events of recent weeks and
something more fundamental: he is conducting an investigation by making himself
into the investigation's instrument in a specific way. He is using his own
self-destruction as operational cover.

The plan — which Holmes has constructed in its full complexity, as he always
does — is to become so visibly destroyed that he is hospitalised, and that
the specific hospital that hospitalises him is the one connected to Culverton
Smith, and that once inside he can gather the evidence required to expose Smith
while Smith, believing Holmes to be dying and unable to harm him, will admit
to his crimes.

This is the "Dying Detective" plan from the Conan Doyle story — Holmes feigning
illness to expose a killer — updated to a contemporary context in which the
feigning is genuine deterioration managed to a specific operational end. Holmes
is not simply pretending. He is actually damaging himself, intentionally, as
a tactical choice.

The specific quality of this — the specific willingness to do serious harm to
himself in the service of exposing a killer — is one of the episode's most
disturbing elements and one of its most characteristic: Holmes's specific
relationship to his own body, his own health, and his own welfare has always
been instrumental rather than personal.

--- WATSON DRAWN BACK IN ---

Holmes's sister Eurus — introduced in this episode in a specific way that will
be developed in the series finale — is involved in the specific chain of events
that brings Watson back into Holmes's orbit. She has appeared to Watson in a
previous encounter, in a different guise, and her specific management of Watson
in this episode is part of the episode's most significant late revelation.

Watson is drawn back in by the specific alarm of Holmes's state: whatever his
feelings about Holmes and about Mary's death, he cannot watch Holmes die. His
specific professional response — the doctor in him, the specific care that has
always been as fundamental to his character as his military background —
reasserts itself.

Their reunion — in the specific circumstances of Holmes's crisis — is handled
with the weight of everything that has happened between them: Mary's death,
Watson's blame, the specific broken quality of a friendship that has been
through more than most friendships survive. It is not a comfortable reunion.
It is a necessary one.

--- THE HOSPITALISATION AND SMITH'S ADMISSION ---

Holmes is hospitalised. Culverton Smith — who has been approached by Holmes
and who has been managing the specific situation with the careful attention
of someone who is accustomed to managing his secrets — comes to the hospital
to visit the apparently dying detective. He has been told by Holmes (as part
of the plan) that Holmes knows what he is and what he does. Smith, who has
a specific pathology around admission — who needs to tell people and who needs
those people to be unable to act on what they know — responds to Holmes's
apparent incapacitation with the specific relief of someone who can finally speak.

He admits what he does. He describes the specific pleasure of the killing.
He discusses it with Holmes in detail — partly taunting, partly genuinely
confessing — in the specific belief that Holmes is dying and that what he says
here will go no further.

Watson, present in the room, listens.

The admission is being recorded. The specific evidence that Holmes's plan was
designed to produce — Smith's own confession, in Smith's own voice, describing
his specific conduct — is assembled in the hospitalisation sequence with the
precision of a plan that has been constructed to reach exactly this point.

--- THE REVELATION: FAITH AND EURUS ---

The episode's most significant revelation is delivered at its close. The woman
who appeared to Watson as Sherlock's unknown therapist, and the woman who came
to Holmes as Culverton Smith's daughter Faith, and the woman who appears in
the episode's final scene — are all the same person: Eurus Holmes.

Eurus is Sherlock's sister — a sibling whose existence has not been mentioned
previously in the series, and whose specific capabilities (she is, in the
show's mythology, more intellectually gifted than even Sherlock) and specific
history (she has been institutionalised since childhood) will be the subject
of the Series 4 finale.

She has been managing Watson, approaching Holmes in disguise, and inserting
herself into the episode's events with the specific operational intelligence
of someone who was anticipating everything that would happen and who positioned
herself to be present at every significant moment.

Her appearance at the episode's close — pointing a gun at Watson, outside
Baker Street, in the episode's final shot — is the cliffhanger that leads
into the finale.

--- WATSON AND HOLMES: THE RECKONING ---

Before the episode's conclusion, Watson and Holmes have the confrontation that
the episode has been building toward. Watson's specific rage about Mary's death —
and everything he has not been able to say to Holmes about it — is delivered
in a scene of considerable emotional intensity. Cumberbatch and Freeman play
it with the accumulated weight of four series of this relationship: two men
who have been through too much together to simply walk away and who are also
genuinely damaging each other in specific ways.

Holmes's response to Watson's anger — the specific quality of what he says
and does not say, what he acknowledges and what he cannot acknowledge — is
one of Cumberbatch's finest performances in the series. It is a scene that
treats both men as fully adult and fully human: neither heroic nor villainous,
simply two people managing the specific damage of specific events and trying
to find a way back to something functional.

--- RESOLUTION ---

Culverton Smith is arrested and charged on the basis of his recorded confession
and the additional evidence assembled through the investigation. The specific
danger he represented — the specific vulnerability of the people in his hospitals,
the specific extent of what he did and for how long — is addressed through
the legal process.

Holmes survives his self-inflicted deterioration. He recovers. The episode
ends with the cliffhanger of Eurus's appearance, setting up the series finale.

--- NOTES ON THE EPISODE ---

"The Lying Detective" is the finest episode of Sherlock's Series 4 and one
of the finest in the entire series. Toby Jones's Culverton Smith is the show's
most effective villain since Moriarty: a man whose specific horror is rooted
in ordinariness and social acceptability rather than in theatrical menace. The
episode's handling of Watson's grief and Holmes's self-destruction is the most
emotionally direct work the show has done. And the Eurus revelation — delivered
in the episode's final moments — reframes everything that has happened across
the series with the specific skill that the show applies to its best structural
surprises.
