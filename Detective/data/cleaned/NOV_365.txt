A Red Herring without Mustard is the third book in Alan Bradley’s Flavia de Luce series and follows eleven‑year‑old chemist and sleuth Flavia in the English village of Bishop’s Lacey as she unravels two linked crimes: a vicious attack on a Gypsy fortune‑teller and a bizarre murder that appears on the de Luce family estate. The plot is a classic English‑village mystery, but with distinctly dark, gothic, and personal undercurrents, especially around the question of who Flavia truly is. Below is a long, detailed, spoiler‑filled plot synopsis told in clear chronological order, covering the full story, the killer(s), the motives, and how everything is solved.
Flavia’s first encounter with the gypsies

The novel opens in the early 1950s in the village of Bishop’s Lacey, where Flavia de Luce lives at the family estate, Buckshaw, with her distant, grief‑stricken father, Colonel de Luce, and her two older sisters, Daphne and Ophelia, who alternately torment and ignore her.

Flavia, forever obsessed with chemistry and poison, has asked a local Gypsy fortune‑teller, Fenella Faa, to read her fortune. The old woman is unsettlingly accurate, making Flavia feel both exhilarated and unnerved.

That night, as the Faa caravan camped on the edge of the de Luce estate, a fire breaks out in the tent. Flavia unintentionally contributes to the blaze, and the entire caravan is destroyed. Fenella and the family escape, but they are left homeless and humiliated. Guilt‑ridden, Flavia secretly offers them a place to camp in a remote part of the de Luce property called The Palings, without telling her father or the estate staff.
The assault on Fenella Faa

Not long after the gypsies move onto the estate, tragedy strikes. Flavia stumbles upon Fenella in her caravan, brutally beaten and almost dead. The old woman has been bludgeoned in a vicious attack, and the caravan is again a scene of ruin.

The assault throws the village into a moral panic. The gypsies have long been scapegoats: years earlier, a baby went missing in Bishop’s Lacey, and many locals blamed the travelers for kidnapping the child. The attack on Fenella is immediately interpreted as an act of retribution—someone from the town taking brutal revenge on the woman who they believe stole the baby.

Flavia refuses to accept this simple explanation. She suspects the attack is connected to the missing‑baby case, to the secrets of the de Luce family, and perhaps even to her own curious connection to the Faa clan. Motivated by guilt over the fire and a hunger for a real mystery, she begins to investigate the assault.
Introduction to the granddaughter, Porcelain

Flavia’s investigation brings her into direct contact with the Faa family, most importantly Fenella’s intelligent, sharp‑tongued granddaughter, Porcelain Faa. When they first meet, Porcelain is so furious about Flavia’s earlier role in the caravan fire that she holds a knife to Flavia’s throat—though it turns out to be only a butter knife, turning the moment into a darkly comic standoff.

Impressed by Flavia’s nerve and intelligence, Porcelain cautiously allies with her, and the two girls form an uneasy partnership. They begin to probe the attack together, asking questions about the missing baby, the villagers’ hatred of the gypsies, and the strange sect on the riverbank nearby known as the “Hobblers,” who hold secret night‑time meetings along the river.
The burnt‑down cottage and the second victim

While Flavia and Porcelain dig into the Fenella assault, a second crime erupts at Buckshaw. The Palings, the remote estate ground where the gypsies were camping, include an old cottage that serves as a kind of caretaker’s house. The cottage is set on fire, and inside they find a body: a man known locally as a layabout and handyman, often seen lurking around the de Luce property.

The man is found dead in a grotesque position, hanging from the rafters, and the scene is made even more sinister by the fact that he is clutching an old key in his hand and surrounded by sooty, half‑burnt debris. The police are quick to assume he was smoking while sleeping and accidentally set the fire, but Flavia is not convinced. The timing of the fire, its location, and the way the body is positioned suggest a more deliberate act.
The fork in the nose and the “red herring”

The most bizarre detail in the second corpse is a silver fork embedded in the man’s nose, as if he were punctured postmortem. The fork is ornate and old, and it does not fit the profile of a typical household utensil.

Inspector Hewitt, the local police officer, is as horrified as he is baffled, and the village is thrown into a gossip spiral about the “devil’s fork” and the gypsies’ supposed involvement. The villagers see the fork and the burning building as symbolic: a mixture of the occult and punishment. Flavia, however, views it clinically as a clue that points to a specific, intimate motive rather than mob justice.
The link to the missing baby and the Hobblers

As Flavia investigates both crimes, she uncovers a thread connecting the 1940s missing‑baby case, the gypsies, and the Hobblers, the secretive religious sect that meets on the riverbank at night. The baby went missing around the time the gypsies were in the village, and the leaders of the Hobblers preached that the child’s disappearance was a sign of God’s judgment on the town.

Flavia learns that the missing child was not the first such disappearance linked to the Hobblers; earlier, the sect had driven out or ruined several families it considered “morally corrupt.” The suspicion is that the Hobblers believed the baby would be “claimed” by the Gypsies because of the community’s “sinfulness,” and that the assault on Fenella is a kind of delayed “purgation” of that old sin.
The portrait and the identity of the killer

The case takes a deeply personal turn when Flavia is separately drawn into a subplot involving a local artist, an elegant woman named Miss Minton, who has a striking oil painting in her possession—a portrait of a young woman who looks eerily like Flavia. The portrait is linked to the long‑lost baby: the woman in the painting resembles the child’s mother, and the features in turn match Flavia herself.

Flavia begins to suspect that the missing baby is not actually a baby at all: it is a secret child, born to one of the de Luce family members, and the “kidnapping” story was a cover‑up engineered by the Hobblers and the village elders to hide the illegitimacy. The baby was taken not by the gypsies, but by a member of the sect, who then raised the child in secrecy, perhaps under the Faa family’s name.

The true killer in the “burnt‑cottage” murder is ultimately revealed to be a member of the Hobblers’ inner circle—one who had kept the truth of the baby’s origins for decades and who saw the gypsies’ arrival and the Fenella assault as a threat to the carefully constructed lie. The man in the cottage had discovered evidence that threatened to expose the sect’s manipulation of the missing‑baby case, and the Hobblers’ leader arranged for him to be killed and for the evidence (possibly a key or a document) to be destroyed in the fire.
How the mystery is solved

Flavia’s solution hinges on three things:

    The fork in the nose, which she recognizes as a family heirloom, tied to a specific person in the Hobblers’ leadership.

    The key in the dead man’s hand, which Flavia deduces was meant to lead investigators to a hidden document or locket that proves the baby was not abducted by the gypsies.

    The portrait, which so closely resembles her own features that it forces Flavia to confront the possibility that the missing baby is somehow related to her own bloodline, possibly even a half‑sibling or a previously unknown cousin.

By threatening to expose the portrait and the key, and by challenging the Hobblers’ leader with his knowledge of the sect’s past, Flavia pushes the guilty man into a near‑confession. The police and the village slowly accept the truth: the assault on Fenella was a distraction, and the real crime was the murder of the man who knew too much about the Hobblers’ role in the missing‑baby conspiracy.
The secret about Flavia and the “red herring” theme

The closing act of the novel is less about the criminals’ fate and more about Flavia’s own backstory. The fork heirloom, tied to the Hobblers, also hints at a broader family connection between the de Luces, the sect, and the gypsies, suggesting that Flavia is not as isolated as she believes.

The title A Red Herring without Mustard is a dark joke: the “red herring” is, in classic mystery form, a false clue used to mislead the detectives—but in this story, it is the entire town’s obsession with the gypsies’ supposed guilt that is the real red herring, without the usual must‑mustard punchline. The villagers’ prejudice blinds them to the real culprit, who sits comfortably among them, hidden behind respectability and religion.

By the end, Flavia is both delighted with her own brilliance and shaken by the possibility that the mysteries of her own identity are only beginning to be uncovered. The case is “solved,” but the biggest questions—about the missing baby, the portrait, and the hidden child—are left open, setting the stage for later books in the series.
