A Suitable Vengeance is a prequel set several years before A Great Deliverance, centering on a fraught house party at Thomas Lynley’s family estate in Cornwall, where old romantic betrayals and family resentments explode into two murders. The mystery slowly reveals that both killings are rooted in intertwined motives of revenge—particularly Deborah’s desire to punish Simon St. James for an old betrayal and Peter Lynley’s bitter hatred of his brother—until the true killer is exposed and Lynley’s future relationships with Deborah, Simon, and Helen are permanently reshaped.
Before Cornwall: Old Betrayals and New Engagement

Years before the novel opens, Deborah Cotter had been deeply in love with Simon St. James, Lynley’s closest friend. On the night she believed Simon would declare his love, he unexpectedly rejected her, and in a moment of hurt and impulsive rage she slept with Thomas Lynley instead. That one night created a lasting triangle: Simon was then horrifically injured in an accident and left disabled, and Deborah, humiliated and heartbroken, fled to America, where she cut off contact and nursed a desire to make Simon “pay” for what she saw as betrayal.

While Deborah is in America, Lynley—eighth Earl of Asherton, already a rising figure at Scotland Yard—slowly courts her at a distance. He visits her repeatedly, calls her, and over time Deborah becomes his lover and agrees to marry him, deciding she will return to England as his fiancée with a carefully nurtured plan to confront Simon and exact a kind of emotional vengeance for the past.

At the same time, Lynley’s younger brother Peter has fallen into cocaine addiction and financial desperation. Their father has died about a decade earlier, and the family rift that followed—especially between Lynley and his mother, the Countess—has never fully healed; Peter feels abandoned, resentful of Thomas’s title, money, estate, and career, and he fantasizes about forcing Thomas to “pay” for years of neglect.
The House Party at Howenstow

The main action begins when Lynley brings his fiancée Deborah to Howenstow, the Lynley family estate in Cornwall, to meet his family and friends and formalize the engagement. To round out the house party, he invites:

    Simon St. James, his closest friend, still in love with Deborah but determined to hide it.

    Lady Helen Clyde, the sophisticated, observant woman who will later become Lynley’s great love.

    Peter Lynley, Lynley’s coke‑addled younger brother.

    Sidney St. James, Simon’s younger sister, who is in a relationship with Justin Brooke, a biochemist.

    Justin Brooke himself, Sidney’s partner, an abusive man researching a new drug called Incomet.

    Deborah’s father, who works as valet/houseman to Simon.

The gathering is tense from the start. Lynley and his mother have been estranged for about fifteen years, and he must navigate their brittle relationship under the eyes of his fiancée. Simon is forced to watch the woman he loves presented as another man’s bride‑to‑be, while still bound to Lynley by deep friendship. Sidney is being brutalized by Justin, who controls and assaults her, and her situation adds a raw note of real violence to the emotional drama. Lynley must also confront his mother’s lover, Dr. Roderick Trenarrow—a local physician and cancer researcher—whose long‑term relationship with the Countess helped widen the family rift after the Earl’s death.

Peter arrives at Howenstow with barely concealed hostility and a plan of his own. Incapable of supporting his drug habit, jealous of Thomas’s position and wealth, and feeling abandoned since childhood, Peter intends to force financial and emotional “payment” from his older brother during the house party.​
Deborah’s and Peter’s Vengeances

Deborah arrives in England with a dual purpose: to marry Lynley and to enact her “suitable vengeance” on Simon. She is no longer the “woman scorned” of several years earlier, but she has not forgiven Simon for his rejection; she plans to confront him emotionally and make him feel the pain she has carried. Her engagement to Lynley is genuine, but entangled with unresolved feelings and a desire to settle old scores.

Peter, meanwhile, frames his own vendetta against Thomas as another form of “vengeance.” He resents being penniless, addicted, and dependent while his older brother has an estate, a title, a Bentley, and rank in London; Peter openly intends to force Thomas to acknowledge and materially compensate him for years of emotional abandonment. Both Deborah and Peter arrive at Howenstow carrying secret agendas that will distort their reactions when violence erupts.​
The First Murder: The Journalist

In the nearby village of Nanrunnel, a local journalist known as a womanizer is found savagely murdered. His killing is gruesome and immediately disrupts whatever fragile calm the house party at Howenstow had managed to maintain; what was supposed to be an engagement celebration turns into the backdrop for a murder investigation.

Cornwall police take the lead, but Lynley and Simon—both highly capable investigators in their own right—are drawn into the case almost at once. The journalist’s reputation for seducing women and prying into private business gives everyone in the area potential motives: cuckolded husbands, scandal‑shy families, people whose secrets he might have threatened to expose.

Because the murder occurs so close to Howenstow and because the journalist has had contact with people connected to the estate, suspicion brushes past Lynley’s family and guests from the start. Lynley attempts to “gingerly assist” the Cornwall police while steering them away from misdirected theories like gun‑running plots or complicated self‑sacrifice scenarios; at the same time, he cannot ignore the possibility that someone in his own circle may be involved.
The Second Murder: Justin Brooke

Shortly after the journalist’s death, Justin Brooke is murdered. Brooke, a biochemist working on the drug Incomet, has been violently abusing Sidney; he rapes her during the stay at Howenstow, further enraging those who care about her and widening the pool of those who might wish him dead.

Brooke’s research, as well as his cruel behavior, becomes part of the investigative web. Because Brooke is tied to Sidney, Simon’s sister, and because he is physically present at the Lynley estate, suspicion again circles around the house party and those attached to Howenstow.

At this point, Lynley and Simon are fully embroiled in two murders that are, on the surface, separate incidents but are linked by proximity, by the overlap of potential suspects, and by the emotional turmoil enveloping the house. The atmosphere at Howenstow becomes increasingly claustrophobic: everyone is under scrutiny, everyone has something to hide, and loyalties begin to fracture.
Suspicions Around Peter, Trenarrow, and the Estate

The investigation develops several key suspect lines:

    Peter Lynley: His drug addiction, erratic behavior, and resentment of his brother make him look volatile and potentially violent. He may have had contact with the victims, and his desperation for money could connect him to broader plots.

    Dr. Roderick Trenarrow: As the Countess’s lover and a cancer researcher, Trenarrow has intellectual and professional links to Brooke’s world of biochemistry and new drugs. His long‑term entanglement with the Lynley family gives him deep knowledge of their secrets, and he has his own reasons to fear scandal.

    The Lynley estate manager: As the person responsible for much of Howenstow’s day‑to‑day running, the manager has access, knowledge of comings and goings, and potential financial or personal grievances.

Lynley and Simon work alongside the local police but also try to “steer them away” from certain blind alleys. There are theories floated about gun‑running and smuggling in the coastal area, about noble self‑sacrifice (someone killing in order to protect a loved one), and even suicide disguised as murder; Lynley and St. James, with their more sophisticated investigative instincts, quietly counter these, focusing instead on the psychological and relational tangle around Howenstow.​
Emotional Entanglements and Rising Tension

As the murders are examined, the emotional undercurrents within the house party intensify. Simon’s concealed love for Deborah, Deborah’s unresolved anger toward Simon, and her entanglement with Lynley all shape how they behave under pressure—what they reveal, what they hide, and how they interpret each other’s actions.

Lynley himself is caught between roles: he is both a Scotland Yard officer and a son, brother, fiancé, and friend. The revelations about Peter’s addiction, about his mother’s long‑standing relationship with Trenarrow, and about Deborah’s complicated past with Simon force Thomas to confront truths he has tried to avoid. His personal loyalties and his duty as a detective are in constant conflict, especially as evidence begins to point toward members of his own family and innermost circle.

Sidney’s victimization by Brooke adds another fracture line: she is traumatized, ashamed, and reluctant to talk; people around her have strong protective impulses that could translate into violence, or into lies to shield her from further pain. All of this means that the same small group of people are bound together by overlapping secrets about sex, violence, addiction and betrayal, making the process of getting to the truth extraordinarily messy.
How the Mystery Is Solved

Lynley and Simon’s investigation gradually strips away the red herrings:

    They discount elaborate gun‑running or conspiracy theories that do not match the specific, personal motives emerging from interviews.​

    They work through false leads involving supposed self‑sacrificing paternal acts or suicides disguised as murder.​

    They closely examine the ties between Brooke’s research on Incomet, Trenarrow’s medical work, and anyone who might wish to profit from or suppress drug‑related information.

Crucially, they focus on the emotional and familial context rather than only the technical details of the crimes. The journalist’s death is tied to what he knew or was close to revealing about people in the area—including people connected to Howenstow—while Brooke’s murder intersects directly with the rage and fear generated by his abuse of Sidney and the stakes of his Incomet research.

Piece by piece, Lynley and St. James reconstruct movements and opportunities around each murder, connect the victims to specific secrets, and identify who had the strongest combination of motive, means and chance. The solution implicates someone inside the extended family circle, making the case “far outside their jurisdiction—and a little too close to home,” as they must confront the possibility that their own efforts to shield loved ones have helped foster lies and misdirection.
Reveals, Motives, and “Suitable Vengeance”

By the end, it becomes clear that these two murders are not random crimes but the culmination of years of seething grievances and attempts at revenge within and around the Lynley family.

    Deborah’s intention to make Simon pay emotionally, and Peter’s determination to punish Thomas, form a thematic backdrop of vengeance that mirrors the more concrete, lethal revenge driving the killer.

    The journalist is killed because of what he knows or threatens to expose about the private lives and hidden acts of people tied to Howenstow; silencing him is itself a kind of vengeance or self‑protection.

    Brooke’s death arises from a convergence of motives: his abuse of Sidney, his dangerous research, and the threats he poses to various people’s reputations and futures.

The “suitable vengeance” of the title plays out on multiple levels: romantic, familial, and homicidal. The final unmasking forces Lynley to accept that people he loves are capable of profound harm—if not by killing, then by lying, withholding, or manipulating the truth—and that his own past actions (and inactions) have helped set the stage for tragedy.
Aftermath and Future Relationships

When the killer is exposed and the immediate danger passes, the emotional fallout is significant. The events at Howenstow permanently alter Lynley’s relationships with Deborah, Simon, and Helen, and form the psychological backstory that will haunt them in later novels.

    Lynley’s engagement to Deborah, already complicated by the history with Simon, is no longer a simple romantic promise; it is colored by everything that has come to light about their past and about the costs of their choices.

    Simon and Deborah’s shared history and enduring feelings are now out in the open, setting up the dynamics that will carry through the rest of the series.

    Lady Helen, seeing all of this, becomes more deeply entwined with Lynley’s inner life, even as she observes how blind he can be to the darker currents in his own world.

The Cornwall case also shows what Lynley is like without Barbara Havers at his side; readers glimpse how much he benefits, in later books, from Havers’s working‑class skepticism and refusal to be impressed by aristocratic privilege. In the prequel setting of A Suitable Vengeance, everyone is still protecting each other, lying out of love, shame, or pride, and those protective lies are exactly what let the murders happen and nearly obscure the truth.
