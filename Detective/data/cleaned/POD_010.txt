THE HOST AND THE INVESTIGATION

Dave Cawley is an investigative reporter for KSL Newsradio in Salt Lake City, Utah,
who spent years investigating the case that became Cold Season 1. His investigation
is not amateur — he is a professional journalist with a formal investigative background —
and Cold is one of the most thoroughly documented and meticulously evidenced true crime
podcasts ever produced. Cawley assembled thousands of pages of documents: police
records, court filings, forensic reports, financial records, and personal communications.
The podcast's extraordinary detail reflects years of pre-production investigative work
that preceded the recording.

Cold is also distinguished by its length: Season 1 runs to more than 35 episodes, an
extraordinary commitment to a single case that reflects both the complexity of the
subject and Cawley's determination to document it completely. It is not for casual
listeners looking for a quick story; it is an immersive, comprehensive investigation
that rewards sustained attention.

THE CASE: THE DISAPPEARANCE AND MURDER OF SUSAN POWELL


Susan Powell

Susan Cox Powell was a 28-year-old wife and mother living in West Valley City, Utah,
who disappeared on December 6-7, 2009. She was married to Josh Powell, and they had
two young sons: Charlie, aged four, and Braden, aged two. She vanished from her home
while her husband and sons were allegedly on a camping trip in the middle of a December
Utah night.

Susan was known to friends and family as a warm, devoted mother who had been expressing
increasing fear and concern about her marriage and her husband in the months before her
disappearance. She had told people — specifically her father Chuck Cox — that if anything
happened to her, Josh had done it. She had made arrangements to ensure this was on
record. She did not disappear without warning those who loved her.


Josh Powell

Joshua Powell was Susan's husband. He was, by the accounts of almost everyone who knew
him, a difficult, controlling, and deeply dishonest man. He had a history of financial
fraud and deception. His relationship with his father, Steve Powell, was disturbing in
specific ways that emerged during the investigation. He had an unusual family dynamic
that raised serious concerns among people who encountered the Powell household.

On the night of Susan's disappearance, Josh Powell told police that he had taken his
two young sons — ages four and two — camping in the West Utah desert at midnight in
December, in freezing temperatures, and had not taken a phone. He claimed to have
returned home and found Susan gone. The statement was not believed by investigators,
and Josh Powell became the primary suspect in his wife's disappearance immediately.

He was never arrested or charged, because there was insufficient evidence for
prosecution. He remained the prime suspect throughout the investigation.

THE INVESTIGATION: WHAT CAWLEY ESTABLISHES


The Camping Story and Its Inconsistencies

Cawley documents the specific inconsistencies in Josh Powell's account of the camping
trip with extraordinary precision. The specific location he claimed to have gone to,
the conditions that night, the equipment he had or did not have, the timeline he
provided, the behavior of the boys as described by people who saw them the following
morning — all of it is examined in detail.

The camping story does not hold together. The specific location where Josh claimed to
have been has been investigated, and the evidence does not support his account. Cawley
constructs the alternative picture — what likely happened in the Powell home on the
night of December 6-7, 2009 — from the available physical and circumstantial evidence.


Steve Powell

Josh Powell's father, Steve Powell, is one of the most disturbing figures in the entire
case. During the period following Susan's disappearance, while Josh was living with his
father in Puyallup, Washington, investigators discovered that Steve Powell had accumulated
a massive collection of secretly recorded video of Susan Powell — recordings made in
his home without her knowledge, covering years of her presence in the house. The specific
nature of Steve Powell's obsession with his daughter-in-law, and the specific content
of the recordings, is documented by Cawley in detail.

Steve Powell was arrested and convicted on charges of voyeurism related to recordings
of other women, and served prison time. His relationship with Josh and the specific
dynamics of the Powell family are examined in depth throughout the season.


The Boys and the Custody Battle

Charlie and Braden Powell survived their father's camping trip and were present in the
home throughout the investigation period. As Josh remained a suspect, custody of the boys
became a major legal battleground between Josh (and the Powell family) and Susan's parents,
Chuck and Judy Cox.

The custody proceedings are documented in detail by Cawley — the specific legal arguments,
the evaluations of the boys, and the mounting evidence about the Powell household's
unsuitability as an environment for two young children. The boys showed specific behaviors
in supervised visitation and in interviews that were deeply concerning to social workers
and investigators.


February 5, 2012: The Attack

On February 5, 2012, Josh Powell was having a supervised visitation with his sons at
his home in Graham, Washington. The social worker who brought the boys to the house for
the visit was refused entry — Josh opened the door, brought the boys inside, and closed
the door before the social worker could enter. The social worker immediately called 911.

Josh Powell then attacked his sons with a hatchet and set fire to the house using
gasoline he had prepared in advance. Charlie and Braden died in the fire. Josh Powell
also died in the fire. He had planned the murder-suicide in advance.

This event — the deliberate murder of his own children by the man who almost certainly
killed their mother — is the most devastating moment in a devastating case. Cawley
handles it with appropriate solemnity and care.


What the Investigation Reveals About Susan's Fate

Susan Powell's body has never been found. Josh Powell took the specific information
about where she was to his death. Various searches of desert areas in Utah have been
conducted, including searches of specific locations suggested by evidence in Josh's
possession and by witness accounts, without recovering her remains.

Cawley's investigation establishes, through the accumulation of evidence, what almost
certainly happened: Josh Powell killed Susan Powell in their West Valley City home on
the night of December 6-7, 2009, disposed of her body in the Utah desert, and then
constructed and maintained the camping story for more than two years, even as he
remained the primary suspect and was watched by investigators.

CHUCK AND JUDY COX

Susan's parents — Chuck and Judy Cox — are among the most compelling figures in the
podcast. They are people of extraordinary resilience and determination who have fought
for their daughter and subsequently for their grandsons' safety with every legal tool
available to them, and who lost both the fight to save Susan and the fight to protect
Charlie and Braden.

Their ongoing pursuit of answers — including their continued work to search for Susan's
remains and their advocacy for changes to family court procedures that could protect
children in similar situations — is documented throughout the season. Their grief, and
their refusal to let that grief become the end of the story, is one of the season's
most affecting elements.

SIGNIFICANCE OF COLD SEASON 1

Cold Season 1 is widely regarded as one of the finest true crime podcasts ever produced,
distinguished by the depth and precision of Cawley's documentation, the seriousness
with which it treats a genuinely devastating case, and the absence of sensationalism
in its treatment of events that would be easy to exploit.

The Susan Powell case is one of the most tragic in recent American true crime: a woman
who knew she was in danger, who documented her fears, who told her family what to do
if something happened — and whose fears were entirely justified, and whose warning was
not enough. The subsequent murders of her children add a dimension of horror that goes
beyond most true crime cases. Cawley's treatment is equal to the material.
