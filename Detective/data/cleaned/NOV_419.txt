NOV_419 — Kingdom of the Blind
Louise Penny, 2018
Full Plot Synopsis (Inspector Gamache #14)

WHERE WE ARE IN THE SERIES


Aftermath of Glass Houses

Kingdom of the Blind follows the events of Glass Houses (#13), in which Gamache made a
genuinely agonizing decision: allowing a significant quantity of opioids — fentanyl on a
massive scale — to flow into circulation in order to catch the drug cartel responsible for
flooding Quebec with narcotics. His calculation was that controlled distribution, followed
by a decisive takedown of the cartel's leadership, would save more lives than trying to
intercept individual shipments. It was a decision with devastating potential consequences,
and several people associated with his plan are now facing serious legal scrutiny. Gamache
himself has resigned as Chief Superintendent of the Sûreté du Québec and is under investigation.

The novel opens with Gamache in a state of suspended uncertainty — not arrested, not
cleared, not restored to his former role. He is a man waiting to see what the consequences
of his choices will be, quietly living in Three Pines and trying to hold the moral weight
of what he decided.

THE WILL AND THE FARMHOUSE


A Strange Summons

Gamache is notified, along with two other people he has never met, that they have been
named as liquidators in the will of an elderly woman named Bertha Baumgartner, who has
recently died in rural Quebec. A liquidator in Quebec law (similar to an executor elsewhere)
is responsible for administering the estate. The problem is that no one in the group knew
Bertha Baumgartner, no one can imagine why she chose them, and the three named liquidators
find each other immediately suspicious and mystifying.

The two strangers are Myrna Landers — Gamache's friend and the owner of Three Pines'
bookshop, who is equally baffled — and a young man named Benedict Pouliot, an unremarkable
and not particularly competent twenty-something whose connection to the dead woman is
equally opaque.


The Farmhouse

The estate Bertha left behind is a decaying farmhouse in the Quebec countryside, apparently
of no particular value. But wills rarely make people strangers to danger in crime fiction
without reason, and Bertha's will quickly reveals itself to be more complex than it
initially appeared.

Inside the farmhouse, the three liquidators find evidence that Bertha was not quite who
she appeared to be. The farmhouse contains hidden elements — rooms, compartments, a
history that was carefully concealed — and Bertha's past, which surfaces gradually, turns
out to be far more significant than her modest death suggested.

THE BAUMGARTNER FAMILY


Death in the Family

The situation escalates when a member of Bertha's family — one of her adult children —
is murdered. The death brings the Baumgartner family into sharp focus: they are a
complicated group of people with a complicated relationship to their mother and to whatever
she left behind. The farmhouse and its secrets have real value to some of them in ways
that aren't immediately apparent, and the will — with its strange choice of liquidators —
suggests Bertha had specific intentions that the family did not know about.

Gamache, though no longer holding an official position with the Sûreté, begins investigating.
He has standing as a liquidator and personal motivation, and the investigation of the
Baumgartner murder becomes intertwined with his effort to understand why he and Myrna and
Benedict were chosen.


What Bertha Was Hiding

The excavation of Bertha's past reveals a woman of hidden depth — someone who made
consequential decisions decades ago that have reverberated forward to the present. The
specific nature of her secret, and why it made her a target (or made her choice of
liquidators a strategic act of protection), is the central mystery of the estate plot.

The farmhouse's physical secrets — the hidden spaces, the buried history — are
metaphorically consistent with the series' recurring theme that the most dangerous things
are what people choose not to see.

THE OPIOID CRISIS AND GAMACHE'S RECKONING


The Drugs Are Circulating

The fentanyl that Gamache allowed into circulation in Glass Houses is now killing people
in Quebec. Not abstractions — real people, identified in the novel, whose deaths are the
direct consequence of Gamache's decision to let the drugs flow in order to catch the
cartel. The toll is reported in quiet, steady accumulation throughout the novel. Gamache
knows about each death. He counts them.

This is one of Penny's most sophisticated moral constructions: she does not let Gamache's
decision be simply "correct" in hindsight. The cartel has been identified and action is
being taken, but people are dying in the meantime, and Gamache carries each death as a
personal weight. His certainty that he made the right call — which he maintains — coexists
with full awareness of what it has cost.


Jean-Guy Beauvoir

Beauvoir is now working in the Sûreté under new leadership — his position changed by the
aftermath of the events in Glass Houses. His relationship with Gamache is that of a son
with a father who made a terrible and arguably justifiable decision that has put them both
in professional jeopardy. Beauvoir's loyalty is not in question, but the emotional weight
of what Gamache did — and of being associated with it — is real.


The Investigation into Gamache

The formal investigation into Gamache's conduct regarding the fentanyl operation runs
throughout the novel. He is being scrutinized by people who believe he acted recklessly
or criminally. His defense is that he acted in the best judgment he had, with the
information he had, and that the alternative — continued unchecked distribution by the
cartel — would have been worse.

Whether the formal investigation will vindicate him or destroy him is left unresolved through
much of the novel, creating sustained professional jeopardy alongside the Baumgartner case.

THREE PINES


Clara, Myrna, and Ruth

Three Pines continues to function as the novel's emotional core. Clara Morrow is
navigating her life after Peter's death (from an earlier novel). Myrna's role as liquidator
puts her directly in the main plot. Ruth Zardo, the fierce old poet, provides the series'
characteristic combination of cruelty and unexpected grace. Olivier and Gabri's bistro
remains the gathering place.

Penny uses the village community to ground the novel's more abstract moral arguments in
the specific texture of people who love each other — the reminder that the stakes of
Gamache's decisions are not abstract policy but actual human lives lived in places
like this.

THE TITLE AND ITS MEANING

"Kingdom of the Blind" refers to the proverb: "In the kingdom of the blind, the one-eyed
man is king." Penny uses it to examine what it means to see clearly in situations where
everyone around you is choosing not to — whether that clarity is a gift or a burden, and
what responsibility it creates. Gamache is a man who sees things others choose to look
away from, and the novel asks what that seeing costs him and those around him.


RESOLUTION

The Baumgartner murder is solved. Bertha's secret is revealed and its significance to the
present situation is fully explained. The three liquidators — Gamache, Myrna, and Benedict —
understand why they were chosen, and the choice turns out to have been an act of
considerable trust and foresight on Bertha's part.

The opioid investigation arc does not fully resolve in this novel — it is part of a larger
arc that extends into subsequent books — but the immediate crisis stabilizes. Gamache's
formal jeopardy begins to clarify. The cartel operation's consequences are not over but
the worst of the immediate threat has passed.

The novel ends, characteristically, with Three Pines — with the village intact, the
people still there, still making dinner and reading books and being kind to each other
and being difficult with each other, which is the series' answer to every question about
whether goodness can survive.
