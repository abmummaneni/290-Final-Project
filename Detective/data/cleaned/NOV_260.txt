The Kalahari Typing School for Men by Alexander McCall Smith is the fourth novel in The No. 1 Ladies’ Detective Agency series and centers on Mma Precious Ramotswe and her assistant Mma Grace Makutsi as they navigate work, relationships, and quiet moral dilemmas in Gaborone, Botswana. This is a long, detailed, spoiler‑filled plot synopsis told in chronological order, focusing on the main storylines, character motivations, and how every major thread is resolved.
Mma Ramotswe’s household and new worries

The story opens with Mma Ramotswe still engaged to Mr J.L.B. Matekoni, the owner of Tlokweng Road Speedy Motors. Her detective agency has moved into the garage compound, and Mma Makutsi now works in both the agency and the garage office, trying to keep accounts straight as money is tight.
Mma Ramotswe is happy with her life but concerned about her two foster children, Motholeli and Puso. Motholeli, a bright teenage girl in a wheelchair, is coping well, but Puso, their younger foster son, is struggling at school and acting out, partly because he feels insecure and fatherless.

Mma Ramotswe also worries about Mr J.L.B. Matekoni, who has recently been depressed and listless; she is trying to help him return to his usual cheerful, responsible self.
Mma Makutsi’s typing school idea

Mma Makutsi, who is doing excellent work at the garage and at the agency, soon realizes that her salary is barely enough to support her ailing brother and her own modest lifestyle. She wants more money for necessities and for a few small luxuries, such as better clothes.
One evening, she notices that many men in offices still do not know how to type, even though typing skills are becoming essential. She also realizes that many men avoid taking classes at the Botswana Secretarial College because they do not want to be taught by women or to study alongside them.

This observation sparks an idea: she will open a “Kalahari Typing School for Men,” a special evening school where men can learn to type without feeling embarrassed in front of women.
Launch of the Kalahari Typing School

Mma Makutsi borrows a dozen old typewriters from her former school, the Botswana Secretarial College, and secures a room at the local church where one of the younger apprentices at the garage worships. The church is willing to lend the space for a small fee, and Mma Makutsi places a simple advertisement in the local newspaper.
The response is overwhelming: men from all over Gaborone sign up, and the school quickly fills three evening classes. Mma Makutsi is proud of her enterprise and begins to earn extra income, which eases her financial worries.

Among the students is a polite, handsome man named Mr Bernard Selelipeng, who becomes one of the most attentive learners. He is charming, respectful, and seems genuinely interested in Mma Makutsi.
Mma Makutsi’s romance with Mr Selelipeng

Mr Selelipeng begins to flirt gently with Mma Makutsi, inviting her to tea and then to the cinema. She is flattered and thrilled; at 32, she has never had a serious relationship, and Bernard seems like a good man.
They start dating secretly, and Mma Makutsi falls in love with him. He tells her that he is divorced and “free to marry,” which reassures her that this relationship has a future. She begins to dream of a proper engagement and marriage, something she has long wanted.

Mma Ramotswe, noticing changes in Mma Makutsi’s behavior, is quietly curious but allows her assistant her privacy. She trusts Mma Makutsi to make sensible decisions, though she is slightly wary of how quickly the romance is moving.
The client who wants to make amends

Alongside Mma Makutsi’s typing‑school storyline, Mma Ramotswe takes on a new case from a man named Mr Molefelo. He is a prosperous civil engineer in Lobatse, owner of a hotel and an ostrich ranch, and appears to be a respectable, well‑dressed man.
Mr Molefelo explains that when he was a young student at the Botswana Technical College many years earlier, he had a girlfriend who became pregnant. At the time, abortion was illegal in Botswana, and he desperately needed money to pay for the procedure. He did not have the funds, so he stole a radio from his host family, the Tsolamoseses, to sell and raise the 100 pula required.

After the abortion, he became angry and distant from his girlfriend, and they broke up. He now feels deep guilt about both the theft and the way he treated her. He wants to “make amends” and asks Mma Ramotswe to find the two women involved: the young woman he got pregnant and the wife of the family he stole from.
Mma Ramotswe’s investigation

Mma Ramotswe takes the case to heart, driven by her belief that people should try to repair the wrongs they have done. She begins searching for the two women, using her network of contacts and the slow, methodical approach that characterizes her work.
She first tracks down the woman he once loved, a woman named Tebogo, who has gone on to marry, have children, and live a quiet life. She is a widow now, and her eldest daughter is studying to become a nurse.

Mma Ramotswe then finds the wife of the Tsolamoses family, who had never known the full truth about the stolen radio. Mr Molefelo had always kept silent, and the family had only assumed the theft was a random misfortune.
Mr Molefelo’s restitution

With Mma Ramotswe’s help, Mr Molefelo meets both women. He apologizes to Tebogo, explaining his guilt and offering to help her financially. He pays for the nursing‑school education of her eldest daughter, giving her a better future.
He also goes to the Tsolamoses home and confesses that he was the one who stole the radio all those years ago. He repays the loss in money and in heartfelt apology, and the family is surprised but generally forgiving.

Through this process, Mr Molefelo finds a sense of peace; he has finally faced the consequences of his youthful crime and tried to repair the harm he caused. Mma Ramotswe approves of his repentance and views the case as a quiet moral success.
The new rival detective agency

Meanwhile, a new competitor opens in Gaborone: the Satisfaction Guaranteed Detective Agency, run by Cephas Buthelezi, a self‑proclaimed “ex‑CID, ex‑New York, ex‑cellent!” detective of Zulu origin.
Buthelezi runs advertisements that subtly mock the No. 1 Ladies’ Detective Agency, implying that only a man can do proper detective work. Mma Makutsi is particularly offended and worries that the flashy rival will steal their clients and drive them out of business.

However, when Mma Ramotswe finally meets Buthelezi, he himself admits that he is giving up the detective business. He complains about the low fees people pay and the difficulty of the work, and he decides that detective work is not worth the effort.
This deflates much of the tension around the rivalry; the threat turns out to be more comical than dangerous, and Mma Ramotswe can continue her work without serious competition.
The case of the cheating husband

A separate, smaller case comes to the agency when a woman visits Mma Ramotswe, worried that her husband is cheating on her. She first went to the Satisfaction Guaranteed Agency but was dissatisfied with the service, so she turns to Mma Ramotswe as a second choice.
Mma Ramotswe investigates discreetly and discovers that her assistant, Mma Makutsi’s boyfriend, Mr Bernard Selelipeng, is in fact a married man who has been lying to Mma Makutsi about his marital status.

The wife confronts Mma Ramotswe, very upset that her husband is deceiving a good woman. She does not want to cause Mma Makutsi unnecessary pain, but she also wants to stop the affair and protect her own marriage.
The collapse of Mma Makutsi’s romance

Mma Ramotswe handles the situation with her characteristic gentleness. She speaks privately with Mr Selelipeng, warning him that his deception is hurting two women and that he must end the affair. Told he will be exposed if he does not, he reluctantly agrees to break things off with Mma Makutsi.
He then tells Mma Makutsi that their relationship is over, offering a vague excuse that he is “not ready for commitment.” Mma Makutsi is heartbroken, though she tries to hide her feelings. She is deeply hurt to learn that Bernard is married, and she feels naïve and betrayed.

Mma Ramotswe comforts her, expressing sympathy and reminding her that she is a good, intelligent woman who deserves a honest and faithful partner. The episode underscores the novel’s gentle moral tone: deceit and selfishness are quietly condemned, while kindness and integrity are rewarded.
Puso’s problems at school

Alongside the adult plots, the novel also follows Puso’s struggles. He is being verbally bullied at school by other boys, which makes him feel small and ashamed. In turn, he sometimes directs his anger outward, occasionally acting out toward Mma Ramotswe and Mr J.L.B. Matekoni as if they are to blame for his unhappiness.
Mma Ramotswe consults Mma Potokwani, the kindhearted matron of the orphanage where Motholeli once lived. Mma Potokwani advises that Puso needs a stronger father figure and that Mr J.L.B. Matekoni should take on a more active, disciplinarian role in the boy’s life.
Mr J.L.B. Matekoni becomes a father

Following Mma Potokwani’s advice, Mr J.L.B. Matekoni begins to spend more time with Puso, taking him on outings, teaching him practical skills at the garage, and setting clearer boundaries. He is firm but also affectionate, and over time Puso responds positively.
Puso’s behavior improves both at home and at school, and the bullying diminishes as he gains confidence. Motholeli, meanwhile, continues to be a bright and resilient girl, providing a quiet counterpoint to Puso’s emotional turbulence.

The episode marks an important step in the family’s growth: Mr J.L.B. Matekoni fully embraces his role as a father, and Mma Ramotswe’s unconventional household stabilizes into something more like a real family.
The picnic and the novel’s close

The story ends with a large, joyful picnic attended by most of the major characters. The apprentices from the garage, Mma Ramotswe, Mma Makutsi, Mr J.L.B. Matekoni, Mma Potokwani, Mma Boko, Mr Molefelo, and his family all gather for food, music, and conversation.
The picnic is a celebration of community and small, everyday happiness. Mma Makutsi is still recovering from her heartbreak, but she is also proud of her typing school, which continues to run successfully. Mma Ramotswe is pleased that her clients have found some measure of peace and that her family is healing.

The novel closes on this warm, low‑key note, emphasizing the quiet, humane themes that define the series: forgiveness, second chances, and the importance of treating people with respect and kindness.
