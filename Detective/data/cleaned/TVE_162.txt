FULL SERIES SYNOPSIS — SPOILERS INCLUDED

--- BACKGROUND AND CONTEXT ---

Season 3 of "Only Murders in the Building" was released on Hulu in 2023.
It picks up from the final moments of Season 2, in which a new murder was
established: Ben Glenroy (Paul Rudd), a major Hollywood star, collapsed on
stage during the opening night of Oliver Putnam's Broadway show "Death Rattle
Dazzle." Ben Glenroy was apparently poisoned. He survived that incident but
was then found dead in the Arconia elevator.

Season 3 is notable for an expanded cast — Meryl Streep joins as Loretta Durkin,
a theatre actress with a complicated history — and for its setting in the
world of Broadway theatre, which gives the season a distinctive visual and
social atmosphere.

--- THE VICTIM: BEN GLENROY ---

Ben Glenroy is a famous movie star who has agreed to do Oliver's Broadway
show — a significant coup for Oliver, whose theatrical career has been in
decline. Ben is charming on the surface and complicated beneath it: he has
a history of difficult behavior, a complicated relationship with his brother
Dickie (Jeremy Shamos), and a past that various people connected to the show
have reason to regard with anger or fear.

His death in the Arconia elevator — after the earlier onstage poisoning —
establishes the season's central question: who killed Ben Glenroy, and why?

The three podcasters — Charles, Oliver, and Mabel — take up the investigation
of Ben's murder, producing a new season of their podcast while navigating the
specific social world of the Broadway production.

--- THE BROADWAY PRODUCTION AS SETTING ---

The world of "Death Rattle Dazzle" provides Season 3 with an extraordinarily
rich cast of suspects. The production includes:

Loretta Durkin (Meryl Streep) — an older actress of considerable talent
but limited career success, for whom this Broadway role is a life-changing
opportunity. Her relationship to Oliver develops into a genuine romantic
connection, which gives Oliver an emotional arc of unusual warmth for a
show that usually treats his love life as comedy.

Donna DeMeo (Linda Emond) — the show's producer, a formidable woman whose
management of the production and its various crises is not always transparent.

Clifford (Wesley Taylor) — the show's choreographer, one of several people
with a complicated history with Ben.

Tobert (Jesse Williams) — a documentary filmmaker who has been filming the
making of the show and who becomes romantically involved with Mabel.

Joy (Andrea Martin) — a production figure with her own connection to the
show's history.

--- THE FIRST POISONING ---

The investigation of Ben's murder requires understanding the earlier onstage
poisoning that he survived. Someone in the production — with access to Ben
before or during the performance — administered a poison that should have
killed him, and didn't because of either the wrong dose or an unexpected
biological response.

The specific substance used, how it was administered, and who had the access
and opportunity to poison Ben on opening night are established through the
investigation of the production's backstage world.

--- CHARLES AND THE ACTING CAREER ---

Charles Haden Savage gets a significant personal arc in Season 3: he is cast
in the show (replacing Ben after Ben's death makes continuing the production
complicated) and rediscovers something of his former self as an actor. His
relationship with the show, with Oliver, and with the specific experience of
performing gives him a warmth and confidence that has been missing in previous
seasons.

His romantic life is also developed: a relationship that begins in the context
of the production investigation.

--- OLIVER'S HEART CONDITION ---

Oliver is dealing with a health scare in Season 3 — a cardiac issue that his
doctor has identified and that he has been managing with characteristic
theatrical denial. The possibility of his death — treated in the show's
typically light-but-genuine tone — gives his relationship with Loretta and
with his son Will (Hunter Norris) additional emotional weight.

The specific question of what Oliver would leave behind — the show, the podcast,
the relationships he has built — is a thread that the season handles with more
sentimentality than the earlier seasons, in a way that works because Loretta's
presence gives it genuine emotional grounding.

--- LORETTA AND HER SECRET ---

Loretta Durkin, Meryl Streep's character, is one of Season 3's great pleasures
and also its central misdirection. She is warm, genuine, clearly talented, and
clearly in love with Oliver. She is also concealing something significant.

The investigation gradually reveals that Loretta has a specific connection to
Ben Glenroy's past — a connection that predates the Broadway production and
that gives her a motive for wanting him dead. The nature of this connection,
and whether it means Loretta is the killer, is the season's sustained
central ambiguity.

Loretta's connection to Ben involves a child — specifically, a son she gave
up for adoption years ago, who grew up to have a connection to Ben that
resulted in harm. Loretta's maternal grief and rage are the emotional engine
of her secret, and the specific revelation of what Ben did to someone she loved
makes her guilt plausible and her motivations sympathetic even if her actions
were wrong.

--- DICKIE GLENROY ---

Ben's brother Dickie is an important and underestimated figure in the
investigation. His relationship with Ben — a lifetime of being overshadowed
by his more famous brother, of managing Ben's career and cleaning up Ben's
messes — contains specific information about what Ben was really like and
about the specific people he had wronged.

Dickie's loyalty to Ben's memory and his management of Ben's affairs after
death create both obstacles and information for the investigation.

--- THE KILLER: DONNA DEMEO ---

The killer of Ben Glenroy is Donna DeMeo, the producer. Her motive is
connected to the show and to Ben's behavior: Ben was threatening the production —
through his conduct, his demands, or specific actions that would have destroyed
the show — and Donna, for whom the show represents an enormous financial and
professional investment, decided that Ben had to be stopped.

The specific mechanism of the killing — the two attempts (the onstage poisoning
and the elevator death), why the first failed and the second succeeded, and
how Donna managed to access Ben in both instances — is established in the
final episodes' investigation and revelation.

Loretta, despite her secret and her own feelings about Ben, did not kill him.
Her apparent guilt was partly her own tortured conscience about her wishes
and partly the investigation's misdirection. She is cleared.

--- RESOLUTION ---

Donna DeMeo is identified and exposed as Ben Glenroy's killer. The investigation
of the Broadway production murder — conducted through the podcast and through
the three podcasters' embedded position in the world of the show — reaches
its conclusion.

The season ends with several personal resolutions: Oliver and Loretta's
relationship is given a definitive status; Charles's acting career has been
renewed; Mabel and Tobert have their own conclusion. And the show's final
twist — the Season 3 cliffhanger that establishes the direction of Season 4 —
involves a new death and a new suspect that sets up the next investigation.

--- NOTES ON THE SERIES ---

Season 3 of "Only Murders in the Building" is the most theatrical of the three
seasons — in both the literal sense (Broadway is its world) and the tonal sense
(it is more openly sentimental and more willing to be moved by its own emotional
material). Meryl Streep's Loretta is one of the show's finest creations, and
her performance of a woman who has lived a life of quiet disappointment and
sudden late-life love is genuinely moving. The mystery construction is solid,
the misdirection around Loretta is effective, and the resolution is satisfying
in the show's characteristically warm mode.
