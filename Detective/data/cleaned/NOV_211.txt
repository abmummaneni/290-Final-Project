--- BACKGROUND AND SETTING ---

"The Underground Man," published in 1971, is widely regarded as one of Ross
Macdonald's greatest novels and represents the peak of his mature style. It
received extraordinary critical attention on publication — the New York Times
Book Review gave it a rare front-page review. The novel is set during a
catastrophic wildfire burning in the hills above Santa Teresa (Macdonald's
fictionalized version of Santa Barbara, California), and the fire functions
simultaneously as a literal plot element, a symbol, and an organizing metaphor
for the novel's central themes.

As in Macdonald's other late novels, the present mystery is the surface
eruption of crimes and secrets from fifteen or more years in the past. The
private detective Lew Archer functions here as a kind of archaeologist of
family tragedy — digging down through layers of present violence to find the
original wound.

--- THE FIRE AND THE MISSING MAN ---

The novel opens with Lew Archer at home, watching the wildfire eating through
the Santa Teresa hills. His neighbor's young grandson — a small boy named
Ronny — is visiting when the boy's father, Stanley Broadhurst, arrives. Stanley
is agitated, distracted, and in a hurry. He picks up Ronny and drives off with
him into the danger zone toward the burning hills.

Archer, concerned, follows. He arrives at the Broadhurst house and finds a
scene of crisis: Stanley has disappeared into the fire-threatened hills with a
young woman who is not his wife. The woman is Jean Broadhurst's friend, a
young drifter of uncertain history. Stanley's wife Jean is distraught and
bewildered.

Stanley Broadhurst is found dead shortly thereafter — stabbed, not killed by
the fire. He has been murdered in the hills. And little Ronny, the boy in the
car, is missing.

Archer is now working on two tracks simultaneously: the murder of Stanley
Broadhurst and the location of the missing child Ronny.

--- THE BROADHURST FAMILY ---

The investigation of Stanley's death requires Archer to piece together the
Broadhurst family history, which is troubled in the ways that Macdonald's
families typically are — surface prosperity over deep dysfunction.

Stanley Broadhurst is the son of Leo Broadhurst, a man who has been dead for
fifteen years. Leo died in a fire — another fire — in the hills above Santa
Teresa, under circumstances that were ruled accidental but have never been
entirely clear. Leo Broadhurst was apparently the kind of man who made enemies:
he was involved with money, with other men's wives, with the complicated social
life of the California upper-middle class. His death left a widow, a son (Stanley),
and a tangled legacy of unresolved relationships.

The young woman who drove off with Stanley before his death — the catalyst of
the whole crisis — turns out to have a connection to Leo Broadhurst's history.
Her presence, apparently casual, is actually the result of a long sequence of
cause and effect stretching back to Leo's death fifteen years earlier.

--- THE MIDDLE GENERATION: THE RELATIONSHIPS ---

Archer's investigation moves between the present and the past, between the
living and the dead. The cast of characters includes:

Ellen Kilpatrick, a woman from the Santa Teresa area who was involved with Leo
Broadhurst before his death and may have had a child by him — a child whose
existence and identity become central to the mystery. Ellen is now older,
damaged, carrying the weight of the past with her.

Al Sweetner, a rough, violent man connected to the young woman who drove off
with Stanley. Sweetner has a criminal history and a connection to the buried
past of the Broadhurst case. He is dangerous and he is moving through the
novel's present action with the energy of someone who wants something badly.

The young woman herself — whose true identity and parentage are part of what
Archer uncovers — is a figure displaced by the old events: a child, possibly,
of Leo Broadhurst's affair with Ellen, raised without knowledge of her real
origins, and now caught up in the consequences of those origins without fully
understanding why.

The Broadhurst family's lawyer, their neighbors, and various figures from the
community who knew Leo in his lifetime are all interviewed, and each provides
another piece of the mosaic.

--- THE FIRE AS METAPHOR ---

Macdonald uses the wildfire throughout the novel not just as atmosphere but as
thematic material. The fire was started by a careless human act — a cigarette
dropped in dry brush — and it burns indiscriminately, consuming the beautiful
and the ugly alike, the homes of the wealthy and the scrub hills above them.
The fire in the present mirrors the fire fifteen years ago in which Leo
Broadhurst died. Hidden under both fires is human carelessness, human passion,
human violence — things that smolder for years and then, given the right
conditions, erupt.

The underground man of the title refers to Leo Broadhurst — literally buried
in the earth, but also to the buried nature of the past and its secrets. The
present crimes are performed by people who are, in various ways, trying to
keep the underground man underground — to prevent what was buried from surfacing.

--- WHAT HAPPENED FIFTEEN YEARS AGO ---

The truth that Archer excavates is as follows:

Leo Broadhurst was not a good man. He was handsome, charming, and predatory.
He had affairs. One of those affairs produced a child — the young woman now
tangled in the present action. When Leo's death came, it was not entirely
accidental: he was killed, or his death was arranged or assisted, by someone
who had been wronged by him or who feared what he would do next.

The "fire" that killed Leo was set, or at least allowed to happen, by human
agency. The person responsible for Leo's death has been living with that secret
for fifteen years.

Stanley Broadhurst's murder in the present is connected to his own attempt
(or someone's fear of his attempt) to uncover the truth about his father's
death. Stanley may have found something — a document, a witness, a piece of
knowledge — that made him dangerous to the person who killed Leo.

Al Sweetner's violence in the present is connected to his own relationship
with the buried history — he may have been involved in Leo's death or in the
cover-up, or he may be trying to prevent the young woman from discovering her
true identity and its implications.

--- FINDING RONNY ---

The search for the missing child Ronny runs parallel to all of this. Ronny is
eventually found — alive — having been taken and then lost in the confusion
of the investigation and the fire. His safety and return are a rare note of
hope in a novel dominated by loss and death. Children in Macdonald are almost
always innocent victims of the violence done by the previous generation, and
Ronny's survival is the one thing the novel offers as an unambiguous good.

--- THE KILLER(S) IDENTIFIED ---

Archer assembles the complete picture. The person who killed Leo Broadhurst
fifteen years ago and the person who killed Stanley Broadhurst in the present
may be the same individual or may be two people connected by the same buried
secret. (In the novel's structure, the present killer is someone whose
connection to the old event is fully revealed only at the end.)

The killer's motive combines old guilt with present fear: fifteen years of
living with what they did to Leo, and then the terror of Stanley coming close
to the truth.

The resolution involves Archer bringing the full picture to the police — not
a triumphant exposure but a weary, sorrowful reconstruction. Nobody emerges
from this case without damage. The young woman learns truths about her origins
that are painful. Jean Broadhurst has lost her husband. Stanley's family is
broken. And the old wound from Leo's death, once opened, cannot be cleanly
closed again.

--- RESOLUTION ---

The killer is arrested and faces prosecution for Stanley's murder (and possibly,
depending on what can still be proved at this distance, for Leo's). Ronny is
returned to his mother. The fire eventually burns itself out.

Archer closes the case. He is older in these late novels — more tired, more
melancholy, more aware of the limits of what detection can accomplish. He can
find the truth. He cannot undo the damage.

--- THEMES AND CRITICAL STANDING ---

"The Underground Man" represents the fullest expression of Macdonald's central
insight: that the present is always haunted by the past, that family violence
is generational, and that California's beautiful surface conceals layers of
old wrong. The novel's ambition is literary as much as it is genre-driven —
Macdonald is working in the tradition of Faulkner and Fitzgerald as much as
Hammett and Chandler. The wildfire is one of American crime fiction's great
sustained metaphors, and the novel as a whole is one of the genre's serious
claims to the status of literature.
