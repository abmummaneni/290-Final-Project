SERIES CONTEXT: MIDSOMER MURDERS AND TOM BARNABY


The Show and Its World

Midsomer Murders is a long-running British detective drama set in the fictional county of
Midsomer — a pastoral, seemingly idyllic English landscape of thatched villages, rolling
green hills, church fêtes, and hedgerows that conceals, with magnificent improbability, an
extraordinary rate of violent death. The show is a deliberate, affectionate, and often
darkly comic extension of the golden age English village mystery tradition — the world of
Agatha Christie, Dorothy L. Sayers, and Margery Allingham, updated to contemporary
television but preserving the essential formula: a beautiful, enclosed rural community;
a web of social relationships, old grievances, and hidden secrets; and a murder (usually
several) that requires patient, methodical unraveling by an intelligent detective.

Chief Inspector Tom Barnaby — played throughout the show's first thirteen series by John
Nettles — is the series' anchor. He is a man of considerable intelligence and considerable
patience: thorough, unhurried, apparently genial, and capable of sitting through an
enormous amount of village politeness and misdirection before arriving at the truth. He
is not flashy or eccentric in the Poirot manner; he is a solid, professional detective
whose greatest skill is attention — the capacity to notice what others overlook and to
hold contradictions in mind until they resolve.

His home life with his wife Joyce is a consistent background warmth in the series — she is
an active, independent woman with her own interests and opinions, and the Barnabys have
a real marriage rather than a domestic backdrop. His sergeant changes over the years;
in this inaugural episode he is partnered with DS Gavin Troy, a younger man who is
competent but less experienced and who provides a useful foil for Barnaby's slower,
more reflective approach.

The Killings at Badger's Drift is the pilot episode of the entire series and is based
directly on Caroline Graham's first Barnaby novel of the same name, published in 1987.
The adaptation was written by Anthony Horowitz, who shaped the tone and world of the
television series in its early years with considerable skill.

THE VILLAGE OF BADGER'S DRIFT


Setting

Badger's Drift is a small, pretty, and intensely enclosed English village of the kind
the Cotswolds or the Home Counties produce with reliable perfection: a church, a pub,
cottages of various vintages, a handful of established families who have lived there
for generations, and the particular social atmosphere of a community where everyone
knows everyone and privacy is a polite fiction rather than a practical reality.

The village is, as always in Midsomer, not the innocent pastoral idyll it appears. Beneath
the pleasant surface of flower gardens, neighborly conversation, and summer fêtes, there
are resentments that go back years, desires that have never been properly acknowledged,
and secrets that have been maintained at considerable psychological cost. The murder — or
murders — that Barnaby comes to investigate are not alien intrusions into this world;
they are, as always, the natural product of it.

THE DEATH OF EMILY SIMPSON


An Old Woman's Walk

Emily Simpson is an elderly woman, retired, a nature lover and botanist of the amateur
but genuinely knowledgeable kind that English village life historically produced in
abundance. She goes for a walk in the woods near Badger's Drift — a routine she has
followed for years — and does not come home.

She is found dead in the woods. The initial assessment is natural causes: she was elderly,
she was alone, and she appears to have had a heart attack. The local doctor certifies her
death accordingly. The police see no reason to treat it otherwise. Emily Simpson was old,
she died in the woods, and that is sad but not suspicious.

Her friend Miss Lacey thinks otherwise.


Miss Lacey's Conviction

Doris Lacey — Emily's closest friend, another elderly village woman — is absolutely certain
that Emily did not die of natural causes. She has no evidence for this in any technical
sense; she has the conviction of someone who knew Emily well and who knows that Emily
was frightened in the days before her death. Emily had seen something in the woods. She
had told Doris this, without telling her what — something that disturbed her deeply,
something she had not known how to process or to whom to report.

Doris takes her conviction to the police. She is not treated seriously. She is a sweet
old lady with grief and suspicion, and the police have a death certificate from a doctor.

She takes her conviction to Chief Inspector Barnaby, and Barnaby — in the first
demonstration of the quality that will define him across the entire series — listens
carefully and does not dismiss her.


Barnaby Looks Again

Barnaby has the post-mortem examined more carefully. The medical evidence, looked at
with fresh eyes, raises questions that support Doris's instinct. Emily's death was not
straightforwardly natural. She was killed — the heart failure was real, but it was
induced by shock or by something else, and the circumstances of her death in the woods
were not as simple as they were assumed to be.

The investigation into Emily Simpson's death is now a murder investigation, and it begins
with the question Barnaby has to answer first: what did Emily see in the woods?

THE COMMUNITY OF BADGER'S DRIFT


The Established Families

Barnaby's investigation requires him to map the social landscape of Badger's Drift with
care. The community is small enough that the number of people who could have known Emily's
habits — including her routine walk in those specific woods — is limited. It is also the
kind of community where the relevant history goes back a long way, and where present
behavior is shaped by things that happened years or even decades ago.

The principal families and individuals Barnaby encounters include:

The Rainbirds — David Rainbird and his sister Katherine. David is an unpleasant, somewhat
brutish man who lives with Katherine in a relationship that is immediately uncomfortable
to observe. Katherine is quiet, subdued in a way that suggests suppression rather than
contentment, and clearly under her brother's domination. David is aggressive when
questioned and deeply hostile to the investigation.

Henry Trace and his wife Phyllis. Henry Trace is a local landowner of middling importance
— comfortable, established, with the slightly complacent confidence of a man who has
always been among the larger fish in a small pond. Phyllis is socially active and
involved in the village's various committees and organizations. Their son Michael is a
young man of artistic inclination who is working on a local project.

Dr. Lessiter, the local GP who certified Emily's death. He is defensive about the
original certification, as any doctor in his position would be, and the re-examination
of his conclusion by Barnaby creates professional friction.

Judith Lessiter, the doctor's wife, who has her own social position in the village and
her own relationships with various community members.

Various other villagers who populate the investigation's background and who contribute
pieces of the social picture Barnaby is assembling.


What Emily Saw

The central investigative question — what Emily Simpson witnessed in the woods on the day
before her death — is the key to everything else. Barnaby and Troy work through the
geography of the woods, the sightlines, who might have been there, and what activity
in those woods would have been shocking enough to frighten a sophisticated, sensible
woman who had spent decades observing nature in that landscape.

The answer is sex. Emily, walking her usual route through the woods, came upon two people
in a sexual encounter — a sexual encounter that was, in the specific social context of
Badger's Drift, deeply transgressive. She was not a prurient woman; she would have looked
away and continued walking and thought nothing more of it under ordinary circumstances.
But these were not ordinary circumstances. What she saw was not simply adultery or
indiscretion — it was something whose specific social and familial dimensions made it
scandalous and potentially devastating to those involved.

What Emily saw was an incestuous relationship between two members of one of Badger's
Drift's established families.

THE SECOND MURDER


Doris Lacey Is Killed

While Barnaby is investigating Emily's death, Doris Lacey — the friend whose insistence
launched the investigation — is also killed. She is murdered in her home, the killing
made to look as much like an accident or natural event as possible, in the same pattern
as Emily's death.

Doris's murder confirms two things: first, that Emily's death was indeed murder, since
whoever killed Emily clearly felt threatened enough to kill again; and second, that the
killer is monitoring the investigation closely enough to know that Doris had gone to the
police and that she remained a threat. Doris did not know what Emily had seen — she knew
only that Emily had seen something — but that was apparently enough to mark her for death.

The killing of an elderly woman who was, in practical terms, not a credible witness to
anything specific is presented by the show as the act of a person in a state of serious
fear. The killer is not cool and strategic; they are frightened, and their response to
being frightened is disproportionate violence.

BARNABY'S ACCUMULATING PICTURE


Mapping the Relationships

As Barnaby builds his picture of Badger's Drift's social relationships, the pattern of
concealed connections becomes more complex. The Rainbird siblings — David's domination
of Katherine, her apparent resignation — are particularly striking. David's hostility to
Barnaby's investigation is too aggressive to be merely the ordinary defensiveness of a
man who dislikes the police. He is frightened, and the specific quality of his fear
suggests he knows what Emily saw.

The Trace family's involvement is also present. Henry Trace's position as local landowner
gives him access to the woods and knowledge of who uses them. Michael Trace's movements
and his particular social relationships with the Rainbirds are relevant.

Barnaby is specifically good at the kind of investigation that requires social intelligence
alongside forensic reasoning — understanding who people are to each other, what the
specific emotional textures of relationships suggest about what might have happened. The
village of Badger's Drift reveals itself to him gradually, each conversation adding a
detail to the picture.


The Question of Who Killed Emily

The method of Emily's death — the induced shock — required both proximity to Emily in the
woods and knowledge that she had been there and what she had seen. The killer had to have
been one of the two people Emily observed, or someone acting for them. The specific nature
of what Emily saw narrows the field of suspects significantly: the transgressive
relationship she witnessed was specific enough that Barnaby, once he has established what
she saw, can cross-reference it with the social map of the village and identify who was
most threatened by her knowledge.

THE REVELATION


The Incestuous Relationship

The relationship Emily witnessed was between David Rainbird and his sister Katherine —
an incestuous relationship that had been ongoing and that both of them had been concealing
from the village. The specific horror of exposure in a closed community like Badger's Drift,
where the Rainbirds are known to everyone and where social reputation is a form of
practical currency, provides the motive for murder.

David Rainbird is the dominant partner in every sense — the initiator, the more aggressive
personality, the one whose response to threat is violence. His killing of Emily Simpson
was a decision to eliminate a witness before she could tell anyone what she had seen.
His killing of Doris Lacey was a decision to eliminate anyone else who might draw the
investigation toward what Emily had witnessed.

Katherine Rainbird's position — whether she knew about the murders, whether she was
complicit, whether she was herself a prisoner of her brother's dominance in every aspect
of their relationship — is one of the episode's more uncomfortable threads. She is
presented as a woman whose agency has been so completely suppressed by David that the
question of her guilt is genuinely complicated.


Barnaby's Confrontation

Barnaby assembles his case, identifies David Rainbird as the killer, and the episode
culminates in the confrontation and resolution. The arrest is carried out. The full
truth of what happened to Emily and Doris is established and formally accounted for.

The episode is notable for handling the incest element with restraint — it is not
sensationalized or played for shock, but treated as what it is: a concealed reality
that produced violence when it was threatened with exposure. The show's tone, even in
its pilot episode, is already characteristic: gentle on the surface, quietly unflinching
about the darkness it finds underneath.

SIGNIFICANCE OF THE PILOT

The Killings at Badger's Drift establishes every element that will define Midsomer
Murders for the next two-plus decades: the beautiful English countryside functioning
as a backdrop to extraordinary violence; the social complexity of enclosed village
communities as the engine of crime; Barnaby's patient, observant methodology; the
domestic warmth of his home life with Joyce; the partnership dynamic with his sergeant;
and the show's particular tonal balance of the cozy and the dark.

Caroline Graham's original novel was well-served by the adaptation. Anthony Horowitz
understood exactly what made the source material work and translated it faithfully while
shaping it for a television format and a television audience.

John Nettles's performance as Barnaby — immediately assured, immediately specific,
immediately recognizable as this particular character — is one of British television
drama's better casting achievements, and this first episode demonstrates fully why
the role and the show would last as long as they did.
