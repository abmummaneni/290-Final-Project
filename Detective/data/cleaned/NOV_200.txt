AUTHORSHIP NOTE

This book is by Sean Cullen, not Lilian Jackson Braun (who wrote the Cat Who... mystery
series). The Hamish X series is a Canadian children's adventure-comedy series for readers
roughly ages 8–12.

SERIES CONTEXT


Who Is Hamish X?

Hamish X is a boy of uncertain origin with extraordinary physical abilities, including
exceptional strength, agility, and combat skill, which are connected to his unusual
mechanical boots — heavy, bronze-colored footwear that seem to be part of him and that
give him powers he does not fully understand. He does not know who made him, who his
parents are, or exactly why he has these capabilities.

He is pursued by a shadowy organization called the Orphan Adjustment Bureau (OAB), which
collects and exploits orphaned children. Hamish moves through a picaresque world of
bizarre institutions, strange adults, and orphans in need of rescue.

His companions are Mimi, a fiercely capable girl, and Parveen, a brilliant South Asian boy
with an extraordinary talent for engineering and invention. Together the three form the
core team.

The series is written in a metafictional style — the narrator frequently addresses the
reader directly, makes jokes about the conventions of the genre, and inserts parenthetical
asides. The tone is comic, affectionate, and occasionally absurdist.

THE SETUP


Arriving at a New Location

In this second installment, Hamish, Mimi, and Parveen find themselves in a new environment —
another strange institution or community in Cullen's eccentric world. The "really dangerous
pie" of the title is central to the plot: a pie (or the process of making it, or what is
inside it) that poses a genuine threat, connected to the villainous operations that are
always swirling around Hamish and his friends.

The OAB's agents are in pursuit. The children must simultaneously navigate their new
environment, solve the immediate problem of the pie and its dangers, and stay ahead of
the forces trying to capture Hamish.


The Pie's Danger

The pie in question is connected to the novel's villain — an adult figure whose culinary
operations conceal something much more sinister. In keeping with the series' tone, the
premise is deliberately absurd: a pie that is genuinely dangerous, in a world where
absurdity is the operating logic. But Cullen grounds the absurdity in real stakes — the
children are in actual danger, even when the source of that danger is comedic.

What makes the pie dangerous — whether it contains something, whether it is a vehicle for
control or harm or both — is the central mystery that Hamish, Mimi, and Parveen must
investigate and resolve.

THE INVESTIGATION AND ACTION


Parveen's Inventions

Parveen's engineering talent is deployed throughout the novel, as in all Hamish X books.
He constructs or improvises devices that assist the team in ways that are both clever and
fanciful. Cullen uses Parveen's inventions as a vehicle for genuine enthusiasm about science
and engineering, even within the absurdist framework.


Mimi's Resourcefulness

Mimi's role is consistently the most grounded of the three — she is the emotional and
practical center, the one who keeps the team functional when Hamish is being heroic and
Parveen is being technical. Her toughness and warmth are consistent across the series.


Hamish's Boots and Their Secrets

The boots remain mysterious and central. They respond to danger, they provide capabilities
Hamish cannot fully control or predict, and they are one of the running threads connecting
the series — the question of what Hamish is and who made him is advanced incrementally
with each book.

In this installment, the boots contribute to the resolution of the pie-related crisis in
a way that also advances Hamish's understanding of his own nature.

RESOLUTION

The danger posed by the pie (and its associated villainous operation) is neutralized by
the combined efforts of Hamish, Mimi, and Parveen. The OAB agents are evaded. The
institution or community they were in is left safer than they found it — in keeping with
the series' consistent pattern of Hamish improving situations for other children even while
his own situation remains unresolved.

The three friends escape to the next adventure, no closer to the full truth of Hamish's
origins but slightly better equipped to face it. The narrator wraps up with characteristic
asides to the reader, jokes about cliffhangers, and promises of more to come.

The overall tone is that of a series deeply committed to the idea that children are
competent, courageous, and capable of saving themselves — and each other.
