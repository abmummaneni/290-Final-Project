--- BACKGROUND AND CONTEXT ---

"Line of Duty" Series 4 was broadcast on BBC One in April–May 2017, having
moved from BBC Two and achieving the show's highest ratings to date — averaging
over seven million viewers per episode and becoming one of the most-watched
dramas in British television. It consists of six episodes directed by Douglas
Mackinnon and written by Jed Mercurio, and stars Adrian Dunbar as Superintendent
Ted Hastings, Vicky McClure as DS Kate Fleming, and Martin Compston as DS Steve
Arnott, with Jed Mercurio casting Keeley Hawes to return in an entirely new
role as DCI Roz Huntley.

Series 4 represents a significant escalation in both the show's institutional
mythology and its specific dramatic ambition. The AC-12 team is now investigating
a case with direct connections to the corrupt network established in previous
series, the "H" mythology is advanced with specific new information, and the
central character of Roz Huntley is one of the show's most complex and most
morally demanding creations.

--- THE OPERATION TRAPDOOR CONTEXT ---

AC-12's interest in this series is triggered by the investigation of Operation
Trapdoor — a major police investigation into a series of murders attributed
to a suspect called Tim Ifield. Trapdoor has been led by DCI Roz Huntley, and
its specific conduct raises specific concerns: evidence has been managed in
ways that don't fully add up, the case against Ifield has been built with a
specific speed and a specific certainty that AC-12's institutional experience
finds suspicious, and the specific circumstances of Ifield's death — which
occurs while he is under investigation — require examination.

The dead suspect, Tim Ifield, was a forensic scientist connected to the case.
His death, in the context of the investigation of which he was both a participant
and a suspect, is the immediate trigger for AC-12's formal involvement.

--- DCI ROZ HUNTLEY ---

Roz Huntley (Keeley Hawes) is the show's central subject — a Detective Chief
Inspector whose management of the Trapdoor investigation is AC-12's primary
concern. She is a senior and accomplished detective, a woman who has navigated
the specific difficulties of a professional environment that has not always
been structured to support female advancement, and who has reached her position
through genuine capability and considerable determination.

She is also, as the investigation will establish, conducting herself in ways
that are not entirely compatible with the standards AC-12 exists to enforce.
The specific nature of her misconduct — what she has done, why she has done it,
and what her specific relationship to the corrupt network underlying the series'
mythology is — is the season's central question and is managed with the sustained
ambiguity that Keeley Hawes's extraordinary performance sustains across six
episodes.

Hawes's Huntley is a significantly different characterisation from her Series
2 Denton: where Denton was weathered and defensive, Huntley is confident,
polished, and in command in a way that makes her specific vulnerability — when
it emerges — more surprising and more affecting. She is one of the finest
performances in the show's history.

--- HUNTLEY'S PERSONAL SITUATION ---

Running through the series is Huntley's specific personal situation: she has
a health issue — a condition affecting her arm that is progressively worsening —
and a marriage whose specific dynamics are part of the picture the investigation
assembles. Her specific management of her health, and the specific choices she
makes about concealing it from both her institution and her family, is one of
the series' sustained threads.

Her husband Nick (Lee Ingleby) and the specific dynamics of their relationship —
which prove to be considerably more complicated than the surface suggests — are
part of the picture that the investigation eventually builds.

--- THE CORRUPT NETWORK AND THE MURDERS ---

The murders that Operation Trapdoor was investigating — the specific killings
that Ifield was suspected of and that Huntley built a case around — are
connected, the investigation gradually establishes, to the broader criminal
network that has been present in the show's mythology since Series 2.

The balaclava gang — introduced in Series 3 — is connected to the murders.
The specific individuals whose connection to both the murders and to the corrupt
network AC-12 has been pursuing are identified through the investigation, and
the specific mechanism by which Trapdoor's conduct served the interests of
that network rather than justice is established.

The specific person responsible for the murders — the real killer, rather than
the suspect Huntley built the case around — is one of the series' central
revelations, and the identification of that person requires engaging with the
specific evidence that Trapdoor's conduct was designed to suppress.

--- ARNOTT AND THE ASSAULT ---

Steve Arnott is attacked and seriously injured during the investigation — pushed
down a flight of stairs in an incident that has the specific character of a
deliberate targeted assault rather than an accident or a random crime. The
attack, which leaves him with serious injuries requiring sustained recovery,
is connected to the investigation and to the specific people who have reason
to want AC-12's inquiry suppressed.

Arnott's specific response to the attack — the specific psychological and
physical dimensions of recovering from a serious assault while managing the
professional demands of the investigation — is one of Series 4's most significant
character threads. Martin Compston's performance of Arnott's recovery is one
of the quieter achievements in the series: a sustained portrayal of someone
managing trauma and its consequences while trying to keep doing the job.

--- WHAT HUNTLEY ACTUALLY DID ---

The full picture of Roz Huntley's conduct is assembled across the six episodes
with the sustained ambiguity and procedural care that defines the show's
treatment of its central subjects. She is not straightforwardly corrupt in
the Gates or Cottan mode: her specific conduct is rooted in a specific situation
that arose during the Trapdoor investigation, and the specific choices she made
in response to that situation are comprehensible even when they are clearly wrong.

Huntley tampered with evidence in the Trapdoor case — specifically, she managed
the forensic evidence in ways designed to point toward Ifield and away from
the investigation's actual direction. Her specific reasons for doing this —
the specific threat she faced, the specific situation she was managing, and
the specific calculation she made about what the best available option was —
are established through the investigation.

Her complicity in the broader corrupt network is more ambiguous than her
specific misconduct in the Trapdoor case. The investigation must establish
whether she is primarily a detective who compromised herself in a specific
situation or a more deeply embedded member of the organised corruption that
has been the show's central concern across its run.

--- THE "H" MYTHOLOGY ADVANCES ---

Series 4 advances the overarching "H" mythology — the question of the senior
corrupt officer whose existence Cottan's dying gesture gestured toward in
Series 3 — with specific new information. The specific characteristics of "H,"
the specific level of seniority required to have done what the evidence suggests
this person has done, and the specific candidates whose positions and histories
match the profile are all given development in Series 4.

The investigation does not identify "H" — that resolution is reserved for
later series — but it moves the question significantly forward and provides
the specific evidence that will be material to the eventual identification.

--- RESOLUTION ---

The Trapdoor investigation is resolved: the real killer of the original murder
victims is identified, the specific conduct of the investigation that was designed
to suppress that identification is established, and Huntley faces the specific
legal and professional consequences of what the investigation has found about
her.

The specific outcome for Huntley — the specific legal circumstances of her
case, and the specific degree of her culpability as established by the
investigation — is handled with the moral complexity that the show has brought
to all its central subjects. She is not simply a corrupt officer. She is a
person who made specific wrong choices in specific circumstances, and the
investigation's accounting of those choices is precise rather than wholesale.

--- NOTES ON THE SERIES ---

Series 4 of Line of Duty is the show at its broadest cultural reach and, arguably,
at its most polished. The move to BBC One brought a larger audience without
compromising the show's specific qualities — the procedural rigor, the sustained
ambiguity, the interview sequences, the institutional critique. Keeley Hawes's
Huntley is her finest performance in the show and one of the finest in the
series' guest star tradition. Douglas Mackinnon's direction is the series'
most cinematic to date. And the specific advancement of the "H" mythology
gives the season an investment in the show's long-form project that sets up
the later series with considerable skill.
