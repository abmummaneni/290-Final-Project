TVE_039 | Poirot: Lord Edgware Dies | Director: Brian Farnham | TV Episode
================================================================================
FULL EPISODE SYNOPSIS — SPOILERS INCLUDED
================================================================================

--- BACKGROUND AND CONTEXT ---

"Lord Edgware Dies" is a two-part episode of ITV's Agatha Christie's Poirot,
first broadcast in April 2000. It is directed by Brian Farnham and stars David
Suchet as Hercule Poirot, Hugh Fraser as Captain Hastings, Philip Jackson as
Chief Inspector Japp, and Pauline Moran as Miss Lemon. It is based on Christie's
1933 novel of the same name, which is notable for featuring one of the series'
most technically daring solutions — an alibi trick that depends on impersonation
and the specific quality of celebrity recognition in the early 1930s.

The episode belongs to the later, more lavishly produced period of the Suchet
Poirot series and is one of the stronger entries of that era.

--- THE WORLD OF THE EPISODE: THEATRE AND SOCIETY ---

The episode is set in London's theatrical and aristocratic social world of the
early 1930s. The glittering, slightly precarious world where stage celebrities
and the old aristocracy overlap is rendered with period precision — the parties,
the gossip, the specific social currency of fame and title.

The central figure of this world, at the episode's opening, is Jane Wilkinson —
an American actress of extraordinary beauty and charisma who is the talk of
London. She is currently the wife of Lord Edgware, a cold and reputedly sinister
English nobleman from whom she desperately wishes to be divorced. She wants
to marry the young Duke of Merton, who is deeply besotted with her. Lord
Edgware, she believes, is blocking the divorce.

--- JANE WILKINSON'S REQUEST ---

Jane Wilkinson approaches Poirot — characteristically, she has heard of him
and decides he is the person to help her — and asks him to intercede with
Lord Edgware on her behalf. She wants Poirot to persuade Edgware to grant
her a divorce.

Poirot is somewhat charmed by Jane, as most men are, and agrees to visit
Lord Edgware on her behalf. He and Hastings go to Edgware's London house.

The interview with Lord Edgware is strange and unsatisfying. Edgware is cold,
unpleasant, and peculiar — but he tells Poirot something that surprises him:
he has already written to Jane some months earlier agreeing to the divorce.
He has no objection. As far as he is concerned, Jane is free to remarry.

Poirot is puzzled. Why has Jane told him that Edgware is refusing the divorce,
when in fact he has already agreed to it? He reports this to Jane, who seems
equally puzzled — or claims to be.

--- LORD EDGWARE'S MURDER ---

That night, Lord Edgware is murdered in his study. He is stabbed in the neck.
The butler admits that a woman called at the house that evening: a woman he
identifies as Lady Edgware — as Jane Wilkinson — who was shown into the study
to see her husband.

Jane Wilkinson, when questioned, has an apparently perfect alibi: she was at
a dinner party all evening, with a dozen witnesses who can confirm she was
never absent.

This is the episode's central puzzle: the butler and other witnesses place
Jane Wilkinson at the scene of the murder. A dozen dinner party guests place
her simultaneously at a party across town. She cannot have been in both places.
One set of witnesses must be wrong — but which?

--- THE ALIBI INVESTIGATION ---

Poirot investigates both sets of witnesses with characteristic care. The dinner
party alibi is extremely strong: twelve people saw Jane throughout the evening,
and she was the center of attention — impossible to miss or confuse. The butler's
identification of the woman who called at the house is also apparently confident:
he saw Lady Edgware, whom he knows, and he let her in.

The specific quality of Jane Wilkinson's celebrity is Poirot's first clue.
Jane is extraordinarily famous — her face is on every magazine, her mannerisms
are widely known, her distinctive way of speaking and moving have been impersonated
by every impressionist and comedian in London. She is, in short, one of the
most recognizable and most imitable women in England.

This observation directs Poirot toward a specific possibility: the woman the
butler saw was not Jane Wilkinson. She was someone impersonating Jane Wilkinson —
and doing so at a level of skill that convinced a butler who knew Lady Edgware
well enough to recognize her. The impersonation required both physical
resemblance and the specific performance of Jane's famous mannerisms.

--- THE PARIS CONNECTION ---

The investigation expands to cover Lord Edgware's household and his family.
Among the people with connections to Edgware is his nephew, Ronald Marsh —
a young man who is Edgware's heir and who has been treated badly by his uncle.
Ronald has financial motive: Edgware's death benefits him.

Another figure is Carlotta Adams — a young American actress and impressionist
who is famous for her remarkably accurate celebrity impersonations. Her act
features multiple well-known figures, and among them is a celebrated impression
of Jane Wilkinson. Carlotta's impersonation is so accurate that she has been
mistaken for Jane in various social situations.

Carlotta Adams is found dead — she has died of an overdose of veronal (a
barbiturate). Her death is ruled accidental. Poirot does not believe it.

In Carlotta's possession is a small gold box — a gift, recently received —
that connects her to a specific person. The gold box and its engraving provide
one of the episode's key physical clues.

--- WHAT HAPPENED ---

Poirot's reconstruction of events is as follows:

Carlotta Adams was hired — for a substantial sum — to impersonate Jane
Wilkinson on the night of Edgware's murder. She went to Edgware's house,
wearing clothes and a wig that matched Jane's appearance, and performed
the impersonation for the butler and whoever else saw her. She then left.
Edgware was subsequently killed by the actual murderer, who used Carlotta's
visit to establish that "Jane" had been at the house that evening — framing
Jane for the murder.

The actual killer is not Jane. The killing was carried out by someone else
who had their own motive to kill Edgware and who engineered the Carlotta
impersonation scheme to point suspicion at Jane.

Carlotta was then killed — the overdose was administered — to prevent her
from identifying who had hired her for the impersonation.

--- THE KILLER: RONALD MARSH ---

The killer is Ronald Marsh, Edgware's nephew and heir. His motive is financial:
Edgware has been a cruel and controlling uncle, and Ronald stands to inherit
substantially from his death.

Ronald arranged the entire scheme: he hired Carlotta Adams to impersonate
Jane Wilkinson on the night of the murder, knowing that the framing of Jane —
with her excellent alibi — would create maximum confusion and divert the
investigation from himself. He calculated that the contradiction between the
butler's testimony and the dinner party alibi would make the case so complicated
that his own guilt would never surface.

He killed Carlotta after the fact to prevent the impersonation scheme from
being traced back to him.

The gold box found in Carlotta's possession — with its specific engraving —
connects to Ronald through the chain of its provenance. Poirot traces the box
and establishes the connection.

--- RESOLUTION ---

Ronald Marsh is identified and arrested. The impersonation scheme is exposed.
Carlotta Adams's death is reclassified as murder rather than accidental overdose.
Jane Wilkinson is cleared of any involvement in Edgware's death.

Jane, freed from the marriage she wanted to escape and now genuinely cleared
of murder, is left to pursue her relationship with the Duke of Merton — though
the specific emotional texture of that prospect is slightly different after
the events of the case.

--- NOTES ON THE EPISODE ---

"Lord Edgware Dies" is a strong mid-period Suchet Poirot production. The
impersonation alibi — using a famous person's imitability as a murder weapon —
is one of Christie's more ingenious constructions and requires precise
rendering to be both fair and surprising. The episode handles the 1930s
theatrical world with real period atmosphere, and Suchet's performance of
Poirot's growing certainty about the impersonation mechanism — the specific
moment when the idea crystallizes from the observation about Jane's celebrity —
is one of his more quietly effective scenes in the series.
