BACKGROUND AND CONTEXT

The Man Who Could Not Shudder is one of Carr's most overtly supernatural-seeming novels and centres on the "impossible crime" subgenre for which he was the undisputed master. The title is a deliberate allusion to the Grimm fairy tale "The Boy Who Could Not Shudder" — the story of a youth who cannot feel fear and who must be taught what it means to shudder. Carr's novel inverts the fairytale tone: the rationalist who cannot shudder in the presence of the apparently supernatural will be forced, by the end, to confront something that even a sceptic finds disturbing. The novel features Dr. Gideon Fell — the enormous, wheezing, enormously learned detective with his forest of sticks and his beer-barrel physique — as the investigator who must explain the inexplicable.

The book was published in 1940 and was, by Carr's prolific standards, a middling entry in his catalogue — more readable and entertaining than his critics suggest, but less celebrated than his masterpieces (The Hollow Man, He Who Whispers, The Judas Window). It also contains a notable spoiler for Agatha Christie's The Murder of Roger Ackroyd, which is disclosed during an in-text discussion of detective fiction.

THE HAUNTED HOUSE — LONGWOOD

Longwood House is a stately English country house with a distinctly troubled history. Two decades before the events of the novel, the house was closed following a deeply strange incident: the butler — a frail, elderly man of over eighty — was found dead, apparently crushed by the chandelier, which had fallen from the ceiling. The inexplicable element was that the chandelier was old and sturdy and had no mechanical reason to fall — unless the butler had, for reasons no one could explain, grabbed the chandelier and swung on it until it broke free. The image is darkly absurd: an octogenarian butler performing circus acrobatics before his death. The house was subsequently left empty for years.

There are also reports of a chair that, unprompted, appeared to leap off the wall at visitors. And a grandfather clock that started of its own accord, despite not having been wound.

Longwood is purchased by Martin Clarke, a wealthy, rakish young man who is fascinated by the supernatural and has a collector's enthusiasm for the genuinely mysterious. He refurbishes the house and decides to host what he calls a "ghost party" — a weekend invitation to a group of friends and specialists to experience and investigate Longwood's paranormal reputation firsthand.

THE NARRATOR AND THE GUESTS

The story is narrated by Bob, a young man who attends the houseparty with his fiancée Tess. Bob is an intelligent, level-headed everyman through whose eyes the events are filtered. He and Tess represent a kind of grounded normality against which the house's strangeness is measured.

Among the other guests:

— THE LOGANS: A married couple in obvious difficulty. Mrs. Logan has been conducting an affair — the man in question appears to be Martin Clarke himself, or at least that is the implication that hangs over the party. Mr. Logan is tense, suspicious, and clearly aware that something is wrong in his marriage.

— Various others, including individuals with specialist knowledge of psychology, physics, and the supernatural — the "experts" Clarke has assembled to investigate and validate the haunting.

— DR. GIDEON FELL: The enormous, cape-wearing, beer-addicted doctor and detective, who is conveniently present at (or easily accessible to) the houseparty. Fell is one of the great figures of Golden Age detection — vast in body and intellect, genial in manner, devastating in analysis.

THE FIRST IMPOSSIBLE MURDER

On the morning after the party arrives, Mr. Logan is shot to death in the library. The circumstances are, by any rational standard, impossible.

Several witnesses are present. Mrs. Logan and at least two others are in the room when it happens. They all see the same thing: the revolver, which has been hanging on the wall as part of a decorative display of antique firearms, appears to rise of its own accord from its mounting pegs, level itself, and fire — killing Logan with a bullet through the forehead. The gun then falls to the floor. No one touched it. There is no mechanical device visible. The room has no hidden spaces that could conceal an operator.

This is the quintessential Carr scenario: an apparently supernatural event that must have a rational explanation, described in such vivid detail that the reader is genuinely baffled. The question Fell must answer is not just who killed Logan, but how it was physically possible for a gun to shoot itself.

ADDITIONAL STRANGE EVENTS

The shooting is not the only uncanny event at Longwood. Chairs continue to move unprompted. The chandelier swings without visible cause. There is a fire — threatening, though eventually contained. Tess, the narrator's fiancée, feels a ghostly hand grip her ankle in the dark. The atmosphere of genuine unease that Carr creates is one of the novel's great achievements: even knowing intellectually that there must be a rational explanation, the cumulative effect of the incidents is deeply unsettling.

Clarke, far from being frightened, is delighted — this is what he came for. His enthusiasm in the face of events that are frightening everyone else is itself slightly sinister.

THE SECOND MURDER AND INVESTIGATION

The houseparty does not end with Logan's death. Further violence follows, including at least one additional death among the party. Fell investigates — but, notably, Fell is not present from the beginning of the novel. He arrives somewhat late, after the initial events have been established, and investigates with less than his usual support (his regular associates, including Hadley, are not available). This somewhat undermines the typical Fell dynamic, and several reviewers have noted that the novel would be stronger if Fell were more central from the start.

Nevertheless, Fell works methodically through the physical evidence, the relationships between the guests, and the architectural history of Longwood.

THE SOLUTION — THE ELECTROMAGNET

The explanation for the flying gun is, by Carr's standards, slightly disappointing but clever in its own right: electromagnetism. The revolver is not an ordinary antique. It has been fitted, or placed near, an electromagnet concealed within the wall — a device that, when activated, causes the gun to be pulled sharply and forcefully in a specific direction. The operator can control the timing and direction of the pull. When the electromagnet was activated, the gun jerked violently off its pegs, swung on its axis (having been positioned and weighted so that the barrel would sweep in the direction of the target), and discharged when the hammer struck an activating device during the motion.

The electromagnet requires someone to activate it remotely — and therefore requires a killer who had access to the trigger mechanism while appearing to be an innocent bystander or even a victim.

The chandelier incident years earlier was also engineered: the old butler was lured or manipulated into a position where the chandelier — rigged to fall — came down on top of him. The original death at Longwood was also murder, not haunting.

THE KILLER

The killer is Martin Clarke himself. Clarke purchased Longwood specifically because of its reputation for hauntings — which he himself had engineered, or which he knew had been engineered by whoever had used the house before him, possibly the previous owner who had the butler killed. Clarke is a man who has constructed an elaborate fraud: the "haunted house" reputation is the cover he needs to commit murder and escape suspicion, because any killing at Longwood will be attributed to the supernatural phenomena.

His motive for killing Logan is connected to the affair with Mrs. Logan. Clarke had been involved with her and, for reasons that become clear on investigation, needed Logan dead. The "ghost party" was conveniently timed — a social event that gave him a houseful of witnesses who would all testify to the impossible, inexplicable flying gun, providing perfect cover.

The butler's death decades earlier is connected: either Clarke was involved in that too, or he purchased Longwood specifically because someone had already proven that the location's supernatural reputation could be manufactured and weaponized.

The precise details of how Clarke arranged the earlier incidents — the swinging chair, the moving furniture — are also explained by Fell: these were produced by hidden mechanisms, some mechanical and some electromagnetic, installed in the house during the refurbishment. Clarke had the run of the house during renovation and installed the apparatus then.

THE RESOLUTION AND FELL'S EXPLANATION

Fell assembles the household (more or less) for the traditional explanation scene. The key to the impossible murder is the electromagnet; the key to identifying Clarke is the establishment of his access to the trigger mechanism during the moments when everyone else's movements can be accounted for, combined with three clues planted in the narrative that, in retrospect, point clearly to Clarke's guilt. One Goodreads reviewer noted that these clues are deliberately placed "in plain view" but easily overlooked — they concern physical details about the room, the gun's weight and balance, and Clarke's behaviour on the morning of the shooting.

The solution also involves an epilogue set approximately two years after the events — a structural device Carr uses to deliver Fell's final explanation, which some readers have found unsatisfying in its delayed delivery. The passage of time allows Carr to resolve several loose ends about what happened to the surviving characters.

Clarke cannot escape justice despite his cleverness, because Fell's reconstruction of the mechanism is meticulous enough to be presented as evidence.

LITERARY NOTES AND SELF-REFERENCE

The novel contains several in-text references to other detective fiction that are unusual for the genre. Carr has Dr. Fell — or another character — reveal the solution to Melville Davisson Post's short story "The Doomdorf Mystery," and more egregiously, explicitly disclose the identity of the killer in Agatha Christie's The Murder of Roger Ackroyd. This has been noted by multiple reviewers as one of the more inconsiderate authorial choices in the Golden Age canon, and readers who have not yet encountered Roger Ackroyd are warned that The Man Who Could Not Shudder will ruin it for them.

The title's reference to the Grimm fairy tale is not incidental: Carr is playing with the idea of the rationalist detective as the man who cannot be frightened by apparent supernatural phenomena, because he knows there must be a rational explanation for everything. Like the fairy tale's boy, the detective must be educated — not in how to shudder, but in understanding the mechanisms that produce shuddering in others.

CRITICAL ASSESSMENT

The Man Who Could Not Shudder is considered a second-tier Carr — well above average for the genre as a whole, but not among his half-dozen masterpieces. Its strengths are the genuinely atmospheric haunted house setup, the vividness of the impossible gun-shooting scene, and Carr's unfailing ability to maintain suspense and forward momentum. Its weaknesses are the somewhat disappointing electromagnetic solution (functional but not elegant), the late arrival and relative under-use of Fell, and the extensive reliance on coincidence in the plot's architecture. It is, however, an entirely satisfying read for fans of impossible crime fiction, and the gun-shooting scene alone is worth the price of admission.
