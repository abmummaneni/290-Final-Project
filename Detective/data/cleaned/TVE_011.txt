--- BACKGROUND AND CONTEXT ---

"Murder Under Glass" is a Season 6 episode of Columbo, airing January 30, 1978.
It is directed by Jonathan Demme — then a young director building his career,
a decade before "The Silence of the Lambs" would make him a household name —
and stars Louis Jourdan as Paul Gerard, one of the most elegant and composed
of all Columbo killers. The episode is set in the world of high-end restaurant
culture and food criticism, using its milieu with genuine sophistication: the
world of great cuisine, of restaurants with three-star pretensions, of the
specific power that a major food critic can exert over careers and livelihoods.

--- THE KILLER, THE VICTIM, AND THE MOTIVE ---

Paul Gerard is the most powerful and feared food critic in Los Angeles —
possibly in the country. His reviews can make or destroy restaurants. His word
on a chef is the word that matters. He is cultivated, French, impeccably
dressed, and in absolute command of every room he enters.

He is also a blackmailer. His power over the restaurant world has given him
leverage that he exercises systematically: chefs and restaurateurs who want
good reviews, or who fear devastating ones, pay Gerard — not officially, but
through mechanisms he has carefully constructed. He is, in effect, running
an extortion operation disguised as food criticism.

Vittorio Rossi is an Italian restaurateur and chef who has been one of Gerard's
long-standing extortion targets. For whatever reason — the amounts demanded
have become too high, or Rossi's financial situation has changed, or he has
simply reached a limit — Rossi has decided to stop paying and to expose Gerard.
This would destroy Gerard's reputation and his income.

Gerard poisons Rossi. The method is characteristic of a man who moves in the
world of food and wine: Rossi is killed with a toxin introduced into something
he ate or drank during or after a meal. The poison is exotic and effective —
the death appears, initially, to be from natural causes or at most from some
kind of allergic reaction or health episode.

--- THE ELEGANCE OF THE SCHEME ---

What makes Gerard's murder scheme particularly elegant is the professional
camouflage it provides. A food critic is perpetually in the company of people
eating and drinking. He has access to food, wine, and the preparation of meals
in a way that most people do not. A poison administered through food — in a
restaurant, at a dinner, in any of the dozens of occasions where a critic
eats — is almost impossible to connect specifically to the critic rather than
to the many other people involved in the meal's preparation and service.

Gerard is confident in this confidence. He is a man of extraordinary refinement
and self-possession, and his assessment of his own scheme is that it is as
close to perfect as human planning can achieve.

--- COLUMBO ENTERS ---

Columbo investigates Rossi's death and, characteristically, is not satisfied
with the natural-causes or health-episode explanation. Something about the
manner of death — the specific symptoms, the timing, the context — tells him
this was deliberate.

His investigation leads him toward Gerard. The dynamic between them is
immediately distinctive: Gerard is one of the most socially intimidating people
Columbo has ever encountered, and he deploys this intimidation consciously.
He is not aggressive — he is far too refined for that — but his manner makes
it clear that he considers Columbo to be beneath his social level, and that
he finds the detective's persistence mildly amusing rather than threatening.

Columbo, for his part, is genuinely fascinated by Gerard's world. He loves food.
He talks about food, about restaurants he has eaten at, about Mrs. Columbo's
cooking. His apparent enthusiasm for the gastronomic culture Gerard represents
is both genuinely authentic and strategically useful: Gerard finds it charming
and contemptible in equal measure, and in doing so reveals more than he intends
about his own operations.

--- COLUMBO LEARNS ABOUT FOOD ---

A distinctive and much-loved element of this episode is Columbo's immersion in
the world of haute cuisine. He visits restaurants, he eats meals that Gerard
reviews, he talks to chefs and restaurateurs about the economics and culture
of fine dining. He is genuinely enjoying himself. And in the process of enjoying
himself, he is learning exactly how Gerard's extortion scheme works, who has
been paying, and what specific leverage Gerard has over the restaurant world.

The victims of Gerard's extortion are reluctant to speak, for obvious reasons —
they are paying to maintain their good reviews, and talking to police about
it would expose both themselves and their payments. But Columbo's manner, which
puts people at ease and seems unthreatening, extracts from them enough to build
a picture.

--- THE POISON AND THE PROOF ---

The central challenge of the investigation is establishing the specific poison
used, connecting it to Gerard, and proving that he had the opportunity to
administer it. The poison is not a common one — it is something specific and
unusual, which tells Columbo something about the killer's knowledge and access.

Gerard's specific knowledge of toxicology — beyond what an ordinary food critic
would possess — is an avenue Columbo pursues. How does a food critic come to
know about a specific rare toxin? The answer to that question, when it comes,
is part of what closes the case.

The opportunity question is addressed through the reconstruction of the final
meal or encounter between Gerard and Rossi — when they were last together, what
was consumed, and what the specific mechanism of introduction would have been.

--- THE FINAL TRAP ---

The episode builds toward a scene in which Columbo effectively turns Gerard's
own weapon against him — involving food, wine, or the specific circumstances
of a meal — to establish his guilt in a way that Gerard cannot deflect.

The specific mechanism is consistent with the episode's thematic elegance:
Columbo uses the world of food and the specific knowledge he has accumulated
about how poison can be introduced into a dining situation to force Gerard
into an impossible position.

--- RESOLUTION ---

Paul Gerard is exposed and arrested. His extortion scheme — the full mechanism
of how he has been using his critical power to extract money from the restaurant
world — is dismantled as part of the investigation's conclusion.

The elegantly constructed scheme of poisoning through food, which should have
been undetectable and untraceable, is defeated by a detective who paid genuine
attention to a world that Gerard assumed was too refined for a man like Columbo
to navigate.

--- NOTES ON THE EPISODE ---

"Murder Under Glass" is one of the series' most atmospherically distinctive
entries. Jonathan Demme's direction captures the specific visual world of
high-end restaurant culture with a sensuousness that matches the episode's
subject. Louis Jourdan's Gerard is one of the most elegant and self-possessed
of all Columbo's antagonists — he never loses his composure, and his contempt
for Columbo is so refined as to be almost a compliment. The episode's central
pleasure is Columbo learning to navigate a world of deliberate exclusivity
and using that navigation to dismantle the person who assumed the world was
his exclusive domain.
