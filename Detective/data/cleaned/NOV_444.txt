THE SETUP: A CORPORATE RETREAT


Snoop

The novel centers on a tech company called Snoop — a music-sharing app that has become
extremely successful, perhaps more than its founders expected. The company is at a
crossroads: a buyout offer has been made by a large corporation, and the founding partners
are split on whether to accept. The division is significant and has generated real tension
among the team's inner circle.

To try to resolve the dispute and move forward as a unit — or at least to work through
the conflict in a setting removed from the London office — the company has organized a
team retreat at a luxury ski chalet called Chalet Perce-Neige in the French Alps.


The Chalet and Its Staff

The chalet is high in the mountains above a small resort town. It is staffed by two
employees: Erin, a chalet host who manages the accommodation, cooking, and logistics; and
Danny, who handles the outdoor and ski-related logistics. Erin narrates approximately half
the novel, alternating with third-person sections focusing on Liz, one of the company's
co-founders.

Erin is a professional chalet host who has worked the Alps season after season — competent,
perceptive, and largely invisible to the guests she serves, which gives her an observer's
perspective on the group dynamics unfolding around her.


The Snoop Team

The company's inner circle at the chalet includes:

Liz Bentley — one of the two original co-founders. She and her partner Topher founded
Snoop, and her relationship with the buyout offer is complicated by both financial and
personal stakes.

Topher St. Clair-Bridges — the other co-founder. Wealthy, charming, the more publicly
visible face of the company, and firmly in favor of the buyout.

Eva van den Berg — a senior employee and major stakeholder, strongly opposed to the sale.

Tiger McAllister — another senior figure, also opposed to the sale.

Ani — a shareholder whose vote is genuinely undecided and whose position is therefore
pivotal.

Miranda and Elliot — additional employees and stakeholders.

Carl — Liz's husband, not a company employee but present as a partner.

The buyout vote hangs over the entire retreat. The factions are clear: Topher's group
wants to sell; Eva and Tiger's group does not; Ani's vote will likely determine the outcome.

THE AVALANCHE


The Mountain Closes

Shortly after the Snoop group arrives, the weather turns catastrophic. A major storm
arrives, followed by the disaster the Alps always hold in reserve: an avalanche. The
avalanche does not destroy the chalet, but it buries the access road completely and
triggers closure of the ski lifts and the mountain itself. Communication infrastructure
fails — mobile signals are lost, the landline is down, and the chalet's internet is gone.

The group is completely cut off. No rescue can get in. They cannot get out. Emergency
services know approximately where they are but cannot reach them.

They have supplies for several days. What they do not have is safety — because someone
in the chalet, it becomes clear, is killing them.

THE KILLINGS BEGIN


One Goes Missing

The first death occurs during the storm. A member of the group leaves the chalet — goes
out onto the mountain during conditions that no experienced person would choose. The body
is eventually found in the snow, and the circumstances of how they came to be outside in
the storm are not consistent with a voluntary decision.

Erin, who as a chalet host has responsibility for the welfare of her guests, is immediately
concerned. Danny is equally troubled. The two of them are in the position of professional
service staff who are suddenly in a completely different situation — not hosts managing
luxury experiences but two people trying to keep a group alive while understanding that
one member of that group appears to be killing the others.


The Locked Room Problem

With the chalet sealed by snow and the group reduced to the people inside it, what follows
is a classic locked-room construction in a contemporary setting. Each death reduces the
number of people who could be responsible. The killings are not random — they appear
targeted, which suggests motive, which draws investigative attention back to the buyout
dispute and the financial stakes around the Snoop sale.

If the vote is the motive, the question becomes: who benefits from specific people being
removed before a vote is taken?


The Financial Architecture

Ware unfolds the details of Snoop's ownership structure carefully: who owns what percentage,
what the buyout price implies for each shareholder, what voting rights each person holds,
and how the death of specific individuals affects the outcome of the sale decision. The
corporate mechanics are not incidental to the plot — they are the map of motive.

As members of the group die, the vote calculus shifts. And whoever is killing is clearly
aware of that calculus.

ERIN AND LIZ


Two Perspectives

The alternating narratives of Erin and Liz provide two very different angles on the crisis.
Erin sees the group from the outside — she observes the social dynamics, the alliances and
resentments, the way people behave when they think the hired help isn't paying attention.
Liz is inside the group's history and politics, carrying the weight of what Snoop means
to her and what its sale would mean.

Liz's perspective also carries its own secrets and its own complications. Her position on
the buyout — and why she holds it — is more complex than it initially appears, and her
personal history with various members of the group adds dimensions that become relevant
as the killings narrow the field of suspects.


Erin's Professional Composure

Erin's role as a service professional in a crisis is one of the novel's quieter pleasures.
She is the person in the room who has the most practical competence — she knows the chalet,
the mountain, the logistics of survival in Alpine conditions — and yet her social position
in the group is the lowest. The guests do not initially look to her for leadership. Her
gradual imposition of practical authority over the crisis is realistic and satisfying.

THE REVELATION


Who Is Killing and Why

The killer is identified through the convergence of the financial motive map and the
accumulating physical evidence of each death. Ware has constructed the voting structure
of Snoop with enough precision that the reader can, in principle, work out whose death
serves whose interest — the puzzle is solvable, though she manages her reveals carefully
enough that the solution is not obvious in advance.

The specific killer's identity and the full account of their motivation — the combination
of financial self-interest and more personal elements of the Snoop founding story —
resolves into a picture that is both credible and chilling. Corporate stakes produce
real violence; the buyout dispute that seemed like a business problem turns out to be
something that someone was willing to kill multiple people over.

The confrontation between Erin and/or Liz and the killer occurs within the still-sealed
chalet, with rescue imminent but not yet arrived — a standard thriller timing that Ware
manages with competence.

RESOLUTION

Rescue finally arrives as conditions allow. The killer is dealt with. The deaths are
accounted for. The Snoop buyout — which has been decided by death rather than by the
vote that was supposed to resolve it — has an outcome, though not the one that was planned.

Erin survives, her competence and nerve fully demonstrated. Liz's arc through the novel
is resolved through her confrontation with the truth about the company she built and the
people she built it with.

The novel ends with the mountain accessible again, the chalet no longer sealed, and the
survivors extracting themselves from the Alpine winter into which the retreat descended.
One by One is a focused, efficiently constructed closed-circle thriller — the classic
form updated to a contemporary corporate setting, with the snow doing the work that locked
doors or fog-bound islands do in the genre's historical examples.
