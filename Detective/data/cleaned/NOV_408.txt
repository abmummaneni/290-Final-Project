A Stranger in the House — Full Plot Synopsis
Shari Lapena, 2017

The Night Everything Changes
Tom Krupp arrives home one evening to find his wife, Karen, inexplicably gone. Dinner is burning on the stove, her purse and phone are left behind, and her car has vanished. Alarmed, he calls the police. Within hours, Karen is found — she has been in a serious car accident in a dangerous part of Bridgeport, a neighborhood she has no obvious reason to visit. She survives, but when she wakes in the hospital, she claims to remember nothing: not the crash, not why she was there, not what she was doing that night.
Tom is relieved she's alive. But the questions pile up immediately.

The Neighbor Watching
Across the street lives Brigid Cruikshank and her husband, Robert. Brigid is quietly obsessed with Tom and Karen — envious of their seemingly perfect marriage, their beautiful home, and particularly of Karen herself. She watches them more than she admits. On the night of Karen's accident, Brigid noticed Karen leaving the house in a hurry, behaving oddly, and driving away erratically. She tells the police what she saw, positioning herself as a helpful witness, but her motives are layered with jealousy and a complicated desire to insert herself into the Krupps' lives.
Robert, meanwhile, is uncomfortable with his wife's fixation and increasingly uneasy as events unfold.

A Body in Bridgeport
The situation darkens considerably when police discover that a man named Programa — a small-time criminal named Ernie Potts — was found shot dead in that same Bridgeport neighborhood on the same night as Karen's accident. A gun is found near the scene, and ballistics eventually tie it to the shooting. The detective assigned to the case, Detective Rasbach (a recurring character from Lapena's first novel, The Couple Next Door), begins to suspect Karen was involved in the killing. She was in the area, she has no alibi, and she cannot — or will not — explain herself.
Karen is eventually charged with murder.

Karen's Secret Past
As the investigation deepens, it emerges that "Karen Krupp" is not who she appears to be. Before she met Tom and built her comfortable suburban life, Karen had a different identity entirely. She was running from something — or someone. She had carefully constructed a new life, covering her tracks, and Tom knows nothing of her real history. Karen's amnesia, whether genuine or performed, becomes her shield. She genuinely cannot remember the night of the accident, but she is also instinctively guarding secrets she has spent years protecting.
Her lawyer works to build a defense, but Karen's inability to explain her presence in Bridgeport — and her resistance to revealing her past — makes the case extremely difficult.

Tom's Wavering Trust
Tom loves Karen deeply and initially stands by her without question. But as anomalies accumulate — her secret past, unexplained absences in her history, the locked drawer in their home he'd never thought to question — his certainty erodes. He begins to wonder whether he ever truly knew his wife. He is portrayed as a fundamentally decent, somewhat passive man who built his happiness on an idealized version of Karen, and now that image is fracturing.
Brigid, sensing Tom's vulnerability, draws closer to him under the guise of neighborly support. Her attentiveness edges toward something more predatory, though she frames it to herself as genuine care.

What Karen Actually Remembers
Karen's memories return in fragments. Slowly, the reader learns what she has been hiding: before becoming "Karen Krupp," she was involved with a deeply dangerous, abusive man — a criminal. She escaped that life violently and has been living in fear of being found. When she learned that this man, now revealed to be Ernie Potts (or connected to him and his world), had tracked her down and was closing in on her, she drove to Bridgeport to confront the threat before it reached her home and her husband.
She went there to protect Tom. She did not want him to know the truth about her past.

The Night Reconstructed
On the night in question, Karen met Ernie Potts. There was a confrontation. A struggle. The gun went off. Potts was killed. Whether it was self-defense or something more deliberate is the crux of the legal and moral tension in the novel. Karen fled in her car, panicked and in shock, and crashed before she could get home. The trauma — both the confrontation and the crash — caused her genuine memory loss, at least initially.
Her amnesia, in other words, was real. But it was also, once partial memories surfaced, something she chose to sustain as a defense.

Brigid's Unraveling
While Karen's story is being reconstructed, Brigid's behavior becomes increasingly erratic and sinister. It emerges that Brigid has been lying to the police — her account of events on the night of the accident was not entirely truthful. More disturbingly, Brigid has a secret of her own that mirrors and complicates Karen's story in a deeply unsettling way.
Brigid, it is revealed, also went to Bridgeport that night. She had been following Karen, driven by obsession. And crucially — Brigid witnessed far more than she told Detective Rasbach.

The True Twist
The novel's central reversal is this: it was not Karen who shot Ernie Potts. When Karen confronted Potts, the situation was dangerous and escalating — but Brigid, who had followed Karen and was watching from nearby, fired the shot that killed him. Whether she did so to protect Karen, or to bind herself to Karen through shared guilt, or out of some darker impulse, is left with psychological ambiguity. Brigid then watched Karen flee and crash, and said nothing.
Brigid has spent the entire novel allowing Karen to be charged with a murder she herself committed, while positioning herself as a concerned neighbor and, gradually, as a comfort to Tom.

Confrontation and Resolution
Karen, as her memories return more fully, comes to understand what actually happened. The tension culminates in a confrontation between the two women. Brigid, cornered by the truth, is ultimately exposed. Detective Rasbach, piecing together the physical and testimonial evidence, arrives at the same conclusion.
Brigid is arrested for the murder of Ernie Potts. Karen's charges are dropped.

Aftermath
Tom learns the full truth of Karen's past — who she was, what she ran from, and why she kept it from him. The marriage is shaken to its foundation. Karen's deception, even though it stemmed from fear and a desire to protect him, represents a fundamental breach of the life they built together. Lapena leaves their future genuinely uncertain — Tom still loves Karen, but love built on an incomplete truth is fragile.
Karen, for her part, is finally free of the secret that has shadowed her for years. Whether that freedom is worth what it cost is the quiet, unresolved question the novel ends on.

Core Themes Running Through the Plot
The novel is structured around the idea that the people closest to us are often strangers in some essential way — that we build intimacy on partial knowledge and projection. Karen is a stranger to Tom because she withheld her past. Tom is a stranger to Karen because she never trusted him with the truth. And Brigid is a stranger to everyone, including herself — her obsession masquerading in her own mind as love and friendship, when it is something far more dangerous.
