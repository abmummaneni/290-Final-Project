--- BACKGROUND AND CONTEXT ---

"Chameleon: The Michigan Plot" is an investigative podcast produced by Campside
Media, hosted by journalists Josh Dean and Witnessed co-host Jaclyn Nastri,
released in 2021. It is the second season of the Chameleon series (following
"Hollywood Con Queen") and covers the 2020 plot to kidnap and potentially
murder Michigan Governor Gretchen Whitmer — one of the most significant
domestic terrorism cases in recent American history, arising directly from
the political polarization and anti-government radicalization accelerated by
the COVID-19 pandemic and its attendant public health restrictions.

The case resulted in federal prosecutions, a controversial series of trials
with mixed outcomes, and profound questions about the role of FBI informants
and undercover agents in domestic terrorism investigations — specifically whether
the FBI's involvement crossed the line from monitoring a genuine plot to
entrapment.

--- THE CONTEXT: MICHIGAN IN 2020 ---

The podcast opens with the specific political context of Michigan in 2020.
Governor Gretchen Whitmer had implemented strict COVID-19 public health
restrictions — stay-at-home orders, business closures, mandatory masking —
that generated intense opposition from a significant portion of the state's
population. Anti-lockdown protests drew large crowds to the state capitol
in Lansing, some of them armed, in direct confrontation with Whitmer's policies.

President Trump's public statements characterizing Whitmer as a tyrant and
using language that critics saw as incitement contributed to the political
atmosphere. The specific combination of anti-government ideology, Second
Amendment absolutism, pandemic frustration, and political polarization created
the conditions from which the kidnapping plot emerged.

--- THE WOLVERINE WATCHMEN AND THE MILITIA NETWORK ---

The plot's origins were in the Michigan militia world — a loose network of
armed groups that had existed in various forms for decades but that in 2020
were experiencing a resurgence of activity and radicalization. The podcast
traces the specific organizations and individuals involved:

The Wolverine Watchmen was a Michigan militia group whose members included
several of the key defendants. The group had been engaging in paramilitary
training, conducting surveillance of government facilities, and discussing
various forms of violent action.

Barry Croft, a Delaware resident, was one of the more ideologically extreme
figures involved — a man with an existing FBI file whose anti-government views
went beyond ordinary political discontent into something that investigators
classified as domestic extremism.

Adam Fox was the operational leader of the Michigan group — a man living in
a vacuum repair shop's basement who became the central figure in the kidnapping
planning.

Brandon Caserta, Ty Garbin, Kaleb Franks, and Daniel Harris were among the
other defendants who faced federal charges.

--- THE PLOT ---

The plan, as it developed through the spring and summer of 2020, was to kidnap
Governor Whitmer. Depending on which participants' accounts are accepted, the
plan variously included taking her to a remote location in Wisconsin and holding
a "trial," blowing up a bridge to impede law enforcement response, and killing
her. The specific intent — kidnapping, political statement, or murder — was
contested throughout the legal proceedings.

The group conducted surveillance of Whitmer's vacation property in northern
Michigan. They purchased tactical equipment and weapons. They conducted
training exercises at remote locations in Michigan and Ohio.

--- THE FBI'S INVOLVEMENT ---

The podcast's most significant and contested contribution is its detailed
examination of how extensively the FBI was involved in the plot's development
and whether that involvement constituted entrapment.

The FBI had multiple informants embedded in the group — at least twelve, as
subsequent reporting established. Key figures in the plot were FBI informants
or undercover agents:

Steve Robeson, the leader of a Wisconsin militia group who connected various
participants, was an FBI informant.

Dan Chappel was an informant who was central to the Michigan group's planning.

An undercover FBI agent known as "Red" played a significant role in the group's
activities.

These informants and agents were not passive observers: they participated in
meetings, drove participants to training exercises, provided logistical support,
and — the defense argued — were actively involved in escalating the plot from
frustrated talk to operational planning.

--- THE ENTRAPMENT DEBATE ---

The central legal and ethical question the podcast explores is whether the FBI's
involvement in the plot crossed the line from legitimate undercover investigation
to entrapment — specifically, whether the agency's informants and agents were
not simply monitoring a genuine plot but were actively helping to create one
by pushing participants who might not have acted independently toward operational
action.

The podcast interviews defense attorneys, legal experts, and people connected
to the case who present the full spectrum of views:

The government's position: the defendants independently conceived of and
planned to kidnap Governor Whitmer, and the FBI's involvement was necessary
to monitor and eventually disrupt a genuine threat to a public official's life.

The defense position: the FBI's informants were essential drivers of the plot,
without whom the various participants — many of whom were alienated, troubled,
or easily manipulated — would never have come close to an operational plan.
The government manufactured the conspiracy it then prosecuted.

--- THE ARRESTS ---

The FBI arrested fourteen people in connection with the plot in October 2020.
Six faced federal charges for the kidnapping conspiracy: Adam Fox, Barry Croft,
Ty Garbin, Kaleb Franks, Brandon Caserta, and Daniel Harris. Eight others
faced state charges for terrorism conspiracy and other offenses.

The arrests were publicly presented as the disruption of a serious domestic
terrorism plot and generated significant national media attention.

--- THE TRIALS AND THEIR MIXED OUTCOMES ---

The federal trials produced results that reflected the genuine legal complexity
of the entrapment question:

Ty Garbin pleaded guilty and cooperated with the government, testifying against
his co-defendants.

Kaleb Franks also pleaded guilty.

The first federal trial, in April 2022, ended with the acquittals of Brandon
Caserta and Daniel Harris — the jury found that the government's case had not
overcome the entrapment defense — and mistrials on the counts against Adam
Fox and Barry Croft, whose juries deadlocked.

Fox and Croft were retried in August 2022 and convicted on all counts. Fox
was sentenced to sixteen years and Croft to nineteen years in federal prison.

The state trials of the Wolverine Watchmen defendants similarly produced
mixed results, with some acquittals and some convictions.

--- THE PODCAST'S ANALYSIS ---

The podcast does not take a simple position on the government's conduct.
It documents the genuine threat that the group represented — people conducting
surveillance of the Governor's home and discussing killing her were not simply
expressing political frustration — while also taking seriously the defense's
evidence that the FBI's informants were more than passive monitors.

The specific question the podcast raises — how much of the operational capacity
of a domestic terrorism plot is created by the government's investigation of
it? — is one without a comfortable answer, and the podcast presents it as
a genuine question for democratic society about the limits of undercover
investigation.

--- SUMMARY ---

"Chameleon: The Michigan Plot" is a thorough and genuinely balanced examination
of one of the most significant domestic terrorism cases of recent years. Its
coverage of the entrapment debate gives the podcast a depth that simple crime
narrative would lack, and the mixed trial outcomes — acquittals alongside
convictions — reflect the genuine legal complexity of a case where the
government's role in the plot's development was more extensive than is
comfortable for a straightforward prosecution narrative. The resolution is
partial: some defendants were convicted, some acquitted, and the underlying
questions about FBI conduct in domestic terrorism cases remain unresolved.
