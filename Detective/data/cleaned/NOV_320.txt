"Broken Harbour," published in 2012, is the fourth novel in Tana French's

--- THE SETTING: BRIANSTOWN ---

Brianstown is a ghost estate — one of the partially built housing developments
that proliferated across Ireland during the Celtic Tiger boom and that were
abandoned in various states of completion when the financial crisis of 2008
ended the construction financing that sustained them. The estate was built on
the Wicklow coast, near the specific location that Scorcher knows — and the
reader gradually learns — as Broken Harbour: a place with a specific personal
significance in his history.

The ghost estate setting is one of the novel's most significant achievements.
It is a landscape of arrested ambition: streets laid out but mostly empty,
show houses that were never sold, infrastructure that was installed and never
used, and a small number of families who bought their houses before the crash
and who are now living in an unfinished estate with no neighbours, no community,
and no realistic prospect of either. The specific quality of the ghost estate —
the uncanny sense of a future that was built and then abandoned — is the
novel's governing atmosphere.

The Spain family lives in one of Brianstown's few occupied houses.

--- THE SPAIN FAMILY ---

Pat Spain and Jenny Spain and their three children — Jack, Emma, and baby
Connor — moved to Brianstown with the specific hopes of the Celtic Tiger's

What the investigation finds:

Jenny Spain is dead. She was stabbed, multiple times, in the kitchen.
The two older children, Jack and Emma, are dead. They were suffocated.
Pat Spain is alive but critically injured — beaten, and left near death.
Baby Connor was unharmed.

The scene at the Spain house is one of the most disturbing in the Dublin Murder
Squad series: a family almost entirely destroyed, in a house in the middle of
a mostly empty estate, with no witnesses and no immediately obvious explanation

--- NOTES ON THE NOVEL ---

"Broken Harbour" is among the finest of the Dublin Murder Squad novels and
one of the most sustained and most politically specific crime novels of the
post-2008 financial crisis period in European fiction. Its specific achievement
is the rendering of the Celtic Tiger's specific human cost — the ghost estate,
the trapped family, the specific pressure of economic catastrophe on a specific
person's specific psychological vulnerability — as both a character study and
a social portrait of real precision. Scorcher Kennedy is one of the series'
most complex narrators: a man whose specific rigour is simultaneously his
greatest professional quality and the mechanism of his personal blindness,
and whose investigation of the Spain case is inseparable from his investigation
of his own history with Broken Harbour.
