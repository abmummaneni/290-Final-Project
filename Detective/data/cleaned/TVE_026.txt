TVE_026 – Columbo: Caution: Murder Can Be Hazardous to Health (continued)

The murder is not a simple hanging. The entire setup is designed to look like a professional suicide:

    Eric, overwhelmed by guilt over a botched operation, writes a note confessing his failure,

    Leaves the hospital,

    Then returns to the empty operating room,

    And hangs himself from the overhead lights or surgical equipment.

The note is emotionally raw and specific, mentioning the failed surgery, the patient’s name, and the guilt he feels. The police are inclined to close the case as a tragic suicide. Columbo, however, notices several inconsistencies right away.

First, the timing of the note. The suicide note is written in a condition that suggests calmness and deliberation, yet the forensic evidence shows that Eric’s hands were under stress when he wrote it—almost as if he was being forced to write under duress. Second, the position of the body does not match the way someone who has just committed suicide would typically hang; the angles and the way the ligature is set suggest the body was placed, not self‑suspended.

Columbo begins to dig into the operation itself. The “failed” surgery was a heart procedure on a wealthy patient whose family now feels aggrieved. The hospital administration is eager to blame the surgeon and quietly close the case, partly to protect the institution from a malpractice suit. The pressure to accept the suicide story is strong, but Columbo is not convinced that the guilt storyline fits Eric’s personality; Eric was known to be arrogant and overconfident, not the kind of man who would suddenly collapse into self‑abnegation.

As Columbo investigates, he uncovers the affair between Joanna and Henry McKinnon. The affair is hidden but not entirely invisible: a misplaced lab coat, a forwarding of personal letters, and a pattern of late‑hour meetings all point toward a secret relationship. The financial motive becomes obvious: if Eric dies, Joanna inherits his practice and estate, and McKinnon gains a powerful ally and a share of the spoils by association.

Columbo then turns to the details of the hanging. He quietly re‑creates the scene in the operating room, examining the lights, the height of the equipment, and the steps required to actually perform the self‑hanging. The logistics don’t line up. To hang himself in the position discovered, Eric would have needed to stand on something that leaves no trace, and the way he was found suggests he was placed there after death. The light‑fixture is too high for a spontaneous act, and the knot in the rope is too tight and precise for someone in a panic.

The final clue is the alcohol level in Eric’s blood. The suicide story implies he went home, drank, and then came back to the hospital. But the tox screen shows a level of alcohol that does not match the timeline of the “drinking and returning.” The drinking and the leaving would have taken longer than the time that actually passed. The only way for the blood‑alcohol level to be that high is if the drinking and the murder happened in a much more compressed window—and that window matches the time when only a few people were in the hospital, including McKinnon and Joanna.

Columbo also notes that the “failed” surgery itself is not as clean as the hospital claims. The operation actually went well; the patient died of complications that are not clearly attributable to surgical error. The family’s anger is real, but the case is not a slam‑dunk malpractice claim. The suicide note exaggerates the failure to make the guilt story more believable.

Bringing this all together, Columbo realizes that the suicide is a two‑step frame‑up:

    McKinnon and Joanna set up the idea that Eric was guilty of malpractice,

    McKinnon then murders Eric in the operating room, stages the hanging,

    And engineers the note so that the hospital will accept the suicide narrative.

In the final scene, Columbo walks through the timing and the logistics. He explains how the body must have been placed there, how the note was written, and how the alcohol levels and the operation’s actual outcome don’t match the official story. He points out that the only people with access to the operating room at the precise time and with a motive to blame the operation are McKinnon and Joanna.

McKinnon, cornered, breaks first. He tries to defend himself by claiming he was only trying to protect the hospital’s reputation, but Columbo points out that the note, the hanging, and the blood‑alcohol levels all point to deliberate murder, not professional guilt. Joanna, realizing that the full truth will come out, refuses to continue the pretense.

Columbo arrests McKinnon for the murder, and Joanna is implicated in the cover‑up. The episode ends with the hospital’s public image intact, but the truth quietly buried: the suicide is exposed as a murder designed to protect the institution from scandal, and the real villain is a surgeon who used his knowledge of the operating room to hide a crime in plain sight.
