TVE_007 | Columbo: Last Salute to the Commodore | Director: Patrick McGoohan | TV Episode
================================================================================
FULL EPISODE SYNOPSIS — SPOILERS INCLUDED
================================================================================

--- BACKGROUND AND CONTEXT ---

"Last Salute to the Commodore" is a Season 5 episode of Columbo, airing
March 2, 1976, and is one of the most unusual and debated episodes in the
entire series. It is directed by Patrick McGoohan — the actor who later became
one of the most celebrated Columbo killers in his acting appearances — here
in the director's chair. The episode is widely noted for its departure from
the standard Columbo formula: unusually for the series, the killer is not
immediately obvious to the viewer, and the episode functions more as a
traditional whodunit than as the inverted mystery for which Columbo is famous.

The episode is also notably more comedic and broad in tone than most Columbo
entries, with physical comedy, an extended and somewhat chaotic investigation
style from Columbo, and a large cast of suspects all of whom behave in
suspicious or eccentric ways. Critical opinion on the episode is divided:
some viewers find the departure from formula fresh and enjoyable; others feel
it does not play to the series' strengths.

--- THE SETTING AND THE VICTIM ---

The setting is the world of yacht racing and the sailing community of the
Southern California coast — a world of wealthy, competitive people who organize
their social lives around their boats and their regattas. The central figure,
the "Commodore" of the title, is Charles Clifton: a crusty, domineering, and
deeply unpleasant patriarch who runs the Clifton family and the Clifton marine
business with iron authority. He is the kind of man who has made enemies of
almost everyone around him through decades of bullying, manipulation, and
absolute insistence on control.

Clifton's family and associates include: his son-in-law Swanny Swanson, who
manages the business and bitterly resents Clifton's treatment of him; his
daughter Joanna, Swanny's wife; his other son-in-law Mac; various business
associates and sailing companions; and Sergeant Kramer, Columbo's partner
who features more prominently than usual in this episode.

Clifton is found dead — drowned, apparently, or killed in a manner that could
initially be attributed to a nautical accident.

--- THE UNUSUAL WHODUNIT FORMAT ---

Because "Last Salute to the Commodore" does not follow the standard inverted
mystery format, the viewer does not know at the outset who killed Clifton.
This is the episode's primary structural departure. Multiple characters had
reason to want Clifton dead — his business practices, his family tyranny, and
his specific treatment of the people around him have generated genuine hatred
in several directions.

The suspects are:

Swanny Swanson: the most obvious candidate. He has endured years of humiliation
from Clifton and has the most direct access and the clearest motive. He is
also the most overtly anxious figure in the episode.

Joanna: Clifton's daughter, who may have her own reasons for wanting her
father gone beyond simply defending her husband.

Mac: the other son-in-law, who also has a specific grievance.

Various others connected to the sailing world and the Clifton business.

--- COLUMBO'S INVESTIGATION ---

Columbo's investigation in this episode is notably broader and more chaotic
than usual — he seems to be everywhere at once, talking to everyone, appearing
to follow dead ends, and projecting an even more extreme version of his
usual apparent disorganization. McGoohan's direction leans into the physical
comedy of Columbo's eccentricities: the rumpled raincoat, the cigar, the
car, the perpetually forgotten "just one more thing" are all present and
somewhat amplified.

Columbo investigates the yacht, the marina, the Clifton home, and the business.
He talks to all the suspects multiple times. He pays particular attention to
the physical evidence of how Clifton died: the specific circumstances of a
death in water, what the body's position and condition indicate about how the
death occurred, and the specific features of the yacht that are relevant to
reconstructing the night.

He also pays close attention to the relationships within the family and
business — the specific texture of who hated whom and to what degree, which
grudges were longstanding and which were more recent, and whose behavior
in the aftermath of the death is most inconsistent with genuine grief or
shock.

--- THE KILLER: SWANNY SWANSON ---

The resolution of the episode reveals that Swanny Swanson killed the Commodore.
His motive — years of accumulated resentment and a specific immediate trigger
connected to the business or the family situation — is confirmed through
Columbo's investigation. The specific evidence that establishes Swanny's guilt
rather than any of the other suspects is assembled from the physical evidence
of the yacht and from inconsistencies in the accounts of the night.

The revelation scene is handled in the episode's characteristic broad-comedic
tone — not a quiet, devastating confrontation in the Cassidy-killer manner
but something louder and more farcical, consistent with the episode's overall
register.

--- A SECOND MURDER ---

The episode includes a second killing — someone who saw too much or knew too
much about Clifton's death and had to be silenced. This second murder, committed
to protect the original scheme, is standard Columbo episode structure but is
used here partly to narrow the field of suspects and partly to demonstrate that
the original killer is capable of further violence.

--- RESOLUTION ---

Swanny Swanson is identified and arrested. The maritime world of the Clifton
family is left to deal with the consequences of the patriarch's death and the
exposure of his killer.

--- NOTES ON THE EPISODE ---

"Last Salute to the Commodore" is a genuine curiosity in the Columbo canon.
Its whodunit format breaks the series' fundamental promise to the viewer —
you know who did it, and the pleasure is watching Columbo prove it — and
replaces it with a more traditional mystery dynamic that many fans feel the
show is less well suited to. The comedic tone is more extreme than usual.
McGoohan as director is clearly having fun, and the episode has its defenders
who value it precisely for its eccentricity. It is, however, generally ranked
among the weaker episodes of the classic era, and the departure from formula
is widely cited as the primary reason.
