--- BACKGROUND AND SETTING ---

"The Keepsake," published in 2008, is the seventh novel in Tess Gerritsen's
Rizzoli & Isles series. It is one of the series' most distinctive entries
because its central horror is rooted not in conventional criminal violence but
in the world of archaeology and natural history museums — the preservation and
display of human remains, the ethics of collecting, and the history of how
human bodies have been treated as objects of curiosity or scientific study
across centuries. The novel draws on the real history of mummies, curiosity
cabinets, and the long tradition of displaying human remains in Western
museums.

Jane Rizzoli and Maura Isles are both central to this novel, and their
partnership — by now fully established as one of crime fiction's strongest
female pairings — is given full expression.

--- THE MUSEUM DISCOVERY ---

The novel opens at a Boston natural history museum where a collection of
artifacts and specimens is being catalogued and assessed. Among the items in
storage — the vast proportion of any museum's collection that is not on display —
a mummified human figure is discovered. This in itself is not remarkable; museums
routinely hold mummified remains from various cultures and periods.

What is remarkable is what further examination reveals: the mummy is not ancient.
It is recent. The mummification is not the result of natural processes or the
deliberate preparation practices of an ancient culture but of modern artificial
preservation — someone has mummified a recently killed person and stored the
body in the museum's collection, apparently for years.

The body is that of a young woman. She has been dead for some time, but the
cause of death was not natural — she was murdered. She has been preserved and
stored in a way that suggests both a sophisticated knowledge of mummification
techniques and a particular psychological relationship to the act of keeping
the body.

Maura Isles performs the forensic examination and identification process.
The challenge of working with a mummified body — extracting evidence of cause
of death, establishing identity, determining time of death — requires unusual
methods and expertise.

--- THE COLLECTOR ---

The investigation establishes that the mummified woman is not the only preserved
body connected to a specific perpetrator. Other bodies are found — or the
evidence of other bodies is found — suggesting that the killer has a pattern:
he (or she) kills, preserves the victims through a form of mummification, and
keeps them. The bodies are "keepsakes" — trophies maintained in a very specific
physical form.

The psychological profile that emerges from this pattern is disturbing in its
specificity. This is not a killer who kills and disposes of victims. This is a
killer who kills and keeps — who has a relationship with the bodies after death,
who is invested in their preservation and continued existence. The mummification
is not concealment but a form of continued possession.

The museum connection is key: the killer has specific knowledge of mummification
techniques, possibly professional or academic, and has used the museum's storage
system — deliberately or with inside access — as a hiding place for one of the
preserved bodies.

--- THE ARCHAEOLOGICAL WORLD ---

The investigation draws Rizzoli and Isles into the world of Boston-area
archaeology and museum studies — a community of scholars, curators, and
collectors who deal professionally with human remains and who have the specific
knowledge that the killer appears to possess.

A character named Josephine Pulcillo — a museum archaeologist — becomes central
to the investigation. She is a professional whose work involves the study and
handling of human remains, and her expertise is both valuable to the
investigation and potentially significant in establishing who among her
professional circle might have the knowledge and psychological profile consistent
with the killer.

The investigation also extends to the world of private collectors — people who
collect curiosities and unusual objects, including preserved human remains, in
the tradition of the old "Wunderkammern" or wonder cabinets of the early modern
period. This history of collecting human bodies and biological specimens as
objects of fascination and display provides context for the killer's psychology:
they are, in a distorted way, participating in a long human tradition of treating
the dead as collectibles.

--- JOSEPHINE PULCILLO IN DANGER ---

As the investigation progresses, it becomes clear that Josephine Pulcillo is
in danger. The killer's interest in her is not simply professional — she has
become, for whatever reason rooted in the killer's psychology, the next intended
keepsake. The threat to her escalates as Rizzoli and Isles close in on the
killer's identity.

The race to identify the killer before Josephine becomes another preserved
body is the novel's central source of tension in its second half. Rizzoli's
determination to prevent another victim from being added to the collection
drives the investigation forward with increasing urgency.

--- THE KILLER'S IDENTITY AND HISTORY ---

The killer is identified from among the people connected to the museum world
and the archaeological community. Their specific knowledge of mummification
techniques, their access to the museum's storage systems, and their psychological
relationship to the collected bodies are all consistent with a specific person
in the community.

The motive is rooted in the killer's personal history — a history that involves
loss, obsessive attachment, and the specific displacement of those feelings onto
the act of preserving bodies. The killer is not simply a sadist but a deeply
disturbed person whose pathology has a comprehensible (if horrifying) structure.

--- RESOLUTION ---

Josephine Pulcillo is found and protected before she can become the killer's
next keepsake. The killer is identified, confronted, and arrested. The preserved
bodies are recovered and their identities established, allowing their families
whatever resolution is possible given the circumstances.

The museum's storage system is implicated in how the crime was concealed for
so long — the sheer volume of objects in museum storage, most never examined,
provides a hiding place that was almost perfectly opaque.

--- MAURA ISLES'S THREAD ---

Maura Isles's personal life continues to develop in this novel. Her relationship
with Father Brophy — the priest with whom she has been emotionally entangled
across several books — remains unresolved and painful. The specific case of a
woman who was possessed (in a literal sense — kept, preserved, owned) after
death resonates with Isles's own investigation of what it means to be
possessed by a relationship that cannot be fully expressed.

Gerritsen consistently uses the forensic cases of the Rizzoli & Isles series
to reflect thematically on the personal situations of her main characters, and
"The Keepsake" is a strong example of this technique.
