--- BACKGROUND AND CONTEXT ---

"Line of Duty" Series 5 was broadcast on BBC One in March–April 2019. It
consists of six episodes directed by John Strickland and written by Jed Mercurio,
and stars Adrian Dunbar as Superintendent Ted Hastings, Vicky McClure as DS
Kate Fleming, and Martin Compston as DS Steve Arnott, with Stephen Graham
joining as the season's central subject, DS John Corbett.

Series 5 is the most complex and most emotionally demanding series in the show's
run to that point — a season in which the boundaries between the corrupt and
the uncorrupt are drawn more ambiguously than ever before, in which the "H"
mythology reaches its most significant development, and in which the specific
moral cost of undercover policing is explored with the directness and the
seriousness it deserves.

The season is also notable for the sustained question it raises about Ted
Hastings himself — a question that is managed across six episodes with
extraordinary skill, placing the show's most trusted central character under
genuine suspicion for the first time.

--- JOHN CORBETT AND OPERATION PEAR TREE ---

DS John Corbett is an undercover officer deployed within the OCG — the organised
crime group that has been central to the show's mythology since Series 2. He
has been deep undercover for an extended period, embedded within the OCG at
a high level, and his specific operational intelligence is potentially the most
valuable the operation has generated.

Stephen Graham's Corbett is one of the show's finest creations: a man of
genuine courage and genuine capability who is also, after an extended period
of deep cover work, showing the specific psychological damage that sustained
undercover deployment produces. The line between the role and the person has
blurred in specific ways. He has done things in the character of his cover
identity that he would not have done as a police officer, and the specific cost
of that — the specific things he has participated in, witnessed, and sometimes
facilitated — is present in him in ways that the performance makes visible
without melodrama.

Corbett is conducting his own investigation of "H" — the senior corrupt officer —
from inside the OCG. He believes he has identified who H is, and the specific
intelligence he is trying to pass back to the legitimate investigation is the
season's central operational objective.

Series 5 engages more directly than any previous season with the specific ethics

The most significant development in Series 5 is Corbett's specific suspicion
that Ted Hastings is H. He has assembled, from inside the OCG, a picture of
the senior corrupt officer that points toward Hastings — the specific

This suspicion — and Corbett's specific efforts to gather evidence against

centre has been corrupt all along — is managed with the care of a production

The season ends with the "H" question significantly advanced but not resolved —

--- NOTES ON THE SERIES ---

Series 5 of Line of Duty is the show at its most narratively daring. The
sustained question of Hastings's guilt — managed across six episodes without
collapse into either obvious innocence or obvious guilt — represents a level
of trust in the audience and a level of commitment to genuine ambiguity that
is rare in popular television. Stephen Graham's Corbett is the series' finest
single-season creation: a performance of extraordinary depth that makes the
specific cost of undercover policing entirely real. And the series' specific
achievement — maintaining dramatic credibility while putting the show's own
moral centre under genuine suspicion — is one of British television drama's
more impressive tightrope walks.
