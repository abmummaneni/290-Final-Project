--- BACKGROUND AND CONTEXT ---

"In Pursuit of the Proper Sinner," published in 1999, is the tenth novel in
Elizabeth George's Inspector Lynley series. It is one of the longest, most
complex, and most morally demanding novels in the series — a book whose specific
title refers not simply to the investigation's subject but to the sustained
difficulty of establishing exactly who is the "proper sinner" in a case where
culpability is distributed across multiple people in multiple ways, and where
the investigation's conclusion is considerably more troubling than a simple
identification of a single guilty party.

The novel involves two murders on the Derbyshire moors — two bodies found in
a single location, two different victims, killed in different ways at different
times — and the specific investigation of those two deaths generates a portrait
of the interconnected lives of the people responsible that is one of George's
most sustained and most psychologically complex achievements.

Thomas Lynley is the lead investigator, with Barbara Havers as his sergeant.
Their working relationship in this novel is under specific strain — events in
previous novels have created specific tensions between them that the investigation
must navigate alongside the case's specific demands.

--- THE TWO VICTIMS ---

The first victim found on the moor is TERRY COLE — a young London man who was
camping on the Derbyshire moor with a woman. He has been savagely beaten to
death. He is not the primary intended victim; he seems to have been in the
wrong place at the wrong time in a specific and devastating sense.

The second victim is NICOLA MAIDEN — a young woman, the daughter of Andy Maiden,
a retired detective who once worked with Lynley. She is found nearby, dead from
a single stab wound. She and Terry Cole were together on the moor; they were
apparently killed separately, possibly by different people, possibly at different
times.

The discovery of two victims — a woman from a police family and a young man
whose connection to her is initially unclear — generates the investigation's
primary complexity. Why were they together on the moor? What was Nicola Maiden's
real life, which her retired-detective father manifestly did not fully know?
And why was Terry Cole killed, when the specific brutality of his death seems
disproportionate even by the standards of the violence that killed Nicola?

--- NICOLA MAIDEN'S REAL LIFE ---

The investigation of Nicola Maiden produces one of the novel's most sustained
and most uncomfortable revelations: that the young woman her father believed
he knew — a law student with ordinary friends and ordinary ambitions — was
in fact living a considerably more complex and more dangerous life than he
understood.

Nicola had been working as a prostitute — high-end, client-specific, operating
through a specific network that catered to wealthy and sometimes powerful clients.
Her specific clientele, and the specific relationships she had developed with
specific clients, are directly relevant to the circumstances of her death.

The investigation of Nicola's professional life requires Lynley and Havers
to navigate the specific world of high-end sex work in a way that the investigation
cannot avoid: who were her clients, what specific relationships did she have
with them, and what in those relationships could have generated the motivation
for her killing?

--- ANDY MAIDEN AND HIS KNOWLEDGE ---

Andy Maiden — Nicola's father, the retired detective — is one of the novel's
most complex figures. His specific reaction to the investigation of his daughter's
death is not simply the grief of a bereaved parent: it is the specific grief
of someone who is discovering, during the investigation, that he did not know
his child. His specific management of what he learns — what he discloses to
the investigation and what he conceals — is one of the investigation's most
sustained and most morally demanding threads.

Andy knows things. He knew things before the investigation began that were
relevant to Nicola's death, and his specific choices about what to do with
that knowledge — made from a combination of grief, protective instinct, and
the specific perspective of a former detective who understands what investigations
do to the families they move through — are directly connected to the resolution.

--- TERRY COLE'S DEATH: THE SEPARATE KILLER ---

Terry Cole's death is investigated simultaneously with Nicola's, and the
investigation gradually establishes that the two killings were committed by
different people.

Julian Britton's specific motive for killing Terry Cole is rooted in a specific

Julian Britton is not a calculating villain: he is a person who committed
a specific act of violence in specific circumstances, and the investigation's

--- NICOLA MAIDEN'S DEATH: THE PROPER SINNER ---

The investigation of Nicola's death — the single stab wound, the more controlled
quality of the killing compared to the ferocity visited on Terry Cole —
establishes a different picture from Terry's murder.

The specific person who killed Nicola Maiden is her father, ANDY MAIDEN.

The specific circumstances of Andy's killing of Nicola are the novel's most
devastating revelation. He knew about Nicola's professional life — had discovered
the specific nature of her sex work — and his specific response to that knowledge
was not simply the grief of a father but something more complex and more
terrible: a specific judgment, made from within the specific framework of a

This is not a spontaneous act of passion in the conventional sense. It is the
specific outcome of a specific set of beliefs about honour, about shame, about
what a daughter's conduct means for a father — beliefs that are rendered in
the novel with the full weight of their genuine human roots rather than simply
as a convenient explanation for an extreme act.

The "proper sinner" of the title is Andy Maiden. The investigation's long
pursuit of who killed Nicola arrives, finally, at the person who was present

--- THE MORAL WEIGHT OF THE RESOLUTION ---

The resolution of "In Pursuit of the Proper Sinner" is among the most morally
complex in the George series. Two killers are identified — Julian Britton for
Terry Cole, Andy Maiden for Nicola. The legal processes that follow are the
conventional consequence.

But the title's specific question — who is the proper sinner? — is not simply
answered by the identification of the two killers. Andy Maiden killed his
daughter from within a specific moral framework that produced his act; Nicola
Maiden lived a specific life that she kept from her father for specific reasons;
Terry Cole was killed by Julian Britton for reasons that are comprehensible
if not excusable. The distribution of culpability across the novel's full cast —
who bears responsibility for what, and in what proportion — is genuinely
complex, and the novel does not resolve it into simplicity.

--- NOTES ON THE NOVEL ---

"In Pursuit of the Proper Sinner" is one of the most ambitious and most fully
realised novels in the George series. Its specific achievement is the sustained
management of a double murder investigation whose two separate killers and two
separate victims create a moral landscape of genuine complexity. The revelation
of Andy Maiden as Nicola's killer is one of the most devastating moments in
the series — not a surprise twist but the conclusion of a sustained, patient
investigation of a specific human situation. George's willingness to take her
characters to the furthest possible reaches of what specific people are capable
of, while maintaining the full weight of their humanity, is the quality that
distinguishes the Lynley series at its best.
