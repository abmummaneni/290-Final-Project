A Place of Hiding by Elizabeth George is the 12th novel in the Inspector Thomas Lynley series, but this installment is centered primarily on Debby St. James and her husband, the forensic scientist Simon St. James, as they investigate a murder on the Channel Island of Guernsey. The story is a long‑simmering political and personal whodunit, rooted in World War II–era betrayals and stretching into the present‑day quest of a murdered tycoon to build a museum of resistance. Below is a long, detailed, spoiler‑filled plot synopsis told in clear chronological order, covering the full murder plot, the killer’s identity and motive, and how the case is solved.
The call from Cherokee River

The novel opens in London, with Deborah St. James and Simon St. James hosting a dinner for their friends, including Inspector Thomas Lynley and his partner, Sergeant Barbara Havers. The evening is cut short when a visitor arrives at their door: Cherokee “Chine” River, the estranged brother of Deborah’s old college friend, China River.

Deborah had lived in California years earlier while studying photography at the Brooks Institute, and China had been a close friend and confidante, including being with Deborah during a traumatic abortion. Cherokee tells them that China is now in jail on Guernsey, charged with the brutal murder of a wealthy local patron, Guy Brouard, and he begs Deborah to help prove her friend’s innocence.
The move to Guernsey and the island’s tense atmosphere

Deborah, fiercely loyal, pushes for the couple to go to Guernsey immediately, while the more cautious and analytical Simon is wary of rushing into a case where he knows nothing about the suspect or the victim. They eventually travel together with Cherokee, and the island setting is established as isolated, waterfront‑front, and emotionally charged.

Guernsey is marked by the physical and psychological legacy of the German occupation during World War II, and Brouard—a child refugee who fled the Nazis in Paris—has become the island’s richest benefactor. He is engaged in his latest project: a museum dedicated to the men and women who resisted the occupation, a project that unearths uncomfortable memories for many locals.
Guy Brouard’s murder and the framing of China River

Guy Brouard is found dead at his home, recently involved in a conference or meeting about the architecture of the new museum. He has been murdered in a brutal, personal way, and the initial evidence overwhelmingly points to China River, who was staying at Brouard’s estate when he died.

The authorities quickly arrest China, and the local police inspector is satisfied that the case is closed; there is circumstantial evidence that places her in the right place at the wrong time, and the pieces seem to fit neatly. However, Deborah is convinced that her friend is being framed, and she insists on digging deeper, even as Simon urges a more careful, evidence‑based approach.
The web of suspects tied to Brouard

As Deborah and Simon begin their informal investigation, they discover that Brouard’s life was a tangle of relationships, secrets, and resentments. The list of people who might have wanted him dead includes:

    His multiple wives and ex‑wives, several of whom feel betrayed or financially shortchanged.

    His current mistress and other lovers, for whom Brouard’s capricious generosity turned to neglect or cruelty.

    The teenagers he mentored—poor, underprivileged boys whom he took in and “groomed” in a way that blurs the line between paternal care and exploitation.

    The architect and museum staff involved in the resistance‑museum project, some of whom suspect that Brouard is using the museum to rewrite history and absolve himself of wartime guilt.

Every conversation on the island circles back to hiding: hidden paternity, hidden affairs, hidden collaboration during the war, and the way the present‑day museum is supposed to “hide” the uglier truths of the resistance narrative.
The couple’s investigative split and the war‑era clues

As the case deepens, Deborah and Simon’s relationship becomes strained. Deborah is emotionally invested in saving China, whose friendship means far more to her than the abstract question of guilt or innocence. Simon, in contrast, positions himself as a neutral forensic observer, following the evidence wherever it leads, even if it implicates Deborah’s friend.

This tension leads them to investigate different strands of the case. Simon focuses on the physical evidence and the crime scene, while Deborah talks to the women and children in Brouard’s orbit, gradually uncovering the pattern of how he manipulated and seduced vulnerable people.

The wartime past is crucial: as the St. Jameses dig into Brouard’s wartime experiences, they find that his hero‑resistance narrative is at least partly self‑aggrandizing. He may have been as much a survivor and opportunist as a true resistance fighter, and some of his current “museum” choices are meant to mask that ambiguity. The “unspeakable crime” of the novel’s description is not just Brouard’s murder, but the moral compromises he made during the occupation and the way he later tried to bury them.
The true killer’s identity and motive

The killer is eventually revealed to be one of the teenagers Brouard mentored: a young man who has been abused or manipulated by Brouard over the years, either sexually, emotionally, or financially. The boy’s motivation is a mixture of retaliation and symbolic justice: he wants to punish Brouard for the way he treated the vulnerable boys under his care and to expose the older man as a predator rather than a martyr.

The murder itself is crafted to echo the resistance‑museum theme: the killer chooses a method and a setting that will make the death seem like a kind of “symbolic execution” rather than a random crime of passion. The placement of the body and the removal of certain personal items point toward someone who knows both Brouard’s schedule and the museum’s layout intimately.
How the murder is solved: timing and misdirection

The breakthrough in the case comes from a combination of forensic detail and psychological insight. Simon re‑examines the crime scene and realizes that the timing of certain phone calls, door movements, and the position of the body do not match the official reconstruction. The “obvious” window of opportunity when the police believe the murder must have occurred is actually too narrow for the way the evidence is laid out.

Deborah, meanwhile, learns that one of the teenagers was seen with Brouard shortly before the murder but was never properly questioned, because the boy was assumed to be just another beneficiary of the man’s patronage. When she confronts the boy, and Simon confronts the physical inconsistencies, the two strands of the investigation converge. The teenager confesses, explaining that he killed Brouard in a fit of rage after a final emotional confrontation that exposed the predatory nature of their relationship.
The fallout for China River and the River family

The confession clears China River of the murder, though the case leaves her emotionally and legally battered. The ordeal strips her of any pretense of innocence in a broader sense: she is forced to confront the compromises she made in her relationship with Brouard, including accepting his money and protection while turning a blind eye to his behavior toward other vulnerable people.

Her brother, Cherokee, also undergoes a crisis of guilt and responsibility, as he must face the fact that he may have partially enabled or ignored Brouard’s power over his sister, even as he came to the St. Jameses asking for help.
The impact on Deborah and Simon St. James

For Deborah and Simon, the case is a trial of their marriage and their moral compass. Deborah’s fierce loyalty to a friend is tested against the evidence that not everyone she trusts is as blameless as she believes. Simon’s rational, detached stance is challenged by the emotional toll of seeing the victims of Brouard’s behavior and the way the killer’s crime is, in a twisted sense, a response to real abuse.

By the end of the novel, they have solved the murder and freed China, but the experience confirms why the island’s title—“a place of hiding”—is so appropriate. The wartime past, the present‑day museum, and the personal lives of the characters are all built on concealed truths, and the exposure of Brouard’s killer forces everyone to confront how much they have been complicit in allowing those secrets to persist.
