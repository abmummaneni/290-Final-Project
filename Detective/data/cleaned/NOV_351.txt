THE NIGHT OF THE MASSACRE


Kansas, 1985

On the night of January 2, 1985, three members of the Day family were killed in their
farmhouse in rural Kansas: Patty Day, the mother, and two of her daughters, Michelle and
Debby. The only survivors were seven-year-old Libby Day, who escaped through a window into
the frozen night, and her fifteen-year-old brother Ben.

Based largely on Libby's testimony — the word of a traumatized seven-year-old — Ben Day was
convicted of the murders. The trial became a national sensation, partly fueled by theories
of Satanic ritual abuse that were sweeping America in the mid-1980s. Ben has been in prison
ever since.

LIBBY IN THE PRESENT


A Woman Shaped by Disaster

Twenty-five years later, Libby Day is in her early thirties and has built almost nothing.
She is sharp-tongued, deeply unlikable by her own assessment, and has survived primarily
on donations from sympathetic strangers who sent money to the orphaned girl with the tragic
story. That money is nearly gone. She has no job, no relationship, no direction. She is
not so much damaged as hollowed out — a person for whom the night of January 2nd is not a
memory but the entire context of existence.

She believes Ben killed her family. She has always believed it. She testified to it as a
child and has never publicly questioned it.


The Kill Club

Libby is approached by a man named Lyle Wirth, who belongs to an organization called the
Kill Club — a group of true-crime obsessives who investigate famous murder cases, some of
whom believe convicted killers are innocent. The Kill Club has a significant number of
members devoted to the Day family case, and the dominant theory among them is that Ben is
innocent and the real killer has never been found.

Libby is contemptuous and mercenary about this. She agrees to participate — to meet with
Kill Club members, to look into the case, to even visit Ben in prison — not because she
believes any of it, but because they will pay her for access and information. She needs
the money.

THE INVESTIGATION: PRESENT AND PAST


Dual Timeline

The novel alternates between Libby's present-day investigation and chapters set on January 2,
1985 — the last day of the Day family, narrated from the perspectives of Patty Day and Ben
Day. This structure means the reader accumulates context for what actually happened that
night as Libby accumulates it through her investigation, with the 1985 chapters providing
a more truthful account than the one Libby has always believed.


Patty Day in 1985

Patty is desperately poor, behind on the farm mortgage, and about to lose everything. She
is a good woman in an impossible situation — a single mother of four children with no
resources and no obvious way out. She is exploring an option that is both terrible and
understandable: a plan to have herself killed for the life insurance money, which would
save the farm and provide for her children.

She has made contact with a man who will arrange it. The plan is proceeding. She does not
fully understand all of the forces she has set in motion.


Ben Day in 1985

Ben is fifteen, troubled, and deeply under the influence of a girl named Diondra — older,
manipulative, and involved with a group of teenagers who have dabbled in Satanic imagery
and ritual, largely as teenage rebellion. Diondra is pregnant with Ben's child, something
she has told Ben but no one else. She is frightened and controlling about it.

Ben is also being accused at school of molesting a young girl — an accusation that is false
but that has been made by a troubled student and is gaining traction. The Satanic abuse
hysteria of the era is already attaching itself to Ben before the murders even happen.

On the night of January 2nd, Ben was not home for most of the evening. He was with Diondra.


The Actual Killer

Through Libby's investigation and the accumulating 1985 chapters, the truth emerges
piece by piece. Patty's plan to be killed for the insurance money involved a man named
Runner Day — the children's absent, alcoholic, abusive father — and his associates. The
man Patty hired was connected to Runner's world.

The killer who came to the farmhouse that night was a man named Clint — hired through
that chain of contacts. He killed Patty as arranged. But Michelle, the older daughter,
witnessed something and was killed to eliminate the witness. Debby was killed for the
same reason.

The murders were not Satanic ritual. They were a tragic, botched, cascading consequence of
a desperate mother's last attempt to save her children from poverty.


Libby's False Testimony

Seven-year-old Libby did not consciously lie. But she was a traumatized child, coached
(not maliciously, but recklessly) by investigators who were already committed to the
Satanic abuse narrative, and she told the story she was led toward. She genuinely
believed at the time that she had seen Ben. She was wrong, or her memory of that night
was shaped by suggestion and terror into something that fit the narrative the adults
around her needed.

DIONDRA AND THE MISSING CHILD

One of the novel's most chilling threads involves what happened to Diondra's baby. Ben
protected Diondra throughout his arrest, trial, and imprisonment — refusing to name her,
refusing to establish that he was with her that night and therefore had an alibi, because
doing so would expose her pregnancy and destroy her. He sacrificed his own freedom to
protect her and the child.

Diondra, it emerges, did not reciprocate this loyalty. She had the baby — a girl — and
raised her under assumed circumstances. The baby grew up. She is an adult now, living in
Libby's present. Finding her is one of the investigation's most urgent threads, because
she can place Ben elsewhere on the night of the murders.

RESOLUTION

Libby finds Diondra and the grown daughter, Crystal. Diondra, after twenty-five years,
is still dangerous — she is not a person who faces consequences willingly. The confrontation
with Diondra is physically dangerous for Libby.

The truth is established: Ben is innocent. He did not kill his family. He was with Diondra.
The actual chain of events — Patty's insurance plan, Runner's associates, the hired killer —
is fully reconstructed.

Ben's exoneration process begins. After twenty-five years in prison for murders he did not
commit, the path to his freedom opens.

Libby is changed by the investigation in ways she does not fully have language for. She
spent her entire adult life believing her brother was a monster. He was not. The guilt of
her false testimony — however innocent its origins — is something she will carry. But she
is also, for the first time in decades, a person who did something real, who pursued truth
at personal risk, and who got it right.
