FULL EPISODE SYNOPSIS — SPOILERS INCLUDED

--- BACKGROUND AND CONTEXT ---

"A Murder Is Announced" is a two-part BBC Miss Marple adaptation first broadcast
in February 1985, directed by John Madden (later the director of "Shakespeare
in Love"). It stars Joan Hickson as Miss Marple and is based on Christie's
1950 novel of the same name — widely considered one of the best Marple novels
and one of Christie's most inventive plots. The adaptation is faithful and
atmospheric, capturing the specific postwar England of the original: rationing,
social change, the dissolution of old certainties, and the particular vulnerability
of people who are not quite who they claim to be.

--- THE ADVERTISEMENT ---

The mystery begins with an extraordinary event in the village of Chipping
Cleghorn: a notice appears in the local gazette, "The North Benham News and
Chipping Cleghorn Gazette," announcing that "A murder is announced and will
take place on Friday, October 29th, at Little Paddocks at 6:30 p.m." The notice
is signed with the address of Little Paddocks, the home of one Letitia Blacklock.

The village is fascinated and amused. Most assume it is some kind of parlor
game — a mystery evening arranged for entertainment. At the appointed time,
several of Letitia Blacklock's neighbors and acquaintances find themselves at
Little Paddocks, curiosity drawing them there.

The lights go out at exactly 6:30. A figure appears in the doorway holding
a torch and a gun, demanding to know where Letitia Blacklock is. Before anyone
fully understands what is happening, shots are fired — and when the lights come
back on, the gunman is dead on the floor, and Letitia Blacklock has a small
wound on her ear.

The dead man is eventually identified as a small-time criminal, a foreigner
of uncertain provenance. Nobody in the village knows him.

--- THE HOUSEHOLD AT LITTLE PADDOCKS ---

Letitia Blacklock lives at Little Paddocks with a small household:

Dora Bunner ("Bunny") — Letitia's old school friend, now dependent on her,
a sweet and somewhat vague woman.

Mitzi — a foreign refugee who works as cook and housekeeper, highly strung
and prone to dramatic declarations.

Patrick and Julia Simmons — young people who claim to be Letitia's cousins
and who have been living with her.

Phillipa Haymes — a young woman who works as a gardener at a nearby property
and has a room at Little Paddocks.

--- THE INHERITANCE CONTEXT ---

Inspector Craddock, the detective assigned to the case, investigates the dead
gunman's identity and his possible connection to Letitia Blacklock. The
investigation reveals a specific financial context that becomes central to
the mystery.

Letitia Blacklock's late employer and close associate, Randall Goedler, left
his fortune to his wife Belle, with the provision that if Belle died without
children, the entire fortune would pass to Letitia Blacklock. Belle Goedler
is elderly, very ill, and expected to die soon. When she dies, Letitia stands
to inherit an enormous sum.

Anyone who kills Letitia before Belle Goedler dies would therefore prevent
Letitia from inheriting. The inheritance motive is clear: someone wants Letitia
dead before she can receive the Goedler fortune.

--- MISS MARPLE ARRIVES ---

Miss Marple comes to Chipping Cleghorn to visit her old friend Dolly Bantry
(whose husband Arthur Bantry recently died), who now lives nearby. She hears
about the murder announcement and its aftermath with great interest and inserts
herself into the investigation with her characteristic gentle persistence.

Her specific role in this episode, as in most of the BBC adaptations, is to
be the person who sees what the official investigation misses — the human
truth of the situation rather than the forensic or procedural truth.

--- DORA BUNNER'S DEATH ---

The investigation becomes more urgent when Dora Bunner dies. Bunny is found
dead — apparently from an overdose of sleeping tablets, officially attributed
to accident or suicide. But Bunny, in the days before her death, had been
saying things — half-remembered observations about the night of the announced
murder, small comments about the people at Little Paddocks — that were
potentially significant. Miss Marple had been listening carefully to Bunny,
and she is certain that Bunny's death was murder: she was killed because she
knew something, or was about to remember something, that would expose the truth.

Bunny's death is the emotional core of the episode. She was a gentle, harmless
woman. Her murder for what she accidentally knew or might say is one of the
more cold-blooded acts in the Marple canon.

--- THE IDENTITY QUESTION ---

Miss Marple's investigation leads her to the central question of the entire
case: who is Letitia Blacklock?

The real Letitia Blacklock is not the woman living at Little Paddocks. The
woman who calls herself Letitia is actually Charlotte Blacklock — Letitia's
younger sister, who was always overshadowed by her more capable sibling.
The real Letitia died some years ago, under circumstances Charlotte managed
to conceal. Charlotte assumed her sister's identity and her position, and has
been living as "Letitia" ever since.

This substitution is the key to everything. Charlotte — as "Letitia" — stands
to inherit the Goedler fortune. But if anyone who knew the real Letitia well
recognized that Charlotte was not her, the entire scheme would collapse. The
person Charlotte was most afraid of was someone from their shared past who
might see through the impersonation.

--- THE GUNMAN AND THE PLAN ---

The gunman who appeared at Little Paddocks on the night of the announced murder
was hired by Charlotte herself — she arranged the whole event. The purpose
was not to kill "Letitia Blacklock" but to kill a specific person who was present
in the room that night: someone who knew the real Letitia and who could expose
Charlotte's false identity.

The darkness, the confusion, the gunshots — all of it was designed to create
chaos in which a specific death could occur and be attributed to the gunman.
The gunman himself was not supposed to survive: he was killed by Charlotte to
prevent him from identifying who had hired him.

Charlotte's own wound — the graze on her ear — was part of the staging, to
make her appear to be the intended victim rather than the author of the evening's
violence.

--- THE SPECIFIC TARGET ---

The person Charlotte arranged to have killed in the darkness — the person the
gunman's gun was actually aimed at — was someone present in the room who knew
the real Letitia. The specific identity of this person and their relationship
to the Blacklock sisters is the thread that Inspector Craddock and Miss Marple
follow to the truth.

Miss Marple's recognition of Charlotte's false identity comes through the
specific observation that only the real Marple method produces: small behavioral
details — things Charlotte says or does that are slightly inconsistent with
the life history "Letitia" is supposed to have had, things Bunny said before
she died, things about the way Charlotte responds to certain names or references
that tell Miss Marple that she is not who she claims.

--- RESOLUTION ---

Charlotte Blacklock — living as Letitia — is exposed and arrested for the
murders of the gunman, Dora Bunner, and the person killed in the original
darkness. The full scheme — the assumed identity, the inheritance plan, the
arranged murder disguised as an announced entertainment — is laid bare.

Inspector Craddock's formal investigation and Miss Marple's observational
analysis together produce the evidence needed. The Goedler fortune passes to
its rightful heir rather than to Charlotte.

--- NOTES ON THE ADAPTATION ---

"A Murder Is Announced" is often cited as the finest of the Hickson BBC Marple
adaptations. The identity-substitution at its heart is one of Christie's most
audacious structural conceits, and the adaptation handles it with the gradual,
precisely timed revelation it requires. John Madden's direction is confident,
the supporting cast is strong, and Hickson's performance of the scene in which
she confronts Charlotte with the truth is among the most powerful in the series.
