FULL EPISODE SYNOPSIS — SPOILERS INCLUDED
================================================================================

--- BACKGROUND AND CONTEXT ---

"Try and Catch Me" is a Season 6 episode of Columbo, airing November 21, 1977.
It stars Ruth Gordon as Abigail Mitchell, a celebrated mystery writer — one of
the most beloved guest killers in the entire Columbo series. Gordon's performance
is extraordinary: she plays Abigail as warm, funny, grandmotherly, and entirely
ruthless, creating a character who is simultaneously completely charming and
completely guilty. The episode is directed by James Frawley and is consistently
ranked among the top tier of classic Columbo episodes.

The episode is notable for its moral complexity: the viewer's sympathies are
genuinely with the killer for much of the episode, because her victim deserved
what happened to him — he had caused the death of her beloved niece — and
because Abigail herself is so thoroughly lovable. Columbo's obligation to
bring her to account despite this is one of the series' most poignant variations
on its central theme.

--- THE KILLER AND THE BACKSTORY ---

Abigail Mitchell is a famous and successful mystery writer, elderly, sharp as
a tack, and currently dealing with the company of Edmund Galbraith — her niece
Phyllis's husband. Phyllis has recently died, officially by drowning in a boating
accident. Abigail knows, with complete certainty, that Edmund killed Phyllis
for her money. The insurance and inheritance that Phyllis's death generated
has made Edmund financially comfortable, and he has shown not a moment of
genuine grief.

Abigail is too old, too known, and too careful to be naïve about what Edmund
is. She is also a mystery writer — she has spent her career constructing
murder plots. And she decides to use that expertise.

--- THE MURDER: THE VAULT ---

Abigail invites Edmund to her study to discuss Phyllis's estate and some papers.
Her study contains a large, old vault — a substantial safe built into the wall,
large enough for a person to enter. She asks Edmund to look at some papers inside
the vault. He enters. She closes the door and locks it. Edmund is sealed inside
with a limited air supply. He dies.

The vault murder is one of Columbo's most ingeniously horrible killings —
not shot or stabbed but sealed alive and left to suffocate, the perfect locked-
room murder by the one person who could construct such a thing: a mystery writer
who has spent decades thinking about how to kill people without being caught.

Abigail then constructs a false timeline: Edmund left the house, she thought;
she hasn't heard from him since; she has no idea where he went. The vault is
her secret. It should stay sealed indefinitely.

--- COLUMBO MEETS ABIGAIL ---

When Edmund's disappearance becomes a police matter, Columbo is assigned.
He goes to see Abigail Mitchell, and the episode's central dynamic is immediately
established: these two people genuinely like each other. Columbo admires Abigail —
her books, her mind, her manner. Abigail recognizes in Columbo exactly the
kind of intelligent, persistent detective she has written about for forty years.

They talk about mystery fiction. She asks him what kind of detective he is —
does he work by inspiration or by accumulation of detail? He says, modestly,
detail. She says she thought so. The conversation has the quality of two
professionals recognizing each other across a professional divide.

And underneath the warmth, each of them knows exactly what the other is.

--- COLUMBO'S INVESTIGATION ---

Columbo's investigation is focused from the beginning on Abigail, but he cannot
establish it as focused — she is a beloved elderly woman, there is no obvious
evidence, and the vault is sealed. His visits to Abigail are some of the most
delightful in the series: they talk about mystery writing, they share meals
(she cooks for him), they discuss detective methodology. She is, within these
conversations, actively testing whether he can catch her, and occasionally
offering advice on how a fictional detective might crack a case like this one.

Columbo identifies the vault as significant. He has no reason, initially, to
focus on it — it is just part of the study's furniture — but something about
it catches his attention. His interest in the vault is observed by Abigail,
who recognizes that he has noticed what she hoped he would not.

The episode has Columbo working the problem of the vault — how can he get it
opened? Abigail controls access to her own property. He cannot simply force
it. He must find a legal or practical mechanism to open the vault without
Abigail's cooperation.

--- THE LOCKED VAULT PROBLEM ---

This is the episode's central procedural puzzle. Columbo knows, or strongly
suspects, that Edmund's body is in the vault. But a vault in a private residence
requires a specific legal basis to force open. He works to establish that basis
while continuing his apparently casual, warm visits to Abigail.

Abigail, understanding what Columbo is doing, tries to engineer a situation
in which the vault's contents are destroyed or made unavailable before he can
reach them. Whether she changes the combination, whether she tries to access
the vault herself to deal with its contents, or whether she simply waits and
hopes — her management of the situation is conducted with the same careful,
intelligent attention that characterizes everything else she does.

--- THE VAULT OPENED ---

Columbo succeeds in gaining access to the vault through whatever legal or
practical mechanism he assembles. The body of Edmund Galbraith is found inside.

The evidence of what happened — Abigail's fingerprints, the mechanism of the
vault's lock, and the reconstruction of events on the day Edmund disappeared —
establishes Abigail's guilt.

--- THE CONFRONTATION ---

The final confrontation between Columbo and Abigail is one of the most
emotionally complex in the series. Columbo does not enjoy what he has to do.
He tells her that he knows Edmund killed Phyllis — he is not unsympathetic to
why she did what she did. But it is still murder. And it is his job.

Abigail accepts this with composure. She is not broken by the exposure — she
is too formidable a person for that. But there is a sadness in the final scene
that reflects both characters' awareness of the cost of Columbo's obligation.
He found her. He always finds them.

--- RESOLUTION ---

Abigail Mitchell is arrested for the murder of Edmund Galbraith. The episode
ends without triumph — Columbo does not celebrate, and the viewer's feelings
are mixed in exactly the way the episode intended.

--- NOTES ON THE EPISODE ---

"Try and Catch Me" is one of the most discussed Columbo episodes because of
its moral complexity. Abigail is guilty of murder and Columbo must bring her
to justice. But Galbraith was a murderer who escaped justice. The law is not
simply on the right side here, and the episode knows it. Ruth Gordon's
performance is the series' benchmark for a sympathetic killer. The dynamic
between her and Peter Falk is full of genuine mutual affection that makes
the ending genuinely melancholy rather than simply satisfying.
