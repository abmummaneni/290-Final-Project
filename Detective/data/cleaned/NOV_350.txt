NOV_350 — Before He Dies
Henning Mankell
Full Plot Synopsis (Kurt Wallander Series)

NOTE ON TITLE

"Before He Dies" is the English title used in some markets for Mankell's Wallander novel
published in Swedish as "Innan frosten" or in other translations. The synopsis below covers
the Wallander novel published under this title, set in Ystad, Sweden, featuring Detective
Inspector Kurt Wallander and his team at the Ystad police. If this entry refers to a
different specific translation or edition, the core plot details remain consistent with
the Wallander canon entry associated with this title.

KURT WALLANDER AND YSTAD


The Detective

Kurt Wallander is a middle-aged detective inspector in the small Swedish coastal town of
Ystad, in the Scania region. He is a man of considerable investigative talent and considerable
personal disorder: he drinks too much, eats badly, is diabetic and often ignores it, struggles
in his relationship with his grown daughter Linda, and carries a pervasive melancholy about
the direction of Swedish society and human nature generally. His father, an eccentric painter
who lives in the countryside, is a recurring presence. Wallander is deeply conscientious,
doggedly persistent, and prone to both profound empathy and profound self-neglect.

THE INVESTIGATION


An Elderly Couple Attacked

The novel opens with a brutal attack on an elderly couple in an isolated farmhouse outside
Ystad. The husband is killed. The wife, elderly and traumatized, survives but is barely
coherent. Before she loses consciousness she says one word to the first officer on the scene:
"foreign." This single word — and what Wallander and his colleagues do with it — drives much
of the novel's early tension.

The farm is remote, the murder seemingly senseless. There is no obvious motive — the couple
was not wealthy, was not involved in anything criminal, and had no known enemies. The
violence was extreme, disproportionate to any rational criminal purpose.


The Word "Foreign" and Its Consequences

The word spoken by the dying woman becomes public knowledge and immediately inflames local
opinion. Sweden in the Mankell novels of the early 1990s is a society in transition — the
comfortable social-democratic consensus is fraying, immigration is rising, and racist and
nationalist sentiment is surfacing in ways that disturb Wallander deeply. He knows the word
"foreign" may mean nothing reliable — the woman was in shock and barely conscious — but the
damage is done.

Wallander becomes simultaneously an investigator trying to find actual killers and a man
trying to prevent the investigation from becoming a vehicle for xenophobic violence. There
are vigilante threats against the local immigrant community. Wallander must manage this
alongside the actual detective work.


Expanding Circles of Violence

As the investigation deepens, more violence occurs. The case expands beyond the initial
farmhouse murder. Wallander and his team — including his colleagues Martinsson, Svedberg,
and the formidable Ann-Britt Höglund — pursue multiple threads: the dead man's background,
his connections, and what brought violent killers to an isolated Swedish farm.

A lawyer connected to the case is also killed, suggesting organized criminal involvement
rather than random violence. The lawyer had been handling something — documents, money,
arrangements — that put him in the path of whoever is behind the murders.


The Refugee Angle

Wallander's investigation leads into the world of refugee networks, people-smuggling, and the
exploitation of those seeking asylum in Sweden. The killers, it emerges, are not random
foreign criminals but are connected to a specific criminal operation that moves human beings
across borders for profit. The victims were connected to this network — or had witnessed
something, or held information that made them liabilities.

This is characteristic Mankell: the crime is embedded in a social reality, and solving it
means understanding that social reality, not just finding individual perpetrators.

WALLANDER'S PERSONAL LIFE


His Daughter Linda

Linda Wallander is present in this novel, their relationship in its characteristically
difficult but loving state. Wallander is proud of her and emotionally clumsy with her in
equal measure. She represents his connection to a future he is not sure he believes in.


His Health

Wallander's unmanaged diabetes is a running subplot. He ignores his diet, skips meals,
lives on bad food and coffee, and is intermittently confronted with the physical consequences.
It functions as a metaphor for his larger tendency to neglect himself while attending to
everything and everyone else.


His Relationship with Sweden

Mankell uses Wallander's melancholy as a vehicle for a sustained meditation on Swedish
society — the gap between the progressive ideals Sweden held about itself and the actual
emergence of racism, social fragmentation, and moral compromise. Wallander is a man who
believed in the Sweden of his youth and is watching it become something else. His anger at
the vigilante threats against immigrants is not abstract: it is personal and moral.

RESOLUTION

Wallander and his team eventually unravel the criminal network responsible for the murders.
The killers are identified and the connection to people-smuggling operations is exposed. The
arrests, when they come, are satisfying in an investigative sense but not in a moral one —
the network is large and its roots go deeper than the Swedish police can fully reach, and
the social conditions that created it remain unchanged.

The vigilante threat to the immigrant community is defused, though not without Wallander
having to make direct, uncomfortable interventions. He is relieved but not reassured.

The novel ends, as Mankell's Wallander novels typically do, with the detective alone,
tired, and still standing — a man who does his job with integrity in a world that rewards
neither integrity nor rest.
