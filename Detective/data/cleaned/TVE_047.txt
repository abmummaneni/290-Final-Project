FULL EPISODE SYNOPSIS — SPOILERS INCLUDED

--- BACKGROUND AND CONTEXT ---

"Sad Cypress" is a feature-length episode of ITV's Agatha Christie's Poirot,
first broadcast in February 2003. It is directed by David Moore and stars
David Suchet as Hercule Poirot. It is based on Christie's 1940 novel of the
same name — a Poirot novel with an unusually romantic and emotionally intense
quality, whose title is taken from a song in Shakespeare's "Twelfth Night"
("Come away, come away, death / And in sad cypress let me be laid"). The
Shakespearean epigraph signals the novel's concern with love, loss, and the
specific relationship between passion and destructive action.

The episode is notable for its framing device: it opens with the protagonist
Elinor Carlisle in the dock, already charged with murder, and then unspools
the events that led her there in the form of Poirot's investigation, which
runs parallel to and eventually disrupts the prosecution's case against her.
This structure — beginning at the point of apparent conviction and working
backward and forward simultaneously — gives the episode an unusual dramatic
tension. The question is not simply who committed the crime but whether an
innocent person will be convicted before the truth is found.

--- ELINOR CARLISLE IN THE DOCK ---

The episode opens with Elinor Carlisle on trial for the murder of Mary Gerrard.
Elinor is a beautiful, composed, somewhat reserved young woman of good family
who has been charged with poisoning Mary — a young woman of lower social
standing who grew up on the grounds of Hunterbury, the country estate that
Elinor is about to inherit from her aunt, Laura Welman.

The case against Elinor is superficially compelling. She had motive: the man
she loves, Roddy Welman, had become infatuated with Mary Gerrard — a beautiful
girl of uncertain origins who had been befriended and educated by the dying
Laura Welman. If Elinor married Roddy as expected, Mary represented a threat
to that future. And if Elinor inherited the estate, Mary might have some claim
on a portion of it through Laura Welman's stated intentions.

The method was morphine — the same drug being used to manage Laura Welman's
pain in her final illness — which Elinor had both knowledge of and access to.
And the opportunity: Elinor was the one who prepared and served the food at
the specific gathering where Mary became ill and subsequently died.

Poirot, who has some knowledge of Elinor's situation, attends the trial and
watches. He is not satisfied with the prosecution's case — not because the
evidence is weak, but because something about the human picture it presents
does not fit. Elinor Carlisle does not strike him as someone who would kill
a woman out of romantic jealousy and social insecurity. He begins his own
investigation.

--- HUNTERBURY AND LAURA WELMAN ---

The backstory of the case is rooted in the world of Hunterbury Hall, the
country estate of Laura Welman — Elinor's aunt, who was the family's wealthy
matriarch. Laura had suffered a series of strokes and was dying when the
events of the case occurred. She was attended in her final months by a
professional nurse, Nurse Hopkins, and by her family including Elinor.

Also at Hunterbury, in the cottage on the grounds, lived Mary Gerrard and
her father Gerrard the lodge keeper. Mary had grown up on the estate and
had been particularly favored by Laura Welman, who had paid for her education
and treated her with a special warmth that went beyond the ordinary attention
of a landowner to an employee's child.

The specific nature of Laura's interest in Mary — why she was so particularly
devoted to this one girl — is one of the case's underlying questions, and
its answer is connected to the full truth of the murder.

--- RODDY WELMAN AND THE LOVE TRIANGLE ---

Roddy Welman is Elinor's cousin and the man she has been in an understanding
with — not formally engaged, but with the kind of long-established expectation
of marriage that comfortable families arrange for their children without
explicit declaration. He is charming, somewhat weak, and constitutionally
susceptible to beauty. When he encounters Mary Gerrard, he falls immediately
and hopelessly in love with her, and his management of this development — his
inability to simply end his understanding with Elinor before pursuing Mary,
his continued presence in Elinor's life while his feelings have entirely
transferred — is the specific cruelty that defines his character.

Elinor knows about Roddy and Mary. She is too intelligent and too observant
not to know. And her specific response to the knowledge — the composure, the
continued dignity, the refusal to make a scene — is precisely what the
prosecution reads as cold-blooded calculation rather than wounded pride.

--- THE DEATH OF LAURA WELMAN ---

Before Mary Gerrard's murder, Laura Welman herself dies. The death is not
immediately suspicious — she was gravely ill — but there is a specific question
about the management of her morphine and whether her death was assisted in
ways that were not strictly sanctioned. This thread is part of the investigation's
background.

More significantly, Laura died without making a new will — she had apparently
intended to make provision for Mary Gerrard but died before she could do so.
This means the estate goes to Elinor and Roddy as originally intended, and
Mary Gerrard receives nothing. This financial situation is relevant to the
case's motive mapping: it both gives Elinor less reason to fear Mary (Mary
has no legal claim on the estate) and suggests that someone wanted Laura dead
before she could change her will in Mary's favor.

--- MARY GERRARD'S DEATH ---

Mary Gerrard died of morphine poisoning — the morphine introduced, the
prosecution argues, into sandwiches that Elinor prepared when she and Nurse
Hopkins came to the Hunterbury cottage to clear out Mary's belongings, now
that the estate was Elinor's. Elinor made the sandwiches. Elinor served them.
Mary ate them and died.

Nurse Hopkins, who was present, ate the same sandwiches and was unaffected.
The prosecution's explanation is that Elinor selectively poisoned Mary's portion
and not Hopkins's — a specific act of targeted murder requiring some specific
method of introducing the morphine into only one portion.

--- POIROT'S INVESTIGATION ---

Poirot investigates by reconstructing the specific circumstances of Mary
Gerrard's death and the events at Hunterbury leading up to it. His method
in this episode is characteristically comprehensive: he does not simply examine
the day of the murder but the whole preceding period — the social dynamics at
Hunterbury, the specific people and their relationships, and the particular
events of Laura Welman's final months.

He is particularly interested in Nurse Hopkins. The nurse who was present at
the meal, who ate the same sandwiches without harm, whose specific professional
knowledge of morphine is at least as extensive as anyone else's in the
household — and whose account of the events is the prosecution's primary
supporting testimony. Poirot finds, in Hopkins's account, several things that
are oddly precise about what she saw and oddly vague about what she did.

He also investigates Mary Gerrard's history — specifically the question of
her parentage, which is one of the episode's most carefully handled threads.
Mary's father was nominally the lodge keeper Gerrard, but the specific warmth
Laura Welman showed her, and the particular attention paid to her education
and future, suggests a different explanation.

--- THE TRUTH: NURSE HOPKINS AND MARY'S ORIGINS ---

The truth that Poirot assembles has two interconnected parts.

The first concerns Mary Gerrard's parentage. Mary was Laura Welman's
illegitimate daughter — born of a relationship Laura had before her marriage,
the child she was forced to give up, who by chance came back into her life
as the child of the man employed on her estate. Laura's specific devotion to
Mary was maternal, not philanthropic. Her desire to change her will to provide
for Mary was the belated desire to do right by a daughter she had never been
able to claim.

The second part concerns who knew this. Nurse Hopkins knew. She had access
to the kind of personal and medical history that a nurse attending a dying
woman might learn, and she specifically knew — or had strong evidence —
that Mary was Laura's daughter. This knowledge gave her a specific interest
in Mary's future: if Mary was Laura's legitimate or acknowledged child,
she might have had a claim on the estate that superseded Elinor's.

But Hopkins's interest was not in Mary's wellbeing. It was in the letters —
specifically, in letters or documents that established the truth of Mary's
parentage and that Mary herself had recently come into possession of,
apparently through Laura's final communications or through documents at
Hunterbury. These letters, if they established Mary's identity as Laura's
daughter, had financial and legal significance. In the wrong hands — or rather,
in a position where they would never come to light — the estate remained
cleanly Elinor's.

Nurse Hopkins killed Mary Gerrard. She had the morphine, she had the
professional knowledge to administer it selectively, and she had the specific
opportunity during the preparation and serving of the food. The sandwiches
were poisoned by Hopkins, not by Elinor. The apparent impossibility of
selectively poisoning one portion — which the prosecution treated as evidence
of Elinor's premeditation — was in fact entirely possible for someone with
Hopkins's specific access to the food before it was served.

Hopkins's motive was the letters establishing Mary's identity: with Mary dead,
the letters irrelevant, and Elinor convicted, the entire estate question was
resolved in a way that benefited Hopkins through arrangements she had made
with respect to Laura Welman's final estate management.

--- ELINOR FREED ---

Poirot presents his findings to the relevant legal authority in time to disrupt
the prosecution of Elinor. The specific evidence he provides — pointing to
Hopkins as the killer, explaining the morphine administration method, and
establishing the motive in the letters and Mary's parentage — is sufficient
to cause the case against Elinor to collapse.

Elinor Carlisle is acquitted. She is not exonerated in the full emotional sense —
the experience of the trial, and the events leading up to it, have taken
a significant toll — but she is legally free.

--- THE ROMANTIC RESOLUTION ---

The episode handles the question of Elinor and Roddy's relationship with
Christie's characteristic lack of sentimentality. Roddy, whose infatuation
with Mary drove the social dynamics that led to the trial, is not a villain
but he is not admirable. His charm and his weakness are both visible, and
the question of whether Elinor would want him back — whether the love that
existed before the episode's events is recoverable — is left as a genuinely
open question rather than a happy ending.

--- NOTES ON THE EPISODE ---

"Sad Cypress" is one of the more emotionally rich Poirot productions. The
framing device — beginning in the courtroom, with Elinor already on trial —
gives the episode an urgency and personal stakes that the standard mystery
structure lacks. The question of whether Poirot will find the truth in time
is not rhetorical here: the legal machinery is running, the conviction is
possible. David Moore's direction handles the emotional temperature with care,
and the performances — particularly the actress playing Elinor — carry the
weight of a woman trying to maintain dignity under intolerable pressure.
The romantic triangle, and its specific emotional damage, is given genuine
gravity rather than being treated as simply a plot mechanism.
