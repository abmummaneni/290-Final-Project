Vera: Hidden Depths — Plot Synopsis (Series 1, Episode 1, 2011)
DCI Vera Stanhope, a forthright, late-middle-aged, drink-loving senior detective with the Northumberland and City Police, is grieving the recent death of her father when she is called to the scene of a disturbing murder. Vera is uninvolved in the crime; she is the lead investigator. She is assisted by DS Joe Ashworth, her trusted younger sergeant, who is also uninvolved in the crime and is awaiting the birth of his child with his wife Seline. Vera's other team members, DC Holly Lawson and pathologist Dr. Billy Cartwright, are likewise uninvolved professional witnesses to the investigation.
The first victim is Luke Armstrong, a 15-year-old boy with learning difficulties, who is strangled in his family bathtub and laid out in water strewn with wildflowers and candles. Luke is a victim, not a perpetrator. His widowed mother Julie Armstrong, a working-class woman of middle social status, discovers the body when she returns from a rare night out in Newcastle with her boyfriend Gary Wright. Julie is uninvolved in the murders; she has a firm alibi for Luke's killing because she was with Gary at the time. Julie is concealing nothing material to the case beyond the ordinary embarrassment of dating again so soon after her husband's death. Luke's younger sister Laura Armstrong, who slept through the attack in the same house, is a witness in the sense of being on the premises, but she saw nothing and is uninvolved.
Gary Wright, a womanizing sound technician and one of a tight group of male birdwatching friends, is initially a suspect because of his proximity to Julie and his unreliable history with women. Gary has a partial alibi for Luke's murder — he was with Julie in Newcastle — and he has no compelling motive. Gary is uninvolved in the killings, though he is concealing the casual infidelities typical of his character.
A second body is then found on a Northumberland beach: Lily Marsh, a beautiful young primary school teacher, throttled and arranged in a tidal rock pool surrounded by flowers in the same ritualistic style as Luke. Lily is a victim, but she is also the linchpin of the hidden relationship that drives the case: Lily had been conducting a secret affair with a much older married man who was depositing money into her bank account as her "sugar daddy." Lily was also pretending to be merely a prospective tenant of a beach house owned by the Calverts, when in fact she had been intimately involved with the male head of that household and was angling to insert herself into the family.
The body is discovered by a group of four middle-aged birdwatching friends celebrating a birthday: Dr. Peter Calvert, a pompous and philandering university botany lecturer of high-middle social status; Clive Stringer, a quiet, socially awkward museum curator devoted to Peter; Samuel Parr, a librarian and writer; and Davy Sharp. Also present at the scene was Peter's wife, Felicity Calvert, a younger, outwardly serene homemaker who tends the Calverts' beautiful garden and looks after the couple's young son James Calvert. James is a child witness who finds Lily's body alongside the adults and is otherwise uninvolved.
Peter Calvert is concealing the fact that he was Lily Marsh's secret older lover and the source of the money in her account; this is the illicit affair at the heart of the case. Peter has a motive in the love/lust category and a motive to silence Lily before she exposed the relationship and destroyed his marriage and reputation. He has no firm alibi for Luke's killing and is on the beach for Lily's discovery. Vera at first deduces that the lover must be Peter and presumes he is therefore the killer. Clive Stringer would do almost anything for Peter and is concealing his knowledge of Peter's affair, giving him a motive of loyalty/manipulation by Peter, but Clive is not the killer.
Luke Armstrong's involvement turns out to be that he is the link between the two murders. His best friend, a boy named Tommy (Thomas), drowned months earlier in what was ruled an accident. Tommy had photographed Lily in a compromising encounter with her older lover and drowned trying to retrieve his camera when it fell into the sea. Luke had inherited the camera and the knowledge of the photographs and had used them to attempt a small, naive act of blackmail. Tommy's father, initially suspected because of a criminal background, is uninvolved in either murder and has an alibi (he was in prison or otherwise accounted for during Luke's killing).
The resolution: the killer is revealed to be Felicity Calvert, Peter's wife. Felicity has a motive of jealousy and protecting family/status — she discovered her husband's affair with Lily Marsh and learned of Luke's blackmail attempt threatening to expose it. Rather than confront Peter, Felicity strangled Luke Armstrong in his bath to destroy the blackmail and then strangled Lily Marsh on the beach to eliminate the rival who threatened her marriage, her home, and her son's family life. She arranged both bodies in water and flowers, drawing on her gardener's aesthetic sensibility, to disguise the murders as the work of a stylized serial killer and divert suspicion away from a domestic motive. Felicity had no genuine alibi for either killing; she had presented herself as the calm, house-bound wife and exploited the assumption that a soft-spoken middle-class mother could not be a strangler. Vera cracks the case by tracing the missing camera and the money trail back to Peter's affair, then recognizing that the person with the strongest motive to protect that secret was not Peter himself but the wife who stood to lose everything. Felicity is arrested for both murders. Peter Calvert, though morally implicated as the adulterer whose actions triggered the killings, is not charged because he committed no crime; he is left exposed and humiliated but legally untouchable, while Clive Stringer is left bereft by the exposure of the friend group's secrets.
