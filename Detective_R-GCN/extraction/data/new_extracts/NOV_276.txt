The Stonecutter (Camilla Läckberg, 2005) — Plot Synopsis

Note on framing: Stenhuggaren (English title The Stonecutter or The Stone Cutter) is the third novel in Läckberg's Patrik Hedström / Erica Falck series set in the Swedish coastal town of Fjällbacka in the province of Bohuslän. It was published in Swedish in 2005 and translated into English by Steven T. Murray for HarperCollins in 2010. The novel uses an alternating dual-timeline structure: a present-day police procedural investigating the drowning of a seven-year-old girl, and a 1923-onward historical strand about a stonecutter and a wealthy heiress whose intergenerational legacy of abuse turns out to feed directly into the present-day crime. Note that some published reviews (notably Kirkus) misstate the family relationship between Lilian Florin and Niclas; I follow the Wikipedia and original-text consensus that Lilian is Charlotte's mother (the maternal grandmother of the murdered child), not Niclas's mother. Note also that the resolution names a single perpetrator with a clear motive that flows from the historical strand; my reconstruction of the resolution below is based on the published summaries and the clear thematic logic of the dual narrative — Läckberg's secondary "Agnes" thread is a setup paid off by revealing that the present-day killer is Agnes's adopted daughter Mary's daughter, Lilian Florin.

The investigators are uninvolved in the crime as perpetrators. Detective Inspector Patrik Hedström of the Tanumshede police is Erica's fiancé and the new father of their baby daughter Maja. He is exhausted and torn between work and home. He is uninvolved in any crime. Erica Falck is a writer, journalist, and amateur sleuth, friend of Charlotte Klinga, suffering severely from postpartum depression that she is concealing from Patrik and from herself; her concealment behavior is purely personal-medical and not criminal. Other police: Detective Chief Inspector Bertil Mellberg (lazy and incompetent, divorced, briefly hosting his teenage son Simon, uninvolved); DI Martin Molin (Patrik's preferred partner, uninvolved); DI Ernst Lundgren (Patrik's incompetent assigned partner, who hampers the investigation, uninvolved as a perpetrator); DI Gösta Flygare (uninvolved); receptionist Annika (uninvolved); forensic pathologist Tord Pedersen (uninvolved). Erica's sister Anna Maxwell and her abusive husband Lucas Maxwell appear in a subplot — Lucas is uninvolved in the central crime but is a domestic-abuse perpetrator against Anna; the subplot ends in a cliffhanger that bridges to the next novel.

The murder victim is Sara Antonsson, a seven-year-old girl with DAMP (Deficits in Attention, Motor control, and Perception, a Scandinavian diagnostic category overlapping with ADHD), described as wild and difficult, daughter of Erica's friend Charlotte. Sara is uninvolved in any crime as a perpetrator — she is a child victim. She is found drowned in a fisherman's lobster net. The post-mortem shows that she did not drown in the salt water of the sea where her body was recovered, because her lungs contain only fresh water — meaning she was drowned elsewhere (in a bathtub) and then dumped into the sea to disguise the killing as an accidental drowning. She has no motive against anyone, no relevant alibi questions, and no concealment behavior; she is a child caught in an adult-generated trap. Her disappearance was not even noticed before her body was found.

Sara's family lives in an unstable extended-household arrangement. Her mother Charlotte Klinga is uninvolved in the murder as a perpetrator; she is a young mother of middle social status, recently returned to Fjällbacka, devastated by Sara's death, suffering through her own difficult adjustment to her new baby Albin (Sara's infant brother). Charlotte has no motive and was not at the crime scene at the time of the killing; she is concealing nothing material, though her deteriorating marriage is an open secret. Her infant son Albin becomes a second target later in the novel — found still alive but with ashes in his mouth, an unmistakable echo of the historical strand and a key clue to the killer.

Sara's father Niclas Antonsson is a tall, blond general practitioner of middle social status, uninvolved in Sara's murder as a perpetrator. He has no motive against his daughter. His alibi for the time of Sara's death is initially shaky — the detectives note that he could not be located when the body was identified. Niclas is concealing two things: an ongoing extramarital affair with Jeanette Lind (a hidden romantic relationship that the police investigation exposes), and a connection to a paedophile ring uncovered as a side investigation — Niclas is implicated in the molestation/abuse of a troubled child named Sebastian Rydén, whose father is Rune Rydén. This abuse is unrelated to Sara's murder but is exposed during the Patrik investigation; Sebastian later commits suicide and names Niclas in his suicide note, leading to Niclas's arrest on the abuse charges as a separate strand of the case. Niclas's hidden relationships are therefore the affair with Jeanette and the abuse network. He is also estranged from his religious-fanatic father Arne Antonsson, a sexton, and rejected the family name Antonsson when he married Charlotte (the Wikipedia summary records a slight inconsistency on this, but the surname-rejection signals the depth of his estrangement from his father). Arne is uninvolved in the murder as a perpetrator. Aina, Niclas's medical assistant, is uninvolved.

Sara's grandmother Lilian Florin is the central villain of the present-day crime. She is Charlotte's mother, Sara's maternal grandmother, and the matriarch of the Florin household where Charlotte, Niclas, Sara, and Albin all live; she is also nominally caring for her bedridden second husband Stig Florin. Lilian's first husband, Charlotte's biological father, was Lennart Klinga, deceased. Lilian is described throughout the novel as an "acerbic hag" who is conducting a long-running, vicious feud with her next-door neighbor Kaj Wiberg. Lilian's motive for killing Sara is psychopathic tendencies inherited and absorbed across generations (see below) combined with a Munchausen-syndrome-by-proxy (Factitious Disorder Imposed on Another) pattern in which she derives meaning and attention from being the put-upon caretaker of a sickly or troubled household — she has been quietly poisoning, manipulating, and harming the children and adults in her care for years to maintain her role as the indispensable matriarch. She is concealing the fact that she has been progressively harming her bedridden husband Stig (who is bedridden because of her treatment, not because of natural illness). She is concealing the fact that she drowned Sara in the bathtub of the Florin home and then dumped the body in the sea to disguise the killing. She is concealing the fact that she subsequently attempted to kill her infant grandson Albin by feeding him ashes — a method that exposes her, because of its specific symbolic resonance with the historical strand. Her alibi for the time of Sara's death is the bland alibi of being the home-bound grandmother in a house full of family; her alibi unravels when the detectives realize the killer must have had bathroom access at the Florin house and the calm to drown a struggling child without raising the household. She was physically present at the crime scene — the crime scene is her own bathroom. Her motive type is psychopathic tendencies, with notes of jealousy (of Charlotte's life and of Sara as the focus of family attention), revenge (against the long catalogue of slights she nurses), and a need for control over the household.

The historical strand begins in 1923 in Strömstad. Anders Andersson is the eponymous stonecutter — an honest, hard-working young man of low social status. Anders is uninvolved in any present-day crime; he is an indirect victim of the historical strand, killed by his own wife. He is dead before the present-day action begins. Agnes Stjernkvist is the spoiled, beautiful daughter of Anders's boss, the wealthy stone-quarry owner August Stjernkvist (born Persson). Agnes is the historical villain of the novel. She seduces Anders for sport, intending to leave him before her father discovers the affair, but becomes pregnant. Her father forces them to marry and exiles them to a small house in Fjällbacka. Anders genuinely loves her; she despises him. Agnes gives birth to twin sons. Her motive throughout her life is a combination of psychopathic tendencies, entitlement (she resents being reduced from heiress to stonecutter's wife), and pure malice. She has no alibi for any of her crimes because her crimes are committed in the privacy of her own home. She is concealing the fact that she deliberately sets fire to the family house, killing Anders and both her twin sons. This is her first murder. She emigrates to the United States and lives there for decades, returning to Sweden in the 1950s with Mary, a street girl she has picked up and "adopted" as her daughter. Agnes systematically abuses Mary in a pattern that prefigures the present-day abuse: she locks Mary in the cellar, starves her, and feeds her ashes, which she calls "Humility" (Swedish Ödmjukhet). Agnes is concealing her abuse of Mary from her second husband Åke (whom she frames and blames for the household problems), and she begins an affair with a married man named Per-Erik behind the back of his wife Elisabeth. Agnes's victims in the historical strand are therefore Anders Andersson, the twin sons, and Mary (a long-term abuse victim).

The hidden relationship at the heart of the novel — the connection that ties the historical and present-day strands together — is that Mary, Agnes's adopted and abused daughter, is the mother of Lilian Florin. Lilian was raised in the cellar-and-ashes household environment that Mary inherited from Agnes's abuse. Lilian absorbed both the abuse she witnessed and the methodology, and has reproduced it in her own household across two further generations: against her bedridden husband Stig, against her grandchildren Sara and Albin. The ashes-in-the-mouth method used against Albin is the smoking-gun clue that links Lilian to the inherited Agnes-Mary lineage of abuse, since "Humility" is the family-private name for the ash-feeding ritual. Other concealed connections: Niclas's affair with Jeanette Lind; Niclas's involvement in the Sebastian Rydén abuse case; the Kaj-Lilian feud (which Lilian has weaponized to make her neighbor a convenient false suspect, particularly to push suspicion onto Kaj's autistic son Morgan).

Other suspects who turn out to be uninvolved: Morgan Wiberg, Kaj's adult son, a video-game designer with Asperger syndrome who lives largely isolated in his room. Morgan had seen and spoken to Sara on the day she died and had a difficult, friction-laden relationship with the wild child. Suspicion falls heavily on him because of his autism (the police and community misread his social affect as guilt), but he is uninvolved as a perpetrator and has no motive beyond annoyance at Sara's disruptions of his routine. He has a partial alibi (he was in his computer room) and was not at the bathroom-crime-scene. He is concealing nothing material; his apparent evasiveness is a manifestation of his autism. Kaj Wiberg and his wife Monika are uninvolved in the murder; Kaj has a motive of feud-revenge against Lilian but no motive against Sara, and he was not at the crime scene. Frida, Sara's young playmate, and her mother Veronika are uninvolved witnesses.

Concealment behaviors are pervasive: Lilian conceals the murder, the long-term abuse of Stig, the attempted murder of Albin, and the inherited abuse pattern from Mary. Niclas conceals the affair and the Sebastian Rydén abuse. Erica conceals her postpartum depression. Lucas Maxwell conceals his abuse of Anna. The Agnes-Mary-Lilian generational lineage has been concealed for nearly eighty years.

The resolution: Patrik Hedström solves the case when the second incident — the discovery of infant Albin still alive but with ashes in his mouth in his crib at the Florin house — connects the present-day case to the buried family history Erica has been unraveling on the side. The "ashes/Humility" method is the bridge: it is too specific to be coincidence. The investigators retrace the maternal lineage from Lilian back through Mary to Agnes Stjernkvist and the 1920s house fire that killed Anders Andersson and the twins. They establish that Lilian inherited the methodology of cruelty from a mother who had been the lifelong victim of it. The bathtub of the Florin house yields the forensic confirmation that Sara was drowned there before her body was transferred to the sea. Lilian is identified as the killer, motivated by psychopathic tendencies, control of the household, and the inherited pattern of caregiver-perpetrated abuse; she has no alibi that survives scrutiny once the household-as-crime-scene is established. She was physically present at the murder. The villain of the present-day strand is therefore Lilian Florin, mother of Charlotte, grandmother of Sara and Albin, daughter of Mary, granddaughter (by adoption) of Agnes Stjernkvist. The villain of the historical strand is Agnes Stjernkvist, who killed Anders Andersson and her twin sons by arson and who spent her life abusing Mary. The two crimes are separated by eighty years but are causally linked through three generations of women, with abuse being passed down intact from Agnes to Mary to Lilian and finally enacted against Sara. As a side casualty, Niclas Antonsson is arrested separately for his involvement in the Sebastian Rydén abuse network, the boy having taken his own life and named Niclas in his suicide note. The novel closes with Patrik solving the case, Erica beginning to confront her postpartum depression, Charlotte left widowed-by-arrest and motherless-by-arrest, infant Albin saved, and a cliffhanger involving Anna and Lucas Maxwell that carries into the next novel.
