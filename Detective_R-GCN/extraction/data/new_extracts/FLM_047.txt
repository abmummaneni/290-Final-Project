Important framing note for your dataset: Zodiac is unusual in this collection because it dramatizes a real-life unsolved case. The film does not deliver a courtroom-level reveal of a perpetrator. The Zodiac Killer's true identity remains unknown in real life and the film is deliberately ambiguous. The closest the film comes to naming a villain is heavy circumstantial implication that Arthur Leigh Allen was the killer, framed through Robert Graysmith's personal conviction rather than legal proof. Fincher and screenwriter James Vanderbilt explicitly preserve doubt. If your downstream extractor needs a single "perpetrator" label, Arthur Leigh Allen is the film's strongly-implicated suspect; if it can accommodate "unsolved" or "implied but unproven," that is the more accurate label.

The film opens on July 4, 1969, at a lovers' lane in Vallejo, California, where an unknown man approaches a parked car and shoots Darlene Ferrin and Mike Mageau with a handgun. Darlene Ferrin, a young white waitress, is a victim and dies at the scene. Mike Mageau, a young white man, is also a victim but survives, becoming a witness who will later, in 1991, identify Arthur Leigh Allen from a mugshot lineup as his attacker. Darlene Ferrin had reportedly been stalked by a strange man known to acquaintances as "Lee" who attended a party of hers shortly before her death; this hidden prior acquaintance between Darlene and her likely killer is one of the threads Robert Graysmith later uncovers. The film also dramatizes a 1969 attack at Lake Berryessa, where a hooded man wearing a crossed-circle symbol stabs Bryan Hartnell (a young man who survives, becomes a witness, and is uninvolved in the crime) and Cecelia Shepard (who dies of her wounds and is purely a victim), and the 1969 shooting of San Francisco taxi driver Paul Stine in Presidio Heights, who is killed by a passenger and is purely a victim. A 1966 attack on Cheri Jo Bates in Riverside is also raised as possibly connected, with Cheri Jo Bates as another victim, uninvolved beyond being killed.
The shared killer, who calls himself the Zodiac in taunting letters and ciphers mailed to the San Francisco Chronicle, is the film's villain. The Zodiac's motive is presented as a mixture of psychopathic tendencies, glory-seeking, and power: the killer explicitly tells the Chronicle that he kills for sport — quoting The Most Dangerous Game's line that man is "the most dangerous animal" — and that he wants to collect victims as "slaves" for the afterlife. The Zodiac has no apparent personal motive against any individual victim; the attacks are presented as substantially random. The Zodiac is concealing his identity through hood, ciphers, and pseudonym, and is concealing the fact that he writes the letters from a position of someone who appears, on the surface, to be an ordinary Bay Area resident. He has no alibi that the film tests directly, because his identity is never legally confirmed. He is physically present at every crime scene at the time of every crime.
Arthur Leigh Allen is the film's strongly-implicated suspect, gradually built up through circumstantial evidence as the most likely candidate to be the Zodiac. Arthur Leigh Allen is a white middle-aged man, a former elementary school teacher fired for inappropriate conduct with children, working later as a hardware store clerk in Vallejo. He has a possible motive of psychopathic tendencies (a documented history of violence and pedophilia), glory-seeking through the Zodiac persona, and entitlement. Arthur Leigh Allen has only weak and shifting alibis for the times of the murders, and detectives Toschi, Armstrong, and Mulanax are unable to break or confirm them. He is concealing his ownership of bloody knives shortly after the Lake Berryessa stabbing (per his brother's wife's tip), is concealing his use of a Zodiac-brand wristwatch bearing a crossed-circle logo identical to the killer's symbol, and is concealing his proximity to victim Darlene Ferrin (he lived nearby and was likely the "Leigh" at her party). He has no firm alibi for any of the killings, and the film places him geographically near several of the crime scenes. The hidden relationship between Arthur Leigh Allen and Darlene Ferrin — the prior acquaintance disguised as strangers — is one of the film's key reveals via the witness Linda del Buono. However, hard evidence exonerates him on paper: the handwriting expert Sherwood Morrill insists Allen did not write the Zodiac letters (Allen is purportedly ambidextrous, complicating this), fingerprints from the Stine cab do not match, and decades later DNA from a Zodiac envelope partially excludes him. He is therefore the prime suspect but never charged. Arthur Leigh Allen died of a heart attack in 1992 before he could be re-interviewed.
Other named suspects are raised and discarded. Rick Marshall is a former projectionist tipped to Graysmith as a possible Zodiac; he has a possible motive of psychopathic tendencies and a handwriting and film-buff connection to the cipher references, no clear alibi, but is ultimately set aside. Bob Vaughn, a friend of Marshall who lives in a creaky old house Graysmith visits in one of the film's most frightening scenes, is concealing the fact that he, not Marshall, wrote certain movie posters whose handwriting Graysmith was investigating; Bob Vaughn has no proven involvement in the murders but is presented as an unsettling possible accomplice or red herring. Both are uninvolved in any provable sense.
The investigators are uninvolved in the crimes and serve as the film's protagonists. Robert Graysmith is a young white male political cartoonist at the San Francisco Chronicle, of middle social status, married to Melanie (a young white woman, a librarian, uninvolved in the case but who eventually leaves him and takes their children because of his obsession). Graysmith has no motive in the criminal sense; his driving motive is obsession with solving the case and authorship/glory in the journalistic sense (he later writes the bestselling books Zodiac and Zodiac Unmasked on which the film is based). He receives anonymous heavy-breathing phone calls during his investigation, mirroring calls made to Darlene Ferrin's family, suggesting the killer or someone tracking him is watching. Paul Avery is a white middle-aged crime reporter at the Chronicle, alcoholic and drug-using, divorced, who collaborates with Graysmith and receives a personal death threat from the Zodiac, after which he becomes paranoid and his health collapses; he is uninvolved in the crimes and dies in 2000 of pulmonary emphysema. Inspector David Toschi is a white middle-aged San Francisco Police homicide detective of high professional status, the lead investigator on the Paul Stine murder, partnered with Inspector Bill Armstrong; both are uninvolved in any crime. Toschi was, in real life, briefly accused of forging a 1978 Zodiac letter himself but was cleared, a thread the film touches on. He is concealing nothing material to the case. Other detectives — Jack Mulanax in Vallejo, Ken Narlow in Napa, Captain Marty Lee at SFPD — are likewise uninvolved professional investigators. Attorney Melvin Belli, contacted by the Zodiac on a televised talk show, is uninvolved.
The resolution: The film does not produce a legal solution, and that is the point. The investigation goes cold by the late 1970s as jurisdictions fail to share information, handwriting and fingerprint evidence formally exonerate Arthur Leigh Allen, and Toschi is reassigned. Graysmith, by then divorced and unemployed, continues alone. Through library records, witness re-interviews, and a tip identifying "Leigh" as the man who frightened Darlene Ferrin, Graysmith assembles a circumstantial case pointing to Arthur Leigh Allen. In December 1983, Graysmith walks into the Ace Hardware store in Vallejo where Allen works as a clerk and the two stand face-to-face in silence; Allen's name tag reads "Lee." The film treats this stare as Graysmith's personal solving of the case — emotional certainty, not legal proof. End-title cards then report that in 1991, Mike Mageau identified Arthur Leigh Allen from a mugshot lineup as the man who shot him at Blue Rock Springs. Arthur Leigh Allen died of a heart attack in 1992 before he could be re-interrogated. A 2002 partial DNA profile from a Zodiac envelope did not match Allen, though investigators in San Francisco and Vallejo refused to rule him out. The San Francisco Police Department deactivated the case in 2004. The closing card states that the case remains open in Napa, Solano, and Vallejo, where Arthur Leigh Allen is still the prime and only suspect. The villain, in the film's framing, is therefore the Zodiac Killer; the strongly-implied (but never proven) identity of the Zodiac is Arthur Leigh Allen; and the case, in legal terms, is unsolved.
