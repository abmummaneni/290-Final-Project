The Killing (AMC): Season 2 (2012) — Plot Synopsis
Note on framing: The Killing is an AMC American crime drama developed by Veena Sud, adapted from the Danish series Forbrydelsen. Season 2 aired on AMC from April 1 to June 17, 2012, across thirteen episodes, and concluded the Rosie Larsen murder investigation that had been left unresolved at the end of Season 1 — a cliffhanger that famously infuriated viewers and critics. The pilot episode was directed by Patty Jenkins (later of Wonder Woman); Jenkins also returned to direct the Season 2 finale, "What I Know." The structure of the two-season Rosie Larsen arc is a classic whodunit with a slow-burn investigation, multiple red herrings, and a final-episode reveal that names the perpetrator(s) explicitly. Synopsis below treats the Rosie Larsen storyline as resolved at the end of Season 2.
The murder victim is Rose "Rosie" Larsen, a seventeen-year-old white American teenage girl of low-middle social status, a high school student in Seattle, daughter of Mitch and Stan Larsen, sister of Tommy and Denny. Rosie is a victim, uninvolved in any crime as a perpetrator. Her body was discovered in the trunk of a campaign car belonging to mayoral candidate Darren Richmond at the end of the pilot episode in Season 1, having drowned in the trunk after the car was driven into a lake in the Discovery Park area of Seattle. She has no motive against anyone. She has, by virtue of being murdered before the action begins, no relevant alibi to test. She was concealing two things from her family that drive the case: her secret occasional employment with the high-end escort service Beau Soleil (where she had been hired briefly under a false name) and her knowledge of her aunt Terry's involvement in the same escort service. She was concealing a third, fatal piece of knowledge by accident: that on the night of her murder she had stumbled into a covert meeting at the Wapi Eagle Casino's tenth-floor construction site and had filmed it with her video camera.
The investigators are uninvolved in the crime as perpetrators. Detective Sarah Linden is the lead Seattle Police Department homicide detective on the Larsen case, a single mother of middle social status to her teenage son Jack Linden, divorced, a former foster child, methodologically obsessive to the point of self-destruction, recurringly engaged to her fiancé Rick Felder (whose patience with her devotion to the Larsen case finally snaps in Season 2 and the engagement ends). Linden is concealing nothing about the case but is concealing her own deteriorating mental health and a previous breakdown during a child-murder investigation in her past. Detective Stephen Holder is Linden's young, recovering-addict, hip-hop-vernacular partner, also of middle social status, of complicated background (a former narcotics officer in recovery, devout in a Narcotics Anonymous program). Holder is uninvolved in the crime as a perpetrator but is concealing a serious early-Season-2 ethical failure: in Season 1 he was manipulated by his supposed mentor Lt. Carlson and intelligence operative James Skinner (Linden's later partner-equivalent, in some accounts a different figure named in Season 2 as one of Linden's seniors) into producing a falsified photograph that framed Darren Richmond at the end of Season 1 — a frame-up that Holder begins Season 2 trying to undo. Holder's motive in the falsification was professional advancement and pressure from his superiors; he comes to deeply regret it and confesses to Linden during Season 2.
The principal red-herring suspect inherited from Season 1 is Darren Richmond, the white middle-aged Seattle city councilman running for mayor on a reform platform, widowed (his wife Lily died in a car accident years earlier), of high social status. Richmond is uninvolved in the murder as a perpetrator. His motive against Rosie is none; the apparent motive constructed by Season 1 was pure misdirection. Richmond has alibis for his various tested moments. He is in fact concealing something dark but unrelated: he had paid the Beau Soleil escort service for a service in which he intended to drown himself in the same lake where Rosie's car was later sunk, on the anniversary of his wife's death — a planned suicide-by-staged-incident that he did not go through with. This concealment fed every false lead in the case. Richmond is shot at the end of Season 1 by Stan Larsen acting on the false-photograph frame-up; he survives but is paralyzed from the waist down. In Season 2, Richmond eventually wins the mayoral election after Jamie's machinations sink the incumbent. Richmond is uninvolved in any crime but is morally compromised by his complicity in accepting Jamie's election-rigging operation once it is fait accompli.
The Larsen family are the grieving family of the victim. Stan Larsen, Rosie's father, is a white middle-aged man of low-middle social status, a former enforcer for a Polish organized-crime figure named Janek Kovarsky, now running a small Seattle moving company, married to Mitch. Stan is uninvolved in Rosie's murder as a perpetrator; he attempts a violent revenge in Season 1 against the wrongly-accused teacher Bennet Ahmed (Rosie's high school teacher, who is uninvolved in the murder as a perpetrator and who is severely beaten by Stan and his coworker Belko in a horrifying mistake). Stan is concealing his criminal past from his children. He is also the person who shoots Richmond at the end of Season 1, acting on the falsified photograph; this is itself a serious crime. Mitch Larsen, Rosie's mother, is a white middle-aged woman of low-middle social status, married to Stan, mother of three. Mitch is uninvolved in Rosie's murder as a perpetrator. She abandons the family for much of Season 2 to travel and process her grief. She is concealing nothing material to the case. Belko Royce, Stan's loyal employee and Rosie's surrogate uncle, is uninvolved in Rosie's murder as a perpetrator but is the person who attempts to assassinate Richmond in the hospital in early Season 2; Belko's motive is misplaced revenge, drawing on the same false frame-up. Belko is killed by police during this attempt. Tommy and Denny Larsen, Rosie's young brothers, are uninvolved.
The accomplices in the actual murder, who emerge as the principal villains over the course of Season 2, are three: Jamie Wright, Terry Marek, and Michael Ames, with the structural complicity of casino chief Nicole Jackson.
Jamie Wright is Darren Richmond's young white American campaign manager, of middle-high social status, unmarried, devoted to Richmond to the point of pathology, ambitious, intelligent, willing to commit serious crimes to deliver the election to his candidate. Jamie is the principal perpetrator of the violence against Rosie. His motive is power, glory, and a possessive ambition that combines his political ambitions with a quasi-romantic attachment to Richmond ("I did it all for you, Darren!"). Jamie has no alibi for the time of Rosie's beating because he is the perpetrator; he is at the Wapi Eagle Casino's tenth-floor construction site at the time. He is concealing the entire campaign-corruption operation, the meeting Rosie overheard, the pursuit and beating of Rosie, and his subsequent panic. His grandfather Ted Wright, a retired Native American figure who knows Jamie better than Jamie realizes, eventually identifies that Jamie's stated alibi for the night of Rosie's murder cannot be verified — a key clue that triggers Jamie's confession to Richmond in the finale. Jamie is killed in the Season 2 finale in a "suicide by cop" confrontation: when Linden and Holder corner him at Richmond's election-night offices, Jamie produces what appears to be a weapon (a phone, in some readings) and is shot dead by Holder.
Terry Marek is Mitch Larsen's sister, Rosie's maternal aunt, a white American woman in her thirties of middle social status, unmarried, a part-time worker for the Beau Soleil high-end escort agency where she has been earning extra income, and a long-running mistress of the wealthy married property developer Michael Ames. Terry is the actual killer of Rosie — the person who pushed Jamie's car into the lake while Rosie was bound and unconscious in the trunk. Her motive is a complex mixture of jealousy, financial ambition, entitlement, and protection of her relationship with Ames: she had built her life around the belief that Ames would leave his wife for her, and when she overheard Ames refusing to back Jamie's plan further (which would have meant Ames remaining with his wife rather than committing himself to Terry), she acted on the spot to eliminate the witness in the trunk and seal the corruption that she believed would secure Ames's commitment to her. Crucially, Terry did not know that the bound girl in the trunk was her niece Rosie — she believed she was killing an anonymous teenager who had overheard the casino meeting. The hidden relationship at the heart of the season is therefore the secret affair between Terry Marek and Michael Ames, concealed from Mitch, Stan, and the Larsen children, and from Ames's wife. A second hidden relationship is Terry's secret employment with Beau Soleil, concealed from her family. A third is that Terry was acquainted with Ames's son, who was friendly with Rosie — meaning Rosie's accidental presence at the casino site at the moment of the meeting was the product of overlapping family connections, not pure coincidence. Terry has no real alibi for the moment of Rosie's drowning because she was at the lake; she has been concealing her presence there for the entire investigation. She is physically present at the moment Rosie dies — driving the car into the water with her own hands. She breaks down in the finale and confesses, sobbing, "I didn't know! I didn't know it was Rosie!"
Michael Ames is the wealthy white middle-aged married property developer, of high social status, married to a woman uninvolved in the case, secretly the long-running lover of Terry Marek. Ames is an accomplice in the corruption conspiracy and a co-conspirator in the disposal of Rosie's body. His motive is financial — the Wapi Eagle Casino waterfront development was poised to enrich him substantially, and the corruption scheme was designed to remove the obstacle of incumbent Mayor Lesley Adams from office. Ames does not personally beat Rosie or drive the car into the lake; he is, at the lakeside scene, the figure who refuses Jamie's request to actively kill Rosie. His refusal — in Terry's hearing — is what triggers Terry's panic and the actual murder. Ames is concealing the affair, the corruption, and his role in the cover-up. He has no alibi for the lakeside scene because he is there. In the finale, Ames evades prosecution: charges against him are quietly dropped through Richmond's intervention, Terry's confession does not legally implicate him in a way prosecutors will pursue, and he walks free — a deliberate moral injustice the show foregrounds in its closing scenes.
Chief Nicole Jackson is the female Native American manager of the Wapi Eagle Casino and a tribal chief, of high social status. Jackson is an accomplice in the corruption conspiracy. Her motive is financial — the casino's expansion via the waterfront-development plan would profit her tribe and her personally. She is concealing the conspiracy and the planting of Native American bones at Mayor Adams's waterfront-development site (a forgery designed to halt his project and damage his re-election). She has no alibi tested in the Rosie murder context because she is not directly present at the murder scene; she is the architect of the political corruption around it. Jackson is shielded by tribal-jurisdiction issues and political maneuvering and largely evades prosecution.
Other supporting characters: Gwen Eaton, Richmond's chief of staff and on-and-off girlfriend, a high-status white American woman, daughter of a U.S. Senator; uninvolved in the murder as a perpetrator but concealing a complicated history with her father. Janek Kovarsky, the Polish gangster connected to Stan's past, uninvolved in Rosie's murder. Mayor Lesley Adams, Richmond's incumbent rival, uninvolved in the murder. Bennet Ahmed, the high school teacher wrongly suspected and beaten in Season 1, fully uninvolved in the murder, suffering lasting injuries from the misdirected attack. Beau Soleil owner / "Adela", the operator of the escort service, uninvolved in the murder but concealing the operation.
Concealment behaviors are pervasive. Jamie conceals the entire conspiracy and the violence. Terry conceals the affair, the Beau Soleil work, and the murder itself. Ames conceals the affair and the conspiracy. Chief Jackson conceals the planted-bones forgery. Holder conceals the falsified Season 1 photograph until he confesses to Linden. Stan conceals his criminal past. Richmond concealed his planned suicide-by-drowning, fueling a season's worth of false leads. Rosie was concealing her Beau Soleil contact, her aunt's involvement in it, and the existence of her video evidence of the casino meeting. Mitch conceals her grief by leaving the family.
The resolution: Sarah Linden and Stephen Holder finally crack the case in the Season 2 finale "What I Know" (directed by Patty Jenkins, written by Veena Sud and Dan Nowak, aired June 17, 2012). The trigger is Ted Wright's offhand comment to Richmond that his grandson Jamie's stated alibi for the night of Rosie's death does not hold up. Richmond confronts Jamie at the campaign office. Jamie confesses everything to Richmond: the planting of the Native American bones at Mayor Adams's development site to sink Adams's campaign; the secret meeting between himself, Chief Nicole Jackson, and Michael Ames at the Wapi Eagle Casino's tenth-floor construction site to coordinate the corruption; Rosie's accidental presence at the meeting with her video camera; Jamie's pursuit of Rosie through the casino and into the woods; Jamie's beating of Rosie and the placing of her body, bound and unconscious, into the trunk of a Richmond campaign car; the call to Ames for help; Ames's arrival at Discovery Park lake with Terry Marek; Ames's refusal to actively kill Rosie; Terry's overhearing of Ames's refusal and her realization that it meant Ames would never leave his wife for her; and Terry's spontaneous decision to release the brake and push the car into the lake, drowning the bound girl whom she did not realize was her own niece. Linden and Holder corner Jamie at the campaign office; Jamie is shot dead by Holder in the ensuing confrontation. The detectives then confront Terry, who breaks down and confesses to driving the car into the lake. Terry is arrested. Ames is implicated but, through Richmond's intervention upon becoming Mayor, evades meaningful prosecution. Chief Jackson similarly evades prosecution. Richmond is sworn in as Mayor of Seattle and immediately learns the full scope of what was done in his name; he closes the door on Gwen and accepts the office on a foundation of corruption he did not initiate but does not dismantle. The Larsen family receive in the mail a video Rosie had been making titled "What I Know," in which she expresses her dreams of travel and her love for her family — a final message from the dead. Stan and Mitch reunite. The detectives sit outside the Larsens' home in a car; Holder gets a phone call about a fresh body found near Sea-Tac airport; Linden gets out of the car and refuses to pursue another case for the moment, asking laconically whether they really got "the bad guy" — the show's closing acknowledgment that the perpetrator-network has been only partially dismantled, that Ames and Jackson have escaped, and that institutional corruption has continued. The villains of Season 2 are therefore Jamie Wright (motivated by power, glory, and possessive devotion to Richmond — beat Rosie and placed her in the trunk), Terry Marek (motivated by jealousy and entitlement around her affair with Ames — actually drowned Rosie, not knowing the victim's identity), Michael Ames (motivated by financial gain — accomplice to the cover-up, escapes justice), Nicole Jackson (motivated by financial gain — corruption mastermind, escapes justice), and the institutional system that lets two of the four walk free.
