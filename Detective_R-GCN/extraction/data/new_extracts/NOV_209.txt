The Blessing Way (Tony Hillerman, 1970) — Plot Synopsis

The novel — Tony Hillerman's debut and the first book of what would become the Joe Leaphorn / Jim Chee series — is set on the Navajo Reservation spanning Arizona, New Mexico, and Utah. Note for the dataset: Jim Chee does not appear in this novel; he was introduced ten years later in People of Darkness (1980). The amazon listing of The Blessing Way as "A Leaphorn and Chee Novel Book 1" is a marketing fiction. Note also that the central rite the novel actually depicts is the Enemy Way, not the Blessing Way; Hillerman's preferred title was The Enemy Way and his editor changed it.

The two central protagonists, both uninvolved in any crime as perpetrators, are Lieutenant Joe Leaphorn, a forty-year-old Navajo Tribal Police detective based in Window Rock, Arizona, married to Emma Leaphorn (a Navajo traditionalist; uninvolved, off-page), and Bergen McKee, a divorced white anthropology professor at the University of New Mexico, an old college friend of Leaphorn, who has come to the reservation to research Navajo witchcraft beliefs for a book. McKee is uninvolved in any crime as a perpetrator and becomes a victim of attempted murder in the climax. Leaphorn's role evolved during drafting; the editors at Harper & Row asked Hillerman to expand Leaphorn after early drafts had McKee as the sole lead. Both men investigate parallel threads that converge.

McKee travels to the Many Ruins Canyon area accompanied by his colleague Dr. Jeremy Canfield, an archaeologist and old friend, of middle social status, married, uninvolved in any crime as a perpetrator. Canfield becomes a victim — he is murdered by the conspirators midway through the novel. The two men plan to be joined by Ellen Leon, a young white woman, the fiancée of one of the conspirators (see below), a researcher of middle social status. Ellen Leon is uninvolved in any crime as a perpetrator; she has been used by her fiancé as cover to reassure his colleagues about his presence in the canyon and is herself in mortal danger by the climax. She becomes a romantic interest for Bergen McKee and is shot but survives. Her hidden relationship is that she is the unwitting fiancée of Jimmy W. Hall, the central villain — she does not know what Hall is actually doing in the canyons.

Joe Leaphorn's parallel investigation begins with Luis Horseman, a young Navajo man who has fled into the desert because he believes he killed a man in a knife fight in Portland, Oregon. Luis Horseman is initially a fugitive but ultimately a victim. He is uninvolved in any of the broader crimes as a perpetrator. He is killed because he stumbles across the conspirators' operation in the canyons; his body is found in a remote location with his mouth full of sand — a Navajo witchcraft killing-signature meant to evoke a "Navajo Wolf" or skinwalker. Luis Horseman has no motive against anyone and no relevant alibi to assess. His body is the catalyst for the case. His brother Billy Nez appears at the Tsosie family's Enemy Way ceremony, where he reveals he found a hat belonging to the supposed witch — an important physical clue. Billy Nez is uninvolved in any crime as a perpetrator and serves as a young witness whose vengeance Leaphorn must restrain, since Billy intends to track the "witch" and kill him. The Tsosie family, who host the Enemy Way ceremony to purge the witchcraft afflicting their livestock and community, are uninvolved as perpetrators; they are witnesses and victims in the cultural sense (their sheep have been slaughtered as part of the conspirators' campaign of terror). Ben Yazzie, a Navajo whose hogan McKee visits, is uninvolved; his livestock have likewise been killed and his family has fled.

The villains of the novel are a three-man Cold War espionage ring operating in the Many Ruins Canyon area. Jimmy W. Hall is the mastermind. He is a white, middle-aged American engineer or scientist with access to federal missile-testing facilities, of high-middle social status, engaged to Ellen Leon. Hall's motive is financial: he is collecting radar telemetry data on missile tests from the Tonopah Range in Utah down to the White Sands Proving Grounds in New Mexico, with the intent to sell the data on the black market for a sum he expects will reach approximately one million dollars (a substantial sum in 1970). Hall has the public alibi of being a legitimate professional with a research presence in the canyons; he is concealing the fact that he is running a clandestine signals-intelligence operation. He is concealing the fact that he killed Dr. Canfield, his accomplices killed Luis Horseman, and he intends to kill Bergen McKee, Ellen Leon, and anyone else who stumbles into the canyon. Hall is physically present at the crime scenes during the killings and has no genuine alibi for any of them; his alibi is structural rather than testimonial — no one is supposed to know he is there at all.

George Jackson (called "the Big Navajo" or simply "George" through much of the narrative) is Hall's chief enforcer. He is a Navajo man originally from Los Angeles with ties to organized crime, a high-paid professional criminal hired specifically because his physical appearance allows him to impersonate a Navajo Wolf — a witch-figure in Navajo belief. Jackson's motive is purely financial; he expects a substantial share of Hall's payoff. Jackson has no alibi for the murders of Luis Horseman and Dr. Canfield because he committed them, and he is physically present at every crime scene. He is concealing the fact that he is the "witch" terrorizing the local Navajo families: he has been deliberately killing livestock, leaving witch-signs, wearing a Navajo Wolf costume (the hat Billy Nez finds belonged to him), and parking his vehicle and camp far from water — a tell that Leaphorn picks up on, since any genuine local Navajo would camp near water, marking the witch as a stranger. Jackson's hidden role is that he is staging witchcraft events as a tactic to drive Navajo families out of the canyon area so that Hall's radar dishes can operate undetected. He killed Luis Horseman by suffocating him with sand because Horseman wandered into the operation by accident.

Eddie is Hall's third confederate, a non-Native American hired thug, working for cash. His motive is financial. He has no alibi for the period of the climax and is physically present in the canyon. He is concealing his role in the operation. Eddie shoots Ellen Leon during the climactic confrontation and falls to his death from a cliff while pursuing McKee.

The hidden relationships in the novel are those between the three conspirators (Hall, George Jackson, Eddie) — who are pretending to be unconnected, with Hall publicly presenting himself as a legitimate researcher, Jackson as a wandering Navajo, and Eddie as an unrelated drifter — and the relationship between Hall and Ellen Leon, who believes herself to be the fiancée of an honest man and has no idea about the espionage operation. Dr. Canfield's signing of his note "John" rather than his actual name "Jeremy" is a clue that the note was written under duress or by his killer, planted to make McKee believe Canfield had voluntarily left.

Concealment behaviors are pervasive. Hall is concealing the radar setup and the murders. Jackson is concealing his identity behind the Navajo Wolf disguise and is concealing the witchcraft scare campaign as the cover it is. Eddie is concealing his presence entirely. Ellen Leon is unwittingly concealing nothing but is being used to provide cover for Hall. The note signed "John" is concealment of Canfield's already-completed murder.

Presence at crime scenes: Hall, Jackson, and Eddie are physically present at all the canyon-area killings. McKee and Leaphorn arrive separately. Billy Nez, hunting the "witch," is present in the canyons during the climax but uninvolved as a perpetrator. Bergen McKee is present at the canyon and survives the climactic violence after killing Eddie (who falls to his death pursuing him) and George Jackson (whom he kills with an improvised wooden spear launched from a sapling-catapult after Jackson shoots him in the back). Joe Leaphorn arrives at the climax having tracked Billy Nez and pieced together the operation independently.

The resolution: Joe Leaphorn solves the case by combining traditional Navajo cultural knowledge with detective work. Key clues include the camp parked too far from water (proving the "witch" was a stranger, not a local Navajo), the missing hat Billy Nez had retrieved (matching Jackson rather than any local witch), the discovery of electric cable and radar equipment in a side canyon, and the realization that an apparent reward case for a missing Army missile was a dead end — pointing toward live espionage rather than scavenging. Leaphorn also realizes that no real Navajo would kill another Navajo for profit in the way Luis Horseman was killed, which redirects suspicion toward an outsider impersonating a Navajo. McKee, alone in the canyon, is shot in the back by George Jackson but kills him by improvising a sapling-and-electrical-cable catapult and launching a sharpened pine stake through Jackson's gun-sight line. Eddie shoots Ellen Leon (who survives) and dies falling off a cliff while pursuing McKee. McKee, wounded, walks toward Hall expecting help; Hall instead tries to manipulate Billy Nez into shooting McKee by claiming McKee is the real witch. Leaphorn arrives in time to prevent this. Hall, cornered, commits suicide by shooting himself in front of Leaphorn while McKee has fainted from blood loss. The federal government, in classic Cold War-era fashion, covers up the affair: the official story released to the public is that Dr. Canfield and Hall died in a car accident that injured McKee and Ellen Leon; George Jackson and Eddie are erased from the record entirely as if they never existed. McKee recovers in the hospital, having confessed his two killings to Leaphorn, and receives a letter from Ellen Leon — now also recovering — expressing her intention to visit, suggesting a future relationship between them. Joe Leaphorn closes the local case and returns to Window Rock and his wife Emma. The witchcraft that the Tsosie family had attributed to a Navajo Wolf turns out to have been a deliberately staged campaign by a Cold War black-market operative and his Los Angeles-Navajo enforcer — a fully human, financially motivated crime dressed up in Navajo supernatural trappings.
