--- BACKGROUND AND CONTEXT ---

"Curtain" is the final episode of ITV's Agatha Christie's Poirot, first broadcast
on November 13, 2013. It is directed by Hettie Macdonald and stars David Suchet
as Hercule Poirot and Hugh Fraser as Captain Hastings in their final appearances
in these roles after twenty-three years. The episode is based on Christie's
1975 novel of the same name — a novel Christie wrote during the Second World
War and placed in a safe deposit box, instructing her publisher to release it
after her death if she did not choose to publish it herself. She eventually
published it in 1975, the year before her death, as a deliberate farewell to
the character she had created in 1920.

"Curtain" is not simply a finale for the television series. It is the death
of Poirot — planned since the beginning of the Suchet series, built toward
across twenty-three years and seventy-two episodes, and finally executed in
an episode that is as ambitious and as emotionally demanding as Christie's
novel. The episode received extraordinary critical attention and is widely
considered one of the finest series finales in British television history.

David Suchet, who is a devout Christian, has spoken about how deeply meaningful
the Poirot character and his ending were to him personally. His preparation
for the final episode and the specific way he plays Poirot in his final hours
reflect an investment in the character that goes beyond the professional.

--- THE RETURN TO STYLES ---

Hastings — now elderly, recently widowed, his daughter Judith grown — is invited
by Poirot to Styles Court, the country house in Essex where they first worked
together in "The Mysterious Affair at Styles" in 1916. Styles has become a
guest house, and Poirot, who is gravely ill with arthritis and heart disease
and who is confined to a wheelchair for much of his movement, has taken a room
there.

The return to Styles is deliberate and significant. Poirot has chosen to end
things — the case, his life — in the place where everything began. The circle
is being closed.

Hastings is troubled by his friend's condition. Poirot is not the man he was:
physically diminished, dependent on medication, spending much of his time in
his room. But his mind — the grey cells — are entirely intact, and this
disproportion between the failing body and the still-functioning intelligence
is one of the episode's most sustained and most affecting elements. Poirot
knows exactly what he is doing. He has always known exactly what he is doing.

--- THE GUESTS AT STYLES ---

The house party at Styles contains a specific set of people whose relationships
to each other and to a particular history are the case's subject:

Colonel Luttrell and his wife Daisy — the owners of the guest house, an unhappy
couple in the specific way of people who have stayed together past the point
where staying together makes sense.

Judith Hastings — Hastings's daughter, who has taken a position as assistant
to a scientist at Styles. She is young, idealistic, and susceptible to the
specific moral framework of the man she is working for.

Dr. Franklin and his wife Barbara — a research scientist and his invalid wife.
Franklin is focused on his research with an intensity that leaves little room
for attention to anything else. Barbara Franklin is unwell and unhappy.

Major Allerton — a man of surface charm and private unpleasantness, who has
a specific romantic interest in Judith Hastings that Hastings finds alarming
for reasons he cannot entirely articulate.

Stephen Norton — an apparently quiet, birdwatching enthusiast who is present
at Styles in a capacity that seems innocuous.

--- X: THE INVISIBLE KILLER ---

Poirot, in his letters to Hastings and in their conversations at Styles, explains
the nature of the case he has come to Styles to address. There have been deaths
connected to specific gatherings — murders that were committed but that cannot
be attributed to a single killer because the actual killer's method is unlike
anything the detection of murder usually encounters.

The killer — whom Poirot calls "X" — does not commit murder directly. X
identifies people who have the potential or the motive to kill someone specific
in their lives, and then manipulates them — through suggestion, through specific
conversational techniques, through the gradual cultivation of a murderous
impulse — into committing the murder. X is never at the scene. X never touches
the victim. X simply arranges the conditions in which someone else's potential
for murder becomes actual.

This method makes X essentially undetectable through conventional investigation:
there is a murder, there is a murderer, and the murderer is prosecuted —
but the person who engineered the murder is never visible in the chain of
evidence. X has been responsible for multiple deaths without ever technically
committing a crime that can be proved.

The concept — the instigator, the person who creates murders without committing
them — is one of Christie's most psychologically sophisticated constructions,
and it is the most appropriate way possible to close a series that has been
about the detection of murder for seventy-two episodes: the final case is
one where detection in the conventional sense cannot work, where the grey cells
must operate on a level above the physical evidence.

--- NORTON AS X ---

Poirot has identified Stephen Norton as X. Norton — the quiet birdwatcher,
the apparently innocuous presence at Styles — is the person who has been
engineering deaths across multiple gatherings and who is present at Styles now
because there is another death to be arranged.

The specific target at Styles — who Norton has identified as susceptible and
who he has been working on — becomes clear through the investigation of the
relationships among the guests. Norton's specific method: the conversations
that plant ideas, the specific suggestions that cultivate an existing tendency
toward violence, the careful management of information that creates the
conditions for a specific person to take a specific action against a specific
other person.

Poirot understands what Norton is and what he is doing. He cannot prove it
through evidence. No court would convict Norton of anything based on what
Poirot knows. And Norton is going to cause someone at Styles to commit murder
unless Poirot acts.

--- POIROT'S DECISION ---

This is the moral and dramatic heart of "Curtain," and it is what makes the
episode — and Christie's novel — one of the most extraordinary things she ever
created.

Poirot kills Norton.

He enters Norton's room during the night and shoots him, staging the death to
appear as suicide. He uses a method that creates the specific appearance of
self-inflicted death. He does what needs to be done to prevent the murder
that Norton is engineering and to end Norton's career of instigated killings.

This is not, by any conventional moral or legal standard, a justified act.
Poirot kills a man who has not, in any technically provable sense, committed
a crime at Styles. The deaths he has engineered are real, but the evidence
for his responsibility is not admissible in any court. What Poirot does is
the specific act of a man who has determined that the law cannot solve this
problem and that he is the only person who can.

The specific weight of this choice — for Poirot, who has spent his career
in the service of the law, who has believed in the law's capacity to serve
justice, who has never allowed himself to be the instrument of extra-legal
violence — is immense. Christie understood that weight. She wrote it to be
immense. And the episode honors that weight absolutely.

Poirot conceals what he did. He stages the suicide. He writes everything down
in a manuscript that is given to Hastings after his death — the full account
of what he knew about X and what he did about it.

--- POIROT'S DEATH ---

After killing Norton, Poirot has one more thing to do. He has been managing
his heart condition with medication — specifically, the medication that keeps
his heart stable is something he has been carefully controlling. He stops
taking it.

He dies shortly thereafter, alone in his room at Styles, having completed
what he came to Styles to complete. His death is not quite suicide in the
conventional sense and not quite natural death: it is the specific act of a
man who has done what he needed to do and who has now decided that his account
is settled.

Hastings finds him. The specific quality of Hastings's grief — for the friend
he has had since 1916, for the man who has been the most significant professional
and personal relationship of his life — is rendered with Hugh Fraser's specific
capability: a grief that is completely real, completely specific, and that the
viewer who has spent twenty-three years with these two men will feel as their own.

--- THE MANUSCRIPT ---

Hastings receives Poirot's manuscript after his death and reads the full
account of what Poirot knew about X and what he did about it. His response —
to what he learns about Norton, to what he learns about what Poirot did, and
to the specific quality of the farewell that Poirot has written for him — is
the episode's final emotional sequence.

The manuscript is Poirot's final gift to Hastings and his final apologia:
an explanation of what he did and why, offered to the one person whose judgment
he cares about, asking for understanding rather than approval.

--- THE MEANING OF THE ENDING ---

Christie's "Curtain" is a meditation on the relationship between law and justice,
between what the detection of crime can achieve and what it cannot, and between
the ideal of the detective as servant of the law and the reality of a detective
who is, after all, a human being with a human moral judgment that operates
in a world where the law is not sufficient to address everything that needs
to be addressed.

Poirot kills Norton not because he has abandoned his belief in the law but
because he understands, in the specific situation he faces, that the law cannot
reach this particular evil. His act is not triumphant or comfortable. It costs
him something that the law cannot give back — the consistency of a life lived
in the service of order. He does it anyway.

The Suchet series has been building to this for twenty-three years. The specific
quality of Suchet's Poirot — his moral seriousness, his genuine belief in the
law and its purposes, his specific relationship to the concept of civilisation
and what it requires — makes the final act both surprising and entirely
inevitable in retrospect. Of course Poirot does this. Of course it costs him
everything. Of course he accepts that cost.

--- NOTES ON THE EPISODE ---

"Curtain" is one of the finest things British television produced in the 2010s.
Hettie Macdonald's direction is at its most restrained — the episode lets
its characters and its situation do the work, without the theatrical excess
that the material might have invited. Suchet's performance in his final scenes
is among the best he has given in the entire run: a man who is dying, who has
done something that cannot be undone, who has left behind the thing he values
most (his integrity as an officer of the law) in the service of a good that
he could not achieve through legitimate means, and who meets his death without
self-pity and without evasion. Hugh Fraser's Hastings is the emotional register
of the viewer throughout — the person who loves Poirot and who must face his
loss and his act and his explanation.
