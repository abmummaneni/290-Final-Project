TVE_164 | A Good Girl's Guide to Murder | Creator: Holly Jackson (adapted by Poppy Cogan) | TV Series
================================================================================
FULL SERIES SYNOPSIS — SPOILERS INCLUDED
================================================================================

--- BACKGROUND AND CONTEXT ---

"A Good Girl's Guide to Murder" is a BBC Three / Netflix series broadcast
in 2024, adapted from Holly Jackson's 2019 young adult crime novel of the
same name by screenwriter Poppy Cogan. It stars Emma Myers as Pippa Fitz-Amobi
and Zain Iqbal as Ravi Singh. The series is a six-episode adaptation covering
the full plot of the first novel in Jackson's trilogy, set in a fictional
English small town called Little Kilton.

The show was a significant streaming success and attracted considerable attention
for its smart, character-driven adaptation of a YA novel that takes its
mystery seriously. Emma Myers's performance as Pip is one of its primary
strengths — she captures the specific quality of a very intelligent, obsessively
focused teenager without making the character either annoying or implausibly
adult.

--- THE COLD CASE: ANDIE BELL ---

Five years before the story's present day, a seventeen-year-old girl named
Andie Bell disappeared from Little Kilton. Her disappearance was quickly
attributed to murder: the prime suspect was her boyfriend, Sal Singh, a sixth-
form student. Sal killed himself shortly after Andie's disappearance, which
was widely interpreted as a confession. No body was ever found, but Sal's
guilt became the accepted local narrative.

The case has been closed — officially and socially. Sal Singh is dead. The
town has moved on.

--- PIPPA FITZ-AMOBI ---

Pip is seventeen — the same age Andie Bell was when she disappeared. She is
about to enter her final year of sixth form and has chosen, as her Extended
Project Qualification (a substantial independent research project), to
investigate the Andie Bell case. Her specific thesis: she does not believe
Sal Singh killed Andie Bell.

Her reasons are both analytical and personal. Analytically, the case has
specific unanswered questions — the absence of Andie's body, specific
inconsistencies in the established timeline, aspects of Sal's behavior and
character that don't fit the accepted narrative of a guilty person who
killed himself to avoid prosecution.

Personally, Pip grew up with Sal's younger brother Ravi Singh as a near-
neighbour. She knew the Singh family. She watched what the accusation did
to them. And she has never fully believed that the boy she knew — through
his family — was capable of what he was accused of.

--- RAVI SINGH ---

Ravi is Sal's younger brother, now seventeen himself. He is also living with
the legacy of his brother's death and the public accusation that destroyed
his family's reputation. He is angry, guarded, and initially deeply hostile
to Pip's project — another person picking over his family's wound, constructing
a narrative about his dead brother for their own purposes.

His transition from hostility to partnership with Pip is one of the series'
central character arcs. He comes to trust her because she is actually seeking
the truth rather than confirming the existing narrative, and his specific
knowledge of his family and his brother becomes essential to the investigation.
The developing romantic tension between Pip and Ravi is handled with the light
touch that the best YA fiction uses.

--- THE INVESTIGATION BEGINS ---

Pip begins her research with the public record — police reports (obtained
through freedom of information), news coverage, and the established facts of
the case — and quickly identifies several people whose accounts of the events
around Andie's disappearance are worth re-examining.

Andie Bell was not quite the victim the public narrative presents. She was
beautiful and popular, but she was also involved in dealing drugs — specifically
selling Rohypnol at sixth-form parties. This hidden life, and the specific
people she dealt with or through, opens a very different picture of who might
have wanted her dead.

The investigation maps Andie's last known movements: where she was, who she
spoke to, what she was doing on the night she disappeared.

--- THE DRUG CONNECTION AND HOWIE BOWERS ---

One of the most significant threads in the investigation is Andie's drug dealing.
She obtained the Rohypnol from a source — a man named Howie Bowers, a local
low-level drug dealer — and sold it at parties. This activity gave her a set
of connections and enemies outside the ordinary sixth-form social world.

The specific people who knew about Andie's dealing, who were connected to the
supply chain, and who might have had reason to silence her are established
through Pip's investigation.

--- THE NIGHT OF THE DISAPPEARANCE: THE TIMELINE ---

Pip reconstructs the timeline of Andie's last night with meticulous attention.
Andie was supposed to meet someone. Her phone records, her movements, and
the accounts of people who saw her indicate a specific sequence of events
that does not fit the narrative of Sal as the last person to see her.

Sal, in fact, has a specific alibi for part of the relevant evening that was
never properly examined — because once he became the accepted suspect and then
killed himself, the investigation stopped looking.

The alibi involves a specific person whose account of being with Sal during
the critical window would, if established, significantly weaken the case
against him. This person has never come forward.

--- THE LIVING KILLER ---

As Pip's investigation develops, it becomes clear that Andie Bell is not
simply missing — she is dead, and her body has been concealed. And the person
who killed her has been living in Little Kilton, part of the community, through
the five years since her death.

The specific identity of the real killer, and their motive, is assembled through
Pip's investigation of the drug dealing, the timeline, and the specific
relationships in Andie's life that the original investigation never properly
examined.

--- MAX HASTINGS ---

One of the most disturbing elements of the investigation is the discovery of
what Andie's Rohypnol dealing was actually being used for. Max Hastings is a
popular and apparently well-liked sixth-former whose specific conduct with the
drug — using it to drug and assault girls at parties — is exposed through
Pip's investigation.

Max is not Andie's killer, but his conduct — and the discovery of evidence
of it — is one of the investigation's most significant findings, and his
attempt to suppress that evidence puts Pip in direct danger. His exposure
is one of the series' most emotionally charged resolutions.

--- THE KILLER: JASON BELL ---

The killer of Andie Bell is her own father, Jason Bell.

The full picture: Andie discovered something about her father — a specific
wrong he had done, a secret he was protecting — and confronted him with it.
The confrontation escalated. Jason Bell killed his daughter and concealed
her body, then allowed the investigation to settle on Sal Singh, who had no
effective means of defence against the community's need to believe in a
guilty party.

Sal Singh, facing accusation in a community that had already convicted him,
and without the specific piece of exculpatory evidence that would have supported
his alibi, killed himself. He was innocent. His death was an additional
consequence of Jason Bell's crime.

The discovery of where Andie's body was concealed is one of the investigation's
final pieces: Pip locates the remains, which confirms both Andie's death and
the specific circumstances that only her killer could have arranged.

--- NAOMI AND THE PERSONAL STAKES ---

Pip's friend Naomi Ward — whose brother Connor was involved in Andie's world —
is caught in the investigation's expanding circle. The specific information
Naomi holds and her attempts to protect her family create the personal stakes
that make the investigation not just intellectual but emotionally costly for Pip.

The investigation of Andie's death touches almost everyone in Pip's world in
some way, which is both one of the series' most realistic elements — small
towns are small — and one of its most dramatically effective.

--- RESOLUTION ---

Jason Bell is exposed as Andie's killer. The evidence Pip has assembled —
the reconstructed timeline, the body's location, the specific motive — is
sufficient to bring the case to the police and to ensure that the truth is
publicly established.

Sal Singh's innocence is confirmed. His family — Ravi, his parents — receive
the vindication that five years of carrying public accusation denied them.

Max Hastings's crimes are also exposed, as a collateral but important
consequence of the investigation.

Pip's EPQ project achieves its purpose: the truth of what happened to Andie
Bell and what happened to Sal Singh is established. The cost — the danger
Pip put herself in, the specific things she discovered about the people around
her — is real and not entirely resolved by the resolution.

--- NOTES ON THE SERIES ---

"A Good Girl's Guide to Murder" is one of the most successful recent UK
adaptations of a YA crime novel because it respects both the intelligence of
its source material and the intelligence of its audience. Pip's investigation
is genuinely logical — the clues are fair, the deductions are grounded —
and the show does not condescend to its teenage protagonists by making them
either magically competent or comically naïve. The discovery that Max Hastings's
conduct was enabled by Andie's drug supply gives the series a specific
contemporary resonance that the novel carries and the adaptation maintains.
