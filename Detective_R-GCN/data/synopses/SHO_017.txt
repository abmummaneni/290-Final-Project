SHO_017 | The Tape-Measure Murder | Agatha Christie | Short Story
================================================================================
FULL PLOT SYNOPSIS — SPOILERS INCLUDED
================================================================================

--- BACKGROUND AND CONTEXT ---

"The Tape-Measure Murder" is a Miss Marple short story by Agatha Christie,
first published in 1941 in the United States in "This Week" magazine and later
collected in "Three Blind Mice and Other Stories" (1950) in the US and in "Miss
Marple's Final Cases" (1979) in the UK. It is set in the village of St. Mary
Mead, Miss Marple's home village, and is a characteristic example of the
St. Mary Mead stories: a crime in a close, familiar community that Miss Marple
solves through analogy with the village's own social world rather than through
any physical heroics.

--- THE VICTIM AND THE DISCOVERY ---

Mrs. Spenlow, a respectable married woman in the village, is found strangled
in her own cottage. The discovery is made by Miss Politt, the local dressmaker,
who had an appointment with Mrs. Spenlow for a fitting. Miss Politt arrives
at the cottage, finds the door unlocked, enters, and discovers Mrs. Spenlow
dead on the floor — strangled with what appears to be a length of tape measure,
or at least with something thin and cord-like.

The local police, in the person of Inspector Slack, are called. Inspector Slack
is a recurring figure in the St. Mary Mead stories — a competent but slightly
arrogant policeman who tends to underestimate Miss Marple and who represents
the official investigative view that the solution to crimes is found through
procedure rather than through village gossip and social comparison.

--- THE SUSPECTS ---

The investigation focuses quickly on the obvious candidate: Mr. Spenlow,
the dead woman's husband. The Spenlows' marriage was not a happy one — this
is established through village testimony and the general awareness of the
small community that knows everyone's business. Mr. Spenlow had reason to
wish his wife gone. He was seen in the area at the relevant time.

However, Mr. Spenlow has an alibi of sorts: he claims to have been elsewhere,
and there are witnesses to various aspects of his movements.

Miss Politt, the dressmaker who found the body, is also a figure of interest.
Her appointment at the cottage, and her specific connection to the tape measure
that may or may not have been the murder weapon, draws scrutiny.

--- MISS MARPLE'S OBSERVATION ---

Miss Marple becomes involved through the simple mechanism of being present
in the village — she is, as always, a keen observer of the social world around
her. She hears the details of the crime from various village sources and almost
immediately notices something that the official investigation has not adequately
examined.

The specific detail that catches Miss Marple's attention involves the tape
measure itself and the question of where it came from. Miss Politt is a
dressmaker — she carries tape measures professionally. The tape measure
associated with the murder is connected to her in a specific way that, when
examined, raises questions about the sequence of events.

Miss Marple also applies her characteristic method: she finds a parallel in
village life. The situation of the Spenlows — the unhappy marriage, the
husband with motive, the convenient discovery by a third party — reminds her
of a specific comparable situation from St. Mary Mead's social history. The
comparison illuminates something about how such situations tend to work and
what the truth usually turns out to be.

--- THE SOLUTION ---

The killer is not Mr. Spenlow, despite his apparent motive and the suspicion
that attaches to him naturally. The killer is Miss Politt, the dressmaker.

Miss Politt had her own reason to wish Mrs. Spenlow dead — a reason rooted in
the specific relationships of the village world that Miss Marple has the patience
and attention to reconstruct. She came to the cottage not for a fitting but to
murder Mrs. Spenlow, and then staged the discovery to make herself seem simply
an innocent witness who happened to find the body.

The tape measure — Miss Politt's professional tool, carried as part of her
dressmaking equipment — was the murder weapon. The detail that Miss Marple
identified as significant was something about the tape measure or Miss Politt's
account of it that did not square with the innocent explanation.

The police, directed by Miss Marple, are able to confirm the truth of her
analysis. Miss Politt's guilt is established and she is arrested.

--- MARPLE'S METHOD ---

The story is a clean example of Miss Marple's characteristic approach: she
finds the human truth of a situation by recognizing its pattern from village
life rather than by forensic investigation. The specific village parallel she
invokes is not described in elaborate detail — Christie keeps these comparisons
economical in the short stories — but it is sufficient to direct her suspicion
to the right person. She then provides enough specific observation (about the
tape measure, about Miss Politt's account of the discovery) to give the police
something concrete to work with.

Inspector Slack, characteristically, is initially unreceptive to Miss Marple's
contribution and then forced to acknowledge its value.
