--- BACKGROUND AND CONTEXT ---

"Spiral" (French: Engrenages) Series 3 was broadcast on Canal+ in France in
2011 and reached international audiences through BBC Four in the same year.
It consists of ten episodes and continues the show's characteristic format:
a single central criminal investigation conducted simultaneously by the police
brigade criminelle under Captain Laure Berthaud and by the examining magistrate
system, with the parallel world of the Paris defence bar represented by
Joséphine Karlsson.

Series 3 is widely regarded as the finest of the Spiral seasons — the point
at which the show achieved its fullest expression of what it was trying to
do and produced television of genuine distinction. The central case is one of
the series' most disturbing and most complex, involving organised crime with
political protection, a killer of unusual psychology, and a specific form of
institutional corruption that the show handles with its characteristic refusal
of simplification.

The series also marks a significant development in several of the show's
ongoing character arcs: Laure's personal life reaches a crisis point, Gilou's
relationship with the criminal informant world generates consequences that
cannot be contained, and Judge Roban's corruption investigation enters a new
and more dangerous phase.

--- THE CENTRAL CASE: THE BODY PARTS ---

Series 3 opens with a discovery that sets the tone for everything that follows:
body parts — human remains — are found in the Seine and in locations around
Paris. The remains are those of multiple victims, and the specific condition
and distribution of the body parts suggest not a single crime but an ongoing
operation: someone has been killing people and disposing of their bodies in
pieces over an extended period, and the remains are only now surfacing.

The investigation of the body parts requires establishing how many victims
there are, who they are, when they were killed, and what the specific
circumstances of their deaths were. The forensic challenge is substantial:
incomplete remains, multiple victims, an unknown timeline, and a disposal
method designed to make identification as difficult as possible.

Laure's team begins the procedural work of establishing the victims' identities
through DNA analysis, dental records, and the specific physical evidence of
how the remains were prepared and disposed of. This work is painstaking and
grim, and the episode establishes from the beginning that Series 3 will operate
in significantly darker territory than the previous series.

--- THE VICTIMS AND THEIR WORLD ---

As the victims are identified, a picture emerges of the world they inhabited:
young people — some of them immigrants, some of them connected to Paris's
criminal margins, some of them simply vulnerable people whose specific
circumstances put them in the wrong place at the wrong time.

The investigation of who these people were and how they came to be where they
ended up leads the team into a specific criminal network operating in Paris —
one connected to the sex industry, to exploitation of vulnerable migrants, and
to the specific mechanisms by which people in precarious legal situations are
made available for harm. This is Spiral's characteristic social territory:
the investigation of violence is simultaneously the investigation of the
conditions that make that violence possible, and those conditions are always
rooted in specific social and institutional failures.

--- THE KILLER: THE SURGEON ---

The central perpetrator in Series 3 is a man of specific and unusual psychology —
a killer who operates with methodical precision and who has been able to kill
multiple people and dispose of their remains without detection for a significant
period. The investigation gradually builds a profile: someone with specific
knowledge of anatomy (hence the efficiency and precision of the dismemberment),
a specific operational capability that allows sustained criminality without
exposure, and a specific connection to the social world from which the victims
come.

The killer's specific psychology is one of the series' central interests:
this is not the explosive violence of passion or the opportunistic violence
of conventional crime, but something more calculated and more disturbing —
a person who has been killing methodically for reasons that are not immediately
reducible to standard criminal motivation. The investigation of what drives
the killer, what specific circumstances produced them, and what specific network
enabled their operation is one of Series 3's most sustained investigative threads.

--- GILOU AND THE INFORMANT WORLD ---

One of Series 3's most significant plot threads involves Gilou and the specific
consequences of his management of informants. Gilou's operational style —
the personal relationships he maintains with people in the criminal world,
the specific favors and accommodations that make those relationships productive —
has been established across the series as both effective and ethically
compromised. In Series 3, the accommodation of these relationships produces
a consequence that cannot be managed or contained.

A specific informant — someone Gilou has been protecting or working with
in ways that exceed official sanction — becomes implicated in the central
case's criminal network. The specific nature of the involvement: whether the
informant is a victim, a peripheral participant, or something more directly
implicated, drives a personal crisis for Gilou that is both professional and
moral. He has obligations in multiple directions — to the investigation, to
Laure, to the informant relationship — and these obligations are no longer
compatible.

Laure is aware of the situation and is managing it with the specific combination
of loyalty to Gilou and professional obligation that has defined her management
of their relationship throughout the series. The specific cost of that management —
what it takes from her, what it risks — reaches a new level in Series 3.

--- JUDGE ROBAN'S INVESTIGATION ---

Roban's parallel investigation into political corruption continues to develop
in Series 3. The corruption he has been pursuing connects to the central case's
criminal network — the specific mechanisms through which organized crime
maintains political protection are being illuminated simultaneously from the
judicial direction (Roban) and the police direction (Laure's team).

The specific politicians and institutional figures whose protection enables
the network are established with greater clarity in Series 3, and the specific
threat this represents to the investigation — and to Roban personally — becomes
more acute. A judge pursuing corruption among powerful people is in a specific
kind of danger that goes beyond the ordinary risks of investigative work, and
Series 3 makes this visible.

Roban's personal life — his loneliness, his specific devotion to the work as
a substitute for ordinary human connection, and his particular vulnerability
to the specific kinds of pressure that his position attracts — is explored with
the series' characteristic economy. He is not a tragic figure in a melodramatic
sense but a specific kind of principled person who has traded human warmth
for professional integrity and who is sometimes uncertain whether the trade
was correct.

--- JOSÉPHINE KARLSSON'S ARC ---

Joséphine's Series 3 arc involves her in the case through the defence work
she takes on — defending people connected to the criminal network, navigating
the specific ethical and practical complications this creates, and making choices
that advance the show's investigation of what it means to do this job in this
system.

In Series 3, Joséphine's relationship with the boundaries of professional
ethics moves to a new level. She makes choices in Series 3 that go beyond
the aggressive but technically legal conduct of the earlier series into territory
that is more clearly wrong — that is not simply clever manipulation of procedure
but specific acts of misconduct that have specific consequences.

Her arc in Series 3 is one of the series' most complex: she is not a villain,
but she is someone whose specific professional gifts and specific personal
history are pulling her toward a version of herself that the earlier Joséphine
would have found alarming. The show does not judge her simply, but it does
not excuse her either.

--- THE INVESTIGATION'S DEVELOPMENT ---

The investigation of the body parts and the killer expands through the series'
ten episodes with the procedural thoroughness that Spiral consistently delivers.
The forensic work on the remains, the establishment of the victims' identities
and histories, the mapping of the criminal network, and the gradual closing
in on the specific person responsible are all rendered with the show's
characteristic attention to the actual mechanics of investigation.

Key developments include:

The identification of specific victims and the reconstruction of their final
weeks — establishing how they came to be in the network and what specifically
happened to them. These reconstructions are among the series' most affecting
sequences: specific lives, specific people, specific circumstances that ended
in specific violence.

The investigation of the medical angle — the forensic evidence of the killer's
anatomical knowledge, and the specific professional world from which that
knowledge might come. The medical or paramedical world as a source of both
the knowledge and the access that the killer's operation requires.

The political protection dimension — the specific connections that have been
keeping the network operational despite its visibility to the police investigation,
and the specific institutional resistance to the investigation that these
connections generate.

--- THE RESOLUTION OF THE CENTRAL CASE ---

The killer is identified and the network is dismantled through the combined
work of Laure's team and Judge Roban's judicial process. The specific evidence
assembled — forensic, testimonial, and financial — is sufficient to bring the
immediate perpetrators to account.

The resolution is handled with Series 3's characteristic complexity: the
immediate criminal accountability is achieved, but the broader institutional
picture — the political protection, the systematic conditions that enabled the
operation — is addressed only partially. The show is consistent in its
understanding that solving a case is not the same as solving the conditions
that produced it.

The specific fate of the killer — the psychological resolution of what they
are and how they came to be that — is handled with the restraint that Spiral
applies to all its antagonists. The show does not provide cathartic explanations
or simple monsters; it provides specific people whose specific histories
produced specific harm.

--- THE PERSONAL FALLOUT ---

Series 3's conclusion generates significant personal consequences for the
central characters:

Gilou's situation — the informant relationship, the specific choices he made
in managing it — reaches a point of formal consequence. The question of what
the justice system will do with what he has done, and what Laure will do in
response, is one of the series' most emotionally weighted conclusions.

Laure herself is navigating both the professional closure of the case and the
personal reality of what the series has put her through. Her specific resilience —
the quality of someone who has been sustaining enormous pressure for a sustained
period and who is still, functionally, upright — is given full expression in
the final episodes.

Roban closes Series 3 with his corruption investigation at a specific point
of development that sets up Series 4: he has advanced it to a stage that makes
the conflict with the people he is investigating unavoidable, and the specific
nature of that conflict will define his arc going forward.

--- NOTES ON THE SERIES ---

Series 3 of Spiral is the show at its most fully realized. The central case
is constructed with unusual care — the gradual revelation of both the criminal
network and the killer's specific psychology is managed with the pacing and
the sustained attention that the best long-form television can achieve. The
character development — particularly Gilou's crisis, Roban's deepening isolation,
and Joséphine's trajectory toward genuine professional compromise — is given
space and specificity. The show's fundamental argument about the French justice
system — simultaneously capable and structurally compromised, staffed by people
who are individually often admirable and collectively often inadequate — is
stated most fully here.
