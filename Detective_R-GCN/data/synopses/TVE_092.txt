TVE_092 | Sherlock: The Hounds of Baskerville | Director: Paul McGuigan | TV Episode
================================================================================
FULL EPISODE SYNOPSIS — SPOILERS INCLUDED
================================================================================

--- BACKGROUND AND CONTEXT ---

"The Hounds of Baskerville" is the second episode of Series 2 of the BBC's
"Sherlock," first broadcast on January 8, 2012. It is directed by Paul McGuigan
and written by Mark Gatiss, and stars Benedict Cumberbatch as Sherlock Holmes
and Martin Freeman as Dr. John Watson. The episode is a contemporary reimagining
of Arthur Conan Doyle's most famous Holmes novel, "The Hound of the Baskervilles"
(1902) — the single most widely read Sherlock Holmes story and the one whose
central image (the enormous, apparently supernatural hound on the Devonshire
moor) has become the most iconic in the canon.

Gatiss's adaptation updates the central mystery from Victorian Dartmoor to
a contemporary government research facility on Dartmoor — Baskerville being
not a country house but a top-secret military base — and transforms the
supernatural hound legend into something that has a specific, rational, and
distinctly modern explanation. The episode is one of the most formally inventive
in the series, using Holmes's drug use and his specific perceptual vulnerabilities
in ways that the original novel doesn't, and delivering a solution that is both
faithful in spirit to Doyle's method and entirely surprising in its specific
mechanism.

--- HENRY KNIGHT ---

Henry Knight arrives at 221B Baker Street in a state of visible distress. He
is a young man who grew up near Dartmoor, and whose father died when Henry
was young in circumstances that Henry has been trying to process all his life.
His father was, he says, killed by a hound — a monstrous, enormous dog on the
moor near their home. Henry was a child when it happened and the memory has
the specific quality of childhood trauma: vivid, disordered, and resistant to
rational analysis.

Henry has been in therapy for years trying to process the memory. And now the
hound is back — or something that looks like it. He has seen it on the moor
again, and the experience has terrified him in ways that have made his daily
life increasingly impossible. He wants Holmes to tell him that what he saw was
real. Or that it wasn't. He wants something definitive to replace the uncertainty
that has been the defining condition of his life.

Sherlock, who is bored — cravingly, dangerously bored, at the beginning of
the episode in one of the series' funniest and most disturbing openings —
takes the case. Not because he is moved by Henry's distress but because the
case is interesting: a man haunted by a gigantic hound, a top-secret military
base, and a twenty-year-old death on a foggy moor. This is exactly the kind
of thing that makes life bearable.

--- DARTMOOR AND BASKERVILLE ---

Holmes and Watson travel to Dartmoor — the specific landscape of the original
novel, rendered here with the contemporary English countryside's particular
combination of tourist infrastructure and genuine wildness. The moor is real
and the moor is beautiful and the moor is the kind of place where, in the
right light and the wrong state of mind, almost anything might seem to be
present in the darkness.

The Baskerville facility — a government research establishment that Henry's
father worked at before his death — looms over the area in a different register
from the Victorian country house of Doyle's original. It is a place where
things are done that the public does not know about: weapons research, perhaps,
or something more specifically biological. The facility is militarised, access-
controlled, and staffed by people who are not forthcoming about what goes on
inside.

Holmes uses Mycroft's government connections to gain access to the facility —
exploiting his brother's official credentials to get himself and Watson inside
under false pretences. This is one of the episode's comic pleasures: Mycroft's
authority being borrowed without his explicit knowledge, in a way that will
presumably generate a specific kind of brotherly response.

--- WHAT HOLMES AND WATSON EXPERIENCE ---

This is the episode's central and most disturbing development. While investigating
on the moor near the facility — specifically in the area where Henry's father
died and where Henry has seen the hound — Holmes sees the hound himself.

This is an experience that should not be possible. Holmes is the rational
intelligence of the partnership: the person who does not see what is not there,
the person whose specific gift is the accurate observation of what is actually
present. And yet he sees, clearly and terrifyingly, an enormous dog on the moor.

Holmes's response to this experience is one of the episode's most interesting
character moments. He does not simply dismiss what he saw — he cannot, because
he saw it. He also does not accept it as a supernatural occurrence, because
he does not believe in supernatural occurrences. What he does is retreat into
the specific coldness that is his defense against emotional and perceptual
disruption: he becomes cold, dismissive, and briefly unkind to Watson in ways
that Watson finds confusing and hurtful, and that Holmes himself uses as a
kind of anchoring — if he can still be analytically cold, he is still himself,
which means what he saw can be explained.

Watson, who is also at the facility and who is also experiencing a sense of
fear and paranoia that he cannot entirely explain rationally, is separately
having his own disturbing experience.

Both of them are being affected by something external to themselves.

--- THE DRUG: UMBREIT'S COMPOUND ---

The solution to the hound of Baskerville — both the original appearance that
killed Henry's father and the current manifestations that Henry and then Holmes
have experienced — is a hallucinogenic drug. The Baskerville facility has been
conducting research into a specific compound — a powerful psychoactive substance
that induces vivid hallucinations of fear: whatever the person most fears becomes
visible to them. The drug was developed at Baskerville by Dr. Frankland, one
of the facility's scientists, and it was the subject of covert experimental
work that was not officially sanctioned.

The drug was released into the environment — into the fog, or into the water
supply of the area around the facility — and has been causing the experiences
that various people have been reporting for years. Henry Knight's father, who
worked at the facility, was exposed to it. Whatever he feared most became visible
to him on the moor. He died of a heart attack induced by the terror of the
hallucination.

Henry, as a child watching his father die in apparent terror of a giant dog,
interpreted what he saw as the specific image that his traumatised child's
mind produced: a hound, enormous and monstrous, killing his father. The image
is not what actually happened, but the fear was real and the death was real.

The facility's environmental contamination has been exposing the local population —
Henry included, and visitors to the area — to the drug intermittently, and the
specific configuration of certain locations on the moor creates conditions in
which the drug is concentrated. Holmes's experience on the moor was the result
of his own exposure: he saw, or thought he saw, whatever his specific fears
produce.

--- DR. FRANKLAND ---

The person responsible for the drug and for the specific use of it that killed
Henry's father is Dr. Frankland — a scientist at the facility who was working
on the compound covertly and who has been managing the specific consequences
of its environmental release. He is not a conventional villain in the sense
of planning calculated murders: he is a scientist who made something dangerous
and has been trying to manage the fallout of its uncontrolled deployment. His
specific culpability in Henry's father's death — whether it was recklessness,
negligence, or something more deliberate — is what the investigation must establish.

The confrontation on the moor in the final act — Holmes, Watson, Lestrade (who
has been called in), and Frankland — is one of the episode's most effectively
staged sequences, using the specific atmospheric properties of the Dartmoor
fog and the specific psychology of a man who understands what the drug does
and who has chosen to use it in a specific way in the confrontation's final
moments.

--- HOLMES AND WATSON'S RELATIONSHIP ---

One of the episode's most significant character developments is what Holmes's
brief moment of genuine fear — and his specific response to it — reveals about
his relationship with Watson. After his coldness following the moor experience,
Holmes eventually explains to Watson that what frightened him was not simply
seeing the hound but the specific experience of doubting his own perception —
of not being able to trust what he saw. This explanation is the closest Holmes
comes to vulnerability with Watson, and it is specific and honest: the thing
that most frightens Sherlock Holmes is the possibility of his own unreliability.
He needs his mind to work correctly. He is frightened of what happens when it
doesn't.

Watson's response — generous, warm, not making more of the moment than it needs
to be — is characteristic of his specific value to Holmes. He receives the
vulnerability without exploiting it, acknowledges it without dramatizing it,
and moves on. This is what Watson does for Holmes, and "The Hounds of Baskerville"
gives it one of its clearest expressions in the series.

--- RESOLUTION ---

The drug is identified, its source established, and Frankland's specific
culpability in the death of Henry's father is established. The investigation
closes the question that Henry brought to Baker Street: his father was not
killed by a supernatural hound. He was killed by a hallucination induced by
a government research compound. The specific horror of this resolution — that
the monster was real, just not in the way Henry understood — is one of the
episode's most characteristic qualities: the rational explanation is as
disturbing in its way as the supernatural one would have been.

Henry Knight is left with a different kind of closure from the one he sought.
He wanted the monster to be either real or not real. What he gets is that it
was both: real enough to kill his father, not real in the sense he feared.
Whether this is better or worse than either simple answer is not the episode's
concern to resolve.

Holmes and Watson return to London. The moor is the moor. The fog is the fog.
In the right conditions, in the right state of mind, what you see is not
necessarily what is there.

--- NOTES ON THE EPISODE ---

"The Hounds of Baskerville" is one of the finest single episodes of the Sherlock
series and one of the most successful adaptations of the original novel in any
medium. Gatiss's decision to root the supernatural hound in government research —
in the specific contemporary anxiety about secret state activities and their
consequences — is both appropriate and genuinely frightening. The episode's
most significant achievement is the sustained use of perceptual unreliability
as a dramatic tool: when Holmes doubts his own senses, the viewer doubts their
own reading of events, and the specific disorientation this produces is managed
with considerable skill. Paul McGuigan's direction of the moor sequences —
the fog, the specific quality of the light, the way the landscape seems to
shift between natural and threatening — is among the finest visual work in
the series.
