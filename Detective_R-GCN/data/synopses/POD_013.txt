THE CASE: THE DISAPPEARANCE OF MATTHEW HICKS


Background

Season 3 of Somebody Knows Something investigates the disappearance of Matthew Hicks,
a 17-year-old boy who vanished on February 12, 2007, in Truro, Nova Scotia, Canada.
Matthew was last seen late at night in the downtown area of Truro. He was wearing
light clothing for the winter conditions and was not prepared for an extended period
outdoors. He was never found. No body has been recovered. No official explanation
for his disappearance has ever been established.

Matthew was a teenager with the specific vulnerabilities of his age and situation.
He had a history of mental health challenges, which contributed to the range of
explanations initially considered for his disappearance — that he had wandered, that
he had suffered a mental health crisis, that he had left voluntarily. His mother,
Crystal Hicks, rejected the idea that he would have left without contact and has spent
years pursuing an alternative explanation.


Crystal Hicks

Matthew's mother Crystal is the family member who works directly with David Ridgen
throughout Season 3. Her determination to find answers about her son — her refusal
to accept the absence of explanation that has persisted for more than a decade — is
the season's emotional spine. Crystal is a mother who has been waiting for eleven years
to understand what happened to her child. Her specific quality of patient, stubborn
refusal to give up drives the investigation as much as Ridgen's journalism does.

Ridgen's relationship with Crystal — like his relationships with all the families
he works with — is respectful, genuine, and unexploitative. He is working for her,
in the sense that her need for answers is the purpose of the investigation, not the
podcast's narrative requirements.

THE INVESTIGATION


Truro and the Night of the Disappearance

Truro is a mid-sized Nova Scotia town — larger than the Cape Breton communities of
Season 2, more urban in character, with a town center and a visible street life even
in February. Matthew was seen in the downtown area late at night, which means there
were potential witnesses — people who were out that night, who may have seen him, who
may have encountered him or observed something relevant.

Ridgen's investigation begins with a systematic reconstruction of Matthew's last known
movements: where he was seen, by whom, at what time, and in what direction he was moving.
This reconstruction involves interviews with people who were in Truro's downtown that
night, cross-referencing their accounts with each other and with the available documentary
evidence.


Possible Explanations

The season examines multiple possible explanations for Matthew's disappearance:

A voluntary departure — that Matthew, for reasons connected to his mental health
situation or personal circumstances, chose to leave Truro. This explanation is examined
seriously, including investigation of whether there is any evidence of him anywhere
after the night of his disappearance. There is none.

An accident — that Matthew, in light clothing in February, succumbed to hypothermia
or suffered an accident and died in a location where his body has not been found.
Truro's geography, including the river and surrounding terrain, is examined in this
context.

Foul play — that Matthew encountered someone on the night of his disappearance who
caused him harm. The specific evidence that points toward this explanation, and the
specific people who may have knowledge of it, are pursued by Ridgen.


The Persons of Interest

As with previous SKS seasons, the investigation identifies specific individuals whose
connection to the events of the night of Matthew's disappearance, and whose behavior
and statements over the years since, make them relevant to the investigation. Ridgen
approaches these individuals — or attempts to — with his characteristic combination
of directness and care.

Some people who have knowledge speak to Ridgen. Others refuse. The pattern of who
speaks and who does not is itself informative.


Physical Searches

Season 3 includes physical searches of locations in and around Truro that are
connected to the possible scenarios for Matthew's disappearance. These searches —
conducted with appropriate authorizations and in some cases with professional search
teams — are both practically oriented and symbolically important for the family.

The act of searching — of physically going to the places where Matthew might be and
looking — is a form of advocacy for his existence and his importance that Crystal Hicks
has been maintaining for years.

THE MENTAL HEALTH DIMENSION

Season 3 handles Matthew's mental health history with particular care. The podcast
neither uses it to dismiss the possibility of foul play (the logic that because Matthew
had mental health challenges, his disappearance must be explicable by those challenges)
nor ignores it as irrelevant context. Ridgen is attentive to Crystal's knowledge of her
son — her specific understanding of what his mental health situation did and did not
make him likely to do — and treats her knowledge as the primary evidence on this question.

More broadly, Season 3 engages with the specific way that mental health history affects
how disappearances are investigated and how missing persons are treated by law enforcement.
The suggestion that someone with mental health challenges has simply wandered or chosen
to leave can, if accepted too quickly, result in an inadequate investigation of cases
that actually involve foul play.

RESOLUTION AND ONGOING STATUS

Season 3's investigation surfaces new information about the night of Matthew's
disappearance and builds a more complete picture of the circumstances. Specific
individuals whose knowledge of the events is suggested by witness accounts and
circumstantial evidence are identified and pursued.

The case does not formally resolve within the podcast. No charges were laid as a
direct result of Season 3. Matthew Hicks remains officially missing.

As with the previous SKS seasons, what the investigation achieves is a public record
of the case, a renewed attention to it in the community and from official investigators,
and the specific value — for Crystal Hicks and for Matthew — of a sustained, serious,
public investigation that refuses to accept the silence around his disappearance as
the final word.


THE EVOLUTION OF SKS

By Season 3, Somebody Knows Something has established itself as one of Canada's most
important true crime productions and one of the most ethically exemplary in the genre
globally. Ridgen's consistent approach — the family partnership, the active investigation,
the public appeal, the refusal of sensationalism — has created a show that serves a
genuine social function beyond entertainment: giving a platform to cold cases that
might otherwise remain permanently unexamined, and giving the families of the missing
a form of advocacy that conventional journalism and law enforcement have not provided.
