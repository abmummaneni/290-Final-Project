--- BACKGROUND AND CONTEXT ---

"Publish or Perish" is a Season 3 episode of Columbo, airing January 18, 1974.
It stars Jack Cassidy in his second of three appearances as a Columbo villain —
here playing a different character, the ruthless publisher Riley Greenleaf.
The episode is set in the world of publishing and features a contract killing
scheme with a layer of hired criminality that Columbo must cut through to reach
the man who ordered it. It is one of the more structurally complex early
Columbo episodes, since the killer does not himself commit the murder but
arranges for someone else to do it.

--- THE KILLER, THE VICTIM, AND THE MOTIVE ---

Riley Greenleaf is a powerful, wealthy, and thoroughly unscrupulous publisher.
He has a contract with a successful author, Alan Mallory, who writes enormously
profitable popular novels. Mallory, however, wants out of his contract with
Greenleaf in order to move to a different publisher. The contract's terms make
leaving legally complicated, but Mallory has found a way to break it or is
on the verge of doing so.

If Mallory leaves, Greenleaf loses the steady income from Mallory's books and
suffers a significant public and financial blow. Greenleaf decides that rather
than allow this to happen, Mallory must die.

--- THE HIRED KILLER: EDDIE KANE ---

Greenleaf does not commit the murder himself. He hires Eddie Kane, a small-time
criminal with a grudge against Mallory — Mallory was apparently the person
whose testimony or actions led to Kane's imprisonment, so Kane has personal
motive as well as financial incentive. Greenleaf provides Kane with money,
a gun, and the opportunity. Kane kills Mallory by rigging an explosion or
shooting, staged to look like an accident or a random criminal act.

The use of a hired killer is Greenleaf's attempt to insulate himself from the
crime: he was nowhere near the murder, he has no obvious connection to Kane,
and the police should have no reason to look past the apparent facts of Mallory's
death toward the publisher who employed him.

--- COLUMBO ENTERS ---

Columbo investigates Mallory's death and, with characteristic efficiency
disguised as bumbling, quickly identifies that the circumstances of the killing
do not match the apparent explanation. Something about the manner of the death,
or the timing, or the specific details of the scene, tells Columbo that this
was arranged rather than accidental or random.

The investigation leads Columbo toward Eddie Kane as the person who physically
committed the killing. But Columbo — and this is where the episode's structure
becomes interesting — is not satisfied with Kane as the full answer. Kane is
a hired hand, not a person with a sufficiently sophisticated motive to have
arranged Mallory's death this way. Someone with resources and intelligence
is behind Kane.

--- ESTABLISHING THE GREENLEAF CONNECTION ---

The core investigative challenge of the episode is establishing the connection
between Greenleaf and Kane — proving that Greenleaf hired Kane and that Kane
acted on Greenleaf's instructions rather than on purely personal initiative.

Columbo works this problem from multiple angles. He examines the financial
trail: money that moved from Greenleaf to Kane, possibly through intermediaries.
He examines the timing: Kane's movements and activities in the period before
the murder, and what directed his attention toward Mallory at this specific
moment. He examines Greenleaf's behavior: the specific ways a person who has
arranged a murder and is waiting to see if the scheme worked behaves, the
slight overcompensation in his expressions of shock and regret.

Greenleaf, played by Cassidy with the same self-assured charm he brought to
Ken Franklin, is confident in the architecture of his scheme. The hired killer
is a buffer. There should be no direct line back to him.

--- COLUMBO'S APPROACH TO GREENLEAF ---

Columbo's visits to Greenleaf are structured around the same dynamic as all
his killer interviews: apparent admiration for the man's success, apparent
confusion about various aspects of the case, the gentle persistent return to
points that Greenleaf wishes would be dropped. Greenleaf manages these visits
with the practiced ease of a man who has dealt with difficult situations
throughout a successful career.

What Greenleaf cannot see — because Columbo is too good at concealing it —
is that each visit is not a conversation but an examination. Columbo leaves
each meeting having noted something new: a slightly inconsistent account, a
piece of knowledge Greenleaf displays that he should not have, a reaction
that does not fit the profile of an innocent person.

--- THE TRAP AND THE PROOF ---

The episode builds toward Columbo establishing, through the accumulated evidence,
that Greenleaf and Kane were connected in the specific way the murder required.
The specific evidence that clinches the case — the detail that Columbo finds
or engineers — collapses Greenleaf's insulation from the crime.

Kane's role in the scheme is fully exposed. The financial connection between
Greenleaf and Kane, or the specific piece of evidence that proves Greenleaf's
direction of the killing, is presented to Greenleaf in a confrontation that
he cannot manage as smoothly as he managed the earlier visits.

--- RESOLUTION ---

Both Greenleaf and Kane face arrest. The scheme — designed specifically to
give Greenleaf deniability by interposing a hired criminal — is dismantled
by Columbo's refusal to accept the obvious surface explanation of Mallory's
death and his patient reconstruction of what was actually behind it.

--- NOTES ON THE EPISODE ---

"Publish or Perish" is interesting for its structural departure from the typical
Columbo formula. In most episodes, the viewer watches the killer commit the
crime personally. Here, the criminal mastermind operates at a remove, which
gives the episode a slightly different texture: Columbo must work backward
not just from crime to killer but through an additional layer of hired criminality
to reach the person actually responsible. Jack Cassidy's second performance
as a Columbo villain is in some respects more interesting than his first: as
Greenleaf, he plays a man whose self-assurance has a slightly harder, more
unpleasant quality than the charming Ken Franklin — a person for whom other
people are simply instruments, which makes his eventual exposure by the most
apparently insignificant detective on the force particularly satisfying.
