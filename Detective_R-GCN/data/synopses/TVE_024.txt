TVE_024 | Poirot: The Mysterious Affair at Styles | Director: Ross Devenish | TV Episode
================================================================================
FULL EPISODE SYNOPSIS — SPOILERS INCLUDED
================================================================================

--- BACKGROUND AND CONTEXT ---

"The Mysterious Affair at Styles" is a two-part episode of ITV's Agatha
Christie's Poirot, first broadcast in September 1990. It is directed by Ross
Devenish and stars David Suchet as Hercule Poirot and Hugh Fraser as Captain
Hastings. It is based on Christie's 1920 novel of the same name — the very
first Poirot novel, in which both Poirot and Hastings were introduced to the
world, and in which Christie established the elements that would define the
series for decades: the Belgian detective, his English friend and narrator,
the country house setting, and the chemistry-based poisoning plot.

The episode holds a specific place in the Suchet series as the origin story —
the case that began everything, produced late in the series run (after many
later Poirot cases had already been adapted) but presented as the founding
investigation.

--- THE SETTING: STYLES COURT ---

The story is set during the First World War. Captain Hastings, recovering from
a wound and on leave from the front, is invited by his friend John Cavendish
to stay at Styles Court, the Essex country house owned by John's elderly
stepmother, Emily Inglethorp.

Emily Inglethorp is a wealthy woman in her seventies who has recently, to the
considerable astonishment of her household, married a much younger man — Alfred
Inglethorp, a dark-bearded, somewhat theatrical figure who is regarded with
suspicion by almost everyone at Styles. The household includes John Cavendish
and his wife Mary; John's brother Lawrence; Cynthia Murdoch, a young woman
whom Emily has taken under her wing; Evelyn Howard, Emily's devoted companion
who intensely dislikes Alfred; and Dr. Bauerstein, a toxicology expert staying
in the area.

Hastings, arriving at Styles, also arranges for Poirot to be nearby — Poirot
is one of a group of Belgian refugees who have been settled in the village,
and Hastings has previously encountered him in Belgium. Poirot is nominally
retired from detective work.

--- THE MURDER OF EMILY INGLETHORP ---

One night, Emily Inglethorp is found dead in her locked room. She had been
seriously ill for some hours — convulsions, extreme distress — and died before
Dr. Wilkins could adequately treat her. The post-mortem reveals she was
poisoned with strychnine.

Emily's locked room presents the first of the episode's puzzle elements: she
locked herself in, there is a door communicating with an adjacent room, and
the specific circumstances of how the poison was administered require careful
reconstruction.

Alfred Inglethorp is the immediate and obvious suspect: the much younger
husband, who everyone dislikes, who stands to benefit from Emily's death, and
who was last seen arguing with her the previous day. He seems almost too
obviously guilty.

Poirot, drawn into the investigation by Hastings, is immediately interested
and immediately cautious about the obvious reading.

--- THE INVESTIGATION ---

Poirot investigates Styles Court with characteristic thoroughness, examining
Emily's room, the communicating door, the household's movements on the night
of the death, and the specific details of Emily's recent behavior — her will,
her financial arrangements, and her relationships with the household members.

Key discoveries:

Emily had recently made a new will — though the specific details of this will
and whether it was signed and witnessed are matters of considerable significance.

The strychnine that killed Emily was connected to bromide powders she had
been taking — a medical preparation that she obtained from Cynthia's dispensary.
The specific chemistry of how strychnine interacts with bromide is central to
the poisoning method.

Evelyn Howard, Emily's devoted companion, left Styles in apparent anger a few
days before the murder, following a dispute about Alfred.

A letter Emily was writing on the night of her death was partially burned in
the fireplace of her room.

--- ALFRED INGLETHORP AND THE ALIBI ---

Alfred Inglethorp appears to have had a rendezvous with a woman on the night
of Emily's death — an apparent alibi that, if true, would place him away from
Styles at the relevant time. But he refuses to use this alibi, and Poirot
is intrigued by his refusal: why would a man who appears to be the primary
suspect in his wife's murder decline to offer an alibi that would clear him?

Poirot specifically tells Hastings that he must prevent Alfred's arrest at
this stage — not because Alfred is innocent but because an arrest would make
it harder to reach the truth. This is one of the episode's most teasing
elements: Poirot seems to be protecting the obvious suspect for reasons that
are not yet clear.

--- THE COMPLEX POISONING MECHANISM ---

The mechanism of the poisoning is the episode's central chemical puzzle. Emily
took bromide powders regularly. Strychnine and bromide, taken together in a
specific sequence, interact in a way that makes the strychnine's fatal effect
more certain and more rapid. Someone who knew this — someone with knowledge
of toxicology — arranged for Emily to consume strychnine in a way that would
be lethal specifically because of her regular bromide use.

Dr. Bauerstein, the toxicology expert staying in the area, is therefore highly
significant. His specific expertise in exactly this interaction is noted.

--- THE BROKEN LOCK AND THE ROOM ---

The locked room is explained by the communicating door between Emily's room
and an adjacent room. The door had a bolt on Emily's side that was supposedly
always kept locked — but Poirot finds that the bolt was broken, and that
the door could be opened from the other side. The person in the adjacent room
had access to Emily's room that night.

The adjacent room was used by Cynthia — who was not in it that night, having
stayed at a friend's house. But someone used that room.

--- THE TRUTH: ALFRED AND EVELYN ---

The solution is one of Christie's most constructed: the apparent enemies are
in fact conspirators.

Alfred Inglethorp and Evelyn Howard are working together. Their apparent enmity —
Evelyn's obvious hostility to Alfred, her departure from Styles in anger —
was entirely performed. They are accomplices in Emily's murder.

Evelyn's close relationship with Emily gave her knowledge of Emily's routines,
her will, and her bromide powder habit. Alfred's position as husband gave him
proximity and access. Together they planned a poisoning that was intended to
look either like natural causes or to implicate someone else.

Alfred refused his alibi because using it — admitting he was with a woman
that night — would expose the woman in question, and more significantly would
direct investigation toward what Alfred was actually doing in the period before
the murder. Poirot understood that the refusal of the alibi was itself a clue:
it told him that Alfred's real activities that night were something he could
not expose without being more directly implicated.

The conspiracy between Alfred and Evelyn — the fake enmity, the departure
designed to look like a break — was the specific deception that Poirot's grey
cells had to penetrate.

--- THE WILL AND THE FINAL PIECE ---

Emily's partially burned letter, and the question of her most recent will,
are resolved in the final revelation. The specific details of the will —
whether Alfred would have inherited under it or not — affected the timing of
the murder and the conspirators' calculations.

Poirot assembles the full case and presents it to the authorities and to the
household. The chemistry of the poisoning, the mechanism of the room, the
false enmity between Alfred and Evelyn, and the specific evidence of their
collaboration are laid out completely.

--- RESOLUTION ---

Alfred Inglethorp and Evelyn Howard are arrested for the murder of Emily
Inglethorp. The household — John and Mary Cavendish, Lawrence, Cynthia —
are cleared.

Hastings, recounting the case in the series' characteristic epistolary frame,
notes that this was his first encounter with Poirot's methods — the first
time he watched the grey cells in operation and the first time he was
reminded that the obvious suspect and the guilty party are not always the same.

--- NOTES ON THE EPISODE ---

"The Mysterious Affair at Styles" is a fitting origin for the Suchet series —
it shows Poirot and Hastings at the beginning, establishes the methodological
principles that will define the series, and delivers a chemical poisoning
puzzle of the kind that Christie enjoyed most in her early career. Suchet's
Poirot is fully formed even here: the precision, the theatrical quality of the
final revelation, the specific pleasure in deploying knowledge that others lack.
The episode also benefits from the added resonance of knowing this is where
everything started.
