================================================================================
FULL PLOT SYNOPSIS — SPOILERS INCLUDED
================================================================================

--- BACKGROUND AND SETTING ---

"The Long Way Home," published in 2014, is the tenth novel in Louise Penny's
Inspector Gamache series. It arrives at a significant moment in the series'
arc: Gamache, who in the previous novel ("How the Light Gets In") finally
brought down Sylvain Francœur and the corrupt faction within the Sûreté du
Québec — at enormous personal and professional cost — has retired from the
Sûreté and is living in Three Pines with his wife Reine-Marie, trying to
recover and rebuild.

The novel takes Gamache out of his role as Chief Inspector and places him as
a private citizen who becomes involved in a case not through official channels
but through friendship. It is also a novel about art — specifically about the
landscape paintings of Peter Morrow, and about what it means to be a serious
artist struggling with the relationship between ambition, ego, and genuine
creative vision.

--- PETER MORROW'S ABSENCE ---

Peter and Clara Morrow are one of the central couples of the Three Pines world.
Clara is a painter who has achieved significant critical success — her work is
acclaimed and she is becoming genuinely significant in the Canadian art world.
Peter is also a painter, once more successful than Clara, now struggling with
a creative crisis that has made producing new work nearly impossible.

In the previous novel, Peter and Clara's marriage ended — they separated, and
Peter left Three Pines to travel and try to find himself artistically. He and
Clara agreed that he would be gone for one year and then return. The year has
passed. Peter has not come home. His letters and communications stopped some
months ago. He has simply vanished.

Clara, worried and unable simply to wait, asks Gamache for help. Though Gamache
is retired and though this is not a police matter, he agrees. The friendship
between Gamache and the Morrows, and his devotion to Three Pines and its people,
makes refusal impossible.

--- RETRACING PETER'S JOURNEY ---

The investigation becomes a journey — literally. Gamache, with Clara and a
small group of friends (including Myrna, the village bookstore owner, and others
from the Three Pines community), trace Peter's movements in the months since
he left.

The trail leads from Three Pines outward through the Canadian and eventually
the international art world. Peter had a plan: he was going to visit specific
teachers, artists, and places that he hoped would help him rediscover his
creative impulse. The itinerary he told Clara about can be reconstructed from
their communications and from what various people along his route remember of
him.

The journey takes the group first through various Canadian art institutions
and communities, then across the Atlantic — to Scotland and France, following
the thread of Peter's stated intentions and the reports of people who encountered
him.

--- ART AND CREATIVE CRISIS ---

A significant portion of the novel is devoted to Peter's creative crisis and
what it means. Peter was always a precise, controlled painter — his work was
technically masterful but, it gradually became clear, somewhat soulless. He
had been producing what critics valued without actually saying anything. Clara's
success, built on work that was messier and more personal and more genuinely
alive, threw his own inadequacy into sharp relief.

Peter's journey was an attempt to break himself open creatively — to find some
way to paint that was less controlled and more truthful. The teachers and artists
he visited were chosen because they represented, in various ways, the kind of
raw creative energy he felt he lacked.

But the journey also reveals something darker about Peter: his competitive
jealousy of Clara, his difficulty celebrating her success, and the specific
ways in which his ego and his need to be the more successful artist damaged
both his work and his relationship with her. The people who encountered Peter
along his route describe a man who was searching genuinely but who was also
carrying significant resentment and confusion.

--- WHAT HAPPENED TO PETER ---

The investigation eventually arrives at the truth. Peter reached France —
specifically the region associated with Canadian painters of a previous
generation, the Scots-Canadian and English-Canadian artists who studied in
France in the early twentieth century (a real tradition Penny draws on with
characteristic care). He made contact with a specific community of painters
and became involved with an artist and teacher there.

Peter is dead.

He was murdered. The specific circumstances of his death — where it happened,
who killed him, and why — are the mystery that the investigation has been
building toward. Peter's body, when found, is in a location connected to his
final artistic work: he was painting in a specific place when he was killed.

The killer is connected to the art world — to the community of painters Peter
had been spending time with, or to the specific transactions (financial or
personal) that his time in France involved. Peter's creative searching brought
him into contact with people whose interests or emotional stakes created a
motive for his death.

--- CLARA'S GRIEF ---

The discovery of Peter's death is devastating for Clara — and for the reader,
who has spent ten novels in the company of the Morrows. Clara and Peter's
marriage was complicated — she knew he was jealous of her success, she knew
he had been difficult to live with, and they had separated. But she loved him,
and the hope that he would return and that things between them might be better
was real.

The grief is not the grief of a perfect love but the harder grief of an imperfect
one: the loss not just of Peter but of the future they might have had if things
had gone differently.

Gamache supports Clara through the investigation and through the discovery with
the steady, compassionate presence that defines him throughout the series.

--- GAMACHE IN RETIREMENT ---

The novel also explores Gamache's experience of retirement. He is a man whose
identity has been inseparable from his role as Chief Inspector for his entire
adult life. Removed from that role — willingly, but still removed — he is
finding new contours for himself. The investigation of Peter's disappearance
is both an act of friendship and a reminder that the detective's eye and the
detective's care are not things he can simply put aside.

The novel raises, gently, the question of whether Gamache's retirement will
be permanent — a question that subsequent novels will address.

--- RESOLUTION ---

The killer is identified and brought to account through the cooperation of
Gamache and the French authorities. The investigation that began as a search
for a missing friend ends with a murder solved and a community's hidden crime
exposed.

Clara returns to Three Pines with the knowledge of what happened to Peter.
Gamache and Reine-Marie remain in Three Pines. The community absorbs the loss,
as it has absorbed other losses, because that is what communities do.
