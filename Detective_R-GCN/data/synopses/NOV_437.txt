NOV_437 | The Last House Guest | Megan Miranda | Novel
================================================================================
FULL PLOT SYNOPSIS — SPOILERS INCLUDED
================================================================================

--- BACKGROUND AND SETTING ---

"The Last House Guest," published in 2019, is Megan Miranda's fifth adult
psychological thriller. It won the ITW Thriller Award for Best Hardcover Novel.
The novel is set in Littleport, Maine — a fictional coastal tourist town with
a clearly delineated social geography: the wealthy summer people who own the
grand houses along the water, and the year-round locals who service that wealth
and whose economic lives are dependent on the summer season. This class dynamic
is not merely backdrop but is structurally central to the novel's mystery
and its moral weight.

The narrator is Avery Greer, a local young woman in her mid-twenties who works
for the Littleport real estate and property management world controlled by
the Loman family. Avery is unusual among the year-round locals in that she
has been brought into the Loman family's orbit — not as an equal, but as a
close associate, mentored by the family matriarch and close friends with their
daughter Sadie.

--- SADIE LOMAN AND AVERY ---

The novel opens a year after the death of Sadie Loman, who was Avery's closest
friend. Sadie was one of the wealthy summer people — beautiful, slightly reckless,
full of the particular energy of someone who has grown up with enough money to
feel that consequences are negotiable. She and Avery formed an unlikely close
friendship across the class divide that usually keeps summer people and locals
apart.

Sadie died a year before the novel's present day. Her death was ruled a suicide:
she was found at the bottom of a cliff on the Loman property, and the
investigation concluded she jumped. Avery has been living with this verdict
ever since, and it has not sat well with her. Sadie was not, in Avery's
understanding of her, someone who would have taken her own life. But Avery
has no evidence to offer against the official finding, and her grief has been
complicated by the knowledge that the night Sadie died, something happened
between them — a fight, a falling out, a rupture in the friendship — that
Avery cannot discuss without explaining things she has kept private.

--- THE PRESENT: A YEAR LATER ---

A year after Sadie's death, the summer season is beginning again. The wealthy
families — including the Lomans — are returning to Littleport. Avery is managing
properties as usual, dealing with the returning summer people and their demands.

Then something changes. Someone has been inside Sadie's room in the Loman
house. A note turns up — possibly in Sadie's handwriting, possibly not —
that suggests the official verdict about Sadie's death may not be correct.
Someone appears to be leaving messages or evidence pointing toward a different
version of events.

Avery cannot ignore this. Her guilt about the fight with Sadie the night she
died, her discomfort with the suicide verdict, and her genuine love for Sadie
drive her to reopen what the official investigation has closed.

--- THE NIGHT OF SADIE'S DEATH ---

Miranda structures the novel around the gradual revelation of what actually
happened the night Sadie died — both the events themselves and the context
that gives them meaning. Avery's account of the night is delivered in pieces,
because the full account requires explaining things she has been hiding.

The fight between Avery and Sadie was real and was serious. It involved something
Avery discovered about Sadie — a secret Sadie was keeping — and Sadie's response
to being confronted was defensive and angry. The nature of this secret, and
what it meant for both of them, is part of what the investigation needs to
establish.

Sadie's behavior in the weeks and months before her death was, in retrospect,
suggestive of someone under significant stress or fear — but this is only
visible in retrospect, and the specific nature of the stressor was not something
Avery understood at the time.

--- THE LOMAN FAMILY AND THEIR INTERESTS ---

The Loman family — Grant, Bianca, and their extended circle — are central
suspects simply by virtue of their position. If Sadie's death was not suicide,
the question of who had motive and opportunity in a tight summer-community world
narrows to people Sadie was close to.

Grant Loman, Sadie's older brother, is particularly relevant. His relationship
with Sadie was complicated in ways the family does not discuss openly. His
behavior in the present day — his reaction to Avery's questions, his management
of the family's response to the anniversary of Sadie's death — is not that of
a simple grieving brother.

Bianca Loman, the family matriarch and Avery's professional patron, has her
own interests in how Sadie's death is remembered and what the investigation
of it might reveal. Her relationship to Avery — the mentorship she provided,
the way she brought Avery into the family's world — takes on different dimensions
as Avery investigates.

--- THE SUMMER PEOPLE AND THE LOCALS ---

The class structure of Littleport is directly relevant to the mystery. The
summer people have resources — legal, financial, social — that insulate them
from consequences in ways the locals simply do not. Avery's position, straddling
both worlds, gives her access to both sides but security in neither.

Several of the summer people who were present the summer Sadie died are relevant
to what happened. Their specific relationships with Sadie — which Avery knew
only partially, because Sadie kept different parts of her life separate —
are established through the investigation.

Parker Loman, Sadie's cousin, and his friends from the summer community had
specific interactions with Sadie that become significant. Sadie's love life —
specifically a relationship she was conducting that she kept from Avery —
is part of the picture.

--- WHAT SADIE KNEW ---

The secret Sadie was keeping — the thing Avery confronted her about — was
information Sadie had about a crime or a wrong committed by someone in the
summer community. Sadie had discovered something, and her discovery put her
in a position of danger: she knew something that someone needed to suppress.

The specific crime or wrong Sadie knew about is the foundation of the murder
motive. The person who killed Sadie did so because Sadie's knowledge — and
whatever Sadie was planning to do with it — was an intolerable threat to them.
The suicide staging was a response to that threat: the most efficient way to
silence Sadie and prevent her from acting on what she knew.

--- AVERY'S CULPABILITY AND GUILT ---

Running through the investigation is Avery's specific guilt: she fought with
Sadie the night Sadie died. The fight was about what Sadie knew and what Avery
thought Sadie should do with it. If Avery had responded differently — if she
had been less confrontational, if she had supported Sadie differently — would
Sadie still be alive?

This question cannot be answered, but it drives Avery's need to find the truth
as much as any desire for justice. She needs to understand what happened not
simply for Sadie's sake but for her own.

--- DISCOVERING THE KILLER ---

Avery's investigation — complicated by the year-old timeline, the social
complexities of the summer-community world, and the active interference of
someone who does not want the truth found — assembles the complete picture
of what happened to Sadie.

The killer is identified from among the people closest to Sadie: someone
whose specific connection to the crime Sadie discovered gave them motive,
whose presence on the cliff that night is explained by the investigation,
and whose staging of the suicide was almost but not quite perfect.

The specific crime Sadie had discovered — the wrong committed by a member of
the summer community whose wealth and position made them confident they would
never be held accountable — is exposed as part of the resolution.

--- RESOLUTION ---

The killer is identified and the truth about Sadie's death is established.
The official finding of suicide is overturned. The person responsible faces
consequences — though the specific form those consequences take is shaped by
the class dynamics the novel has been examining throughout: the process of
accountability for wealthy people in small coastal communities does not
necessarily look like swift justice.

Avery, having found the truth, must also reckon with the full picture of who
Sadie was — including the parts Sadie kept from her. The friendship, viewed
in the light of complete knowledge, is more complicated than Avery understood
while Sadie was alive. This does not diminish the grief but it changes its
texture.

--- MIRANDA'S MAINE ---

"The Last House Guest" is one of Miranda's strongest novels and one of the
best uses of the summer-community setting in recent psychological fiction.
The class dynamics of Littleport — the dependency, the resentment, the
performance of servility that the economic relationship requires — give the
mystery real social weight. Sadie's death is not just a personal tragedy but
a symptom of a world in which wealth buys not just comfort but protection
from consequence.
