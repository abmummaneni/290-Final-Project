--- BACKGROUND AND SETTING ---

"The Hanging Girl" (Danish: Selfies, literally "Selfies," but published in
English as "The Hanging Girl") is the sixth novel in Jussi Adler-Olsen's
Department Q series, published in Danish in 2014 and in English translation
in 2015. It features Carl Mørck, Assad, and Rose of the Copenhagen Police's
cold-case basement unit, and takes the investigation from Copenhagen to the
Danish island of Bornholm and eventually to the sun-cult commune world of
southern Europe.

The novel is notable for an extensive backstory involving a religious cult
operating in Scandinavia and internationally, and for the continued development
of Assad's secret past — a thread that has been building through the series
and that in this novel becomes more urgent and more painful.

--- THE COLD CASE: ALBERTE ---

The case that comes to Department Q is that of Alberte — a seventeen-year-old
girl who died in 1997 on the island of Bornholm. She was found hanging from
a tree, and her death was ruled a suicide at the time. She was a troubled
teenager from a difficult home background, her suicide seemed within the
range of what was known about her life, and the investigation was brief.

When the case reaches Mørck and Assad, certain things about the original
investigation seem inadequate. The physical circumstances of how Alberte was
found hanging — specifically the position of the body and the means by which
she was apparently suspended — raise questions that a more thorough forensic
examination would have addressed. The original investigators, under-resourced
and working with the assumption of suicide, did not press hard enough.

The reopening of the case requires Department Q to investigate both the
circumstances of Alberte's death in 1997 and the people connected to her life
at the time — her family, the community on Bornholm, and anyone who might have
had reason to harm her or reason to see her death covered as a suicide.

--- BORNHOLM AND THE CULT ---

The investigation takes Mørck and Assad to Bornholm — a beautiful, somewhat
remote Danish island in the Baltic, with its own distinct character and
community. The island's specific social world in 1997 is relevant: a small
community where everyone knows everyone, where strangers stand out, and where
the specific social circles of a seventeen-year-old girl can be mapped with
relative precision.

Alberte, it emerges, was connected in the period before her death to a group
that operated on Bornholm and that had the characteristics of a cult or
high-control religious organization. The group was built around the personality
of a charismatic leader — a man who presented a spiritual framework involving
sun worship or a related nature-mysticism tradition, attracting vulnerable
young people with promises of meaning, community, and transcendence.

The cult leader is the novel's central antagonist: manipulative, sexually
predatory toward his female followers, controlling, and willing to use violence
to protect his position and his operation.

--- THE CULT'S INTERNATIONAL DIMENSION ---

The cult did not remain on Bornholm. By the time of the investigation (the
novel's present day), the organization has moved and expanded, operating
in southern Europe — a sun-drenched location consistent with the sun-worship
spiritual framework. The leader and his current followers are living outside
Denmark, which complicates the investigation: Danish law enforcement's
jurisdiction does not simply extend to wherever the cult has relocated.

This international dimension forces Mørck and Assad to work beyond their
usual framework, making contacts and gathering information across borders.
The specific location of the cult's current operation — Spain or a similar
Mediterranean setting — is established through the investigation.

--- WHAT HAPPENED TO ALBERTE ---

The truth about Alberte's death is that she was killed by the cult leader
or at his direction, and the killing was staged as a suicide. Alberte had
become a problem for the cult: she was threatening to expose the leader,
or she had resisted his control in ways that made her dangerous to the group's
continued operation, or she had specific knowledge of the cult's criminal
activities that could not be allowed to reach the outside world.

The cult leader had the means and the motive to arrange her death and to ensure
it was read as suicide. The small-community context of Bornholm in 1997, the
limited investigative resources applied to a seemingly straightforward teenage
suicide, and the cult's ability to suppress or manipulate the testimony of its
members all contributed to the crime's successful concealment for seventeen
years.

--- PRESENT-DAY VICTIMS ---

The investigation reveals that Alberte was not the only person the cult has
harmed. Other young people — drawn in by the same promises, subjected to the
same control and exploitation — have suffered, and some have died. The pattern
of the cult's predation extends across years and borders.

The present-day threat is real: the cult is still operating, the leader is still
active, and vulnerable people are still at risk.

--- ASSAD'S CONTINUING CRISIS ---

Assad's personal crisis deepens significantly in "The Hanging Girl." The thread
that has been building through the series — the suggestion that Assad has
family in dangerous circumstances in the Middle East, that his past involves
things he has kept from Mørck, and that his ability to remain in Denmark and
work for Department Q is under some kind of threat — becomes more acute.

Assad is visibly suffering. His characteristic warmth and humor are still present
but strained. Mørck is aware that something is seriously wrong but cannot get
Assad to open up. The contrast between the warmth of their professional
partnership and the wall Assad maintains around his personal truth is one of
the series' most painful ongoing elements.

The novel does not fully resolve Assad's situation — that will take subsequent
volumes — but it makes clear that the situation is urgent and that Assad is
carrying something that is becoming too heavy to carry alone.

--- ROSE'S INVOLVEMENT ---

Rose, the Department Q administrator whose own complicated personality and
history have been developed across the series, plays her characteristic role:
organizing, supporting, occasionally exceeding her brief in productive ways.
Her specific contributions to this investigation are consistent with her
established capabilities.

--- CONFRONTATION AND RESOLUTION ---

Mørck and Assad track the cult leader to his current location in southern
Europe. The confrontation with the man responsible for Alberte's death and the
cult's ongoing predation is dangerous — the leader has resources, followers,
and a willingness to use violence.

The resolution involves both the immediate criminal accountability for Alberte's
murder (brought to the Danish justice system, however complicated the
international dimensions make this) and the disruption of the cult's current
operations, protecting whatever current members or potential victims can be
reached.

The case is closed. The cult leader faces justice. Alberte's death, seventeen
years after it occurred, is correctly categorized as murder and the person
responsible is held accountable.

--- THEMES ---

"The Hanging Girl" is one of the Department Q series' most explicitly thematic
novels: the exploitation of vulnerable young people by charismatic authority
figures, the way high-control groups use spirituality and community as lures,
and the specific vulnerability of teenagers seeking meaning in a world that
doesn't offer it readily. Adler-Olsen handles the cult material with the same
sociological eye he brings to all his Department Q premises — identifying a
real social phenomenon and using it as the basis for a mystery that illuminates
it.
