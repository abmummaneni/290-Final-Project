Setup: the mansion, the blackmail, and the weapons

In 1954 New England, six strangers are invited, under pseudonyms, to a secluded mansion for dinner:

    Colonel Mustard

    Mrs. White

    Mrs. Peacock

    Professor Plum

    Miss Scarlet

    Mr. Green

They are greeted by the butler Wadsworth and the maid Yvette, and each is told to keep their real identities and government connections secret. Over dinner, tension grows as it becomes clear they all have something to hide.

After dinner, Wadsworth reveals why they are really there: all six guests are being blackmailed by the same man, Mr. Boddy, who has been extorting them over various scandals (adultery, corruption, sex with patients, taking bribes, running a brothel, etc.). Wadsworth says he has called the police to arrive later that night; his plan is to expose the blackmailer and hand him over.

Mr. Boddy arrives and flips the script. He gives each guest a wrapped box containing a weapon from the board game:

    Candlestick

    Dagger

    Revolver

    Rope

    Lead pipe

    Wrench

Boddy suggests they could kill Wadsworth to keep their secrets safe. He turns off the lights. In the dark there is a gunshot, noise, a scream. When the lights come back on, Boddy is apparently dead on the floor.
First body count: Boddy and the cook

The group panics. They argue about whether to call the police now or wait for the officer Wadsworth said was already on the way. Wadsworth explains his own motive: Boddy had blackmailed his wife into prostitution, leading to her death; this is Wadsworth’s attempt at revenge and justice.

They move the body to the study. While they argue in other rooms, someone slips away. The cook in the kitchen is suddenly heard screaming. The group rushes in and finds her stabbed in the back with the dagger, her body hanging from the freezer door.

While they are dealing with the cook, they realize something else: Boddy’s body has disappeared from the study. They search the house and eventually discover Boddy again—this time truly dead, his skull bashed in with the candlestick.

So, very quickly, there are two murders (Boddy and the cook), and at least two different weapons used.
Locking away the weapons and the arrival of the motorist

Wadsworth decides the weapons are the most immediate danger. He gathers the six weapons and locks them in a cupboard in the study, intending to throw away the key. Just as he is about to dispose of it, a stranded motorist arrives at the front door claiming his car broke down and he needs to use the phone.

They let the motorist in, but Wadsworth, wanting to contain the situation, locks him alone in the lounge and then throws a key out the front door into the night. The implication is that the weapons are safe in the locked cupboard, and the key to the cupboard is on Wadsworth’s person.

To search for a possible killer or accomplice, the group decide to pair up and search the mansion. They draw lots, splitting into pairs (e.g., Mustard with Scarlet, White with Wadsworth, etc.) and begin exploring, including the attic, cellar, and old passageways.

While they are scattered, unseen hands go to work:

    The blackmail evidence is burned in the fireplace.

    Someone unlocks the weapons cupboard.

    The isolated motorist in the lounge is bludgeoned with the wrench.

At some point, Mustard and Scarlet discover a secret passage between rooms (for example, between the study and the lounge). They emerge from the passage into the lounge and find the motorist’s corpse. They scream; the others rush over.
The policeman arrives; Yvette, the cop, and the telegram girl die

A police officer shows up at the mansion, having found the motorist’s abandoned car on the road. He comes inside to use the phone and is immediately suspicious of the strange group and their nervous behavior. The guests, desperate not to reveal the murders, contrive to show him a series of rooms staged to look like harmless party scenes, with the bodies hidden or propped up as if drunk or passed out.

While the cop is being distracted, a phone call comes in for Wadsworth—from J. Edgar Hoover. Wadsworth takes the call alone, which heightens the others’ paranoia.

Eventually, the patrolman is left alone in one of the rooms with a working phone. As he starts to realize that something is seriously wrong and prepares to call for backup, the lights suddenly go out—someone has cut the electricity.

In the darkness:

    Yvette (the maid) is strangled with the rope.

    The cop is killed with the lead pipe.

    A singing telegram girl who arrives at the front door is shot with the revolver mid‑song.

The lights come back on. Now there are multiple victims scattered around: Boddy (blunt force), cook (stabbed), motorist (wrench), Yvette (rope), cop (lead pipe), and telegram girl (revolver). All six weapons have now been used.
Wadsworth’s reconstruction and the three different endings

Wadsworth, having “figured it out,” gathers everyone in the hall and proceeds to recreate the entire evening, running frantically from room to room, reenacting movements, pointing out who was where at which time. He ties each victim to one of the guests via blackmail:

    The cook worked for Mrs. Peacock.

    The motorist knew Colonel Mustard from the war.

    Yvette had a connection to Mrs. White and/or Scarlet.

    The cop was on Scarlet’s payroll.

    The telegram girl was a patient of Plum or had ties to another guest.

This establishes motives for different guests to want specific victims dead. Then the film splits into three “solutions”:
Ending A – “How it could have happened” (Miss Scarlet is the killer)

In the first version, Wadsworth says:

    Yvette killed the cook and Mr. Boddy under orders from Miss Scarlet. Yvette had been involved in Scarlet’s prostitution ring in Washington.

    Scarlet then killed Yvette (to silence her) and arranged the deaths of the motorist, the cop, and the telegram girl because each victim could connect Scarlet to illegal activities or blackmail.

Scarlet’s motive: protect and continue her extortion/prostitution empire by eliminating Boddy’s network of informants and witnesses, then seize Boddy’s blackmail files to sell the others’ secrets herself.

Scarlet holds the others at gunpoint and plans to finish off Wadsworth. Wadsworth tells her there are no bullets left. She pulls the trigger; nothing happens. He disarms her just as law enforcement raids the house and the earlier door‑knocking “evangelist” reveals himself to be the chief of police. Wadsworth declares himself an undercover FBI agent, and Scarlet is arrested.
Ending B – “How about this?” (Mrs. Peacock is the killer)

In the second version, Wadsworth accuses Mrs. Peacock of everything:

    She killed Mr. Boddy, the cook, the motorist, Yvette, the cop, and the telegram girl.

    Her overarching motive: protect herself from being exposed for taking bribes from foreign powers, using Boddy and his informants as intermediaries. The other victims each knew pieces of her corruption or could be used as leverage against her.

Peacock, holding them at gunpoint, forces the others to let her leave. She escapes the house and walks to her car, seemingly home free. But outside, she is surrounded and arrested: Wadsworth reveals he is actually an undercover FBI agent and had arranged everything that night as a sting operation to catch her taking or facilitating bribes. The supposed evangelist is again revealed as the police chief/FBI contact.
Ending C – “Here’s what really happened” (everyone is a killer; Mr. Green is the agent)

The final ending presents the canonical “everyone did it” twist. Wadsworth explains that each of the guests, except Mr. Green, committed one murder:

    Mrs. Peacock killed the cook.

    Professor Plum killed Mr. Boddy (the man thought to be Boddy earlier; Wadsworth now says that was actually the real butler).

    Colonel Mustard killed the motorist (who could expose him).

    Miss Scarlet killed the cop (who had been on her payroll).

    Mrs. White killed Yvette (linked to her husband’s infidelity).

    Wadsworth himself killed the singing telegram girl.

Then Wadsworth reveals his biggest twist: he is not the butler. He is the real Mr. Boddy. The man Plum killed earlier in the evening was actually Wadsworth’s butler, used as a decoy. Wadsworth/Boddy gathered everyone to have all his informants (the cook, cop, motorist, maid, telegram girl) eliminated by the guests themselves, thereby wiping out evidence and witnesses while still retaining his leverage.

With all his informants dead and the guests now guilty of murder, Wadsworth/Boddy announces that he plans to continue blackmailing them all, now with even stronger leverage. He pulls a gun and holds the others at bay.

At this point, Mr. Green pulls a revolver of his own and shoots Wadsworth/Boddy dead. He then reveals that he is the undercover FBI agent, sent to infiltrate the group and nail Boddy’s blackmail ring. His earlier claim of being a closeted State Department official was part of his cover. He quips that he’s going to go home and sleep with his wife, signaling his supposed homosexuality was a ruse.

The police and the evangelist‑chief pour into the house and arrest the remaining guests for their respective murders. The film ends on a freeze‑frame note of dark farce: everyone is guilty, communism (as Wadsworth jokes at one point) was “just a red herring,” and the real “solution” is that the house was a nest of mutual blackmail and revenge all along.
