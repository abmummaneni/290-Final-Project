--- BACKGROUND AND SETTING ---

"The Perfect Stranger," published in 2017, is Megan Miranda's fourth adult
psychological thriller. It is set in a small, rural Pennsylvania town where the
narrator, Leah Stevens, has recently moved to start over after her journalism
career in Boston collapsed under circumstances involving a story she published
that could not be verified. The novel is built around questions of identity,
memory, and the verifiability of experience — what we can know about other
people and what we can know about ourselves.

Miranda writes in the first person, using Leah's perspective with the deliberate
ambiguity that characterizes the best domestic-suspense narrators: Leah is not
straightforwardly unreliable, but her account of events is shaped by what she
wants to be true and by gaps in her own knowledge that only become apparent
as the novel progresses.

--- LEAH AND EMMA ---

When Leah left Boston — her career destroyed, her reputation damaged — she ran
into an old friend, Emmy Grey, in a coffee shop. Emmy had also just experienced
a personal and professional collapse. The two women hadn't been close in years,
but in the way of people who meet each other at low moments, they connected,
and Emmy proposed they move together to rural Pennsylvania, where Leah had
a job offer at a local school (she would be teaching journalism) and Emmy
could start fresh.

They found a rental house, moved in together, and Leah began her new life.

The novel opens with Leah coming home to find Emmy gone. Not gone in a normal
way — gone, as in vanished. Her things are still in the house, her car is
there, there is no note, there is no message. Emmy has simply disappeared.

--- THE INVESTIGATION OPENS ---

Leah reports Emmy's disappearance to the local police. The detective who
responds — Kyle Donovan — is immediately relevant: he is a local man, an
ex-boyfriend of a woman who was recently found beaten near the lake and who
is now in a coma. The town is already on edge about this earlier crime, an
attack on a woman named Bethany Jarvitz, which has not been solved.

Donovan is skeptical of Leah's disappearance report, partly because Leah
can provide very little verifiable information about Emmy. She knows her from
years ago. She does not know Emmy's family, her history in detail, or her
precise whereabouts before they reconnected in Boston. Emmy's full name, her
background, her employment history — Leah knows these things only from what
Emmy told her.

As Donovan investigates, a pattern begins to emerge that disturbs both him
and Leah: when he tries to verify Emmy Grey's existence — to contact her family,
to trace her employment, to find records of her — he encounters a trail that
is thin, inconsistent, or simply absent. Emmy Grey, as presented to Leah,
may not be who she said she was.

--- THE PARALLEL ATTACK ---

The attack on Bethany Jarvitz and Emmy's disappearance run as parallel threads
throughout the novel. Bethany is in a coma, unable to say what happened to her.
She was found at the lake with injuries consistent with a violent assault. No
one saw what happened. No obvious suspect was identified.

Leah begins to wonder whether the two events are connected. Did Emmy disappear
because of something connected to Bethany's attack? Was Emmy a witness to
something? Or is the connection more direct?

As Leah investigates on her own — partly because she doesn't trust the police
investigation to take Emmy's disappearance seriously, partly because her
journalistic instincts are engaged — she begins to map the social world of the
town and to identify who had connections to both Bethany and, potentially,
to Emmy.

--- WHO IS EMMY GREY? ---

The investigation into Emmy's identity is the novel's central mystery. Leah
discovers, piece by piece, that the Emmy Grey she knew from years ago and the
Emmy Grey who moved in with her in Pennsylvania may be related but are not
entirely the same person. The name may have been borrowed or adapted. The
specific details Emmy provided about her life do not entirely check out.

This raises a horrible question: if Emmy fabricated aspects of her identity,
what was she running from? And is her disappearance a voluntary flight —
choosing to vanish, as she apparently vanished into her assumed identity before —
or something done to her?

Leah's own position in this is uncomfortable. She vouched for Emmy to the
police, she shared her home with her, and now she cannot verify the most
basic facts about her roommate. Her earlier professional failure — the
unverifiable story that ended her journalism career — rhymes uncomfortably
with this situation: another case of building something on foundations she
didn't check carefully enough.

--- THE CONNECTION TO LEAH'S PAST ---

As the investigation progresses, it becomes clear that Emmy's choice of Leah —
the "coincidental" meeting in the Boston coffee shop, the suggestion to move
together — was not entirely coincidental. Emmy knew Leah. More than that,
Emmy had a specific reason to seek Leah out.

The connection between Emmy's past and Leah's past — specifically between
Emmy's real identity and the story that destroyed Leah's journalism career —
is the novel's most significant revelation. The person Leah's discredited
story was about, or the circumstances surrounding that story, connect to who
Emmy really is and why Emmy needed to be near Leah.

Emmy was not simply a woman in crisis who happened to reconnect with an old
friend. She came to Leah deliberately, because something about Leah's situation
or Leah's knowledge was relevant to Emmy's own circumstances.

--- THE TRUTH ABOUT EMMY AND THE ATTACK ---

Emmy's real identity involves a history of violence or danger — a previous life
in which she was either a victim or entangled with someone who was. Her assumed
identity was a form of protection: she was hiding from someone.

The attack on Bethany Jarvitz and Emmy's disappearance are connected. The person
responsible for both is someone from Emmy's past who found her — who tracked
her through the same kind of investigative process that Leah is now employing —
and who acted when they found her.

Emmy's disappearance is not voluntary. She was taken, or she fled in such haste
that she left everything behind, because the person she was hiding from arrived.

--- DONOVAN AND LEAH ---

The relationship between Leah and Detective Donovan develops over the course
of the investigation — from initial mutual suspicion (he doubts her account,
she resents his skepticism) to a working trust. Donovan's own situation —
his ex-girlfriend is the comatose Bethany, which gives him personal stakes in
solving the lake attack — means he and Leah share a specific investment in
finding the truth that goes beyond the professional.

--- RESOLUTION ---

The person responsible for Bethany's attack and Emmy's disappearance is
identified through the convergence of Leah's investigation and Donovan's official
one. The connection to Emmy's hidden past — the person she was running from —
is fully established.

Emmy's fate is resolved: whether she survived and can be found, or whether
she was killed by the person who found her, is the novel's final question and
its answer is reached in the confrontation of the final act.

The connection between Emmy's situation and Leah's discredited story is explained
in full, giving Leah a different understanding of her own professional past —
what she wrote may have been unprovable, but it was not wrong.

--- NOTES ON THE NOVEL ---

"The Perfect Stranger" is characteristic Miranda: a carefully constructed
first-person suspense novel in which the narrator's knowledge is deliberately
limited in ways that are realistic rather than artificially contrived. Leah
doesn't know what she doesn't know because people have reasons not to tell
her, not because she is magically blind. The novel's central question — how
well can we know anyone, particularly someone who has reason to present a
curated version of themselves? — is handled with the specific contemporary
anxiety that made this strand of thriller so popular in the 2010s.
