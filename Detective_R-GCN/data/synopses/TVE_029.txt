TVE_029 | Poirot: The ABC Murders | Director: Andrew Grieve | TV Episode
================================================================================
FULL EPISODE SYNOPSIS — SPOILERS INCLUDED
================================================================================

--- BACKGROUND AND CONTEXT ---

"The ABC Murders" is a two-part episode of ITV's Agatha Christie's Poirot,
first broadcast in January 1992. It is directed by Andrew Grieve and stars
David Suchet as Hercule Poirot and Hugh Fraser as Captain Hastings. It is
based on Christie's 1936 novel of the same name — one of her most ingenious
constructions and one of the most influential serial killer novels in the
history of crime fiction, predating the American serial killer genre by decades.

The episode introduces the specific structure that would become a serial killer
thriller template: the taunting letters to the investigator, the alphabetical
pattern, the apparent randomness that masks a specific and personal motive.

--- THE LETTERS BEGIN ---

Poirot receives an anonymous letter informing him that a murder will be committed
in Andover on a specific date. The letter is signed "ABC." Poirot takes it
to Chief Inspector Japp of Scotland Yard, who is skeptical — crank letters
are not uncommon — but agrees to alert the Andover police.

On the specified date, in Andover, a woman named Alice Ascher is found murdered
in her tobacco shop. She is an elderly, apparently harmless woman with no
obvious enemies. Beside her body is a copy of the ABC Railway Guide, open to
the Andover page.

A second letter arrives. Another murder will occur in Bexhill. On the specified
date, Betty Barnard, a young woman, is strangled on the beach at Bexhill.
Another ABC Railway Guide is found beside the body.

A third letter. Churston. Sir Carmichael Clarke, a wealthy man, is battered
to death at his estate. Third ABC Railway Guide.

The alphabetical pattern — A, B, C for Andover, Bexhill, Churston — and the
ABC Railway Guides suggest a serial killer working through the alphabet,
killing apparently random victims in alphabetical order.

--- ALEXANDER NAPOLEON CUST ---

A man named Alexander Napoleon Cust begins to shadow the investigation.
He is a sad, ineffectual figure — an epileptic who sells stockings as a
traveling salesman, and whose sales territory has taken him to all three murder
locations around the dates of the killings. He wakes from apparent blackouts
uncertain of what he has done. He finds a stocking among his things after
each murder. He receives letters directing him to specific locations without
knowing who is sending them.

Cust is convinced that he is the killer — that his blackouts have been covers
for murders he committed but cannot remember. He is terrified of himself.
He is in flight.

He is eventually arrested after being present in the vicinity of a fourth
murder in Doncaster — the killing of George Earlsfield at a cinema, breaking
the alphabetical pattern (D for Doncaster, not D for the intended victim's name).

Cust is charged with all four murders. The circumstantial evidence against him
is considerable: he was in all the locations, he had no verified alibi for the
murder times, the stocking was found in his possession. Most of Scotland Yard
considers the case closed.

--- POIROT'S DOUBT ---

Poirot is not satisfied. He has been sitting with the case throughout, receiving
the letters in advance of each murder — the killer specifically chose him as
the audience for the game — and he has been thinking about what the choice of
recipient says about the killer's psychology.

Why send the letters to Poirot? A random serial killer does not typically
have a relationship with a specific famous detective. The choice of Poirot
as recipient implies the killer either knows him, wants to be caught by him
specifically, or wants to use him for a purpose.

Poirot's grey cells identify the problem with the apparent solution: the
murders are not actually random. They are alphabetical only on the surface.
The specific victims — Alice Ascher, Betty Barnard, Carmichael Clarke — were
not chosen randomly. They were chosen because their initials and locations
fit the alphabetical pattern. The pattern was constructed to create the
impression of a series when in fact only one of the murders was the real
target.

--- THE REAL TARGET: SIR CARMICHAEL CLARKE ---

Poirot identifies Sir Carmichael Clarke as the actual intended victim — the
C murder, committed in Churston. The other murders — Alice Ascher and Betty
Barnard — were committed solely to create the serial killer pattern, to make
Clarke's death look like the third in a random alphabetical sequence rather
than a targeted killing.

If Clarke were simply murdered, suspicion would fall on the people with motive
to kill him — primarily his family, since he was wealthy and his death would
benefit specific heirs. By embedding his murder in a serial killing pattern
and attributing all the killings to a single random killer (conveniently
provided in the form of Alexander Cust), the actual murderer insulated himself
from suspicion completely.

--- FRANKLIN CLARKE ---

The killer is Franklin Clarke, Sir Carmichael's younger brother. Franklin
stood to inherit from Carmichael's death and was facing financial difficulties
that made that inheritance urgent. He designed the entire ABC scheme specifically
to provide cover for Carmichael's murder.

Franklin's specific genius was the use of Alexander Cust. He identified Cust —
a man with epilepsy and blackouts, a man who already feared what he might be
doing during his lost time — and manipulated him systematically: sending him
anonymous letters directing him to the murder locations, ensuring he was in
the right place at the right time to be found near each murder, and exploiting
Cust's terror of his own mind to prevent him from seeking clarity about what
was happening.

Cust was not the killer. He was the false suspect, groomed for the role, placed
in the circumstances that would make him the obvious candidate for arrest.

--- POIROT'S TRAP ---

Having identified Franklin Clarke as the killer, Poirot must prove it. The
evidence against Clarke is not immediately obvious — he was not in Andover,
Bexhill, or Churston in any verifiable way. His alibi for Churston is the
target of Poirot's specific investigation.

Poirot arranges a confrontation — bringing together the people connected to
the case and presenting his full analysis, forcing Clarke into a position where
his response reveals what Poirot already knows.

The specific evidence that establishes Clarke's guilt — something about the
letters, about the stocking, about his specific knowledge of Cust — is
presented in the final revelation with Suchet's characteristic theatrical
precision.

--- ALEXANDER CUST CLEARED ---

Cust is exonerated. The man who spent weeks believing he was a murderer,
who turned himself in partly out of terrified self-accusation, is cleared
of all four murders. He is a victim — of Clarke's scheme and of his own
psychological vulnerability, which Clarke exploited with cold precision.

The clearing of Cust is one of the episode's most affecting moments: the
sad, harmless stocking salesman who feared the worst of himself was simply
a pawn in someone else's murder scheme.

--- RESOLUTION ---

Franklin Clarke is arrested and charged with all four murders — the three
decoy murders and the killing of his brother. The alphabetical serial killer
scheme, which fooled Scotland Yard completely, is dismantled by Poirot's
recognition that pattern can be imposed rather than organic, and that the
most elaborate misdirection is most useful when the actual target is close
to home.

--- NOTES ON THE EPISODE ---

"The ABC Murders" is among the finest of the Suchet Poirot productions, and
the source novel is among Christie's most enduring achievements. The serial
killer structure — taunting letters, alphabetical pattern, the relationship
between killer and investigator — influenced an entire genre. The specific
twist (the random serial killer as cover for a single targeted murder) remains
one of Christie's best structural conceits: it requires the reader or viewer
to hold two incompatible readings simultaneously until the solution makes
only one possible. Suchet's performance in the revelation scene — particularly
the moment he explains the real logic of the scheme — is one of his very best
in the series.
