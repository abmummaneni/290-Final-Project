TVE_084 | Sherlock: The Great Game | Director: Paul McGuigan | TV Episode
================================================================================
FULL EPISODE SYNOPSIS — SPOILERS INCLUDED
================================================================================

--- BACKGROUND AND CONTEXT ---

"The Great Game" is the third and final episode of Series 1 of the BBC's
"Sherlock," first broadcast on August 8, 2010. It is directed by Paul McGuigan
and written by Mark Gatiss, and stars Benedict Cumberbatch as Sherlock Holmes
and Martin Freeman as Dr. John Watson. The episode runs ninety minutes and
serves as the series finale, delivering the full introduction of Moriarty while
constructing an extended puzzle sequence involving hostage situations and
countdown threats before ending on a cliffhanger of genuine tension.

The episode draws on several elements from the Conan Doyle canon — the Bruce-
Partington submarine plans, the Golem as an assassin figure, and the mythology
of Moriarty himself — while assembling them into an entirely original plot that
functions as a sustained game between Holmes and an adversary who is his precise
intellectual match.

The specific quality of "The Great Game" within Series 1 is that it moves from
the puzzle-of-the-week format of the first two episodes into something more
sustained and more personal: the case is not a single crime to be solved but
a series of puzzles presented to Holmes by someone who wants to be known, who
wants to be recognized, and whose ultimate purpose is not the crimes themselves
but the relationship between himself and Holmes.

--- OPENING: MINSK AND THE EXPLOSION ---

The episode opens in Belarus — a brief scene of extraordinary efficiency that
establishes both that Holmes has been conducting international work (there
is a reference to a case in Minsk that apparently involved Holmes disrupting
some significant criminal operation) and that the aftermath of such work creates
enemies with operational capability.

Back in London, 221B Baker Street is destroyed by a bomb — an explosion that
is powerful enough to blow out the walls but that, miraculously, leaves Holmes
and Watson largely unhurt. The explosion is clearly a message rather than a
genuine assassination attempt: whoever planted it could have used more explosives
and didn't. They want Holmes to know they are capable of this, not to kill him.

--- THE PINK PHONE ---

Shortly after the explosion, Holmes receives a phone — Jennifer Wilson's pink
phone, the suitcase phone from "A Study in Pink" — now in the hands of whoever
is directing the game. The phone is used throughout the episode to communicate
a series of challenges:

Each challenge is delivered via a recorded message played through a civilian
hostage — a person who has been fitted with explosives and a phone through which
they relay the voice of the game's director. The hostage speaks in a terrified
voice the words they are told to say. When the message is complete, Holmes has
a limited time to solve the puzzle it contains. If he fails, or if he takes
too long, the hostage dies.

The game is structured as a series of increasingly difficult unsolved crimes —
cases from the cold files, crimes that were never solved, mysteries that the
police passed over or gave up on. Each one is a puzzle presented to Holmes
as a test. The game's director — who we come to understand is Moriarty, though
his identity is not immediately confirmed — is demonstrating something: that
Holmes's specific gifts, his specific pleasure in the exercise of his intelligence,
can be turned into a vulnerability. If Holmes cares about solving puzzles more
than he cares about the hostages, the game has already been won.

--- THE PUZZLES ---

The specific cases presented by the game vary in their nature and their
difficulty. One involves an unsolved hit-and-run from years earlier and the
specific reanalysis of physical evidence from the original scene. One involves
a cold case murder whose solution requires specific forensic knowledge. One
involves the apparent suicide of a young man whose death, when examined,
reveals something about a scientific fraud at a prestigious institution.

Each case is solved by Holmes within the allotted time. The specific working —
the rapid deduction, the physical evidence analysis, the combination of logical
elimination and intuitive leap — is the episode's procedural core, and it
is done with the show's characteristic visual energy: the on-screen annotations,
the rapid cuts between observation and deduction, the specific quality of
watching an extraordinary intelligence engage with a problem.

Watson's role through the puzzle sequences is to observe, to ground the
investigation in practical reality, and to provide the specific human response
to the hostage situation that Holmes's tunnel vision on the puzzle sometimes
lacks. Holmes is genuinely brilliant at the cases. He is sometimes genuinely
indifferent to the hostages as people — his attention is on the puzzle. Watson
is not indifferent to the hostages, and his specific moral insistence keeps
the investigation in contact with its human stakes.

--- THE BRUCE-PARTINGTON PLANS ---

Running alongside the game's puzzle challenges is an official case involving
stolen defense documents — the plans for a new submarine propulsion system,
the "Bruce-Partington plans" of the Conan Doyle original. A civil servant
has been found dead on the London Underground, carrying the plans. How did
he die, who took the plans, and why?

The Bruce-Partington case is handled partly through Mycroft Holmes, who brings
it to Sherlock through official channels as a matter of national security.
The case requires a different kind of investigation from the game's puzzles —
it is institutional rather than personal, political rather than criminal in
the ordinary sense — but it connects to the game's larger architecture in
ways that emerge only as both investigations develop.

The solution to the Bruce-Partington case involves an assassin — a large,
imposing figure known as the Golem — who is operating in London on behalf
of interests connected to the stolen plans. The Golem's appearance and the
physical threat he represents are the episode's most visceral danger element
before the climax.

--- MORIARTY: THE FULL INTRODUCTION ---

Through the episode's final section, the game's director is confirmed as
Professor James Moriarty. His appearance — when he finally makes himself
present rather than operating through proxies and intermediaries — is one
of the series' most effective moments. Andrew Scott's Moriarty is a specific
and brilliant choice: not the grandiose Victorian villain of stage tradition
but a young, somewhat slight, casually dressed man who moves through the world
with the specific ease of someone who is never afraid of anything because he
has never encountered anything he could not manage.

The specific quality of Scott's performance is the play between surface
pleasantness and underlying vacancy: Moriarty is friendly in the way that
a very confident child is friendly — unselfconsciously, without social anxiety,
without the specific calculations that most adults bring to social interaction.
He is not pretending to be pleasant. He just doesn't care whether he is or
not, and the absence of that caring is what makes him genuinely terrifying.

His specific interest in Holmes is clarified in their confrontations: Moriarty
has been bored for his entire life by the absence of any intelligence
commensurate with his own, and Holmes represents the first genuinely interesting
problem he has encountered. The game is not primarily about the crimes or the
hostages. It is about Moriarty's need to be recognized by someone capable of
recognizing him. He wants Holmes to see him.

--- THE POOL CONFRONTATION ---

The episode ends at the Reichenbach Falls in the source canon — here translated
into the pool of the Golem's apartment building or, more precisely, into a
public swimming pool in a Brixton sports complex. Holmes and Moriarty meet
directly, alone, and the specific dynamic of their first extended face-to-face
encounter is constructed with great care.

Moriarty has planted a bomb — a bomb vest — on John Watson, who is brought
to the pool by Moriarty's people. Watson is wearing the vest under a thick
coat, controlled remotely, and his presence is Moriarty's specific point: he
is demonstrating to Holmes that what Holmes cares about can be used against
him. Watson is Holmes's vulnerability.

The standoff involves Holmes pointing his gun at the bomb vest on Watson —
he will detonate it himself before letting Moriarty use Watson as a hostage —
and Moriarty's red-dot snipers pointed at both of them. The Mexican standoff
is the episode's climax: Holmes and Moriarty in direct intellectual contact
for the first time, both of them fully present, the specific quality of their
engagement charged with something between fascination and hostility.

Moriarty receives a phone call. Whatever the phone call says changes something —
he becomes abruptly different, withdraws the snipers, departs the pool with
a casual and alarming cheerfulness, telling Holmes that "something came up."
The cliffhanger is not resolved. Holmes and Watson are alone at the pool with
the bomb vest still armed, the snipers gone, and no explanation of what just
happened.

--- THE CLIFFHANGER AND WHAT IT MEANS ---

The unresolved ending — Holmes with his gun pointed at the bomb, Watson in
the vest, Moriarty gone — was one of the most discussed cliffhangers in British
television when the series aired. It was resolved at the opening of Series 2
("A Scandal in Belgravia"), but the specific quality of the ending is worth
noting here: it denies the viewer the satisfaction of a conventional resolution
specifically to leave both the character relationship and the tactical situation
open. The game is not over. It is just beginning.

The phone call that changed Moriarty's behavior — whatever it was — is a hook
for future story. The game's architecture, the specific relationship between
Holmes and Moriarty, and the specific threat that Moriarty represents are all
established fully enough that what follows will be legible, but the specific
confrontation is deferred rather than resolved.

--- WHAT THE EPISODE ESTABLISHES ---

"The Great Game" ends Series 1 by having established: the Holmes-Watson
partnership as genuinely functional and personally deep; Moriarty as a worthy
antagonist for the long term; the specific way the series will use classic
Conan Doyle material as inspiration and raw material rather than as text to
be faithfully reproduced; and the specific visual and narrative language of
the show — fast, witty, genuinely dark when it needs to be, and committed to
taking both its central characters entirely seriously.

--- NOTES ON THE EPISODE ---

"The Great Game" is the most technically demanding of the Series 1 episodes —
the multiple puzzle-within-puzzle structure, the Bruce-Partington subplot, and
the Moriarty introduction all need to be managed simultaneously — and Mark
Gatiss's script handles the architecture with considerable skill. Paul McGuigan's
direction is at its best in the pool confrontation: the specific visual grammar
of the scene — the relative positions, the sightlines, the way the space is
used — is precise and effective. Andrew Scott's Moriarty is an extraordinary
piece of casting that would define the character for years.
