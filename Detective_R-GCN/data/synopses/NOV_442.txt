NOV_442 — Eight Perfect Murders

THE LIST


A Blog Post Written Years Ago

Malcolm Kershaw is the co-owner of a small independent bookshop in Boston called Old Devils.
Years ago, as a bookseller and mystery enthusiast, he wrote a casual blog post for the
store's website: a personal list of the most "perfect" murders in crime fiction — the ones
that seemed most difficult to solve or most cleverly constructed. The list included eight
novels: Agatha Christie's *A.B.C. Murders*, Patricia Highsmith's *Strangers on a Train*,
Ira Levin's *A Kiss Before Dying*, Billy Wilder's adapted *Double Indemnity*, Donna Tartt's
*The Secret History*, Scott Turow's *Presumed Innocent*, Anthony Berkeley's *Malice
Aforethought*, and Agatha Christie's *And Then There Were None*.

It was a list written purely for book lovers. He forgot about it almost immediately.


The FBI Comes to the Shop

An FBI agent named Gwen Mulvey arrives at Old Devils and tells Malcolm that someone is
using his list as a murder manual. Real people are being killed using the methods described
in the books on his list — each real-world murder appears to mirror one of his eight
selections. The murders are scattered across different states and have not previously been
connected, but Mulvey has identified the pattern.

She wants Malcolm's help understanding the literary connections and, implicitly, she wants
to understand whether he has any idea who might have done this and why.

Malcolm is shaken. He says he has no idea.

He is not entirely telling the truth.

MALCOLM'S SECRET


A Marriage and a Death

As Malcolm assists Mulvey and the investigation proceeds, the novel gradually reveals the
truth about Malcolm's personal life. He was married. His wife, Claire, died. The death
was ruled accidental — she fell down a staircase at their home.

Malcolm knows it was not accidental. He knows because he pushed her.

This is the novel's central moral and structural secret. Malcolm is not a man helping to
catch a copycat killer from a position of innocence. He is a man who committed a real
murder — inspired, in part, by the very books on his list. He thought about murder the
way the readers of his blog thought about it: abstractly, intellectually, as a puzzle.
And then one night, in a moment of cold decision, he acted on it.

He has lived with this for years, successfully, in the way that people who are never
suspected live with things.

THE COPYCAT


Who Is Using the List?

As the investigation continues, Malcolm and Mulvey work through the real-world murders
and their literary counterparts. The copycat is not simply mimicking methods — they are
doing so with evident literary knowledge, genuine understanding of the books, and a
specific connection to Malcolm and his list.

Malcolm begins to realize that the copycat may know about him — may know what he did to
Claire. The murders feel, increasingly, like a message to Malcolm specifically rather than
a random crime spree dressed in literary clothes.


The Suspects in Malcolm's World

The investigation turns toward people who knew Malcolm, knew his list, and had access to
information about his life. A former girlfriend. A regular customer at the bookshop. People
who intersected with his life in ways that could have given them the information needed
to both commit the copycat crimes and know about his secret.

THE BOOKS AS ARCHITECTURE

A significant pleasure of the novel is its engagement with the eight source texts.
Swanson uses each murder on the list as a chapter structure and as a way of deepening
the reader's relationship with the crime fiction canon. Characters discuss the books
seriously — what makes the murders in them "perfect," what that concept even means,
and what it reveals about why people read murder mysteries in the first place.

The novel is partly a love letter to crime fiction and partly a critique of it — exploring
the uncomfortable relationship between the reader who aestheticizes murder in books and
the human reality of actual killing.

THE REVEAL


The Copycat's Identity and Motive

The copycat killer is revealed to be someone with direct personal knowledge of Malcolm —
someone who knew what he did to Claire and has been, through the murders, constructing an
elaborate trap: drawing Malcolm into a situation where his own guilt will be forced into
the open, or where he will be destroyed by the investigation that his list initiated.

The motive is revenge of a specific kind — not straightforward but literary, in keeping
with a person who thinks in narrative terms and who has constructed the entire scheme as
a kind of plot, with Malcolm as the protagonist being maneuvered toward a particular ending.


Malcolm Cornered

Malcolm is ultimately cornered — not simply by the FBI investigation but by the architecture
of what the copycat has built. The confrontation forces him to decide what to do with the
truth about Claire: confess and face consequences, or find another way out.

RESOLUTION

The copycat is identified and stopped. The chain of literary murders is ended. Mulvey,
whose investigation has been thorough and whose read of Malcolm has never been entirely
comfortable, arrives at the truth about Claire.

Malcolm does not escape. His confession — or the evidence that forces one — brings his
years of successful concealment to an end.

The novel closes on a note that is both satisfying as mystery plotting and genuinely
uncomfortable thematically. Malcolm is a sympathetic narrator for most of the book —
bookish, self-aware, quietly melancholy — and the revelation that he is also a murderer
who benefited from never being caught is Swanson's deliberate challenge to the reader's
relationship with unreliable, morally compromised first-person narration. We have been,
in a sense, reading his confession all along.
