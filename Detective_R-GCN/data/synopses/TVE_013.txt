--- BACKGROUND AND CONTEXT ---

"The Body in the Library" is a two-part BBC Miss Marple television adaptation,
first broadcast in December 1984, directed by Silvio Narizzano. It stars Joan
Hickson in her first appearance as Miss Jane Marple — the role that cemented
her as the definitive screen Marple, a portrayal widely considered the most
faithful and authoritative ever committed to film. Hickson was seventy-eight
years old when the series began, and her Marple is a precise, observant,
occasionally steely woman of deceptive gentleness. Agatha Christie herself
once wrote to Hickson after seeing her in a stage play that she would be perfect
as Marple someday.

The adaptation is based on Christie's 1942 novel of the same name. The script
is by T.R. Bowen and the production captures both the novel's formal mystery
construction and its specific social world of the early 1940s.

--- THE BODY AND THE DISCOVERY ---

The episode opens at Gossington Hall, the home of Colonel Arthur Bantry and
his wife Dolly. The Bantrys are old friends of Miss Marple. Early one morning,
their butler discovers the body of a young woman in the library. She is dressed
garishly — cheap, flashy clothes, heavy makeup, dyed platinum hair — and she
has been strangled. She does not belong in the Bantry library. Nobody in the
household knows her.

Dolly Bantry, characteristically practical, immediately phones Miss Marple
and asks her to come. She is aware that a body found in one's library creates
immediate social scandal — the implication being that the Colonel had some
indiscreet relationship with this young woman — and she wants the matter cleared
up quickly and discreetly.

Miss Marple arrives at Gossington Hall as the police, led by the young Inspector
Slack, begin their investigation. The body is eventually identified as Ruby
Keene, a young dance hostess employed at the Majestic Hotel in the nearby
seaside resort town of Danemouth.

--- RUBY KEENE AND THE MAJESTIC HOTEL ---

The investigation moves to the Majestic Hotel. Ruby Keene was employed as a
professional dance partner — she danced with the hotel's wealthy elderly guests
for payment, a common arrangement in that era. She was young, pretty, ambitious,
and not particularly sophisticated. She had been cultivating a relationship with
Conway Jefferson, a very wealthy man staying at the hotel. Jefferson, who lost
his wife, son, and daughter-in-law in an airplane accident some years earlier,
is confined to a wheelchair. He has been charmed by Ruby and, in a gesture
of somewhat impulsive emotional generosity, has announced his intention to adopt
her legally and leave her a substantial portion of his fortune.

This announcement caused considerable consternation among the people who
expected to benefit from Jefferson's estate: his son-in-law Mark Gaskell and
daughter-in-law Adelaide Jefferson (the spouses of his dead children, who have
remained with him and who depend on his goodwill). Both stand to receive
significantly less if Ruby becomes Jefferson's adopted daughter and heir.

--- THE SECOND BODY ---

The investigation is complicated by a second death. Another young woman is
found burned in a car — initially thought to be Ruby Keene herself, as Ruby
had not been seen since she disappeared the previous night. This body is
subsequently identified as Pamela Reeves, a local girl who had gone missing.

The existence of two dead young women — one in the Bantry library, one in a
burned car — immediately raises questions about whether the two deaths are
connected and whether the original identification of the burned body was
deliberately arranged to confuse the investigation.

Miss Marple attends to both deaths with her characteristic quiet attention,
watching the behavior of everyone connected to the case, listening to
conversations, and making the specific analogical connections to St. Mary Mead
life that constitute her investigative method.

--- THE INVESTIGATION ---

The formal investigation is handled by Inspector Slack (who tends to dismiss
Miss Marple) and Superintendent Harper of the Danemouth police. A young man
named Raymond West — Miss Marple's nephew — is also tangentially involved.
The professional detective Superintendent Melchett works alongside Slack.

The pool of suspects connected to Ruby's death includes:
Mark Gaskell and Adelaide Jefferson — both with financial motive.
Josie Turner — Ruby's cousin, who was also employed at the Majestic as a
dance hostess and who had arranged Ruby's position there.
George Bartlett — a wealthy, somewhat silly young man who had been paying
attention to Ruby.
Various hotel staff and guests.

--- MISS MARPLE'S ANALYSIS ---

Miss Marple's central insight is that the case involves deliberate misdirection.
The body in the Bantry library was placed there — Ruby Keene was killed elsewhere
and transported to Gossington Hall specifically to associate the crime with the
Bantry household and to draw attention away from the Majestic Hotel.

She also recognizes the significance of the burned body: the substitution of
Pamela Reeves for Ruby Keene in the burned car was an attempt to establish
that Ruby died in a car fire rather than by strangulation. Someone killed
Pamela Reeves specifically to use her body as a substitute.

The analogy from St. Mary Mead that guides Miss Marple involves the specific
psychology of someone who plans elaborately — who constructs multiple layers
of misdirection rather than simply committing a crime and hoping not to be
caught. This kind of planning, in Miss Marple's experience, belongs to a
specific type of person: organized, cold-blooded, and capable of genuine cruelty.

--- THE KILLER: JOSIE TURNER AND MARK GASKELL ---

The killers are Josie Turner and Mark Gaskell, acting together.

Josie Turner is Ruby's cousin. She is also in love with Mark Gaskell, and the
two have been having a secret affair. When Conway Jefferson announced his
intention to adopt Ruby and leave her money, both Josie and Mark stood to lose
enormously: Mark from the reduction of his expected inheritance, Josie from
the loss of Mark's eventual wealth and therefore their future together.

They killed Ruby together. Josie, as Ruby's cousin and colleague, had access
to her and could arrange a meeting. The body was transported to Gossington Hall
to misdirect the investigation. Pamela Reeves was killed in cold blood — entirely
innocent, entirely uninvolved — specifically to provide a substitute burned
body that would initially be identified as Ruby.

The planning required, and the specific coldness of killing Pamela Reeves, is
what Miss Marple identified as the signature of the criminal type she had
recognized: someone who could sacrifice a completely innocent person as a tool.

--- RESOLUTION ---

Miss Marple presents her conclusions to the relevant police authority. The
evidence — physical and circumstantial, assembled through the investigation —
is sufficient to establish Josie and Mark's guilt. Both are arrested.

Conway Jefferson is left to grieve for Ruby, who was genuinely fond of him
even while others plotted against the relationship she represented. The Bantrys
are cleared of any connection to the crime — the scandal that Dolly feared
is resolved.

--- JOAN HICKSON'S MARPLE ---

The production is notable above all for Hickson's performance. Her Marple is
not a cozy figure but a precise, occasionally chilling one — a woman who sees
human nature with total clarity and finds, in the patterns of village life, a
complete guide to the worst of what people do to each other. The scene in which
she identifies the killer type — still, quiet, watching — is characteristic of
the series' approach: intelligence rendered as stillness rather than display.
