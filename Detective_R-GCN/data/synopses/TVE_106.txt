--- BACKGROUND AND CONTEXT ---

"His Last Vow" is the third and final episode of Series 3 of the BBC's
"Sherlock," first broadcast on January 12, 2014. It is directed by Nick Hurran
and written by Steven Moffat, and stars Benedict Cumberbatch as Sherlock Holmes
and Martin Freeman as Dr. John Watson, with Lars Mikkelsen delivering one of
the series' finest guest performances as Charles Augustus Magnussen. The episode
is based primarily on Arthur Conan Doyle's "The Adventure of Charles Augustus
Milverton" — one of the darkest and most morally complex of the original Holmes
stories, in which Holmes burgles a blackmailer's house and witnesses his murder
without intervening.

The episode is the culmination of Series 3's arc, which has been building
through "The Empty Hearse" (Holmes's return from the dead) and "The Sign of
Three" (Watson's wedding), and represents the series' most ambitious and most
morally demanding single episode. It is also the episode in which the long-term
myth arc of the series delivers its most significant revelation about Sherlock's
family.

--- CHARLES AUGUSTUS MAGNUSSEN ---

Charles Augustus Magnussen is introduced as the most dangerous man in Europe —
not because he has physical capability or criminal violence at his disposal
but because of what he knows. He is a newspaper magnate and media figure of
global reach who has built his empire on the systematic collection of personal
information about powerful people — their secrets, their weaknesses, their
vulnerabilities — and who uses this information as leverage to control those
people entirely.

Magnussen does not use money. He does not use conventional threats. He uses
the specific terror of someone who has every secret you most want kept, who
can demonstrate that he has it at a moment's notice, and who will not be bought
or threatened because his specific position — his specific knowledge — places
him above those conventional forms of pressure. Lars Mikkelsen plays him with
an extraordinary specific quality: a man of complete surface pleasantness whose
specific acts of power — the casual demonstrations of what he knows about
whoever he is speaking to — are rendered as the most horrifying thing in the
episode.

His relationship to the people he controls is one of pure dominance. He is
not simply blackmailing them for money — he is holding them in a permanent
state of subjugation, using their secrets to make them useful to him in
whatever way he needs them to be useful. The scale of his operation — the
number of powerful people he controls — makes him effectively untouchable
by conventional means: the people who could stop him are themselves his subjects.

--- MARY WATSON'S SECRET ---

The episode's central revelation concerns Mary Watson (Amanda Abbington) —
John Watson's wife, introduced in Series 3 and established as warm, intelligent,
and entirely devoted to both Watson and, in her specific way, to Sherlock.

Mary is not who she presented herself as. She is a former intelligence operative —
specifically, a former CIA or equivalent agent whose original identity is not
the identity she has been living as when she met and married Watson. Her past
includes operations whose specific nature is highly classified and highly
dangerous.

Magnussen knows Mary's real identity. He has the specific documentation that
establishes who she really is and what she has done — documentation that, if
released, would destroy not just her life but potentially Watson's and potentially
the lives of people connected to her past operations.

The specific revelation of Mary's history — to Watson, to Sherlock, and to the
viewer — is handled with the care that a major character revelation in a
long-running series demands. Watson's response to learning that his wife is
not who he thought she was is one of Freeman's finest moments in the series:
not melodramatic but specific and real, the response of someone who genuinely
loves someone and who is processing the specific disorientation of a major
discovered deception.

--- MAGNUSSEN'S VAULTS: APPLEDORE ---

The investigation of Magnussen is complicated by a specific problem: where
does he keep what he knows? His leverage over the people he controls requires
documentation — the secrets must be recorded somewhere, and that somewhere
must be findable and destroyable if he is to be stopped. The assumption is
that his private estate, Appledore, contains a physical archive — vaults of
some kind that hold the physical documentation of everything he knows about
everyone he controls.

This assumption drives Sherlock's plan: he will access Appledore's vaults, he
will find the documentation, and he will use it as leverage against Magnussen
in a way that ends his power. The specific plan — which involves Sherlock
placing himself in Magnussen's debt by providing access to Mycroft's government
work, ostensibly as a trade but actually as the mechanism for getting inside
Appledore — is one of the most elaborate schemes Holmes runs in the series.

--- THE EMPTY HOUSE AND THE GUNSHOT ---

A significant early sequence in the episode involves Holmes, investigating
a missing person case for Watson, finding himself in a drug house — not for
the first time in the series, as Holmes's history with drug use is a recurring
element — and then confronting a situation that reveals something significant
about who is connected to whom.

The shooting of Sherlock — which happens at a key moment, by Mary, who has
followed Holmes to Magnussen's building and who shoots Holmes to prevent him
from complicating her situation with Magnussen — is the episode's central
dramatic shock. Sherlock is shot and, in one of the series' most formally
inventive sequences, the experience of being shot is rendered through Sherlock's
own perspective: the specific mental process by which he manages the physical
trauma, deploying his intelligence in the service of keeping himself alive by
managing the psychological experience of dying.

The "mind palace" sequences — Sherlock's inner mental architecture — are used
here to extraordinary effect: his unconscious mind organizing the crisis of
the bullet wound into a form his intelligence can manage, calling on the specific
resources of his memory and his relationships to find the motivation to survive.
The specific appearance of Moriarty in these sequences — used as a psychological
tool by Sherlock's own mind — is one of the episode's most disturbing and most
effective formal choices.

--- THE APPLEDORE REVELATION ---

The climax of the episode involves Sherlock, Watson, Magnussen, and the specific
revelation about Appledore's vaults. Mycroft's people arrive at Appledore
expecting to arrest both Sherlock (who has apparently committed treason by
handing over classified material) and Magnussen. The trade — Sherlock providing
Mycroft's files in exchange for Mary's safety — has apparently been completed.

And then Magnussen demonstrates, in the most characteristic way possible,
what his specific power actually consists of:

There are no vaults. There is no physical archive. The documentation that
everyone assumed was stored at Appledore — the physical records of every secret
he holds over every powerful person he controls — exists only in his own mind.
Everything is a "mind palace." Magnussen's specific power is his memory: he
has memorized everything, and the physical documents were used only when needed
as demonstrations of what he possessed, not as a permanent archive.

This revelation — that the leverage cannot be destroyed by finding the location
of the records, because the records are inside a man's head — collapses
Sherlock's plan completely. There is nothing at Appledore that can be seized
or destroyed. Magnussen cannot be stopped by conventional means. He will
continue to control the people he controls because the knowledge cannot be taken
from him.

--- SHERLOCK KILLS MAGNUSSEN ---

Sherlock shoots Magnussen in the head.

He shoots him in front of Mycroft's people, on the lawn of Appledore, in full
view of multiple witnesses. He does not attempt to conceal it. It is a direct
execution, carried out the moment the revelation of the mind palace makes clear
that no other course of action will work.

This is the episode's moral and dramatic climax — more extreme even than the
Orient Express moment in the Poirot series, because here there is no ambiguity
about the act. Holmes commits an act of deliberate murder in front of police
and his own brother, for the specific reason that it is the only way to stop
a man who cannot otherwise be stopped.

The specific weight of this — Holmes, who has always operated within the law
or at its edges, choosing the extra-legal execution of a man who genuinely
cannot be addressed through any other means — is handled with the directness
it deserves. Sherlock knows exactly what he is doing. He knows that he will
face consequences. He does it anyway.

Mycroft's management of the situation — his specific use of his government
position to manage what happens to Sherlock — results in Sherlock being
dispatched on a mission rather than prosecuted. The specific consequences for
Sherlock are real but are managed in ways that reflect Mycroft's specific power
and his specific love for his brother.

--- MORIARTY'S RETURN ---

The episode's final moments deliver the series' most significant cliffhanger:
as Sherlock is departing on his exile mission, Moriarty's face appears on
every screen in Britain — the same image, repeated, with the message "Miss me?"
Sherlock's plane is turned around. The question of how Moriarty, who was
definitively dead at the end of Series 2, can be appearing on British television
is the specific hook for Series 4.

Whether this is literally Moriarty returned from death, a recording he prepared
in advance, or something else entirely is left entirely open at the episode's
close.

--- NOTES ON THE EPISODE ---

"His Last Vow" is the most morally ambitious episode of the Sherlock series
and, in many viewers' estimation, its finest. The Magnussen character is one
of the series' great achievements — a villain whose specific menace is entirely
intellectual and whose specific method of power (the intimate demonstration
of knowledge in the most personal possible settings) is rendered with a physical
specificity that makes it viscerally unpleasant. Lars Mikkelsen's performance
is extraordinary. The Mary revelation is handled with unusual care. And the
ending — Sherlock's execution of Magnussen, the specific cost of that act,
and the Moriarty cliffhanger — is the kind of finale that makes everything
preceding it feel like preparation.
