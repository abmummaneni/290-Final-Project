--- BACKGROUND AND CONTEXT ---

"In Spite of Thunder," published in 1960, is a late entry in John Dickson Carr's
Gideon Fell series. Carr — writing under both his own name and the pseudonym
Carter Dickson for his Sir Henry Merrivale novels — was the undisputed master
of the locked-room mystery and the impossible crime, and his specific achievement
was the consistent construction of murders that appeared genuinely physically
impossible and solutions that were both technically surprising and completely
fair. His plots are among the most elaborately constructed in the detective
fiction canon.

Dr. Gideon Fell is Carr's most beloved detective — a large, wheezing, enthusiastic
man of enormous physical inconvenience and equally enormous intellectual power,
who moves through the world with the specific quality of a Victorian sage dropped
into the twentieth century. He wears a shovel hat and uses two canes, drinks
beer in quantities that alarm his companions, and delivers elaborate discourses
on the locked-room mystery as a literary form (his famous "Locked-Room Lecture"
in "The Hollow Man" is one of the genre's canonical theoretical texts). He
is warm, funny, and genuinely brilliant.

"In Spite of Thunder" is set in Geneva — specifically on the terrace and in
the environs of the Hôtel Belmont above the city — in the period immediately
after the Second World War. The European setting, the specific atmosphere of
a luxury hotel in Switzerland, and the specific cast of characters who gather
there give the novel a tone that is more cosmopolitan and more explicitly
post-war in its sensibility than the English country-house settings of many
Carr novels.

--- THE APPARENT SUICIDE ---

The novel's central event is the death of Audrey Donahue — a woman who falls
from the terrace of the Hôtel Belmont and is found dead below. The death is
immediately certified as suicide: Audrey was a woman of known psychological
difficulty, her specific personal history included previous breakdowns, and
the specific circumstances of her fall — alone on the terrace, at night —
seem consistent with a self-inflicted end.

The problem is that the specific witness evidence does not support suicide.
A man named Brian Donahue — Audrey's husband — was present on the terrace.
He saw something that the initial investigation does not fully account for.
And the specific physical evidence of the terrace — the specific marks, the
specific configuration of what was found there — tells Gideon Fell, when he
arrives, that this was not a suicide.

The impossibility is constructed around the specific moment of the death: the
terrace was not empty, the area below was visible from multiple vantage points,
and the specific configuration of witnesses and sight lines should have made
an unobserved murder impossible. And yet if it was not suicide, it was murder —
and if it was murder, it was committed in conditions that should have made
murder impossible.

--- THE SETTING: GENEVA AND ITS WORLD ---

The Hôtel Belmont and its surrounding Geneva world provide the investigation's
social context. The hotel's guests — a specific collection of post-war Europeans
and Americans, each with their own specific histories and specific connections
to each other — form the suspect pool.

The post-war European setting is used with real attention to its specific
character: the atmosphere of a Switzerland that remained neutral during the
war and that is now a gathering place for people from every side of the conflict,
managing the specific social dynamics of a world whose old certainties have
been destroyed and whose new ones are not yet established. The specific
relationships between the characters — who served where, who did what, who
knows what about whom from the war years — are part of the social fabric the
investigation must navigate.

Among the significant characters:

Brian Donahue — Audrey's husband, an American of specific background, whose
account of what he saw on the terrace is both the investigation's primary
witness testimony and, inevitably, one of its primary subjects of scrutiny.

Eve Ferrier — a woman with a specific connection to Audrey's past whose
specific presence at the hotel at the time of the death is not coincidental.

Donahue's friends and the hotel's other guests, each of whom has a specific
relationship to the events and a specific alibi to be assessed.

--- THE INVESTIGATION: FELL IN GENEVA ---

Fell arrives in Geneva — in the specific way of Carr's detective, immediately
establishing himself as the most distinctive presence in any room he enters —
and investigates the death with his characteristic combination of elaborate
theoretical exposition and precise physical analysis.

His investigation of the terrace — the specific marks, the specific sight lines,
the specific physical evidence of what was and was not present — establishes
that the death was not suicide. The specific mechanism by which Audrey Donahue
was killed while appearing to fall of her own accord is the novel's central
locked-room problem: not a room, but a moment — a moment of death that appeared
to occur in conditions where murder was impossible.

The investigation also reconstructs Audrey's specific history and specific
relationships: the personal connections that gave specific people specific
motives for her death, the specific secrets whose exposure her continued
existence threatened, and the specific dynamics of the marriages and alliances
among the hotel's guests that the death has disturbed.

The title — "In Spite of Thunder" — refers both to the specific atmospheric
conditions of the death (a thunderstorm was approaching when Audrey fell) and
to a specific piece of Shakespeare that appears in the novel, whose meaning
and relevance to the case Fell expounds in the manner characteristic of the
series: with enormous enthusiasm and apparent digression that turns out to be
entirely relevant.

--- THE IMPOSSIBLE CRIME SOLUTION ---

The specific mechanism by which the murder was committed while appearing to
be a suicide — the specific physical trick that the killer used to create the
appearance of a self-inflicted death in conditions where witnesses were present
and where an unobserved murder should have been impossible — is revealed in
Fell's reconstruction.

The solution is technical but fair: all the elements required to understand
it were present in the novel's description of the terrace and its specific
physical configuration. The specific device the killer used exploited the
specific sight lines and the specific lighting conditions of the terrace to
create an apparently witnessed event that was in fact staged — the witnesses
saw what the killer arranged for them to see rather than what actually happened.

This is quintessential Carr: the impossibility is genuine (the situation as
described really does seem to preclude murder), the solution is surprising
(the specific device is unexpected), and the fairness is complete (the
information required to understand the solution was present throughout, properly
read).

--- THE KILLER: EVE FERRIER ---

The killer is Eve Ferrier — the woman with a specific prior connection to
Audrey Donahue whose presence at the hotel at the time of the death was
constructed to appear coincidental.

Eve's motive is rooted in the specific relationship between herself and Audrey —
a history that goes back before the hotel and before Geneva, to specific events
in their shared past whose specific content gave Eve both the motivation and
the specific knowledge of Audrey's psychology that the murder required.

The murder required understanding Audrey's specific patterns of behaviour —
where she went on the terrace, when, and under what conditions — and exploiting
that knowledge to create the specific staged situation that allowed the killing
to take place in apparent view of witnesses. Eve had the specific prior knowledge
of Audrey that made this possible: only someone who knew her well enough could
have constructed the specific scenario the murder required.

Fell identifies Eve through the combined analysis of the physical evidence of
the terrace (which establishes that the death was staged), the alibi reconstruction
(which establishes who had the access and the opportunity the staging required),
and the specific psychological analysis of who had the prior knowledge of Audrey
that the staging demanded. Eve is the only person at the hotel who had all
three.

--- NOTES ON THE NOVEL ---

"In Spite of Thunder" is a late Carr that demonstrates his continued mastery
of the impossible crime form even at a point in his career where his earlier
creative intensity was diminishing. The Geneva setting is used with real
atmospheric skill — the specific post-war European world is rendered with
more political and social awareness than the English country houses of the
earlier Fell novels. The impossible crime solution is characteristic Carr:
technically precise, fair, and genuinely surprising. And Fell himself — always
Carr's warmest and most fully realised creation — is in characteristic form:
digressive, enthusiastic, physically inconvenient, and entirely correct.
