TVE_041 | Poirot: Evil Under the Sun | Director: Brian Farnham | TV Episode
================================================================================
FULL EPISODE SYNOPSIS — SPOILERS INCLUDED
================================================================================

--- BACKGROUND AND CONTEXT ---

"Evil Under the Sun" is a two-part episode of ITV's Agatha Christie's Poirot,
first broadcast in May 2001. It is directed by Brian Farnham and stars David
Suchet as Hercule Poirot. Unlike most episodes in the series, this adaptation
does not feature Captain Hastings or Miss Lemon — it is Poirot essentially
alone, without his usual supporting cast, which gives it a slightly different
dynamic. It is based on Christie's 1941 novel of the same name, set at a
holiday resort on a rocky coastal island and widely regarded as one of the finest
of the Poirot novels — a beautifully constructed puzzle with a particularly
satisfying alibi solution.

The adaptation moves the setting from the English coast of the novel to the
Adriatic — an Italian or Yugoslav coastal island — giving it a Mediterranean
visual richness that suits the story's sun-drenched atmosphere.

--- THE SETTING: DAPHNE'S ISLAND ---

Poirot is on holiday at a clifftop hotel on a small island, staying at the
establishment run by the Gardener family. The hotel's other guests form the
closed world of suspects that the episode will work through with Christie's
characteristic precision. They include:

Arlena Marshall — a spectacularly beautiful, famously glamorous former actress
who is now the wife of Captain Kenneth Marshall. Arlena is the center of
attention at the hotel, drawing the admiring and resentful eyes of everyone
around her. She has a reputation for romantic entanglements and is currently
the object of considerable gossip.

Captain Kenneth Marshall — Arlena's husband, a quiet, contained man who
seems to endure his wife's behavior with stoicism.

Linda Marshall — Kenneth's teenage daughter from a previous marriage, who
regards her stepmother with active hostility.

Rosamund and Horace Darnley — a pair of old friends of Marshall's; Rosamund
is herself attractive and has a history with Kenneth.

Patrick and Christine Redfern — a young married couple. Patrick is visibly
infatuated with Arlena and has been pursuing her openly, to his wife
Christine's barely concealed distress.

Reverend and Emily Brewster — a clergyman and his sister.

Stephen Lane — a clergyman with strong views about sin and wickedness who
has been watching Arlena with something beyond ordinary clerical disapproval.

Odell and Carrie Gardener — the American couple who own and run the hotel.

--- ARLENA MARSHALL IS KILLED ---

The holiday's pleasures are shattered when Arlena Marshall is found dead on
a secluded beach — Pixy Cove, a small beach accessible only by boat or by
a difficult cliff path. She has been strangled. Her body is discovered by
the Reverend Brewster and Patrick Redfern, who had gone looking for her.

Arlena had gone to the cove alone, apparently to meet someone — the obvious
candidate being Patrick Redfern, whose infatuation with her had been visible
to everyone. Redfern, however, has an alibi: he spent the critical period
rowing Christine around the island, in full view of witnesses, before going
with Brewster to the cove.

Captain Marshall also has an alibi: he was in his room all morning, typing
letters, heard by the chambermaid and others, and the letters confirm the
typing time.

Nearly everyone at the hotel has an alibi for the critical period. The case
appears almost impossible.

--- POIROT'S INVESTIGATION ---

Poirot investigates with the resources available to him at an isolated island
hotel — no police laboratory, no Hastings to send on errands, no Miss Lemon
to manage his correspondence. He observes, he interviews, he thinks.

He is particularly interested in several specific details:

The suntan. Arlena was a devoted sunbather who spent hours on the beach every
day achieving a very specific, deep, even tan. Something about the condition
of her body when found is slightly inconsistent with this.

The timing. The window during which the murder could have occurred, given
the various witnesses and alibis, is apparently very narrow. But Poirot is
interested in whether the apparent timing is the actual timing.

The cove. Pixy Cove is secluded and difficult to reach. Arlena went there
for a reason — to meet someone. But who? And when?

Christine Redfern's behavior. Christine is the wronged wife, visibly suffering
from her husband's infatuation with Arlena. Her distress appears genuine.
But something about Christine's specific manner and specific movements draws
Poirot's attention.

--- THE ALIBI TRICK ---

The solution to "Evil Under the Sun" is one of Christie's most elegant alibi
constructions, and it requires Poirot — and the viewer — to realize that the
fundamental assumption about who was killed and when has been manipulated.

The murder did not happen when it appeared to happen. The critical deception
involves Christine Redfern.

Christine is not the innocent wronged wife she appears to be. She and Patrick
Redfern are conspirators. They planned Arlena's murder together — not in a
fit of jealous rage but as a premeditated scheme.

The scheme worked as follows:

Earlier in the morning, while appearing to sunbathe on the main beach, Christine
went to Pixy Cove and strangled Arlena Marshall. The timing of this visit was
early — before the period for which most alibis account.

Christine then returned to the main beach and resumed her sunbathing.

Later, when Patrick and the Reverend Brewster "discovered" Arlena's body in
the cove, the discovery was staged — Patrick knew the body would be there
because he had arranged for it to be there.

The apparent window of the murder — the time when everyone assumed it must
have happened, based on the last confirmed sightings of Arlena alive — was
manipulated by the conspirators.

--- THE SUNTAN AND THE TIMING ---

The key physical evidence is the suntan. Arlena was a dedicated sunbather
and always achieved a deep, consistent all-over tan. But the body found in
the cove did not have the tan that Arlena's body should have had if she died
in the mid-morning sun after her usual routine.

The inconsistency tells Poirot that Arlena was not in the sun in the hours
before she died — she was killed earlier than the apparent timeline suggests,
before she had been sunbathing that day. Christine killed her early, before
the morning's sunbathing session, not during the window that all the alibis
were constructed to cover.

--- THE MOTIVE ---

The motive for the conspiracy between Patrick and Christine Redfern is financial.
Arlena Marshall — before her theatrical career declined and she married Kenneth —
had been involved with a man who defrauded investors, and she was the beneficiary
of money that was not legitimately hers. Patrick Redfern had a specific financial
connection to this earlier story: he or someone he is connected to was damaged
by the fraud, and the money that Arlena controlled would resolve that situation.

Alternatively — and the specific motive varies somewhat between the novel
and various adaptations — the Redferns' conspiracy was motivated by their
desire to access funds that Arlena controlled through her marriage to Kenneth,
which they could not reach while she was alive and which would become accessible
after her death in ways that their legal relationship to the situation made
advantageous.

The precise financial mechanism is complex but the essential point is clear:
the Redferns were not acting from passion but from greed, and Patrick's
ostensible infatuation with Arlena was itself part of the deception — a
performance designed to create a visible suspect (himself, apparently besotted)
while Christine executed the actual killing.

--- LINDA MARSHALL ---

The episode adds complexity through Linda Marshall, the hostile teenage
stepdaughter. Linda had her own dark thoughts about Arlena — she has been
nursing a murderous hatred — and the investigation briefly entertains the
possibility that Linda acted on those thoughts. There is evidence that
implicates her superficially and that she herself, through guilt and adolescent
self-dramatization, does nothing to dispel.

Poirot addresses Linda's situation with characteristic gentleness: she did not
kill Arlena, and her guilt is not about the murder but about thoughts she had
and gestures she made that she feared had somehow caused or contributed to the
death. He relieves her of this burden while establishing that she is not the killer.

--- RESOLUTION ---

Patrick and Christine Redfern are exposed. Poirot presents the complete case —
the early timing of the murder, the suntan evidence, the financial motive, the
specific movements of Christine on the morning in question — and the
impersonation-and-alibi scheme collapses under his analysis.

Both are arrested. The apparent story of the jealous wife and the unfaithful
husband is revealed to be a performance from beginning to end — two conspirators
who played their roles with considerable skill and were undone by a Belgian
detective on holiday who noticed something wrong about a suntan.

--- NOTES ON THE EPISODE ---

"Evil Under the Sun" is one of the most accomplished of the Suchet series —
the Adriatic setting is visually magnificent, the closed-world of the island
hotel is perfectly constructed for the Golden Age puzzle form, and the alibi
solution is both fair and genuinely surprising. The episode is notable for
operating without Hastings and Miss Lemon, which gives Suchet's Poirot an
unusual solitude and self-sufficiency. His observation of the suntan —
the tiny physical detail that unlocks the entire construction — is a model
of the Poirot method: the grey cells applied to the one thing everyone else
has seen but nobody has properly noticed.
