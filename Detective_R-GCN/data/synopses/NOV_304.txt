NOV_304 — Full Dark House
Christopher Fowler, 2003
Full Plot Synopsis (Bryant & May #1)

SERIES CONTEXT

Arthur Bryant and John May are the detectives at the heart of the Peculiar Crimes Unit
(PCU), a small and idiosyncratic London investigative unit that handles cases too strange,
too politically sensitive, or too outside normal police parameters for mainstream
investigation. Bryant is elderly, eccentric, deeply learned in London folklore and occult
history, and often exasperating. May is slightly younger, more conventionally minded, and
emotionally steadier — the anchor to Bryant's erratic brilliance. The series alternates
between their present-day cases and their past, following the partnership across decades
of London's history.

Full Dark House is both the first published novel in the series and the earliest case
chronologically.

THE FRAMING NARRATIVE: THE PRESENT


The Explosion

In the novel's present day, the Peculiar Crimes Unit's offices are destroyed by an explosion.
Arthur Bryant is killed.

John May, survivor and partner of sixty years, begins investigating the explosion and Bryant's
death while simultaneously grieving him. He is given access to Bryant's old case files and
begins reading through the records of their very first case together — the case that began
their partnership in 1940. The novel moves between May's present-day investigation and the
1940 case as recorded in Bryant's notes.

THE 1940 CASE: THE PALACE THEATRE


London in the Blitz

The year is 1940. London is under German bombardment. The Blitz is ongoing — nights of
bombing, days of rubble and smoke, a city trying to function in the shadow of its own
potential destruction. In this atmosphere, a new production is being mounted at the Palace
Theatre in the West End: an ambitious, experimental version of Orpheus and Eurydice.

The production is extraordinary and strange — its director is attempting something
thematically connected to death, the underworld, and transformation, which in Blitz-era
London carries an uncomfortable resonance. The cast and crew are extraordinary: singers,
dancers, designers, and creative people drawn together for an unprecedented show.


The Murders Begin

Members of the company begin dying. The deaths are bizarre and seem to echo themes from
the opera being performed — they involve elements from the myth of Orpheus, as if someone
is staging murders that comment on or parallel the production. The first victim is found
in circumstances that make the theatrical connection unmistakable.

The murders continue as rehearsals and then performances proceed — because in Blitz-era
London, stopping a production for murders would be, in its own way, a capitulation to
chaos. The show goes on, and the killer keeps killing.


Bryant and May's First Meeting

Arthur Bryant, a young and already deeply eccentric detective with an unusual knowledge of
myth, folklore, and the occult, is assigned to the case. He is paired with John May, who
is more straightforwardly conventional but equally sharp. They are immediately an odd couple
— Bryant's methods are intuitive, associative, and draw on obscure knowledge; May's are
more empirical. But the combination works, and their first case is also the beginning of a
friendship that will last sixty years.

Fowler renders young Bryant and May with warmth and precision — they are recognizably the
people they will become, but untested, beginning to find their methods and their trust in
each other.


The Theatre World

Fowler's knowledge of theatrical history is on full display. The Palace production is
richly detailed — the creative personalities, the technical demands of a large-scale
production, the particular world of West End theatre in wartime. Characters include the
director, the lead performers, the stage manager, designers, and the assorted personalities
that make a theatre company. Any of them could be the killer.


The Occult and Mythological Thread

Bryant's investigative instincts draw him toward the mythological and occult dimensions of
the case. The murders are staged with a theatrical intelligence that suggests someone deeply
familiar with the themes of Orpheus — death, transformation, the journey into darkness, the
conditions of return. Bryant reads the murders as a kind of argument or performance, and
pursues the killer through that interpretive framework.

This is characteristic of the Bryant & May series: the supernatural and occult are not
literally present, but Bryant uses them as interpretive tools, understanding that some
killers think in mythological terms and that meeting them in their own framework is the
fastest route to understanding them.

THE RESOLUTION OF THE 1940 CASE

Bryant and May identify the killer within the theatre company — someone whose connection
to the production's themes is personal rather than artistic, and whose murders were a form
of private performance, enacting something they needed to express through the vocabulary of
the myth.

The resolution is achieved against the backdrop of the Blitz — London burning, the theatre
shaking with bombs, the city simultaneously being destroyed and refusing to be destroyed.
The case is closed, the killer is accounted for, and the partnership of Bryant and May is
formally begun.

THE PRESENT-DAY RESOLUTION


Who Bombed the PCU?

May's investigation into the present-day explosion — the attack that killed Bryant — runs
through the novel as a second mystery. As he reads the 1940 case files, he is also pursuing
whoever planted the bomb.

The connection between the 1940 case and the present-day attack is real: something from
that first case — a loose end, a surviving threat, an unresolved consequence — has reached
forward across sixty years to destroy the unit and kill Bryant.

May resolves the present-day case, identifies the person responsible for the bombing, and
understands the connection to the 1940 events.


A Final Twist

The novel ends with a revelation about Bryant that reframes certain elements of what has
been presented — Fowler gives the series opener a final note that is both emotionally
resonant and structurally clever, establishing that these two old men and their long
partnership contain more than any single case can reveal.

The tone throughout is elegiac — a love letter to London, to old-fashioned detective work,
to the particular quality of a partnership that has lasted a lifetime, and to the city's
stubborn capacity to survive.
