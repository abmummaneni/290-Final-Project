NOV_402 — Framed!
James Ponti, 2016
Full Plot Synopsis (Framed! #1 / TOAST series)

SERIES CONTEXT AND AUDIENCE

Framed! is a middle-grade mystery novel aimed at readers roughly ages 8–12. The tone is
energetic, humorous, and fast-paced, with a strong emphasis on smart kids solving crimes
that adults have missed. The mystery plotting is genuine — Ponti constructs real puzzles —
but the register is light and fun throughout.


━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━


FLORIAN BATES AND TOAST


A New Kid in Washington, D.C.

Twelve-year-old Florian Bates has just moved to Washington, D.C., where his parents have
taken jobs: his mother at the National Gallery of Art and his father with the State
Department. Florian is new to the city, new to his school, and immediately notices
things — details, patterns, inconsistencies — that other people walk past without seeing.

He has developed a system he calls TOAST: Theory Of All Small Things. The idea is that
small, overlooked details tell the true story of any situation; large obvious facts are
what everyone looks at, but the small things — the things people don't think to hide
because they don't think anyone is looking — are where the truth lives. TOAST is Florian's
formalized version of the kind of hyperobservant thinking that makes a natural detective.


Margaret

Florian meets Margaret Campbell, a classmate who is the daughter of an FBI agent. Margaret
is confident, socially adept in a way Florian is not, and has grown up around law enforcement
and the specific Washington, D.C. world of government and security. She and Florian form an
immediate partnership — she provides the social navigation and the FBI connections; he
provides the TOAST-driven analytical observation.

Their dynamic is warm and funny. They are a good team.


━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━


THE CRIME


Art Theft at the National Gallery

A significant artwork goes missing from the National Gallery of Art — a painting worth
millions. The theft is sophisticated: it happened without triggering the museum's security
systems, without obvious signs of forced entry or exit, and without clear evidence of who
was responsible.

The FBI is investigating. Margaret's father is involved. Florian, whose mother works at
the museum, has both access and motivation to pay close attention.


Someone Is Being Framed

The investigation quickly reveals that the theft has been constructed to implicate a
specific innocent person — someone who has been carefully positioned to appear guilty.
The framing is deliberate and sophisticated, suggesting the real thieves are not simply
opportunists but have planned carefully and have an agenda beyond the painting itself.

Florian, applying TOAST, begins to see the construction of the frame — the small details
that don't quite fit, the evidence that is just slightly too neat, the things that would
only be visible to someone looking at the small things rather than the large ones.


━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━


THE INVESTIGATION


Florian and Margaret Work the Case

Florian and Margaret pursue the investigation in the way middle-grade mystery protagonists
do: with energy, wit, access to adults who are either helpful or oblivious, and the
particular advantage of being underestimated. Adults do not expect twelve-year-olds to
be solving art theft cases, which means Florian and Margaret can move through spaces and
conversations that would be closed to more obviously official investigators.

Florian applies TOAST systematically — cataloging small observations, building connections
between them, and gradually assembling a picture of what actually happened and who is
responsible.


The Museum World

Ponti uses Florian's mother's position at the National Gallery to give the novel a strong
sense of the museum's physical world — how artworks are stored, handled, and protected;
how security systems work; who has access to what. The procedural detail is age-appropriate
but genuine, giving the mystery real texture.


━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━


RESOLUTION

Florian and Margaret identify the real thieves and the person being framed. The TOAST
methodology proves out — the small things, assembled correctly, tell the complete true
story that the large, planted evidence obscured.

The case is resolved with the FBI's involvement, with Florian and Margaret's work providing
the key insights that the adult investigators had missed. The framed party is exonerated.
The real culprits are identified.

Florian's usefulness to the FBI is established by the case's resolution, setting up his
ongoing relationship with Margaret's father and the official investigative world in
subsequent novels. He is, essentially, recruited as an unofficial junior consultant.

The novel ends with Florian settled into Washington, D.C. — no longer the new kid,
now a person with a purpose and a partner — and with TOAST validated as a system worth
trusting.
