TVE_023 | Poirot: Peril at End House | Director: Renny Rye | TV Episode
================================================================================
FULL EPISODE SYNOPSIS — SPOILERS INCLUDED
================================================================================

--- BACKGROUND AND CONTEXT ---

"Peril at End House" is a two-part episode of ITV's Agatha Christie's Poirot
series, first broadcast in January 1990. It is directed by Renny Rye and stars
David Suchet as Hercule Poirot and Hugh Fraser as Captain Hastings. It is
based on Christie's 1932 novel of the same name, widely considered one of the
finest Poirot novels for the elegance of its central twist — a twist that
genuinely surprises most readers and viewers even when they are expecting
Christie to mislead them.

The episode is set on the Cornish coast and captures the specific combination
of sea air, old houses, and leisured summer atmosphere that Christie uses to
create the sense of a world that seems safe but is not.

--- POIROT AND HASTINGS ON HOLIDAY ---

Poirot and Hastings are holidaying at a hotel in St. Loo on the Cornish coast.
Poirot, characteristically, is enjoying the sea air with ambivalence — he
prefers the indoors, the warmth, and the order of the city — while Hastings
is enthusiastically bronzing himself and enjoying the scenery.

At the hotel they encounter Nick Buckley, a spirited young woman who owns
End House, an old property on the headland overlooking the sea. Nick is charming,
apparently carefree, and — Poirot notices — narrowly escaping death with
remarkable frequency.

A picture falls from a wall and nearly hits her. A car's brakes have been
tampered with. A bee in her hat turns out to have been placed there and could
have caused a severe reaction. None of these incidents, individually, seem
conclusive — but Poirot, who does not believe in coincidence, identifies them
as a series of murder attempts by someone who very much wants Nick Buckley dead.

--- END HOUSE AND NICK'S CIRCLE ---

Nick Buckley is the center of a social circle that gathers around End House
for the summer season. Her friends and connections include:

Freddie Rice — Nick's close friend, a young woman with a drug problem
(she takes chloral) and a complicated personal history.

Jim Lazarus — a wealthy art dealer who is interested in Nick romantically.

Commander George Challenger — a naval officer also attached to Nick.

Michael Seton — a famous aviator who has been making a long-distance flight
around the world and is presumed dead when his plane goes missing over the ocean.

Charles Vyse — Nick's cousin and the family solicitor, who manages the End
House estate.

--- THE INHERITANCE QUESTION ---

Poirot investigates the possible motives for killing Nick. End House is mortgaged
and not particularly valuable in itself. But there is another financial angle:
Nick, it emerges, is the sole beneficiary of Michael Seton's estate — Seton,
the aviator, was extremely wealthy and he and Nick were secretly engaged. If
Seton is confirmed dead, Nick inherits a very large fortune. And if Nick dies
before the will is confirmed, the inheritance would pass elsewhere.

This provides a motive: someone who stands to benefit from Nick's death,
particularly if that death occurs before Nick inherits from Seton, would have
specific financial incentive.

Poirot establishes that Nick recently made a will leaving everything to her
closest friend, Freddie Rice. If Nick dies and subsequently inherits Seton's
fortune, Freddie would receive the Seton inheritance through Nick's will.

--- THE APPARENT MURDER ATTEMPT FOILED --- AND THEN NOT ---

Poirot, convinced that Nick is in danger, persuades her to invite her cousin
Maggie to stay at End House and to switch rooms with her — to create the
appearance that Nick is elsewhere — as a protective measure. Maggie Buckley,
Nick's cousin from Yorkshire, arrives.

The very night of Maggie's arrival, Maggie is shot dead in the garden. She
was wearing Nick's distinctive shawl in the darkness, and the shooter —
who is trying to kill Nick — shot Maggie in the mistaken belief that she was
Nick.

The death of the wrong woman is devastating and confirms everything Poirot
feared. The killer is real, is active, and has now committed actual murder.

Nick is distraught. The household is shattered. The police investigation begins.

--- POIROT'S INVESTIGATION ---

Poirot investigates the murder of Maggie Buckley with the full engagement of
his grey cells. He examines the garden, interviews all the members of the
household and Nick's social circle, and constructs the timeline of the evening.

Several things trouble him:

The shawl — how did Maggie come to be wearing Nick's distinctive shawl in
the garden at night? Nick says she lent it to Maggie. But the specific
circumstances of this lending, and the time at which it occurred, are worth
examining.

Nick's reaction to Maggie's death, while apparently genuine grief, has
something about it that Poirot cannot quite place.

The cocaine — a small quantity of cocaine is found in Nick's room, which Nick
denies all knowledge of. But its presence raises questions about the specific
connections in her social circle.

--- THE TWIST: NICK IS THE KILLER ---

The solution to "Peril at End House" is one of Christie's most celebrated
reversals: Nick Buckley, the apparent target of all the murder attempts, is
in fact the murderer.

Nick engineered all of the "attempts" on her own life. The falling picture,
the brake tampering, the bee in the hat — all were arranged by Nick herself,
carefully calculated to look like assassination attempts while causing her no
actual harm. Their purpose was to establish, before witnesses including Hercule
Poirot, that someone was trying to kill her — creating a context in which the
subsequent death of Maggie could be read as another attempt on Nick that
accidentally killed the wrong person.

Nick's actual plan was to murder Maggie — her cousin, who was the beneficiary
of Nick's own will before Nick changed it. By killing Maggie and making it look
like an accident connected to the series of attempts on Nick, she intended to
inherit Maggie's small estate while also benefiting from the sympathy and legal
cover provided by the "someone is trying to kill me" narrative.

But Nick's deeper scheme involves the Seton fortune. Michael Seton was secretly
engaged to Nick. When Seton's plane went down and he was presumed dead, Nick
stood to inherit his enormous fortune — but only if she was alive to inherit
it. The false murder attempts established her as a vulnerable potential victim,
providing cover for the real murder she committed while also ensuring she would
be seen as a survivor rather than a perpetrator.

The specific mechanism of Maggie's shooting involved Nick giving Maggie her
distinctive shawl — knowing that Maggie would go into the garden in the dark
wearing it, where Nick shot her.

--- POIROT'S REVELATION ---

Poirot's exposure of Nick is one of his most theatrical moments. He assembles
the full case — the specific impossibilities in Nick's account of the various
"attempts," the specific evidence from the night of Maggie's death, the financial
motive — and confronts Nick directly.

Nick's response — the specific quality of her reaction when cornered by the
complete case — confirms what Poirot has assembled. She is arrested.

--- THE COCAINE AND FREDDIE RICE ---

The cocaine found in Nick's room proves to have been planted by Nick herself —
another misdirection, intended to cast suspicion on Freddie Rice (who has a
known drug problem) if the investigation turned in an inconvenient direction.
Freddie is cleared.

--- RESOLUTION ---

Nick Buckley is arrested for the murder of Maggie Buckley. The false murder
attempts she engineered — the picture, the brakes, the bee — are exposed as
her own work. The entire edifice of the victimized young woman under threat
collapses.

Michael Seton, it is eventually confirmed, did die in the aviation accident.
His estate goes through the legal process — but Nick, now facing murder charges,
will not inherit it.

--- NOTES ON THE EPISODE ---

"Peril at End House" is one of the finest Suchet-era Poirot productions. The
twist — that the apparent victim is the killer — is handled with Christie's
characteristic fairness: all the clues are there, and Poirot's discomfort
with certain elements of Nick's account is visible throughout. The Cornish
setting is beautifully used, and David Suchet's performance of the final
revelation scene — the specific combination of satisfaction at the solved puzzle
and genuine sorrow at what a charming young woman turned out to be — is
characteristic of the Suchet Poirot at its best.
