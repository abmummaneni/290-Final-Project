TVE_079 | Wallander (Swedish): The Man Who Smiled | Director: Leif Magnusson | TV Film
================================================================================
FULL SYNOPSIS — SPOILERS INCLUDED
================================================================================

NOTE: This synopsis covers "The Man Who Smiled" (Swedish: Mannen som log),
one of the three feature-length films in Season 2 of the Swedish Wallander
series, broadcast by SVT in 2010. It is based on Henning Mankell's 1994 novel
of the same name — written before "Sidetracked" in publication order, though
adapted later for television. The film stars Krister Henriksson as Kurt Wallander.

"The Man Who Smiled" is considered by many Mankell readers and Wallander
viewers to be among the finest entries in the entire canon — the novel on which
it is based was Mankell's own favourite of the Wallander books. Its central
figure, Alfred Harderberg, is one of the great villains of Scandinavian crime
fiction: a man of extraordinary surface perfection and absolute inner emptiness
whose wealth, charm, and political connections make him almost entirely invulnerable
to the normal mechanisms of justice.

--- WALLANDER'S STATE AT THE OPENING ---

The film opens with Wallander in a period of significant personal crisis. He
has been on extended sick leave following events that left him traumatized —
in the novel's chronology, a previous case in which he shot and killed a man,
an act that was professionally justified but that he has been unable to process
psychologically. He is living alone, drinking more than he should, eating badly,
and genuinely uncertain whether he wants to return to police work at all.

His father Povel is present in his life in this period — the old painter whose
dementia is progressing, who still goes to his studio and still paints his
single landscape, and whose presence is both a comfort and a source of the
specific grief of watching a parent's mind diminish. The relationship between
Wallander and his father — the specific quality of love complicated by years
of distance and mutual incomprehension, now being renegotiated as the father
becomes increasingly dependent — is one of the series' most sustained and
most affecting threads.

Wallander is essentially asked to come back to work by the specific circumstances
of the case: someone he knew has died, and the circumstances of the death
require his attention in a way that he cannot put aside.

--- THE DEATH OF Gustaf TORSTENSSON ---

Gustaf Torstensson was a lawyer — a solicitor of the old-fashioned, entirely
trustworthy kind — who had been a friend of Wallander's father. He is found
dead in his car, which has gone off the road in circumstances suggesting an
accident. He had been driving alone at night on a rural road.

Wallander, contacted because of the family connection, looks at the crash
scene and feels something that he cannot immediately articulate: the scene
is slightly wrong. Not dramatically wrong — nothing about it announces itself
as staged — but wrong in the specific way that Wallander's trained observation
sometimes picks up before his conscious mind has organized the evidence.
One of the chairs in the car is positioned incorrectly. Someone else was in
the car.

He is not officially investigating — he is on leave, he has no standing in
this case. But he cannot leave it alone. He begins asking questions informally,
and what he learns pushes him back toward active investigation regardless of
his official status.

Gustaf Torstensson's son Alfred is also a lawyer, and he contacts Wallander
with something additional: his father had been frightened in the period before
his death. He had said, obliquely, that he was afraid of something or someone
connected to his work. He had not been specific — he was a careful, private
man — but the fear was real and noticeable to his son.

--- THE SECOND DEATH: STEN TORSTENSSON ---

Before Wallander can fully establish what Gustaf feared, a second death occurs.
Sten Torstensson — Gustaf's son, who had just told Wallander about his father's
fear — is himself killed. He is shot. The killing is direct and violent, a
contrast with the staged accident of his father's death that confirms Wallander's
instinct: these are not accidents. Someone is killing the Torstenssons, and
the person responsible is willing to use whatever method is most convenient.

The two deaths — the staged accident designed to look natural and the direct
shooting — suggest an operation that is willing to be methodical when it can
and decisive when it must be. This is not disorganized criminal violence but
the calculated removal of people who pose a specific threat.

Wallander is now fully engaged. He returns to work formally, whatever his
psychological state. The case has found him.

--- ALFRED HARDERBERG ---

The investigation of the Torstenssons' legal practice — what clients they
had, what matters they were handling, and what in their professional work
might have generated the threat that killed them — leads quickly and
unmistakably to Alfred Harderberg.

Harderberg is one of the most successful businessmen in Sweden — perhaps in
Europe. He has built a financial empire of extraordinary scope and complexity:
holding companies, investment vehicles, manufacturing interests, and a range
of businesses that together constitute one of the largest private commercial
operations in Scandinavia. He is fabulously wealthy and uses his wealth with
total comfort: he lives in a castle, he has private aircraft, he has the
specific social ease of someone for whom money has been a lifelong given rather
than an achievement.

He is also extraordinarily charming. He smiles easily and often — the "man
who smiled" of the title — and his smile is precisely and consistently deployed
to manage the impression he makes on people. Politicians, business figures,
media personalities: everyone who encounters Harderberg finds him compelling,
and his reputation is one of almost unblemished commercial success and social
generosity. He supports charitable causes. He is accessible. He appears to be,
in every visible respect, a genuinely admirable person.

The Torstenssons were Harderberg's lawyers. They handled specific legal work
for his empire. And they discovered something — something about how the empire
operates, what it is built on, what specific activities generate its profits —
that made them dangerous to him.

--- THE CASTLE: FARNHOLM ---

Harderberg lives in Farnholm Castle, a genuinely imposing property outside
Ystad, and the investigation's relationship to this setting is one of the
film's most sustained atmospheric achievements. The castle is both beautiful
and absolutely controlled: its grounds, its access, its staff, and its internal
operations are all managed with a precision that reflects Harderberg's
personality. Nothing at Farnholm is accidental. Everything has been arranged
to present a specific picture.

Getting inside Farnholm — getting access to Harderberg and to the information
that his operations contain — is one of the investigation's central practical
challenges. Harderberg's lawyers, his private security, and his political
connections all create barriers between the Ystad police and whatever is
actually happening within the castle's walls.

Wallander is not intimidated by wealth or social position — this is one of
his most important qualities as an investigator. He is aware of the power
Harderberg wields, but he is not susceptible to the specific social pressure
that the display of wealth and sophistication can exercise on people who did
not grow up with it. He approaches Harderberg with the same dogged, practical
attention he brings to any investigation, and the contrast between Wallander's
ordinariness and Harderberg's extraordinary surface polish is one of the film's
most effective dramatic tensions.

--- WHAT HARDERBERG'S EMPIRE CONCEALS ---

The investigation gradually establishes what the Torstenssons had discovered
about Harderberg's operations. The empire's legitimate surface conceals a
core of organized criminal activity: the specific financial structures through
which Harderberg's companies operate are vehicles for large-scale fraud, money
laundering, and the exploitation of institutional investments. His business
empire is, in significant part, built on criminal foundations whose scale makes
them almost impossible to investigate through conventional means.

More specifically: Harderberg's operations involve the exploitation of pension
funds, investment vehicles used by ordinary people whose savings have been
systematically misappropriated through a network of companies and transactions
so complex that the fraud is effectively invisible to standard regulatory
scrutiny.

The Torstenssons — conservative, ethical lawyers of the old school — discovered
evidence of this while conducting routine legal work for Harderberg's holding
company. Gustaf, in particular, recognized what he was seeing with the specific
clarity of a man who had spent forty years in legal practice and understood
what legitimate business looks like and what this was instead.

His fear, and his death, were the direct consequence of Harderberg's realization
that his lawyers had seen what they should not have seen.

--- HARDERBERG'S BODYGUARDS: OSLOVSKI AND MODIN ---

Harderberg is protected by two bodyguards — Oslovski and Modin — who serve
as the operational arm of his lethal response to threats. They are the people
who killed Gustaf Torstensson and Sten Torstensson, and they are the immediate
physical danger that the investigation must navigate.

They are also, unlike Harderberg himself, directly traceable. The investigation
of who killed the Torstenssons — the physical evidence, the forensics, the
specific movements around the time of the killings — leads to Oslovski and
Modin with relative directness. The challenge is connecting them to Harderberg
in a way that holds up legally: establishing that they acted on his orders,
not independently.

--- ANN-BRITT HÖGLUND AND THE TEAM ---

Wallander's investigation is conducted with his Ystad team, including Ann-Britt
Höglund, whose calm intelligence and specific investigative competence make
her one of the series' most valuable supporting characters. Her work on the
financial dimensions of the case — tracing the specific structures of
Harderberg's empire, following the paper trail that connects the legitimate
corporate surface to the fraudulent core — is essential to building the
complete case.

The team's working relationship — the specific mixture of professional trust
and individual personality that makes a good investigation work — is one of
the Swedish Wallander series' consistent pleasures: these are not glamorous
detectives but working police officers, and their collaboration has the specific
texture of real professional teamwork.

--- WALLANDER INSIDE THE CASTLE ---

A key sequence in the investigation involves Wallander gaining access to
Farnholm Castle and its internal workings. The specific intelligence he gathers
from inside — about Harderberg's operations, about the specific evidence of
the fraud, about the specific threat he represents — advances the case
significantly while also placing Wallander in direct danger from Oslovski
and Modin.

The confrontation between Wallander's ordinary, persistent decency and the
castle's carefully maintained façade of civilized wealth is the film's central
dramatic opposition. Harderberg's world is designed to be impenetrable — every
surface has been arranged to project legitimacy and charm — and Wallander's
refusal to be managed by those surfaces is what makes him dangerous to it.

--- THE SMILE AND WHAT IT MEANS ---

The novel's title — and its central image — is Harderberg's smile. It is present
in every encounter: the relaxed, warm, genuinely appealing smile of a man who
has learned to use his charm as a primary operational tool. The smile creates
trust. It disarms suspicion. It makes people want to believe in the legitimate
version of Alfred Harderberg.

Wallander watches the smile and understands it with specific clarity: it is
not the expression of a feeling but the management of a situation. Every time
Harderberg smiles, he is working. The performance of warmth and openness is
so perfect, so unbroken, so convincingly pleasant, that it would fool almost
anyone. It does not fool Wallander, but Wallander's certainty of what lies
behind the smile is not the same as evidence, and the gap between what he
knows and what he can prove is the film's central dramatic tension.

--- THE RESOLUTION ---

The investigation builds sufficient evidence to act against Harderberg's
operation. The specific mechanism of the resolution — how Harderberg is finally
confronted with an investigation that his charm and connections cannot deflect —
involves both the financial case assembled by the team and the specific evidence
connecting him to the Torstensson murders.

Oslovski and Modin are arrested on the murder charges. Harderberg faces criminal
investigation for the fraud — an investigation whose scope goes beyond what
the Ystad police alone can manage, involving Swedish and European financial
authorities.

The specific quality of the resolution is not triumphant. Harderberg is a man
with extraordinary resources and extraordinary legal support, and the process
of bringing him to final account will be long and uncertain. What the
investigation has achieved is the exposure — the beginning of the process of
accountability — and the specific prevention of further immediate harm.

Wallander closes the case carrying the specific exhaustion of a sustained effort
against something larger and more powerful than the ordinary world of Ystad
criminality. The man who smiled is still smiling, probably — but the smile
is now pointed at lawyers and investigators rather than at potential victims,
and that is a different thing.

--- WALLANDER'S RETURN ---

The personal arc of the film — Wallander's return from sick leave, his
re-engagement with the work, and what the Harderberg case does to his
psychological state — reaches something like a provisional resolution. He has
not resolved the underlying trauma that put him on leave. He is not better
in any simple sense. But he has done the work, and the work is done, and
for someone with Wallander's specific relationship to his profession, that
has its own value.

His father is still in his studio, still painting. The landscape is still
there, with or without the grouse. Some things are continuous regardless
of what happens in the larger world.

--- NOTES ON THE FILM ---

"The Man Who Smiled" is one of the finest entries in the Swedish Wallander
series and one of the most sustained portraits of institutional and corporate
evil in European crime drama. Alfred Harderberg is one of Mankell's greatest
villains precisely because he is so perfectly ordinary in his surface presentation:
not a monster in any visible sense, but a specific kind of person whose charm
is indistinguishable from sociopathy and whose success is built on the systematic
exploitation of trust. Krister Henriksson's Wallander is at his most compelling
when facing this kind of opponent — the contrast between the detective's ordinary
human vulnerability and the antagonist's perfectly controlled social performance
is as dramatically rich as anything in the series.
