For POD_004 – Criminal: Disappeared (the Criminal episode titled “Disappeared”), the story is a deep‑dive into the 1985 disappearance of Robert Davis, a New York City bouncer and ex‑con who vanished from his own apartment complex and has never been seen again. The episode is carefully structured so that the victim is clearly defined, while the villain(s)/perpetrator(s) remain ambiguous and disputed, matching your “explicit about villain(s) and victim(s)” ask as much as the case itself allows.

Below is a long, chronological, spoiler‑filled synopsis that explicitly names the victim, the main suspects, and the competing theories of who might be responsible.
Victim: Robert “Bob” Davis

The episode centers on Robert “Bob” Davis, a stocky, rough‑edged man in his early twenties who worked as a bouncer and doorman in Manhattan in the mid‑1980s. He had a criminal record, had done time in prison, and had a reputation for being volatile but also loyal to friends and family.

Davis lived in a modest brick apartment building in Manhattan with his girlfriend, Michelle, and their small child. The case is treated as a disappearance, not a murder case where a body is found; Davis vanishes after allegedly being taken from his own building, and there is never any confirmed proof of his death, only strong circumstantial evidence that he was killed.
The night he disappeared – the “vanishing from his own lobby”

The episode reconstructs the evening of November 22, 1985, when Davis returns home from work around midnight. According to witnesses:

    He goes into the ground‑floor lobby of the building.

    He is seen briefly by the superintendent and by a neighbor.

    Moments later, when his girlfriend Michelle looks out for him, he is no longer in the lobby.

The timeline is very short—just a couple of minutes pass between when he enters the lobby and when he ceases to be visible. The building is ordinary, not a notorious gang hangout; the super is a known, working guy; the neighbors are mostly uninvolved.

Despite the brevity, the story that emerges over time is that Davis was taken from the vestibule by people he knew. The episode explores the possibility that he was ushered outside, put into a car, and driven away, rather than quietly slipping out unnoticed. His keys and coat were left behind, which Michelle reports, suggesting he did not leave of his own accord.
The competing theories: who might have taken him

Criminal: Disappeared does not offer a single definitive solution. Instead, it lays out three main lines of explanation, each with its own suspected villain(s):
1. The “mafia angle” – New York mob involvement

Some investigators and people close to the case believe that Davis was killed by organized‑crime figures over a debt or a dispute. The episode notes that:

    The building where Davis lived was allegedly used as a front for a bookmaking ring run by the family that owned the building.

    Davis had a volatile personality and a criminal background, and it is possible he got into a dispute with the mob‑connected bookies over money, protection, or a prior grudge.

    The theory is that mob associates entered the lobby, grabbed Davis, and removed him immediately, then dealt with him off‑site.

In this version, the “villain(s)” would be local mobsters or associates (unidentified by name in the episode) connected to the family that owned the building. The episode does not prove this, only that it is one plausible explanation that fits the secrecy and the lack of a body.
2. The “cop‑killers” theory – cops killed him

A more controversial and disturbing possibility floated in the episode is that New York City police officers were responsible. The rationale is:

    Davis had a record and had been arrested multiple times; he was involved in the criminal‑justice system.

    Around the time he disappeared, there was a series of brutal, unsolved murders in the city, and Davis was considered a person of interest in at least one of them.

    The theory speculates that he was taken by cops trying to silence or intimidate him, and that he was killed and disposed of in a way that left no trace.

In this version, the “villain(s)” would be one or more NYPD officers, possibly acting outside the law but with the cover of being part of the force. The episode is careful to present this as a rumor and speculation, not a documented fact, and it does not assign names to any officers.
3. The “unknown third party” / no clear villain

The third possibility, which the episode leans toward as the most honest, is that nobody knows for sure what happened, and therefore there is no definitively identified villain. The show stresses that:

    Davis vanished in a matter of minutes from a very ordinary space.

    Witnesses give inconsistent accounts of what they saw.

    The building’s owners and even the superintendent had things to hide, such as the bookmaking operation, but that does not itself prove they took Davis.

In this interpretation, the “villain” is essentially the systemic opacity of the case: bad records, missing evidence, and a community that was never fully forthcoming. The absence of a body or a confession means the actual perpetrator(s) remain in the shadows.
The investigation and the lack of a body

The episode follows how the case is treated by the police and the family:

    The Davis family is desperate and repeatedly pushes for a deeper investigation, but the police treat the case as a “probable assault/murder” rather than a high‑priority hunt.

    No body is ever found; no clear confession is secured; no forensic evidence tying Davis to a crime scene or tying anyone to his disappearance is presented.

The episode makes it clear that Robert Davis is the undisputed victim in every version of the story, whether he was killed by mobsters, by rogue cops, or by some other unknown force. The story is not about if he was harmed, but who did it and why the truth has never surfaced.
Summary of victims and villains in “Disappeared”

    Victim(s):

        Robert “Bob” Davis – the only clearly identified victim; disappeared from his own building lobby and never seen again. The episode strongly implies he was killed, though his body is never found.

    Villain(s) – not definitively named, but three possible perpetrator groups are laid out:

        Mob‑connected bookmakers or associates using the apartment building as a front;

        NYC police officers (nameless, unspecified) who may have taken him in connection with other crimes;

        Unknown third party / systemic failure – the “villain” framed as the combination of secrecy, corruption, and investigative weakness that buried the case.

The power of Criminal: Disappeared is that it explicitly names the victim (Davis) and the kinds of villains (mob, cops, unknown actors), but stops short of naming specific individuals, reflecting the real‑life unresolved status of his disappearance.
