TVE_015 | Miss Marple: 4:50 from Paddington | Director: Martyn Friend | TV Episode
================================================================================
FULL EPISODE SYNOPSIS — SPOILERS INCLUDED
================================================================================

--- BACKGROUND AND CONTEXT ---

"4:50 from Paddington" (known in the US as "What Mrs. McGillicuddy Saw!") is
a BBC Miss Marple adaptation first broadcast in December 1987, directed by
Martyn Friend. It stars Joan Hickson as Miss Marple and is based on Christie's
1957 novel. The adaptation makes one significant change from the source novel:
it introduces the character of Detective Inspector Dermot Craddock (played by
David Horovitch), a recurring figure in the BBC series, in a more prominent
role than the novel gives him.

The episode is notable for its plot, which is one of Christie's most inventive:
a murder witnessed through a passing train window, which means there is initially
no body, no crime scene, and no victim whose identity is known.

--- THE WITNESS ON THE TRAIN ---

Mrs. Elspeth McGillicuddy is traveling by train toward St. Mary Mead to visit
her old friend Miss Marple. As her train briefly runs parallel with another
train going in the same direction, she looks into the window of the passing
carriage and sees, with absolute clarity, a man strangling a woman. The woman
slumps and the train moves on. Mrs. McGillicuddy is horrified and reports what
she saw to the conductor, then to the police, and then to Miss Marple.

The police investigation of the route establishes that no body has been found
and no woman has been reported missing who fits the description. The official
tendency is to doubt Mrs. McGillicuddy's account — an elderly woman, a moving
train, a brief glimpse — but Miss Marple believes her completely. Mrs. McGillicuddy
is exactly the sort of reliable, observant woman who would not invent or
misinterpret what she describes.

Miss Marple reasons out where the body must be. The train line runs past a
large private estate — Rutherford Hall, the property of the wealthy and
eccentric Crackenthorpe family. If the body was thrown from the train, it would
most likely have landed somewhere on or near that property. She determines to
have the grounds searched.

--- LUCY EYELESBARROW ---

Miss Marple cannot conduct a physical search of Rutherford Hall herself. She
enlists the help of Lucy Eyelesbarrow, a brilliant young woman who has made
an unusual career out of high-class domestic service: she is available for
short engagements, is extraordinarily capable at household management, charges
substantial fees, and is in great demand. Miss Marple arranges for Lucy to take
a domestic position at Rutherford Hall and to search the grounds for the body
while performing her household duties.

Lucy is one of Christie's most enjoyable supporting characters — competent,
cool, amused by her situation, and entirely unflappable. Her investigation of
Rutherford Hall from the inside gives the episode its domestic texture.

--- THE CRACKENTHORPE FAMILY ---

Rutherford Hall and the Crackenthorpe family are the setting for the episode's
second half. Luther Crackenthorpe is the elderly, disagreeable, miserly patriarch
who controls the family's substantial assets with great jealousy. His children —
who include sons Cedric, Harold, and Alfred, and daughter Emma — are all
financially dependent on their father and waiting, with varying degrees of
impatience, for his death and the release of the family estate.

The family is shown in the particular state of suspended resentment that
long dependency produces: they have all made compromises, developed bitternesses,
and hidden things from each other. The arrival of Lucy as a domestic employee,
and the subsequent discovery of the body, disrupts the household profoundly.

--- THE BODY FOUND ---

Lucy's search of the grounds eventually locates the body — in the old sarcophagus
in the Rutherford Hall barn or outbuilding, a family relic that has been there
for years. The body is that of a young woman, elegantly dressed, with a broken
neck consistent with strangulation. She has been there for some time.

The body is unidentified initially. Inspector Craddock and the official
investigation work to establish who she was, while Miss Marple, from her position
of knowledge about the family and about human nature, pursues the parallel
question of who among the Crackenthorpe circle killed her.

--- IDENTIFYING THE VICTIM ---

The dead woman's identity eventually points toward a European connection — she
may be French or have French connections — and her presence at Rutherford Hall
is connected to one of the Crackenthorpe sons. The investigation establishes
that she was involved with one of the family members, possibly as a wife or
lover, and that her arrival in England and her appearance on the train were
connected to a claim she intended to make — a claim on the Crackenthorpe
estate that would have been financially significant.

The Crackenthorpe heir situation — who will inherit, on what terms, and whether
legitimate or illegitimate family connections exist — is the financial context
of the murder.

--- ADDITIONAL POISONINGS ---

The plot thickens when members of the Crackenthorpe family are poisoned. Two
of the sons fall seriously ill after a family meal and one dies. This suggests
that the original murder is connected to the family's inheritance situation in
a way that goes beyond the killing of the unidentified woman: someone is
eliminating family members to improve their own position in the inheritance.

The poisonings at a family gathering — where multiple people are at risk —
are the episode's most frightening development and increase the urgency of
identifying the killer.

--- MISS MARPLE'S CONCLUSION ---

Miss Marple's analysis proceeds through her characteristic method: she watches
the family, listens to conversations, notes behavioral inconsistencies, and
applies the appropriate village parallel. The person who killed the woman on
the train, and who poisoned the Crackenthorpe family meal, is someone with
a specific relationship to the inheritance question and a specific connection
to the dead woman's identity and her claim.

The killer is identified from among the Crackenthorpe family or close associates —
someone whose position in the inheritance structure was directly threatened by
what the dead woman represented, and who was capable of both the original
impulsive killing on the train and the more calculated poisonings.

--- THE KILLER IDENTIFIED: EDMUND CRACKENTHORPE ---

The killer is Edmund Crackenthorpe — one of Luther's sons, and the one with
the most direct and most incriminating connection to the dead woman. She was
his wife — or more precisely, she was a woman he had secretly married abroad
and then abandoned, who had come to England to press her claim on the Crackenthorpe
estate as his legal spouse. Edmund killed her on the train when the threat she
posed to his inheritance and his concealed marital history became immediate,
disposed of the body at Rutherford Hall (which he knew intimately), and then
attempted to poison the family meal when the investigation began to close in
on the truth of the woman's identity and her relationship to him.

Miss Marple and Craddock together establish the evidence against him and he
is arrested.

--- RESOLUTION ---

The killer is brought to justice. Mrs. McGillicuddy's account — doubted by the
police and by some of the characters throughout — is vindicated: she saw exactly
what she said she saw, and her willingness to insist on it despite official
skepticism is what set the entire investigation in motion.

The Crackenthorpe family, reduced and damaged by the poisonings and the murder,
faces the future without the secret that has been driving the violence.

--- NOTES ON THE ADAPTATION ---

"4:50 from Paddington" is a particularly satisfying Marple adaptation because
its central conceit — the witness on the train — is both genuinely clever and
genuinely fair: the body must be somewhere, and Miss Marple's deduction of
where is the kind of clear, logical reasoning that the best detective stories
provide. Lucy Eyelesbarrow is a beloved figure whose absence from later
adaptations is sometimes lamented, and her relationship with Miss Marple —
two entirely capable women approaching the same problem from different angles —
is one of the episode's great pleasures.
