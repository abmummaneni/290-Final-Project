TVE_021 – Columbo: Agenda for Murder

The story begins with the murder of Oscar Finch, a powerful, iron‑fisted political lobbyist based in Washington, D.C. He is found shot to death in his office, and the initial investigation suggests a professional assassination by a political rival or a disgruntled lobbyist. The case is messy, high‑profile, and politically charged.

The real killer, however, is Tom Staplin, a brilliant Washington attorney and longtime friend of rising political star Congressman Austin Montgomery. Staplin has built a career out of getting powerful people out of trouble, and he helped Montgomery years ago when the young politician was caught in a serious criminal entanglement. The case was quietly buried, and Montgomery owes his survival and later success to Staplin’s legal skill.

Now, as Montgomery’s star is rising, an old scandal begins to resurface. A journalist is digging into the buried case, and documents may prove that Montgomery’s earlier “clean” record was in fact secured by Staplin’s ethical violations. If the story breaks, Montgomery’s career collapses, and Staplin’s own license and reputation are ruined.

Staplin, confronted with the possibility of total ruin, does not abandon Montgomery. Instead, he decides to kill the problem in the most controlled way possible. He goes to Montgomery’s office under the guise of discussing legal strategy, then murders him in his own office, staging it to look like a political assassination. The killer is meant to appear as a radical protester or extremist who opposes Montgomery’s policies.

To make the cover‑story credible, Staplin:

    Plants evidence linking the “killer” to a left‑wing protest group,

    Leaves a fake note condemning Montgomery’s politics,

    And even commits a smaller, secondary “attack” on another politician to make the extremist narrative more believable.

The secondary crime is a bomb or sabotage attempt on another office, designed to look like the work of the same extremist cell. Staplin, as a lawyer, knows how investigations work and uses that knowledge to shape the police’s assumptions.

Columbo, reassigned to the case, immediately finds the scene too neat. The forensic details—angles of fire, trace evidence, and the way the “assassin” left the building—don’t match a hot‑headed, emotionally charged extremist. Instead, Staplin has left just enough of a “professional” footprint that Columbo slowly begins to suspect the killer is someone who understands the legal and investigative process.

The key clue is the secondary crime on the other politician’s office. The way it is staged has the marks of a lawyer’s mind, not a terrorist’s. The choice of targets, the specific wording of the threat, and the placement of the evidence all mirror tactics Staplin would design in a courtroom. Columbo starts to focus on the people closest to Montgomery’s legal defense.

He learns that Staplin had access to Montgomery’s office, knew his schedule, and also had a motive: his own career and reputation depend on the old case never resurfacing. The more Columbo investigates, the clearer it becomes that Staplin master‑minded the whole chess‑game: he is not only protecting Montgomery, he is manipulating the investigation to keep the true story buried.

The turning point comes when Columbo quietly reconstructs the secondary attack. He realizes that the conditions under which the smaller crime could have been carried out match the kind of inside knowledge a lawyer working for the campaign would have, not a random activist. The location of the planted bomb, the timing, and the way potential witnesses were kept away all point to someone with access to internal security and campaign logistics.

In the final confrontation, Columbo lays out the sequence:

    Staplin used the threat of scandal to justify creating a “bodyguard” presence,

    That presence gave him clean access to Montgomery’s office,

    He then killed Montgomery and staged the assassination,

    And he engineered the secondary attack as a red‑herring “signature” crime.

Columbo confronts Staplin with the fact that the second crime could not have been staged as it was unless the killer had inside information and legal‑level planning skills. Staplin, realizing he’s been outmaneuvered, breaks under the weight of Columbo’s quiet, almost respectful questions and admits to the murders.

The episode ends with Staplin acknowledging that he tried to be clever enough to out‑think the detective, but his own sense of control and moral calculus—killing one man to protect a political career—has collapsed. Columbo arrests him, and the story closes on the irony that the man who built his life on the law became its greatest violator.
