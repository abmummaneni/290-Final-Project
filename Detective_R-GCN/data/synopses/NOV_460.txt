1. Main case timeline: Bethany Waites
Ten years before the book

    Bethany Waites, a young TV journalist on the show South East Tonight, is investigating a VAT‑fraud scheme involving millions of pounds siphoned via fake companies.

    She tells colleagues she’s close to a big break and is receiving anonymous threatening notes at work.

    One night, her car is found at the bottom of Shakespeare Cliff; she’s presumed dead, but her body is never recovered.

    Officially: “accidental” or unsolved. Unofficially: everyone thinks someone in the VAT fraud got rid of her.

Present day – the Thursday Murder Club takes the case

    At Coopers Chase, Joyce suggests the Thursday Murder Club (Joyce, Elizabeth, Ron, Ibrahim) take on Bethany’s cold case.

    They learn from Bethany’s former co‑anchor Mike and others at the TV station about the VAT story, the threatening notes, and the missing millions.

    Only one person was ever jailed for the fraud: bookkeeper Heather Garbutt. Local gangster Jack Mason was suspected but never convicted.

Early investigation steps

    Ron befriends Jack Mason at a snooker club, sounding him out about Bethany and the VAT fraud. Jack insists Bethany’s death was “unnecessary” and that things went further than he wanted.

    The Club starts mapping the fraud’s shell companies and names: among them “Heather Garbutt,” “Carron Whitehead,” “Michael Gullis,” and “Robert Brown MSc” in the paperwork.

    They suspect these are fake identities used to move money, possibly pointing to someone close to the case—perhaps even someone writing their own story into the documents.

Linking Bethany to Juniper Court

    With help from Chris and Donna, the Club gets CCTV and other leads placing Bethany at an apartment building, Juniper Court, on the night of her disappearance.

    The big question: who did she go to see there? Was it a source in the VAT case, a lover, or the person who killed her?

2. Parallel plot timeline: Elizabeth, the Viking, and Viktor
Kidnapping and the “kill or be killed” mission

    Elizabeth and her husband Stephen are kidnapped after a string of anonymous threats.

    They’re taken to a mansion in Staffordshire and meet a tall, expensively dressed Swede whom Stephen christens “the Viking.”

    The Viking orders Elizabeth to assassinate Viktor Illyich, a former KGB colonel known as “the Bullet,” now a freelance operator and staunch opponent of certain cryptocurrency interests.

    If Elizabeth refuses, he’ll kill Joyce. If she succeeds, he’ll let them all live.

Elizabeth’s counterplan with Viktor

    What the Viking doesn’t know: Elizabeth and Viktor are long‑time friends. She has zero intention of killing him.

    She visits Viktor’s London flat with Joyce and a gun. Out of Joyce’s sight, she fakes the assassination: fires a shot into the ceiling, stages the scene, and has Joyce destroy the phone SIM so the Viking can’t track them.

    They smuggle Viktor out in a body bag and bring him to Coopers Chase, where he hides out cheerfully in Joyce’s flat, bonding with her dog.

    When the Viking checks the scene, he sees the bullet hole and assumes the job was done—until he learns Viktor is still alive.

The Viking comes to Coopers Chase

    Realizing he’s been tricked, the Viking heads to Coopers Chase intending to kill Joyce.

    He’s undone by his own nerves and by trusting English hospitality: he accepts tea laced with Viktor’s powerful sleeping tablets and collapses.

    He wakes tied to a chair, surrounded by pensioners, including Stephen, who has been quietly studying the man’s extensive book collection from their earlier abduction.

    By matching titles and scribbled notes with help from Kuldesh Sharma (an antiquarian friend), Stephen identifies the Viking as Henrik, a cryptocurrency entrepreneur whose business interests clash with Viktor’s anti‑crypto stance.

3. VAT fraud cast and motives
Bethany Waites (victim)

    Ambitious, principled TV journalist.

    Motive for others to kill her: she got too close to exposing a VAT‑fraud network; she knew names and methods and was about to go public.

Heather Garbutt (jailed bookkeeper)

    The only person imprisoned for the VAT scheme.

    Official story: she was a key part of the fraud.

    The Club suspects she may be a scapegoat or only partly guilty and that she knows more about who actually took the money and who arranged Bethany’s death.

Jack Mason (local criminal)

    Small‑time gangster with his fingers in the fraud.

    Ron’s snooker buddy.

    He admits Bethany’s death was “unnecessary,” suggesting someone higher‑up made that call.

    Later in the book he’s killed and his death staged as suicide, implying someone is still cleaning up loose ends.

Connie Johnson (recurring villain in prison)

    A drug boss from earlier books, now in prison but with VIP status.

    Held in the same facility as Heather Garbutt.

    Ibrahim visits her as a “therapist” and persuades her to talk to Heather about the fraud, appealing to Connie’s curiosity and hunger for leverage.

    After Connie takes an interest, Heather turns up dead, apparently by suicide—but Connie secretly swaps her note, making it look more like murder and using it to unsettle the outside world and settle some of her own scores.

Andrew (police chief / crime writer)

    A senior cop who also writes crime novels.

    Shows up at Coopers Chase to give a reading and to talk to Ibrahim, partly because Connie’s manipulations and Heather’s staged note have rattled the official investigation.

    The club realizes his fiction contains suspicious echoes of the VAT case: names in the fraud’s paperwork match characters from his books.

4. How the Club cracks the VAT/Bethany mystery
Clue 1: Heather’s “suicide” and Connie’s interference

    Ibrahim spots that Heather’s supposed suicide note isn’t in her handwriting.

    Because Connie swapped the original, this suggests another hand in her death and hints that Heather might have been killed for what she knew.

    This keeps the VAT angle hot and points toward people outside prison who still have something to hide.

Clue 2: The fictional names in the fraud documents

The VAT paperwork includes names such as:

    “Carron Whitehead”

    “Michael Gullis”

    “Robert Brown MSc”

The Club pieces together that:

    “Carron Whitehead” is an anagram / variant linked to Catherine Howard, the protagonist in Andrew’s crime books.

    “Michael Gullis” is the name of a villain in one of Andrew’s novels.

    “Robert Brown MSc” decodes to “Bobbi Brown mascara” (a Joyce‑style lateral leap), emphasizing that these “names” are deliberate constructs, not real people.

From this, they infer that whoever built the VAT fraud structure:

    Knows Andrew’s books well (possibly Andrew himself).

    Or is mocking / hiding behind them.

This ties the VAT scheme, the missing millions, and Bethany’s investigation back toward someone in or near law enforcement/media with knowledge of Andrew’s work.
Clue 3: Juniper Court and Bethany’s last visit

    CCTV shows Bethany visiting Juniper Court on the night she vanished.

    By cross‑referencing residents, contacts, and the fraud network, the Club narrows the possibilities: she was meeting someone directly tied to the VAT money.

    This “someone” is also likely to be the person with the most to lose if she went public—strong motive for murder.

Clue 4: Henrik’s cooperation

    Once Stephen and the Club confront Henrik (the Viking) with his true identity and business history, he realizes he’s been outplayed and his own position is precarious.

    He agrees to stop trying to kill anyone and helps the Club with Bethany’s case, using his finance/crypto knowledge to illuminate how the VAT money moved and how it later intersected with cryptocurrency.

    His input helps confirm who laundered the money and who had the resources to push Bethany’s car off the cliff and stage the scene.

5. Resolution: who did what, and how it ends
Bethany’s case

    The Club ultimately identifies the person (or people) who orchestrated Bethany Waites’s death:

        Someone deeply tied into the VAT fraud,

        With access to police/media information,

        Smart enough to use fictional identities from Andrew’s novels in the paperwork.

    Bethany’s car didn’t “accidentally” go off the cliff; it was forced over once she’d been neutralized or lured into a trap.

    Through a combination of Henrik’s financial insights, Connie’s manipulations, Heather’s altered note, and the Club’s literary clue‑spotting, the murderer and main fraud architect are unmasked and handed to the authorities.

The Viking / Henrik and Viktor

    Elizabeth never kills Viktor; instead they fake his death and keep him hidden.

    Henrik, once exposed and disarmed, abandons the kill‑order, partly because he’s now entangled with far more dangerous people (Connie, old KGB, etc.) than he bargained for.

    The “bullet that missed” is both literal (Elizabeth’s shot into the ceiling) and metaphorical: the attempt to use Elizabeth as an assassin fails, and the people who arranged the kill‑or‑be‑killed plan don’t get the outcome they wanted.

Connie and future trouble

    Connie gets what she wants most: chaos, leverage, and the satisfaction of messing with Ron and the Club from behind bars.

    By falsifying Heather’s note and stirring up questions, she keeps the police and media off‑balance and positions herself as someone whose favor or information you might need in the future.

    She remains very much at large (from a power perspective), setting up her importance in later books.

The Club

    They solve a ten‑year‑old “murder with no body,” expose a major VAT fraud, and neutralize an international hitman, all while dealing with aging, illness, and their complicated personal lives.

    Elizabeth grapples with what she is and isn’t willing to do (even for people she loves) when asked to kill.

    Joyce, Ron, and Ibrahim continue to grow individually: Joyce gains confidence in her instincts, Ron deepens his bond with Jack before Jack’s death, and Ibrahim leans fully into his role as therapist‑detective.
