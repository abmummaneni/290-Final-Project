1. Timeline of the main case
Prologue and setup (before December 27)

    The Thursday Murder Club (Elizabeth, Joyce, Ron, Ibrahim) are back at Coopers Chase, keeping busy with low‑key investigations and their own personal lives.

    Stephen (Elizabeth’s husband) is in late‑stage dementia; Elizabeth is splitting herself between caring for him and their amateur sleuthing.

    Off‑page, a heroin shipment is being smuggled into the UK by drug boss Mitch Maxwell. His second‑in‑command, Dominic “Dom” Holt, uses an antiques shop as a drop‑off to keep it low‑profile.​

December 27 – Kuldesh’s murder

    Mitch’s heroin shipment (a small box worth around £100,000) arrives in the UK. Dom uses the shop of antiques dealer Kuldesh Sharma (an elderly friend of the Club from The Bullet That Missed) as a temporary hide.​

    Kuldesh pays about £50 for a “mystery” box and is told someone will come the next day and buy it back for £500—a common antiques tactic, but in reality it contains heroin.​

    Realizing the box is suspicious and worth far more than he’s been told, Kuldesh hides it instead of keeping it on the shelf. He then makes two important phone calls: one to Nina Mishra (an archaeology/art history professor with antiques connections), and one to another unknown number that the Club later deduce must be Stephen’s.

    Late on December 27, Kuldesh drives to a remote, lonely stretch of road to meet someone about the box. Before he can get out of the car, he is shot in the head and killed. The killer takes nothing obvious, and the hidden heroin is still missing.

After the murder – police and Club get involved

    DCI Chris Hudson and PC Donna De Freitas catch the case, since Kuldesh is linked to Coopers Chase and the Thursday Murder Club.​

    Very quickly, the case is taken away from them by the National Crime Agency. Senior Investigating Officer Jill Regan swoops in, commandeers Chris’s office, and sidelines Chris and Donna, signaling that this is a serious organized‑crime matter.

    The Thursday Murder Club attend Kuldesh’s funeral. Almost no one is there except the Club, Chris and Donna, and Big Dave, a friend of Kuldesh who reveals that Kuldesh bought a shovel just before he died.​

    This detail is crucial: it suggests Kuldesh buried something—the box—rather than leaving it in his shop.​

Parallel subplots start moving

While the main heroin/murder plot advances, the book runs several interwoven threads:

    Online romance scam:

        An elderly man from Coopers Chase, Mervyn, is being catfished by a woman named Tatiana, who keeps asking for more money, supposedly passed through “her friend” Jeremmy.

        Ron and Ibrahim bring in Bob, a retired computer expert, who quickly spots that Tatiana and Jeremmy share the same IP address; it’s a classic scam operation, not real romance.​

    Stephen and the heroin box:

        At some point between Kuldesh’s death and the main investigation, Stephen receives a confusing, dementia‑fogged message from Kuldesh (the second phone call), asking him to keep something safe.

        Stephen, in his muddled but still cunning way, arranges for the heroin box to be buried in the allotment at Coopers Chase, where the gang grows vegetables. He’s the only person alive who truly knows where the heroin is.

Early investigation – Part 2

    The Club learns some key facts from various sources:

        The missing package is heroin from Mitch Maxwell’s operation.

        Dom Holt used Kuldesh as a drop point.

        The box vanished before the next link in the chain could collect it, which explains why people are now being killed over it.

    Elizabeth and Joyce visit Nina Mishra, the academic Kuldesh called before he died. Nina tells them Kuldesh had a lock‑up for “dodgier” items and that he might have been trying to get advice about something suspicious. She and her boss, Professor Jonjo Mellors, suggest that if Kuldesh wanted to sell something illicit, he might have contacted Samantha Barnes, an antiques dealer and accomplished art forger.

    Meanwhile, Ron and Ibrahim, with Bob’s help, continue working the Mervyn catfishing angle, which intersects thematically with the larger theme of older people being targeted and exploited—much as Kuldesh was used as a naïve front in the heroin scheme.​

Escalation – more violence

    Dom Holt is found murdered in his warehouse; Chris illegally breaks in to look around and finds the body. He ropes in Ron and Joyce to pretend they heard a gunshot and called him, so the official scene can be preserved while the Club grabs photos.​

    Mitch Maxwell, enraged and frightened about the missing heroin and the bodies piling up, attacks Ron in his flat, convinced Ron has the box. Ron denies it and calls in the others. The Club sit down with Mitch and, in an odd alliance, compare notes about the missing heroin.

    Garth, a henchman working for Mitch and later connected to other players, reacts more philosophically as the operation unravels; Mitch, less so.​

The box resurfaces – via Stephen

    While all this is unfolding, Stephen’s health continues to decline. He’s increasingly confused but still has flashes of clarity and cunning.

    Elizabeth and Stephen share some intimate, painful scenes about time, memory, and their past. Stephen administers to himself an injection (procured via Viktor, a somewhat shady acquaintance) and peacefully ends his life, with Elizabeth by his side.​

    After his death and cremation, Elizabeth is sleepless and grief‑stricken. Thinking about Stephen and replaying fragments of conversations, she realizes something: the unknown number Kuldesh called before he died must have been Stephen’s, and the shovel detail plus Stephen’s behavior point to Stephen having hidden the heroin in the Coopers Chase allotment.

Finding the heroin

    Part 3 opens with Bogdan (the Club’s quietly dangerous, highly competent friend) digging up the allotment with the Thursday Murder Club looking on.

    He finds the heroin box where Stephen arranged for it to be buried. They now have the central MacGuffin that everyone has been searching and killing for.​

    They hatch a plan: rather than quietly handing it in, they will use the box as bait to flush out the full network of criminals. Ibrahim tips off his patient, jailed drug dealer Connie Johnson, that they have the box, knowing she will spread the news through the underworld.​

2. Characters and their investigative roles
Thursday Murder Club

    Elizabeth Best – former spy, strategic brain of the group, but emotionally wrecked by Stephen’s decline and death. She drives much of the high‑risk plotting and the final sting operation.

    Joyce Meadowcroft – retired nurse, the series’ diarist‑style voice. In this book she also steps up emotionally as Elizabeth falters and has key moments of bravery (e.g., pulling a fire alarm in a crucial scene).

    Ron Ritchie – ex‑union firebrand; tough, big‑hearted, tends to wade into danger (and gets assaulted by Mitch). Often the front‑line muscle and bluster.

    Ibrahim Arif – retired psychiatrist, meticulous and cautious; leads the catfishing counter‑operation and uses his contacts (like Connie Johnson) to spread information in controlled ways.

Victim and heroin chain

    Kuldesh Sharma – elderly antiques dealer, friend of the club. Killed because he diverted a heroin shipment and tried to protect himself and others by hiding it and reaching out to Nina and Stephen.

    Mitch Maxwell – drug boss importing heroin; violent, desperate to recover the missing box; eventually dies of a heart attack while obsessively searching a dump for the heroin.​

    Dominic “Dom” Holt – Mitch’s second‑in‑command, the one who arranged the drop at Kuldesh’s shop; murdered on orders from higher up as part of power plays over the heroin trade.​

    Connie Johnson – recurring villain from earlier books, a drugs kingpin now in prison. In the background here, she quietly orchestrates killings (including those of Dom and Samantha) to take control of the local heroin trade. The Club never realizes she’s pulling strings.​

Other key players

    Nina Mishra – professor and Kuldesh’s friend, with family roots in the antiques trade. Initially appears as a helpful contact; ultimately revealed as Kuldesh’s killer and a would‑be dealer, trying to sell the heroin on her own behalf.

    Professor Jonjo Mellors – Nina’s boss, another academic with connections to art and antiques; part of the network of people who might plausibly handle illicit items.

    Samantha Barnes – antiques dealer and art forger; linked to Kuldesh through the art/antique world. She is murdered by Connie’s orders; Nina is suspected of involvement.

    Garth – criminal associate initially linked to Mitch and later used in Elizabeth’s trap. He agrees to help set up a meeting that will expose Nina.

    Chris Hudson and Donna De Freitas – the local detectives; sidelined by the NCA but continue to collaborate unofficially with the Club. Provide official channels, scene access, and lawful arrests once the Club’s schemes bear fruit.

    Mervyn + Tatiana/Jeremmy – Mervyn is an elderly man being catfished; “Tatiana” and “Jeremmy” are just scammer identities behind one IP address. This subplot mirrors the main story’s themes of exploitation of the elderly.​

3. Motives and villains
Immediate murder: who killed Kuldesh and why?

    Kuldesh is killed over the missing heroin box.

    He hides the heroin instead of passing it on as an “innocent” antiques transaction, which puts him at odds with the drug network.

    Nina Mishra, who knows Kuldesh and is aware he has something valuable, becomes the direct murderer. She wants control of the heroin and the profit it represents.

    So:

        Means – gun, arranged meeting on a lonely road.

        Opportunity – she knows he is worried and seeking advice, and she’s one of the few people he calls.

        Motive – greed (a potential multi‑million payoff if she can flip the heroin) and a willingness to cross moral lines, building on her proximity to both antiques and academia.

Broader criminal motives

    Connie Johnson orders several killings (Dom, Samantha, attempts on others) as part of a strategy to seize control of the entire local heroin pipeline. Everyone else in the chain is expendable to her.​

    Mitch Maxwell and his crew are driven by fear and greed—fear of being blamed for the missing shipment, greed for the money it represents. Mitch’s increasing desperation makes him violent and reckless.

Emotional and thematic motives

    Stephen’s hidden role is not about crime but about love and protection. He takes the heroin box not to profit but to keep it out of the hands of dangerous people, and he hides it where the Club will eventually find it. His dementia makes this both moving and complicated—he’s the only one who truly knows the hiding place, yet he can only communicate it in fragments.

    The Club’s motive is justice and loyalty (to Kuldesh, to each other, and to Stephen’s memory), but also a sense of purpose in later life: solving crimes is how they assert agency and meaning.

4. Major interactions that crack the case

Think of these as the “key interviews and stings” on a murder board.
1) The academic trail: Elizabeth & Joyce vs Nina (and Jonjo)

    Elizabeth and Joyce’s visit to Nina establishes: Kuldesh called her, he had a lock‑up, and he might have been trying to move something dodgy.

    Jonjo and Nina point toward Samantha Barnes as another link, expanding the suspect pool to the broader antiques/art‑forgery world.

    This creates a triangle: Kuldesh – Nina – Samantha, all with potential access to the heroin.

2) The criminal negotiations: Club vs Mitch & Garth

    After Mitch attacks Ron, the Club essentially sits him down and negotiates: they admit they know about the heroin, and Mitch admits the extent of his problem.

    Garth, more pragmatic, becomes a pivot: he’s willing to cooperate in a trap once he realizes Nina is out for herself and Mitch may not survive this mess.

3) The tech sting: Bob, Ibrahim, and the catfishers

    Bob’s identification of the shared IP address links “Tatiana” and “Jeremmy” as the same scammer operation, allowing the Club to close down the catfishing of Mervyn.

    This subplot doesn’t reveal the murderer, but it mirrors the way people exploit older and more vulnerable individuals and gives the Club a test‑run for some of their online investigation tactics.​

4) The emotional key: Elizabeth & Stephen

    Stephen’s confused clues (references to the allotment, odd comments about “keeping something safe”) do not register immediately.

    After his self‑chosen death and cremation, Elizabeth’s grief‑driven reflection leads her to connect the unknown number Kuldesh called to Stephen, and the shovel clue to the allotment.

    This emotional breakthrough gives them the physical evidence—finding the heroin—that underpins the final sting operations.

5) The motorway trap: confronting Nina

    With the heroin box now found and used as bait, Elizabeth arranges for Garth to meet Nina at a motorway service station for a sale: Nina believes Garth is buying the box from her for £5 million.

    Mid‑transaction, the Thursday Murder Club reveal themselves; Nina is confronted with:

        her link to Kuldesh,

        her opportunity to kill him,

        her possession of the heroin box.

    The police (Chris and Donna, or NCA presence) are close enough to step in once the Club have forced Nina to incriminate herself. She is arrested as Kuldesh’s killer and as a key player trying to sell the heroin.

5. Resolution: who the “devils” are and how it ends
Case closure (legal and factual)

    Kuldesh’s murder – solved: Nina Mishra pulled the trigger in the woods to get control of the heroin. She is arrested and charged.​

    Dom’s murder and Samantha’s death – ultimately traced back (for the reader) to Connie Johnson’s orders, part of her bid to take over the heroin trade. The Club, however, remain unaware of Connie’s full culpability; she stays a long‑term series antagonist.​

    Heroin box – recovered by the Club and then disposed of safely (the gang ensures it won’t simply re‑enter the criminal pipeline, working in tandem with law enforcement).

Personal resolutions

    Stephen dies, and his ashes—symbolically important—are ultimately taken abroad. Joyce reveals in the final chapter that the box containing Stephen’s ashes is now in Baghdad, where Elizabeth will soon travel, tying off Elizabeth’s emotional arc for this book while hinting at future adventures.​

    The Club maintain their friendship and purpose despite the grief. Joyce, in particular, grows into more of a leader and emotional anchor as Elizabeth is temporarily hollowed out by loss.

    Mervyn’s catfisher is exposed, saving him further financial and emotional harm and underscoring the Club’s role as protectors of their community.

The “last devil”

    The title points in a few directions:

        The literal last criminal to die in the chain is Mitch, who suffers a heart attack while searching a dump for the heroin, undone by his own greed and obsession.​

        On a thematic level, the “devils” are the various exploiters—drug dealers, scammers, corrupt operators like Connie—and the question is who will be the last of them to pay a price.

        Emotionally, it also invites us to think about mortality within the Club itself: which of these beloved elderly characters will be “the last devil to die,” still causing mischief and seeking justice at the end.
