--- BACKGROUND AND CONTEXT ---

"Broadchurch" is a British crime drama written by Chris Chibnall and directed
by James Strong, first broadcast on ITV in March 2013. Series 1 consists of
eight episodes and tells the story of the investigation of an eleven-year-old
boy's murder in a small Dorset coastal town, following the investigation and
its effects on the community through to a conclusion that was one of the most
shocking in British television drama.

The series was an immediate and enormous success — the final episode was watched
by nine million viewers, making it one of the most-watched British dramas of
the decade — and is credited with establishing a specific template for British
crime drama that several subsequent shows (including its own American remake,
"Gracepoint") have followed. It won multiple BAFTA awards including Best Drama
Series, and its central performances — particularly from Olivia Colman, who
won the BAFTA for Best Actress — are among the finest in British television
of the period.

The show's achievement is the combination of a genuinely constructed mystery —
the killer's identity is surprising and fairly planted — with an unusually
serious and sustained attention to the human cost of murder in a small community:
what it does to the victim's family, what it does to the people surrounding
them, and what it does to the community's understanding of itself and its safety.

--- THE TOWN OF BROADCHURCH ---

Broadchurch is a small Dorset coastal town — fictional, though based on real
locations — whose specific character is that of a community that has existed
for a long time in a specific relationship with the sea and with the specific
social dynamics of a small place. Everyone knows everyone. There is no privacy
in any real sense, though there are secrets — the secrets that small places keep
because everyone has agreed not to speak about certain things.

The town is not wealthy or glamorous. It is working and middle class, with
specific economic pressures from tourism and from the decline of traditional
coastal industries. Its people are specific and fully drawn: they are not types
but individuals, with particular histories and particular relationships that
give the community its specific texture.

The cliff, the beach, and the sea are constant presences — visually and
atmospherically — throughout the series. The specific quality of the Dorset
coast in the early spring, when the series is set, gives the show a beauty
that is in direct contrast to the darkness of its subject matter, and Chibnall
and Strong exploit this contrast throughout.

--- THE MURDER OF DANNY LATIMER ---

Danny Latimer is eleven years old. He is found dead on the beach at Broadchurch
early in the morning. He has been strangled and his body has been moved — he
was killed elsewhere and brought to the beach. He was a local boy, the son
of Mark and Beth Latimer, and the brother of Chloe.

His death is immediately the most significant event in the community's recent
history. A child murdered in a small coastal town is not something that the
community can absorb and move forward from quickly or easily. It changes the
specific understanding that the people of Broadchurch have of their own place:
they believed they were safe. They believed that whatever violence the world
contained was somewhere else. Danny's death proves them wrong.

--- DETECTIVE INSPECTOR ALEC HARDY ---

The investigation is led by Detective Inspector Alec Hardy (David Tennant), who
has just arrived in Broadchurch from another force. Hardy is immediately
distinctive: a man carrying the weight of a previous investigation that went
catastrophically wrong — the Sandbrook case, in which he was the senior
investigating officer and which ended without the resolution it needed, and
which damaged both his professional reputation and his health. He is physically
ill — he has a heart condition that is serious enough to be a genuine physical
limitation — and he is professionally and personally isolated.

He is not warm. He is not easy. He does not manage his presence in the community
in the way that a detective who has to work there long-term might. He is a man
doing a specific job with specific capabilities and he does not have the energy
or the inclination to be liked.

David Tennant's performance is one of the series' most impressive achievements:
he creates in Hardy a figure who is both genuinely difficult and genuinely
compelling, whose specific qualities — the intelligence, the tenacity, the
specific quality of someone who is doing this because he has to rather than
because it is easy — make him entirely real.

--- DETECTIVE SERGEANT ELLIE MILLER ---

Ellie Miller (Olivia Colman) is the local detective sergeant who was expecting
to be appointed to Hardy's position when the case arrived. She knows Broadchurch
in the way that only someone who grew up there and has spent their career there
knows a place: the specific people, their specific histories, the specific
relationships that constitute the community's social reality. She is the
investigation's essential local intelligence.

Her relationship with Hardy is one of the series' central dynamics. They do
not get along, initially, in the way that two very different people who are
suddenly required to work closely together often don't. Hardy is abrasive and
dismissive; Ellie is warm and locally engaged in ways that he doesn't
immediately value. The development of their working relationship — from mutual
exasperation toward something more like genuine respect — is handled with the
care and the humor that Colman and Tennant bring to every scene they share.

But Ellie is not simply Hardy's local intelligence and eventual partner. She
is also a person whose specific position in Broadchurch — whose relationships
within the community, whose specific family situation — becomes directly relevant
to the investigation in ways that are among the most important and most painful
elements of the series.

--- THE COMMUNITY AS SUSPECT ---

The investigation moves through the Broadchurch community with the specific
attention that the closed-world murder mystery format demands: everyone is a
potential suspect, everyone is interviewed, and the investigation's mapping
of the community's specific relationships and secrets generates a portrait of
Broadchurch that is considerably more complicated than its surface suggested.

Key figures in the community who come under investigation include:

Mark Latimer — Danny's father, a plumber, a man who loves his son and whose
specific secrets about his own conduct become significant to the investigation.
He has been conducting an affair, and the investigation of his movements on
the night of Danny's death requires him to disclose this to both the police
and, ultimately, his wife Beth.

Steve Connelly — a psychic who appears in the town after Danny's death and
whose specific knowledge of events — things he should not be able to know —
becomes a puzzling and unsettling factor in the investigation.

Jack Marshall — the newsagent who is revealed to have a criminal past involving
underage girls, information that becomes public and that destroys him, though
he is not Danny's killer. His story is one of the series' most carefully handled
tragedies: the specific horror of what the release of his past does to him, in
a community that has already decided he must be guilty, is a devastating study
of mob justice and its specific cruelty. He takes his own life before the truth
is established.

The Reverend Paul Coates — a local clergyman whose specific relationship with
Danny — he was mentoring him — and with the community is part of the
investigation. He is genuinely grieved by Danny's death and is cleared.

Susan Wright — a reclusive woman who lives in a caravan on the cliff and whose
specific knowledge of events becomes increasingly significant as the investigation
develops. She witnessed more than she has initially disclosed, and her
unwillingness to come forward costs the investigation time.

--- THE PRESS AND THE PUBLIC ---

One of Series 1's significant threads is the intrusion of the national press
into the community. A local journalist, Olly Stevens (Joe Sims), who works
for the town's small local paper, is Ellie's nephew. A national tabloid journalist,
Karen White (Vicky McClure), arrives from London to cover the story.

The specific damage that press attention does to the community — the specific
ways in which media coverage of a child's murder turns the grief of specific
people into content and their lives into a story — is one of Broadchurch's
most consistent preoccupations. Karen White is not simply a villain: she is
a journalist doing a job whose specific ethics are honestly rendered. But the
impact of that job on the people she is covering is shown with equal honesty.
The leak of Jack Marshall's criminal past to the press directly contributes
to his death, and the series is explicit about the moral weight of that outcome.

--- THE KILLER: JOE MILLER ---

The killer of Danny Latimer is Joe Miller — Ellie's husband.

The revelation is one of the most devastating in British television drama,
and it is devastating specifically because of what it means for Ellie. The
woman who has been the investigation's warm heart, whose local knowledge and
genuine care have been the community's point of connection with the investigation —
her husband killed the boy. The investigation that she has been conducting,
the questions she has been asking, the evidence she has been gathering: all
of it has been pointing, without her knowing, toward the person who shares
her bed and her life and her children's home.

The truth of what happened is deeply disturbing. Joe Miller had been grooming
Danny Latimer — developing an inappropriate, sexualised attachment to the
eleven-year-old boy over a period of months, meeting him secretly at night
without Ellie's knowledge. Danny, on the night of his death, told Joe he was
going to tell people what had been happening between them. Joe killed him —
strangled him in a panicked act of violence to prevent disclosure — and then
moved the body to the beach to distance the crime from the place where it
actually occurred.

Joe is not a monster in the theatrical or caricature sense of the word. He is
presented as a man living an ordinary domestic life — a devoted father, a man
Ellie and the community had no reason to suspect, a person who existed entirely
within the fabric of normal Broadchurch life. This ordinariness is precisely
the show's central and most uncomfortable point: the person who committed this
particular horror was invisible, in plain sight, mistaken for safety itself.

--- ELLIE'S RESPONSE ---

Ellie Miller's response to the revelation that her husband killed Danny Latimer
is the series' most powerful emotional sequence. She has to be physically
restrained from attacking Joe after his confession. She is destroyed by what
she has found — not simply the grief of the betrayal but the specific horror
of having been the investigator of a crime committed by the person she trusted
most in the world, and the unbearable knowledge of what her husband was doing
to a child while she was living alongside him in apparent normality.

Olivia Colman's performance in these sequences is simply extraordinary. The
specific quality of her grief — not the operatic grief of melodrama but the
stunned, furious, devastating grief of a person whose world has been demolished —
is rendered with a precision and an honesty that earned her the BAFTA and the
lasting regard of everyone who saw it.

--- HARDY'S RESOLUTION ---

Hardy's arc through the investigation also reaches its conclusion in the finale.
The Sandbrook case — the failed investigation that haunts him — is connected
to the Broadchurch investigation in ways that become clearer as the series
progresses. It is established that Hardy protected someone connected to himself
in the Sandbrook case by failing to fully pursue specific evidence, and the
ending of Series 1 opens the possibility of the Sandbrook case being properly
reopened — which becomes the central thread of his arc in Series 2.

His heart condition — which has been a physical presence throughout — reaches
a crisis point in the finale that is resolved sufficiently to leave him standing
and working, but with a physical situation that has not been permanently addressed
and that cannot be indefinitely deferred.

--- THE COMMUNITY IN THE AFTERMATH ---

The series ends with the community of Broadchurch in the specific state of
aftermath that a revelation like Joe Miller's guilt produces: devastated, unable
to immediately process what it means, needing time that the series does not
provide because the series ends. The specific people whose lives have been in
the middle of the investigation — the Latimers, Ellie, the other characters —
are left at the beginning of whatever comes next.

The Latimer family, in particular, faces a specific and terrible kind of grief:
the knowledge not only that Danny was murdered by someone they knew and trusted
but of the nature of what was done to him in the months before his death. The
series does not soften this. It asks the viewer, alongside these characters,
to sit with what it means.

--- NOTES ON THE SERIES ---

Broadchurch Series 1 is one of the finest British crime dramas of the decade.
Its specific achievement — the sustained attention to what murder does to
the people surrounding it, conducted alongside a genuinely constructed mystery —
is something the format of the eight-episode single-case serial makes possible
in ways that shorter or longer formats cannot. The central performances of
Tennant and Colman are among the best in British television, and the revelation
of the killer — prepared throughout with the fairness that the best mystery
writing demands — is genuinely shocking while being, in retrospect, entirely
prepared for. The series established a template for British crime drama and
established Chibnall as one of its major writers.
