TVE_160 | Happy Valley: Complete Story (Series 1–3) | Writer: Sally Wainwright | TV Series
================================================================================
FULL SERIES SYNOPSIS — SPOILERS INCLUDED
================================================================================

NOTE: This synopsis covers all three series of Happy Valley (2014, 2016, 2023)
as a single complete narrative, since the story of Catherine Cawood and Tommy
Lee Royce runs continuously through all three and reaches its conclusion only
in Series 3.

--- BACKGROUND AND CONTEXT ---

"Happy Valley" is a BBC crime drama written by Sally Wainwright, one of the
finest television writers working in Britain. It is set in the Calder Valley
in West Yorkshire — the "happy valley" of the title, the ironic nickname given
to a region with high rates of heroin addiction, poverty, and associated crime.
The show is simultaneously a police procedural, a character study of
extraordinary depth, a portrait of a specific place and community, and a
sustained examination of trauma, consequence, and what it costs people to keep
going after devastating loss.

The central character is Sergeant Catherine Cawood of the West Yorkshire Police —
a middle-aged uniformed sergeant of exceptional competence and personal toughness
who is raising her grandson Ryan, the child of her deceased daughter Becky.
Becky killed herself after being raped by Tommy Lee Royce, a local criminal.
Tommy Lee Royce is Ryan's biological father. And the entire story of Happy
Valley is the story of what that triangle — Catherine, Tommy, and Ryan — means,
and costs, and finally resolves.

--- SERIES 1: THE KIDNAPPING CASE ---

Series 1 opens with Catherine in the middle of her working life: managing
her team, dealing with drug-related crime in the valley, raising Ryan with
the help of her sister Clare (who is herself in recovery from addiction), and
carrying the grief and anger of Becky's death. Catherine is formidable and
brittle in equal measure — she has held herself together through force of will
for years, and the specific quality of her toughness is recognisable as the
toughness of someone who cannot afford to stop.

The main criminal plot of Series 1 involves a kidnapping scheme. Kevin Weatherill,
a hapless local accountant, is coerced into participating in a plan to kidnap
Ann Gallagher — the daughter of a local businessman — for ransom. The plan is
organised by two local criminals, Ashley Cowgill and Lee Royce (Tommy's brother),
and the scheme quickly goes wrong in ways that escalate from criminal stupidity
into genuine horror.

Ann Gallagher is kidnapped and held captive. The investigation of her disappearance
falls to Catherine's team and to the detective branch, and the search for Ann
becomes increasingly urgent as the kidnappers' control of the situation
deteriorates.

--- TOMMY LEE ROYCE ENTERS ---

Tommy Lee Royce has been released from prison and returns to the valley.
His return is the inciting event that transforms what might have been a
contained kidnapping investigation into something much more personal and dangerous.

Tommy is immediately magnetic and immediately frightening. He is handsome,
charismatic, and utterly without empathy or remorse — a man for whom other
people are simply objects to be used. His history with Becky, and his status
as Ryan's father, makes his presence in the valley intolerable to Catherine.

Tommy becomes involved in the kidnapping — the scheme connects to his criminal
network — and the investigation of Ann Gallagher's abduction becomes entangled
with Catherine's personal history with the man she holds responsible for her
daughter's death.

The climax of Series 1 involves Catherine confronting Tommy directly — a
physical confrontation of extraordinary violence and intensity in which Catherine
is seriously injured but survives, and Tommy is arrested. Ann Gallagher is
rescued. The kidnapping scheme is dismantled.

But Tommy does not face trial for Becky's rape. The evidence was not sufficient.
He is convicted for his role in the kidnapping and returns to prison, carrying
the knowledge that Catherine cannot legally touch him for what he did to Becky.

--- RYAN ---

Ryan is approximately eight years old in Series 1. He knows nothing about his
father's identity or history. He is a bright, affectionate child who is devoted
to his grandmother Catherine. His existence — as the child of the man Catherine
most hates in the world, born of an act of violence that destroyed her daughter —
is the ethical and emotional heart of the show. Catherine loves Ryan completely
and unconditionally. She also knows what he came from.

--- SERIES 2: DEEPER INTO THE VALLEY ---

Series 2, set two years after Series 1, picks up the consequences of the events
that concluded Series 1 while developing a new criminal investigation. Tommy
Lee Royce is in prison. Catherine is still working, still raising Ryan, still
carrying everything she carries.

The main new criminal plot involves a local murder case — a man is killed in
circumstances that connect to organised crime operating in the valley. The
investigation, led now more explicitly by the detective branch with Catherine
as a key figure, moves through the specific criminal ecology of the Calder
Valley: the heroin trade, the specific families and networks that dominate
criminal life in the area, and the ways in which violence in these communities
is both ordinary and devastating.

Catherine's sister Clare becomes romantically involved with Neil Ackroyd,
a man who turns out to have connections to the criminal case — a development
that pulls Clare, who has worked so hard to build a stable recovery, back
toward danger. The specific threat to Clare — her sobriety, her safety, her
independence — is one of Series 2's most painful threads.

A new character of significance is Frances Drummond, a woman who becomes
involved with Tommy while he is in prison and who assists him in various ways
from the outside. Frances is a social worker — a specific professional irony —
and her infatuation with Tommy is rendered as the kind of devastating mistake
that highly intelligent people make when their emotional needs are expertly
targeted by someone with no conscience.

--- TOMMY'S PRISON ACTIVITIES ---

Tommy, in prison, is not inactive. He uses Frances as an outside operative.
He begins to pursue knowledge of and contact with Ryan — his son, whose
existence he is now aware of and whom he intends to claim or influence in ways
that Catherine will find intolerable.

The question of Ryan and what Tommy wants from him — whether it is genuine
paternal feeling (unlikely given everything we know of Tommy) or whether Ryan
is simply another means of tormenting Catherine — is left productively ambiguous
in Series 2. Tommy is capable of enough ordinary human response to make his
interest in Ryan not entirely reducible to malice, but he is also incapable
of putting anyone else's interests above his own.

--- RYAN LEARNS THE TRUTH ---

Ryan, now a teenager, learns the truth about his father in Series 2. The
revelation of Tommy Lee Royce's identity and history — that his father is a
man who raped his mother, drove her to suicide, and is a convicted violent
criminal — is handled with considerable care. Ryan's response is complicated:
he is angry, frightened, and privately curious. He is not simply horrified.
He is a teenager who has grown up without a father and who, however terrible
the truth of that father is, experiences the knowledge of his existence as
something significant.

Ryan's relationship to Tommy — his biological inheritance, his curiosity about
the man, and what he will eventually choose to do with that curiosity — is
established in Series 2 as the thread that will need to be resolved.

--- SERIES 3: THE FINAL RECKONING ---

Series 3, broadcast in 2023, is the show's conclusion. Catherine is approaching
retirement from the police. Ryan is now a teenager of approximately sixteen
or seventeen. Tommy Lee Royce is nearing the end of his sentence and will
soon be released.

The approaching release of Tommy — and what it will mean for Catherine, for
Ryan, and for the community — organises Series 3's tension from its opening
episodes. Catherine has spent twenty years in a sustained state of managed
conflict with Tommy's existence: first as the man who destroyed her daughter,
then as a prisoner whose incarceration provided a kind of safety, and now as
someone who will be free and in the world again.

--- RYAN AND TOMMY ---

Ryan, in Series 3, makes contact with Tommy. This is the development Catherine
has most feared and most tried to prevent — her grandson seeking out the man
who destroyed her family. Ryan's curiosity about his father has not diminished
with knowledge of the truth; if anything, the truth has made the curiosity
more urgent, because Ryan is a young man trying to understand himself and
his origins.

The specific nature of what happens between Ryan and Tommy in Series 3 — what
Tommy wants from the contact, what Ryan is hoping for, and how it develops —
is central to the series. Tommy is not capable of being a good father. But
he is capable of being what Ryan wants a father to be for long enough to do
damage. His management of Ryan — his specific targeting of Ryan's needs and
vulnerabilities — is one of the most disturbing things the series does, because
it shows Tommy applying his predatory intelligence to the one person Catherine
cannot protect by simply refusing access.

--- THE CRIMINAL PLOT: SERIES 3 ---

The criminal investigation in Series 3 involves a murder case — a local
crime that connects to the valley's ongoing criminal ecology — that Catherine
and her colleagues investigate in the final weeks of her career. The specific
case is constructed to resonate with the larger personal themes: questions of
justice, of what people do for love or loyalty, of the specific weight that
old wrongs carry in small communities.

The investigation is not the show's primary focus in Series 3 in the way that
the kidnapping was in Series 1 — Wainwright's attention is on the personal
arc, on the Catherine-Tommy-Ryan triangle reaching its culmination, and the
procedural investigation is in some ways a frame for the more essential drama.

--- THE CONFRONTATION AND CONCLUSION ---

The final episode of Series 3 — and of the show — delivers the confrontation
that the entire series has been building toward. Tommy Lee Royce, released
or about to be released, comes into direct proximity with Catherine for the
last time.

The specific resolution — what happens between Catherine and Tommy, whether
Tommy faces any further justice for what he did to Becky, and what Ryan
ultimately chooses — is handled with the moral seriousness and refusal of
easy comfort that characterises all of Wainwright's writing.

Tommy dies in the final episode. He has set fire to himself in a police car —
a specific, deliberate act that is simultaneously a final act of agency and
a removal of himself from the situation he can no longer control. His death
is not Catherine's victory in any simple sense. It is not a rescue. It is an
ending — the ending of a long war that was never formally declared, fought
in the specific terrain of a West Yorkshire valley, between two people whose
lives were bound together by the worst possible act.

Catherine survives. She retires. Ryan is alive and has made choices that
indicate some version of who he will become — a version that includes knowledge
of his father and the conscious decision not to be him.

Clare is there. The family that Catherine held together through twenty years
of difficulty is intact, damaged and surviving.

--- WHAT HAPPY VALLEY IS ABOUT ---

Wainwright's show is about many things simultaneously, but its deepest concern
is with what it costs women to hold things together — families, communities,
institutions — in the face of violence that is usually male and usually
directed at them or the people they love. Catherine Cawood is one of television
drama's great characters because she is recognisably, specifically human in
her toughness: she is not superhuman, she makes mistakes, she is sometimes
wrong, and she keeps going not through the absence of feeling but through the
specific decision, made over and over again, to keep going anyway.

The valley — beautiful, difficult, full of ordinary suffering — is the world
in which this story takes place, and it is rendered with the specificity of
someone who loves and knows it. The "happy" in the title is not quite ironic
and not quite sincere: it is a name that people in a place give to their home,
whatever the home contains.
