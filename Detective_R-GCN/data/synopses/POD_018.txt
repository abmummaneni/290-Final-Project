THE HEIST


March 18, 1990

In the early morning hours of March 18, 1990 — St. Patrick's Day night into the early
hours of the following day — two men dressed as Boston police officers arrived at the
side entrance of the Isabella Stewart Gardner Museum in Boston's Fenway neighborhood.
They told the security guard on duty that they had received a report of disturbance and
needed to be let in. The guard, Rick Abath, buzzed them through without checking their
identification against a list of legitimate police officers — a violation of the museum's
own security protocols.

Once inside, the two men handcuffed both security guards on duty, taped their eyes and
mouths, and secured them to pipes in the basement. They spent approximately 81 minutes
in the museum, moving through specific galleries, cutting canvases from frames, and
removing works from display. Then they left.

The theft was not discovered until the morning shift arrived hours later.


What Was Taken

The thieves removed 13 works of art with a total estimated value, as of 2018, of
approximately $500 million — the single largest property theft in recorded history.
The stolen works include:

Three Rembrandt van Rijn works: The Storm on the Sea of Galilee (his only known seascape),
A Lady and Gentleman in Black, and a small self-portrait.

A Vermeer: The Concert, one of only 34 known Vermeer paintings in existence.

Five Degas works: four sketches and a small oil painting.

A Manet: Chez Tortoni.

A bronze eagle finial from a Napoleonic flag.

A Chinese vase from the Dutch room.

The selection of works has been analyzed extensively by art historians and investigators
over three decades. The Vermeer and The Storm on the Sea of Galilee are, by almost any
assessment, among the most significant art objects ever stolen. Their absence from the
walls of the Gardner — replaced by empty frames that the museum has kept in place as
testament to the theft — is one of the most remarkable sights in American museum culture.

The museum offered a $5 million reward for information leading to the return of the works.
It has never been claimed.

THE ISABELLA STEWART GARDNER MUSEUM


The Building and Its Character

The Isabella Stewart Gardner Museum is itself an extraordinary object — a Venetian-style
palazzo built by Isabella Stewart Gardner in the late 19th century to house her personal
art collection, which she assembled over decades with the assistance of the art historian
Bernard Berenson. Gardner had very specific ideas about how her collection should be
displayed and maintained: she specified in her will that nothing in the museum should
be moved, added, or removed after her death. The museum must maintain the arrangement
she created.

This means that the empty frames where the stolen works once hung cannot be replaced —
they are there because Isabella Stewart Gardner's will prevents substitution. The museum
literally cannot fill the gaps. It exists in permanent visual testament to what was taken.

THE INVESTIGATION: 30 YEARS AND COUNTING


The FBI and the Investigation

The Gardner theft has been investigated by the FBI's Art Crime Team and by Boston-area
law enforcement continuously since 1990. It is the largest open art theft investigation
in the world. Dozens of leads have been pursued across three decades. No one has been
charged. The works have not been recovered.

The podcast documents the investigation's history in detail, including the specific
leads that have been pursued and where they led. This history includes multiple moments
at which investigators believed they were close — close to the works, close to the
people who took them, close to the intermediaries through whom they might be recovered —
and which led to dead ends or to situations where a recovery became impossible because
of specific circumstances.


The Boston Criminal World of 1990

In the Spring of 1990, Boston's criminal landscape was dominated by the Irish-American
organized crime world centered on the Winter Hill Gang, which had been run by James
"Whitey" Bulger before his flight from Boston in 1994. The Gardner theft occurred in
this specific criminal environment, and investigators' attention has focused for much
of three decades on the hypothesis that the theft was connected to this world.

The specific figure who has attracted the most sustained investigative attention is
Robert Gentile, a Connecticut gangster associated with the New England mob, who is
believed by investigators to have had connections to the stolen works after the theft.
Gentile denied knowledge of the works and refused to cooperate with investigators until
his death in 2014.

Brian McDevitt, a convicted art thief, and various other figures with connections to
both the art theft world and Boston organized crime are discussed in the podcast.


Rick Abath and the Guard Question

Rick Abath — the security guard who let the thieves into the museum — has been a figure
of sustained suspicion and public attention since the theft. He violated the museum's
security protocols by buzzing the fake officers in without verification. In the years
since the theft, various investigators and journalists have speculated about whether his
failure was simply a mistake or whether he was involved.

Horan and Mason interview Abath at length. His account of the night — what he was
thinking, why he made the decision he made, what he has thought about it in the decades
since — is one of the podcast's most human and most complicated threads. He has lived
with the weight of his decision for thirty years. Whether he bears any responsibility
beyond the violation of protocol that he undeniably committed is left as an open question
that the evidence does not resolve.


The Philadelphia Connection

A significant investigative thread in the podcast involves what law enforcement calls
the "Philadelphia hypothesis": the theory that the stolen Gardner works passed through
the Philadelphia organized crime world in the years after the theft, possibly involving
figures associated with the Philly mob and the Boston criminal network.

Specific individuals with Philadelphia connections who have attracted investigative
attention are discussed, including the role of informants whose information about the
works' location has been both promising and ultimately unproductive.


The Reward and the Tip Line

The Gardner Museum's standing $5 million reward for information leading to the return
of the works has generated thousands of tips over three decades. The podcast examines
what has happened to those tips — how they are processed, which have been pursued,
and what the reward structure itself does and does not incentivize.

One of the podcast's observations about the reward is that its structure — it requires
return of the works, not just information — may inadvertently complicate negotiations
with anyone who actually knows where the works are, because the pathway from information
to reward is legally and practically complicated when the person with the information
may themselves be criminally implicated.

WHERE THE WORKS ARE BELIEVED TO BE

The FBI's public position, maintained as of the podcast's production, is that the agency
believes the stolen works were in Connecticut and the Philadelphia area in the years
following the theft, and that they may still exist somewhere rather than having been
destroyed. The specific intelligence behind this position is not fully disclosed.

The possibility that the works have been destroyed — that whoever holds them has found
them impossible to sell and dangerous to keep — is one that investigators and art world
observers have been forced to take seriously as the decades have passed. Stolen works
of this profile cannot be sold in any mainstream art market. Their value to their holders
is primarily as collateral for criminal transactions or as a potential source of the
reward, neither of which has produced a resolution.

THE PODCAST'S CONTRIBUTION

Last Seen is distinguished by the depth of its historical and institutional reporting —
its examination of the museum, its founder, the specific art works stolen, and the
thirty-year arc of the investigation — as much as by its true crime elements. Horan
and Mason bring genuine art historical knowledge to the podcast alongside the criminal
investigation, ensuring that the stolen works are understood as what they are: objects
of irreplaceable cultural significance whose loss is not merely financial but civilizational.

The podcast does not solve the Gardner theft. No one has. But it assembles the most
comprehensive public account of the investigation available, and it makes the strongest
case for specific investigative directions that the evidence supports.
