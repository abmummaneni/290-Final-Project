NOV_108 — N or M?
Agatha Christie, 1941
Full Plot Synopsis (Tommy and Tuppence #3)

SERIES CONTEXT: TOMMY AND TUPPENCE


The Beresfords

Thomas (Tommy) and Tuppence Beresford are one of Christie's most distinctive creations —
a married couple who have worked as amateur spies and investigators since their first
adventure in the early 1920s (The Secret Adversary). By N or M?, it is 1940, the Second
World War is fully underway, and Tommy and Tuppence are middle-aged parents. Their son
Derek is in the military; their daughter Deborah works in a government role. Tommy and
Tuppence themselves are, to their considerable frustration, being kept out of the war
effort — they are too old for active service and the intelligence services have politely
declined to find a use for them.

This is the novel's comic and then genuinely tense premise: two people who were
genuinely capable during the First World War, who have experience, skills, and contacts,
being sidelined while their country fights for its survival.

THE MISSION


A Dying Intelligence Contact

Tommy is unexpectedly approached by an old intelligence contact named Mr. Grant, who
is working for British Intelligence. Grant tells Tommy, in carefully controlled terms,
that there is a serious problem: a German spy network operating in Britain has two key
agents known only by the code designations N and M. Both are believed to be in England,
operating from somewhere on the south coast, and at least one of them is believed to be
hidden at a seaside guest house called Sans Souci.

The British know almost nothing specific about N and M — not their identities, not
their precise locations, not the full scope of their network. But Grant knows they are
real and active, and that at Sans Souci there is at least one of them.

The mission is to infiltrate Sans Souci as an ordinary guest, identify which of the
residents is N or M, and report back. It is genuinely dangerous work — these are
professional enemy agents, and several British contacts who have gotten too close to
the network have already died.


Splitting Up

In a plot maneuver that is both comic and efficient, the intelligence contact approaches
Tommy with the intention of using only Tommy for the mission — specifically not involving
Tuppence, on the grounds that a lone individual can infiltrate more easily and with less
suspicion. Tommy is to go alone and tell Tuppence he has been given a desk job.

Tuppence, who is not fooled for a moment, independently learns enough to understand that
Tommy has been given a mission. She goes to Sans Souci herself, independently, also under
a cover identity. The two of them end up at the same guest house, separately embedded,
each initially unaware that the other is there, each running their own investigation.

This premise — the two of them separately playing undercover roles, each thinking they are
alone in enemy territory — is used for comedy but also for genuine suspense. The same
household contains both of them and at least one professional enemy agent.

SANS SOUCI


The Guest House and Its Residents

Sans Souci is a residential guest house in the fictional seaside town of Leahampton —
the kind of establishment that accommodates longer-term residents rather than short-stay
holidaymakers. In 1940, with the war on, a certain type of person lives in such places:
people who have been displaced, people who are retired and can't get petrol to move
around, people who are waiting for something.

The residents include:

Mrs. Cayley — a nervous, apologetic woman dominated by her blustering, apparently
pompous husband Mr. Cayley, who has been living at Sans Souci for his health.

Major Bletchley — a retired military man, hearty and opinionated, the kind of
Englishman who talks loudly about the war while being safely away from it.

Mrs. O'Rourke — a large, cheerful Irish woman with a sharp tongue and a good
humored manner that Tommy and Tuppence both find slightly too comfortable to be
entirely trustworthy.

Mr. Von Deinim — a young man presented as a German refugee, which in 1940 creates
immediate suspicion regardless of his actual loyalties.

Sheila Perenna — the daughter of Mrs. Perenna, who owns and runs Sans Souci. Sheila
is young, beautiful, and volatile, with Irish Republican sympathies that she makes
little effort to conceal.

Mrs. Perenna herself — the proprietress, Anglo-Irish, apparently hearty and capable,
running her establishment efficiently.

And others who pass through in shorter stays.

Christie uses the boarding house setting — a closed social world of people forced into
proximity who have not chosen each other — with the same economy she uses in the English
village. Everyone has something to hide; the question is what each person is hiding
and whether any of it is treason.

THE INVESTIGATION


Tommy and Tuppence's Separate Observations

Tommy and Tuppence, once they realize they are both at Sans Souci (a moment of domestic
comedy that Christie handles with affection), coordinate carefully while maintaining their
separate cover stories. They share observations, compare suspicions, and divide the labor
of watching the household.

Their analytical approaches are different: Tommy tends toward the systematic and the
evidence-based; Tuppence tends toward the intuitive and interpersonal. Together they cover
ground that neither would cover alone.

Both of them have initial instincts about who N or M might be that turn out to be wrong.
Christie manages her misdirection with the seasoned skill of someone who has spent decades
constructing traps for readers — each of the Sans Souci residents is suspicious in ways
that distract from the real answers.


The Baby

A subplot involves a young woman who comes to Sans Souci with an infant and appears to be
in distress — her situation intersects with the spy network in ways that are not immediately
apparent. Tuppence, whose maternal instincts are not subdued by her espionage activity,
becomes concerned for both mother and child.


Deaths and Near Misses

People connected to the Sans Souci household begin to die or to come into danger. The
network is not passive — its members are aware they are being investigated and begin to
take protective action. Both Tommy and Tuppence are in genuine danger at various points,
and Christie does not entirely defuse the menace with the novel's comedic elements.

THE REVELATION: N AND M


The Unexpected Identity

The reveal of N and M's identities is Christie's central misdirection prize. The most
obviously suspicious residents — Von Deinim, Mrs. O'Rourke, Sheila Perenna with her
Republican sympathies — are all constructed as plausible candidates, and Christie has
managed the social texture of Sans Souci so that suspicion distributes itself across the
household fairly naturally.

The actual agents are Mrs. Perenna — the apparently solid, hearty proprietress of the
guest house itself — and, working with her, another resident whose pleasant ordinariness
has been their perfect cover. Mrs. Perenna's real identity and background, when exposed,
explain her position running a guest house that serves as a hub for the network.

The moment of revelation is engineered by Christie with characteristic care — the
evidence has been present but presented in a light that made it look like something else
entirely.


The Danger at the End

The final confrontation, as the agents realize their exposure is imminent, involves real
and immediate danger to both Tommy and Tuppence. Christie does not pull her punches in
the climax — this is a wartime spy thriller and the stakes are genuine.

RESOLUTION

N and M are identified, the network is broken, and the intelligence mission succeeds.
Tommy and Tuppence's cover stories survive. They have, once again — as they did in the
First World War — served their country at personal risk while everyone around them thought
they were too old and too settled for real use.

The novel ends with the satisfying restoration of the Beresfords' partnership — sharpened
by the adventure, recommitted to each other, and quietly proud of what they managed. The
tone, unlike most Christie, is warm and domestic even at its most tense: these are two
people who genuinely like each other, and watching them work together is as much pleasure
as watching the mystery unravel.
