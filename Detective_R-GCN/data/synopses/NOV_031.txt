The department‑store murder in the window display

The story opens at French’s Department Store, a grand New York emporium owned by Cyrus French. The front window facing the sidewalk contains a showroom display of ultra‑modern European furniture, administered by the store’s display staff. The centerpiece is a folding wall‑bed imported from France, built into a paneled wall and meant to be revealed dramatically at noon each day.

On a given afternoon, the crowd gathers outside as the show begins. The window‑demonstration girl, a display assistant, pushes the control that opens the folding bed. The panel slides down, the bed unfolds—and a body tumbles out, crashing onto the display floor. The murdered woman is Winifred French, Cyrus’s wife, shot twice at close range, the killer’s hand likely still on the gun when she fell into the bed cavity. The body is posed in such a way that the bed shut behind her, hiding her until the public reveal at noon.

The case is handed to Inspector Richard Queen of the Homicide Squad, who arrives with his son, Ellery Queen, the mystery‑writer‑turned‑detective. The Queens quickly realize they are dealing with a murder that has been carefully staged inside store architecture, using the bed‑cavity as a hidden corpse‑locker.
The French family and the private apartment

The Queens learn that French’s building has a private apartment on the top floor where Cyrus and the family live in semi‑seclusion, accessible only by elevator and a spring‑locked door that can be opened from the outside only with a key. The apartment is a high‑ceilinged, book‑filled space dominated by large onyx bookends on the library table, and it serves both as family quarters and as Cyrus’s informal office.

Winifred French had gone to the store the night before, entering alone after closing, and then taken the elevator to the apartment. She was not seen again until the body dropped from the bed. The question is: who could have brought her upstairs, killed her, placed her in the bed, and then locked the apartment from the outside without alerting the guard or the housekeeping staff?

The family is in chaos:

    Cyrus French, the owner, is distraught and largely useless, collapsing into moral dithering.

    Marion French, his daughter by his first marriage, is cool and brittle, already suspected of a secret dislike for her stepmother.

    Bernice Carmody, Winifred’s own daughter from a prior marriage, is missing from the house that night; she has not been seen since the evening before, which feeds suspicion.

The Queens also learn that Winifred was interfering in many spheres: the family, the store’s board, and possibly a drug‑running operation hidden somewhere in the store’s labyrinthine departments. Motives for killing her include money, reputation, and the desire to keep certain illicit activities concealed.
The “French powder” and the onyx bookends

While examining the apartment, Ellery notices three crucial details:

    The onyx bookends on the table have minute bloodstains, indicating that the original attack or at least a violent struggle may have occurred there, not only in the display bed.

    Scattered around the base of the bookends are tiny granules of a white powder. The police initially think it is fingerprint powder, but Ellery realizes it is something more specific—an odd, crystalline residue that does not behave like ordinary dust.

    Various books are open or half‑strewn, including a set of volumes on Eastern European history and exotic politics, which seem out of place in a merchant’s sitting room.

The powder becomes the central physical clue. Ellery and the inspector try to trace its source. The staff at first blame careless crime‑scene officers, but Ellery insists the powder was there before the police arrived—a trace of the killer’s presence or the victim’s presence somewhere else.

Later, it becomes clear that the “French powder” of the title is both:

    A literal substance (perhaps a rare imported talc, cosmetic powder, or even a drug‑related compound),

    And a metaphor for the deceptive “finish” the French store presents to the world—a polished surface over corruption.

The bookends and the powder suggest that someone dragged or pushed Winifred into the library, bled her there, then wiped or partially cleaned the site, scattering a little of the “powder” in the process. The bed‑crime scene is secondary: the murder happened first in the apartment, the body moved to the display bed, then wedged shut in the panel to hide it until the noon show.
The Russian game, the cigarettes, and the books

Ellery also finds traces of a Russian board game (often called “banque”) that was partially set up, indicating that Winifred and perhaps another player had been engaged in a late‑night game the previous evening. The ashtray on the table holds several half‑smoked cigarettes, one of which matches the brand smoked by Bernice, while another matches a brand associated with a male acquaintance. Ellery begins to reconstruct who was in the apartment that night and in what combination.

The book pile is especially significant. Ellery notices that the titles and the manner in which they are opened suggest that someone was using the books as a cover for something else: passing messages, hiding notes, or even using the text as a coded address list. This ties into the drug‑ring subplot, which the Queens gradually uncover. The store is a front for a smuggling operation that uses innocent‑looking parcels and book orders to pass messages and contraband. The “French powder” may be a component of the drugs or a carrier agent, smuggled in via the book department or the display goods.
The suspects and the key to the locked apartment

The Queens begin building a suspect list anchored by four overlapping groups:

    The French family (Cyrus, Marion, Bernice), with possible motives of jealousy, inheritance, or escape from Winifred’s domineering presence.

    Members of the store’s board of directors, who feared Winifred’s interference in business decisions and her threat to expose certain practices.

    Key employees, including the head of security, various managers, and a private secretary (Westley Weaver), who is in love with Marion and has his own secrets.

    Employees tied to the drug ring, including a shady figure known only through his book‑order aliases, who may have killed Winifred when she discovered the smuggling.

The biggest obstacle is the locked‑door problem. The apartment is reached by elevator; the door is a spring‑lock that can be opened from the outside only with a key. The Queens interview the night‑watchman, the elevator operator, and the housekeeper; all swear they did not see Winifred go up again nor return, and no one officially accessing the apartment that night lacks an alibi or a key.

The key‑distribution becomes a logic grid: Ellery mentally assigns which people had access to top‑floor keys, which had reason to be there, and which had the physical strength and opportunity to haul a body from the apartment, down service stairs or in the elevator, into the display bed without being seen.
Ellery’s conditions and the narrowing circle

Ellery, in classic “fair‑play” style, privately lists the conditions the murderer must satisfy, and then he checks off suspects who fail to meet one or more of them. The conditions include:

    Opportunity to shoot Winifred in the apartment (therefore presence in the apartment during a specific window of time).

    Knowledge of the bed‑panel mechanism and the noon‑show routine.

    Access to the apartment key and the ability to lock the door from the outside.

    Connection to the “French powder” (possession, use, or transport).

    A motive strong enough to justify not only murder but also the elaborate staging in the shop window.

As each clue is revealed, the circle of suspects tightens. Ellery focuses especially on:

    Marion, whose coolness and possible resentment of her stepmother.

    Bernice, whose absence the night before looks suspicious but is later explained.

    The secretary Westley Weaver, who has been poking around the book department at night and discovering the drug‑ring’s book‑code.

    A board member with a flimsy alibi and shady merchandise connections.

Marion becomes a red‑herring focus because she went out the night of the murder to confront a director with whom Winifred was rumored to be carrying on an affair; her own late‑night movements are hard to explain but ultimately innocuous.
The reveal of the drug‑ring and the book‑code trap

The real breakthrough comes when the Queens connect the books and the powder to the drug ring. They discover that certain novels in the store’s stock are being used as a code: the owners of the books, the order of purchase, or handwritten notes in the margins convey addresses and quantities for narcotics shipments. The “French powder” is a component of the smuggling system—either a cutting agent, a carrier, or a signature that the parcels are authentic.

Ellery realizes that Winifred must have stumbled on this during one of her late‑night prowls through the store, perhaps while playing bridge or the Russian game with someone who was actually a distributor. She would then have threatened to expose the operation, and the ring’s ringleader decided to kill her.

The key to the reveal is that only one person in the building had:

    Close access to the French apartment,

    Knowledge of the bed‑panel and the show schedule,

    A reason to be in the book department, and

    The drug‑ring connections that the powder and the coded books imply.

That person is not a board member, not Marion, and not Bernice, but an employee who hides behind an administrative role and who has been quietly manipulating the book‑department records to conceal the ring. The Queens see that this person had the opportunity to lure Winifred back to the apartment, kill her, then move the body to the bed cavity, lock the spring‑door, and leave by the normal elevator route, because the killer also had the apartment key from a previous, legitimate errand.
The final denouement and the last‑line twist

In the classic Ellery Queen finale, the suspects are gathered, and Ellery gives an extended, almost lecture‑like explanation, walking through all the clues—

    the blood‑stained bookends,

    the odd “French powder” traces,

    the book‑codes and the drug‑ring,

    the key‑access and the locked‑apartment timeline,

    and the physical evidence of the body‑movement from apartment to display.

As he speaks, he describes the murderer’s actions in the third person, making the circle of known suspects smaller and smaller until only one satisfies all the conditions. The assembled group grows tense; the police exchange glances; the family members brace themselves.

The solution then lands on the last two words of the novel: the killer’s name, revealed in a single line, is the employee who had quietly been managing the book‑department logistics and the apartment‑key access, using the “French powder” as both a cover and an identifier for the drug‑trade, and who realized that Winifred’s interference would expose the whole operation.

The book ends with the killer arrested, the drug ring exposed, and the French family left to deal with the ruins of their reputation and the horror that the polished department‑store surface had concealed a secret crime‑system. Ellery’s triumph is not moral grandeur but pure puzzle‑solving: the “French powder” and the locked‑door are both explained by the same person, whose motives and behavior fit all the conditions he had listed.
