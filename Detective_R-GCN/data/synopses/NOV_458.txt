Framing and structure

The novel is told almost entirely from the first‑person perspective of Blythe Connor, who addresses her story to her ex‑husband, Fox, as a kind of confessional letter after their marriage has collapsed. The present‑day material—Blythe watching Fox’s new family from her car, revisiting the past—is interwoven with flashbacks to her own childhood and to the traumatic histories of her mother, Cecilia, and grandmother, Etta. This braided structure slowly reveals a generational pattern of damaged motherhood and emotional absence that Blythe is desperate to overcome, yet seems unable to escape.
Blythe’s family history

Before Blythe’s own children are born, the book traces the women she comes from. Etta, Blythe’s grandmother, lives on a farm where her first husband dies in a gruesome accident, an event that seems to push her into instability and cruelty. Etta remarries a man named Henry and has a daughter, Cecilia, but Etta is volatile, neglectful, and sometimes deliberately cruel to the little girl, leaving Cecilia with deep emotional scars. Eventually, Etta dies by suicide, hanging herself, confirming Cecilia’s sense that mothers are dangerous and unreliable.

Cecilia grows up and later meets Blythe’s father, Seb; their relationship is fragile, and she cheats on him and tries to leave, but a pregnancy changes everything. Seb insists she keep the baby, and Blythe is born into a home where her mother clearly does not want to be a mother and feels trapped by the role. Cecilia is cold, resentful, and intermittently present until she finally walks out on the family for good when Blythe is about twelve, forcing Blythe to rely on a kind neighbor, Mrs. Ellington, for the semblance of maternal warmth. These early experiences convince Blythe that motherhood is both terrifying and potentially destructive, even as a part of her still longs to “do it right” someday and redeem the line of mothers before her.
Blythe, Fox, and Violet’s early years

As a young adult, Blythe meets Fox Connor in college; he is confident, charming, and seems to offer the stability and normalcy she never had. They marry in their mid‑twenties, and Fox eagerly pushes for children, picturing a conventional, happy family, while Blythe is hesitant because of her family history but ultimately agrees, hoping to break the cycle. Her pregnancy is fraught with anxiety, and the birth of their daughter, Violet, is difficult; afterward, Blythe is plunged into exhaustion, anxiety, and what appears to be postpartum depression.

From the very first days at home, Violet cries inconsolably, and Blythe feels she cannot soothe her, which reinforces her fear that she is failing as a mother. She notices that Violet seems calmer and happier with Fox, and a painful pattern forms: Violet appears to prefer her father, turning away from Blythe, which Fox dismisses as normal baby behavior. As Violet grows into a toddler, Blythe still feels a profound lack of connection; ordinary caregiving tasks feel like battles, and Violet resists being held, cuddled, or comforted by her.

Incidents begin to accumulate that unsettle Blythe. Violet can be deliberately antagonistic toward her mother—pinching, scratching, or staring with a flat affect that Blythe interprets as hostility rather than childish misbehavior. Blythe reports these episodes to Fox, but he repeatedly insists that Blythe is overreacting or misreading normal developmental behavior because of her own trauma and insecurity. The more he dismisses her, the more Blythe begins to doubt her perceptions, even as her unease about Violet hardens into a conviction that there is something fundamentally wrong with her daughter.
Escalating incidents and the question of Violet

Once Violet begins interacting with other children, Blythe notices that she has difficulty getting along and sometimes displays aggression or a disturbing lack of empathy. At a playground, a little boy falls from a play structure and later dies, and although everyone accepts it as a tragic accident, Blythe believes she saw Violet subtly trip or push him. That moment becomes a secret pivot in Blythe’s mind: she is now convinced Violet is capable of serious harm, even though she has no proof and no one believes her.

Fox responds to Blythe’s growing anxiety by pathologizing her, attributing her suspicions to unresolved issues with her own mother and to postpartum depression. He suggests therapy and gently but firmly sides with Violet’s normalcy, reinforcing the narrative that Blythe’s perceptions cannot be trusted. Blythe becomes increasingly isolated inside her own family, acutely aware that she is the only one who sees this darker side of Violet and terrified that she is either right about her daughter or losing her mind.

In the midst of this tension, Blythe becomes pregnant again, and their son Sam is born. This time, the experience of motherhood is radically different: from birth, Blythe feels an immediate, overwhelming bond with Sam, the effortless warmth and connection she had always imagined motherhood would bring. She is attentive, affectionate, and almost obsessive in her devotion, and Sam responds with clear attachment and joy, which reassures Blythe that she can be a loving, competent mother and that her earlier struggles with Violet were not simply her own deficiency.

Even Violet seems, at least on the surface, to adore her baby brother, and the family slips into a fragile equilibrium. Yet Blythe remains on edge, scrutinizing Violet’s interactions with Sam, alert for any sign of malice; small moments—such as rough handling or a cold look—feed her fear that Violet resents him and might hurt him if given the chance.
Sam’s death and the collapse of the marriage

The central catastrophe of the novel occurs when Sam is still an infant. Blythe takes him out in his stroller on a walk, exhausted and distracted, and pauses at a street corner. In a brief, chaotic instant, the stroller rolls off the curb into traffic, and Sam is struck and killed, an event depicted as sudden, brutal, and life‑shattering. To everyone else—including Fox and the authorities—this is a heartbreaking accident attributable to a momentary lapse, but Blythe is certain she saw Violet’s small hands on the stroller, giving it a deliberate push.

Sam’s death obliterates what was left of Blythe’s mental stability. She is consumed by grief and by the conviction that Violet murdered her brother, and she cannot stop replaying the scene, wondering whether she could have prevented it. Fox, already weary of her suspicions, is horrified by the implication that his daughter is a killer and that his wife would think such a thing; instead of sharing her belief, he blames Blythe’s negligence and her fixation on Violet.

Their relationship deteriorates rapidly. Fox withdraws emotionally and physically, looks for comfort elsewhere, and eventually has an affair, which confirms Blythe’s sense that she has lost not only her son, but her husband as well. Blythe’s grief turns into a desperate quest for validation: she probes teachers, doctors, and other parents, trying to get someone to acknowledge that Violet is unusually cold, manipulative, or dangerous, but she mostly receives polite dismissal or concern for her mental health instead.

Finally, Fox leaves Blythe and initiates divorce, gaining primary custody of Violet on the grounds that Blythe’s mental instability and behavior make her an unreliable caregiver. Blythe is granted limited visitation, but their time together is strained and fraught; Violet is alternately distant and spiteful, sometimes lying about things that make Blythe look irresponsible or cruel.
Obsession with Fox’s new family

After the divorce, Fox remarries a woman named Gemma, and they build a seemingly picture‑perfect life together; Fox’s confidence in his own narrative—loving father, new start—only intensifies Blythe’s sense of exclusion and injustice. When Blythe learns Gemma is pregnant, she becomes terrified that Violet might pose a danger to this new baby, especially after what she believes happened to Sam.

Blythe starts surveilling Fox’s new family, parking outside their home and watching their routines, which deepens her status as an outsider looking in on the life she once thought would be hers. Under a false name and manufactured backstory, she orchestrates a chance meeting with Gemma and gradually befriends her, seeking proximity to Violet and to the new baby, Jet, that Gemma eventually has with Fox. As they grow closer, Blythe is torn between guilt over her manipulation and a fierce protective instinct toward Jet, whom she feels she must shield from Violet in ways she failed to shield Sam.

Eventually, Fox discovers Blythe’s deception—realizing that his ex‑wife has insinuated herself into his new wife’s life and home under false pretenses. The revelation is explosive: Gemma feels betrayed, Fox is furious and frightened, and Blythe is cast more firmly than ever as unstable and dangerous in their eyes. Even amid the fallout, Blythe seizes the chance to tell Gemma her full version of events, confessing her suspicion that Violet killed Sam and warning that Violet is capable of harming Jet as well.
Final break with Violet and the unresolved question

Back in the period when Blythe still had visitation with Violet, their interactions had grown increasingly volatile. Violet, now old enough to understand the power she wields, sometimes weaponizes half‑truths and lies to make Blythe seem reckless or abusive. In one alarming incident, Violet runs away while Blythe is responsible for her, causing a panic that further undermines Blythe’s credibility as a caretaker.

The relationship reaches a breaking point when, during a heated confrontation, Blythe grabs Violet’s arm hard enough to leave marks. Violet shows the bruises to Fox, and this becomes the final evidence he needs to cut off Blythe’s contact with their daughter entirely, citing her as a physical danger. For Blythe, this moment is doubly shattering: she has, in her own mind, crossed into the kind of physical harm she always condemned in her maternal line, and she has also given Fox and the courts tangible proof that she is the problem, not Violet.

In the novel’s present, as Blythe narrates the story to Fox, she continues to watch his house, fixated on the safety of Jet and on the unresolved mystery of who—or what—Violet truly is. The book never offers a definitive, objective answer about Violet’s guilt or innocence; instead, it leaves readers suspended between two possibilities: that Violet really is a budding sociopath responsible for the deaths of Sam and the boy at the playground, or that Blythe’s trauma and mental illness have warped her perception so badly that she has misread ordinary childhood behavior as monstrous.

This ambiguity is the core of the novel’s psychological horror. Blythe fears she has inherited an “empathy deficit” that runs through the women in her family, but she also worries that Violet might be the next link in that chain. The story closes with Blythe still speaking to Fox—still begging, in effect, to be believed—while readers are left to judge whether the true danger lies in Violet’s nature, in Blythe’s mind, or in the collision of both within a family that never had a secure foundation to begin with.
