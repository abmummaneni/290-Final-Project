FULL EPISODE SYNOPSIS — SPOILERS INCLUDED

--- BACKGROUND AND CONTEXT ---

"The Wolvercote Tongue" is a Season 2 episode of Inspector Morse, first
broadcast on ITV on December 27, 1987. It is directed by Alastair Reid —
who also directed "The Dead of Jericho" — and is based on Colin Dexter's
short story of the same name, expanded substantially for television. It stars
John Thaw as Morse and Kevin Whately as Lewis.

The episode is one of the most lavishly produced early Morse entries, with
an American tourist group providing a varied cast of suspects and the Oxford
museum world providing a distinctive and visually rich backdrop. It is also
notable for a plot involving a significant historical artifact — the Wolvercote
Tongue, a piece of Saxon jewelry — and for the specific combination of American
tourism, British academia, and murder that gives the episode its particular flavor.

--- THE WOLVERCOTE TONGUE ---

The Wolvercote Tongue is a piece of early medieval Saxon jewelry — a clasp
or brooch in the form of a tongue, made of gold and precious stones, of
significant historical and monetary value. It was separated from its matching
belt buckle centuries ago, and the buckle is held by the Ashmolean Museum
in Oxford. The Tongue, which has been in private ownership, has been donated
to the Ashmolean by its current owner — an elderly American woman named Sheila
Dobrowski — who is bringing it to Oxford specifically to make this donation
and to see the two pieces reunited.

This reunion is a significant cultural event for the Ashmolean and for the
wider community of Anglo-Saxon art scholarship. Sheila Dobrowski is traveling
to Oxford with an American tourist group, accompanied by her husband Howard.

--- SHEILA DOBROWSKI'S DEATH ---

The tourist group arrives at the Randolph Hotel in Oxford. Almost immediately,
Sheila Dobrowski dies — she collapses in the hotel and is found dead. Her
death is initially attributed to a heart attack: she was elderly, she had
traveled, the stress of the trip could explain a cardiac event.

Morse, called to the scene, is not satisfied with this explanation. Something
about the circumstances — Sheila's specific condition when found, or something
about the manner of her death — raises his suspicion. He suspects she was
poisoned.

The Wolvercote Tongue, meanwhile, has vanished. The jewel that Sheila was
carrying — the entire purpose of her Oxford visit — has been stolen either
before or after her death.

--- THE AMERICAN TOURIST GROUP ---

The tourist group that traveled with the Dobrowiski becomes the pool of suspects
and witnesses. Morse and Lewis interview the group members at the Randolph Hotel —
an investigation that requires particular sensitivity given that they are
American tourists who were not expecting a murder investigation on their Oxford
holiday.

The group includes:

Howard Dobrowski — Sheila's husband, now widowed, who is cooperative but
clearly managing complex emotions.

Janet Roscoe — a widow traveling alone, who had an apparent friendship with
the Dobrowiski.

Phil Aldrich — a middle-aged American man with a specific past connection
to Howard Dobrowski.

Cedric Downes — not an American but an English man associated with the tour
group, whose expertise in medieval art and his connection to the Ashmolean
are both relevant.

Various other tour members who provide witness testimony about the group's
movements and dynamics.

--- MORSE'S OXFORD ---

The episode uses Oxford with the full visual richness that the series would
become known for. The Ashmolean Museum — one of the world's oldest public
museums, holding a magnificent collection of art and antiquities — is central
to the plot and is rendered beautifully. The Randolph Hotel, the city's
colleges, the punts on the river, and the specific atmosphere of Oxford in
the late autumn are all present.

Morse's specific relationship to Oxford — his love of the city, his knowledge
of its institutions and their politics, his complicated position as a man of
considerable culture who never quite fit the university world whose city he
inhabits — is given expression through the museum setting and his specific
engagement with the academic figures involved.

--- THE POISON ESTABLISHED ---

The medical examination of Sheila Dobrowski confirms Morse's suspicion: she
was poisoned. The specific substance used and the mechanism of its introduction —
how the poison was administered at or before her collapse — are established
through forensic examination and provide the first concrete evidence that her
death was deliberate.

The poisoning required someone with access to Sheila at a specific moment —
at the hotel, on the tour, or in some other context during the trip. This
narrows the field of suspects to people who were physically near her at the
relevant time.

--- THE WOLVERCOTE TONGUE: ITS HISTORY AND VALUE ---

The investigation of the stolen jewel runs parallel to the murder investigation.
The Tongue's value — both monetary and historical — is sufficient to motivate
theft. But its theft in connection with Sheila's death suggests a more specific
relationship between the jewel and the motive for murder: whoever wanted the
Tongue wanted Sheila dead as part of acquiring it, or Sheila's death was
intended to cover the theft.

The Tongue's provenance — how it came to be in Sheila Dobrowski's possession,
what the chain of ownership had been, and whether there were competing claims
on it — is investigated by Morse through the Ashmolean's records and through
the network of art historians and dealers connected to Saxon antiquities.

A specific competing claim on the Tongue — a person or institution with a
grievance about the Dobrowski ownership — is one avenue the investigation
pursues.

--- CONNECTIONS WITHIN THE GROUP ---

As Morse investigates the tour group, the connections between its members
that predate the Oxford trip become apparent. Phil Aldrich's history with
Howard Dobrowski is significant: there is a past relationship or a past business
dealing between them that is not simply casual acquaintance. The specific nature
of this history — what they shared, what went wrong, what the current state
of the relationship is — is part of what the investigation reveals.

Janet Roscoe's relationship to the Dobrowiski is also not what it initially
appears: her friendship with Sheila was more complicated, and her specific
knowledge of the Tongue and its value is greater than an ordinary tourist friend
would have.

Cedric Downes's expertise and his Ashmolean connections put him in a specific
position of knowledge about the Tongue — and therefore of potential interest
in possessing it.

--- THE KILLER IDENTIFIED ---

Morse assembles the complete picture: the motive for Sheila's death, the
specific person who had access to the poison and the opportunity to administer
it, and the connection between the murder and the theft of the Tongue.

The killer is one of the people in Sheila's immediate circle — someone whose
specific relationship to the Tongue, either through direct financial interest
or through a past dealing with the Dobrowskis, gave them a reason to want
Sheila dead before the donation was completed. The theft of the Tongue and
the murder are connected: the killer intended both, killing Sheila to take the
Tongue before it reached the Ashmolean.

--- THE WOLVERCOTE TONGUE RECOVERED ---

The stolen jewel is located through the investigation of the killer's movements
and their plan for disposing of or selling it. The recovery of the Tongue is
handled in the episode's final act alongside the confrontation with the killer.

The Ashmolean eventually receives both pieces — the Tongue reunited with the
buckle as Sheila intended, her donation fulfilled despite her murder. This
resolution has a quiet poignancy: the dead woman's wish is honored even
though she was killed to prevent it.

--- RESOLUTION ---

The killer is identified, confronted by Morse, and arrested. The murder of
Sheila Dobrowski is solved. The tourist group returns to America, diminished
by what happened in Oxford.

Morse closes the case with his standard mixture of professional satisfaction
and personal melancholy. He has solved the crime. He could not prevent it.
And the city he loves, which presented its most beautiful face to a group of
American visitors, showed them something else underneath.

--- NOTES ON THE EPISODE ---

"The Wolvercote Tongue" is one of the most visually sumptuous early Morse
episodes, using the Ashmolean and Oxford's broader museum and academic world
with genuine appreciation. The Saxon artifact at the center of the plot is
a real category of object — the series did its research — and the combination
of medieval history, contemporary crime, and Oxford atmosphere is handled with
the assurance that would become the series' hallmark. The American tourist group
provides a variety of suspects and a cultural contrast that gives the episode
a different texture from the purely Oxford-centric entries.
