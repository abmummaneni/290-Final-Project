A Better Man by Louise Penny is the 15th book in the Chief Inspector Armand Gamache series and centers on a missing pregnant woman, a devastating spring flood, and the toxic tide of online “cancel culture” that targets both Gamache and the artist Clara Morrow. Everything unfolds under the pressure of rising water and breaking trust. Below is a long, detailed, spoiler‑filled plot synopsis told in clear chronological order, including the full explanation of the killer’s identity, how the murder is solved, and the emotional fallout for the main characters.
Gamache back at the Sûreté

The novel opens in spring, with Armand Gamache returning to the Sûreté du Québec as Chief Inspector of the homicide department, a position he had years earlier and now shares (awkwardly) with his former second‑in‑command, Jean‑Guy Beauvoir, who is about to leave the force for a job in Paris.
Gamache is also under attack from a virulent social media campaign accusing him of misconduct and corruption, part of a broader “cancel culture” theme that runs through the book. The hostility is orchestrated, not random, and it makes his professional life even more fragile.
The pregnant woman goes missing

In the midst of this, Vivienne Godin, a pregnant woman in her mid‑twenties, disappears from her isolated farmhouse in the Eastern Townships of Québec. Her father, Homer Godin, a emotionally volatile man, arrives at the Sûreté headquarters in Montréal begging Gamache to find her, convinced that her husband, Carl Tracey, has killed her.
Tracey is a sculptor and potter who has a documented history of domestic violence; he has been accused of abusing Vivienne before, and local Sûreté agent Bob Cameron has even threatened him in anger, an incident Gamache later reprimands him for.
The flooded Rivière Bella Bella

At the same time, Québec is facing catastrophic spring flooding as the snowmelt swells the rivers far beyond their usual limits. The Rivière Bella Bella, which runs near Three Pines, threatens to breach its banks and flood the village. The provincial government, under the leadership of the Deputy Premier and Chief Superintendent Madeleine Toussaint, rejects Gamache’s proposed flood‑mitigation strategy, leaving the villagers and the Sûreté teams to improvise.
In Three Pines, people are busy building sandbag walls and preparing for the worst, while Gamache is pulled in both directions: the case of Vivienne and the looming flood disaster.
The tense visit to the Tracey farm

Gamache and Sûreté agent Lysette Cloutier travel to the Tracey farm, a remote, squalid property where the river is already beginning to rise. Tracey is openly hostile, confronting them with a pitchfork and refusing to let them in easily.
Gamache calmly defuses the confrontation, uses Tracey’s own phone to call for a search warrant, and then inspects the farmhouse. The interior is filthy and neglected, and the search turns up a recently used kiln in Tracey’s pottery studio but no body and no clear evidence that Vivienne is dead.

Before leaving, Gamache buys Fred, Vivienne’s old dog, whom Tracey was planning to shoot, and takes the animal with him. The gesture is small but charged with compassion.
Vivienne’s body is found in the river

As the flood emergency worsens, the provincial government declares a state of emergency. Gamache is called away to a crisis meeting in Montréal, where his practical suggestions for managing the flood are again dismissed by Toussaint and the Deputy Premier.
Back in the Eastern Townships, the Sûreté team, with local help, decides to dig a diversionary trench on Tracey’s upstream land to try to reduce pressure on the river. While they are digging, Tracey shows up again with a rifle, furious at the damage to his property, but Beauvoir disarms him.

The backhoe then uncovers Vivienne’s duffel bag, buried near the riverbank. The bag contains her personal belongings, and the discovery prompts the team to search a nearby abandoned logging road that leads to a broken, disused bridge.
They find Vivienne’s car abandoned near the bridge and then, farther downriver, they discover Vivienne’s body caught in a jam of ice and debris. Her death is clearly not a simple drowning: the river has concealed a murder.
The investigation points to Carl Tracey

Autopsy and forensic work quickly show that Vivienne was murdered, not simply drowned. The baby girl is still with her, and the body is badly damaged by the river, but enough evidence survives to prove foul play.
Tracey is the prime suspect: he is the abusive husband, the baby is his, and he has no alibi for the night Vivienne disappeared. The emotional and circumstantial evidence all point his way. Gamache, Beauvoir, and Isabelle Lacoste push the case hard, hoping to secure a conviction before the flood and the political chaos overwhelm them.
The social‑media scandal and the undercover Instagram operation

Parallel to the murder case, the social‑media campaign against Gamache intensifies. The trolling overlaps with a related scandal involving Clara Morrow, an artist and resident of Three Pines, whose latest work has drawn vicious online attacks from critics and strangers.
Clara, whose exhibition Gaga has generated both praise and derision, has also invited a famously reclusive art critic, Dominica Oddly, to Three Pines, hoping for a legitimate review rather than more anonymous hate. Oddly’s arrival forces Clara to confront painful truths about her art and her self‑worth, and the whole episode mirrors the novel’s theme of being “seen” and judged in the harsh light of the internet.

In the murder investigation, Lysette Cloutier, under Lacoste’s mentorship, goes undercover online. She creates a fake profile posing as a gallery owner and contacts Tracey’s social‑media manager and mistress, Pauline Vachon, a woman who runs his online presence and also has a sexual relationship with him.
By accessing their private Instagram messages, Cloutier finds evidence that Tracey is having an affair with Pauline and that they discuss Vivienne in callous, exploitative terms. The evidence suggests that Tracey planned to rid himself of Vivienne and her pregnancy, strengthening the case against him.
The case collapses in court

Tracey is arrested and arraigned. However, at the hearing, the case falls apart when the judge rules key pieces of evidence inadmissible. The judge declares that the contents of Vivienne’s duffel bag are “fruit of the poisonous tree” because the search was not properly warranted, and the undercover Instagram operation that Cloutier ran is deemed an illegal search.
The incriminating texts from Pauline Vachon are thrown out, and Tracey walks free. The collapse of the case is a devastating blow not only to the investigation but to Vivienne’s father, Homer Godin, who has been watching the proceedings in a state of barely controlled rage.
Homer’s unraveling and the setup at the Bella Bella

Gamache understands that Homer is a danger to Tracey and arrests Homer after he accidentally strikes Gamache’s wife, Reine‑Marie, in a moment of confusion. The arrest is partly a protective move: keeping Homer away from Tracey while the case is reassessed.
Homer is released when the threat is judged to have passed, but his grief and anger never subside. Meanwhile, the flood continues to menace Three Pines, and the river now becomes both a literal and symbolic boundary between justice and revenge.

Gamache, Beauvoir, and the team begin to re‑examine the admissible evidence more closely. The autopsy and the spore evidence from the bridge railing hint at something more complicated than a simple push‑off‑the‑bridge scenario. Tracey’s behavior during the trial and his lingering love for his daughter, the baby, suggest that he may not be the cold‑blooded murderer the case initially painted him as.
The final confrontation at the river

The tension peaks when Homer and Tracey are brought face to face again, this time at the swollen Rivière Bella Bella, where the river is literally breaking its banks. Homer, in a fit of rage and guilt, tries to kill Tracey, lunging at him with a knife or similar weapon.
To stop the killing, Gamache lies and tells Homer that Tracey is innocent, which momentarily halts the attack. Then, in a moment of twisted self‑sacrifice, Cloutier falsely confesses to the murder of Vivienne, claiming she did it to protect her career or to stop Tracey from abusing Vivienne, thereby throwing herself between Homer and his target.

As Tracey struggles to rise, Homer stumbles backward, loses his footing, and falls into the freezing, churning river. Beauvoir dives in after him, but the current pulls them both under. Gamache, clinging to a wooden post, grabs Beauvoir’s hand, but his grip fails. He throws Beauvoir toward the shore, hoping to save him, and Beauvoir is dragged partway out before being rescued by Bob Cameron, who arrives at the last second.
Homer, however, is swept away and drowns.
The true killer revealed: Homer Godin

In the aftermath, the team pieces together the final truth. Homer Godin is the actual murderer of Vivienne. The revelation is shocking but carefully foreshadowed: Homer had been abusing Vivienne since childhood, and she had begun to extort money from him as a way to escape her abusive husband, Tracey.
Vivienne had arranged to meet Homer on the bridge late at night to confront him, demand more money, and force him to help her leave Tracey. During the argument, Homer, overwhelmed by rage and shame, pushed her off the bridge into the river, where she was swept away and killed.

His intense grief and his hatred for Tracey were real, but they were also projections of his own guilt. By blaming Tracey, Homer had tried to rewrite his own role from abuser and killer to wronged father.
The damage to the characters and personal lives

For Gamache, the case is a moral and emotional ordeal. The collapse of the prosecution against Tracey, the wrongful suspicion of Cloutier, and the death of Homer weigh heavily on him. The case also forces him to confront how he and Beauvoir would have reacted if their own daughter, Annie, had been murdered—a frighteningly personal mirror to the Godin‑Tracey disaster.
Clara Morrow, meanwhile, survives the online storm, but the experience leaves her shaken and more vulnerable. Her invitation to the critic Dominica Oddly ultimately leads to a hard but honest review that forces her to accept her flaws and her strengths as an artist.
The Beauvoir family’s departure and Lacoste’s rise

Beauvoir, Annie, and their son, Honoré, leave for Paris at the end of the novel, marking a major shift in the series. Beauvoir has written Gamache a letter recommending that Isabelle Lacoste replace him as Gamache’s second‑in‑command, a role she has long earned.
Chief Superintendent Toussaint, worried that Gamache is consolidating too much loyal power around him, tries to force Lacoste into early retirement because of an old injury. Lacoste, however, counters by using a video of the river rescue—showing Gamache’s desperate, self‑sacrificing actions—as leverage to protect her position and to remind the higher‑ups of the human cost of their political games.

A Better Man closes on a bittersweet note: the floods are contained, the murder is solved, but the personal costs are high—Homer dead, Beauvoir gone, and the internet’s cruelty still simmering. The title reflects the novel’s central question: in a world of easy rage and opaque motives, can anyone become a “better man,” or at least try, even when the system and the world push them toward vengeance instead of justice?
