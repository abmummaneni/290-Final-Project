--- BACKGROUND AND CONTEXT ---

"Mrs McGinty's Dead" is a feature-length episode of ITV's Agatha Christie's
Poirot, first broadcast in February 2008. It is directed by David Moore and
stars David Suchet as Hercule Poirot, with Zoë Wanamaker returning as Ariadne
Oliver. It is based on Christie's 1952 novel of the same name — a Poirot novel
that Christie herself considered one of her better-constructed works, notable
for its specific use of the "old newspaper photograph" as the investigative
pivot, for the welcome presence of Ariadne Oliver as Poirot's comic foil, and
for a solution whose specific logic is both technically clever and genuinely
surprising.

The episode belongs to the period of the Suchet series in which Ariadne Oliver
was most frequently present — her partnership with Poirot is one of the series'
most consistent pleasures in the later seasons, and "Mrs McGinty's Dead" gives
her one of her most active roles: not simply providing comic counterpoint but
contributing specific investigative intelligence that is directly relevant to
the solution.

The novel and adaptation share a specific structural feature: the case Poirot
is investigating is not the original crime but the possibility that the wrong
person has been convicted of it. Superintendent Spence, a police officer who
had confidence in Poirot's abilities, contacts him with a specific concern:
a man named James Bentley has been convicted of the murder of his landlady,
Mrs McGinty, and is facing execution — and Spence, who conducted the original
investigation, cannot quite suppress the specific unease of someone who is not
entirely certain the right man was convicted. He asks Poirot to look at it.

--- THE ORIGINAL CRIME: MRS MCGINTY'S DEATH ---

Mrs McGinty was a charwoman — a cleaning woman who worked in various households
in the village of Broadhinny — who was found dead in her cottage, killed by
a blow to the head. The murder was followed by a robbery: a small amount of
money that Mrs McGinty was known to keep in her cottage was taken. The case
against James Bentley, her lodger, was circumstantial but sufficient: he had
the opportunity, the money was gone, and his account of the evening was not
entirely satisfactory.

Bentley is not an impressive figure. He is meek, uncertain, and entirely
incapable of presenting himself effectively in his own defence. He was convicted
and is now in prison awaiting his fate.

Poirot goes to Broadhinny to investigate, staying at the local guest house
run by the Summerhayes — a chaotic couple whose specific domestic management
style is a consistent source of comedy and mild suffering for Poirot, who has
strong feelings about his food and his comfort.

James Bentley is cleared and freed. Ariadne Oliver returns to her uneasy
collaboration, and to her deeply held convictions about what creative adapters
do to the work of their source authors.

--- NOTES ON THE EPISODE ---

"Mrs McGinty's Dead" is a strong entry in the later Suchet period, particularly
notable for the specific pleasure of the Poirot-Oliver dynamic in full operation.
The newspaper photograph as investigative pivot — the specific mechanism by
which an old crime reaches into the present through a recognise face — is one
of Christie's most elegant recurring devices, and it is deployed here with
real precision. The solution's specific moral weight — an innocent man was
convicted, a murderer has been living a new life for decades — is handled with
the seriousness the situation requires alongside the comedy that the Oliver
presence and the Summerhayes household consistently provide.
