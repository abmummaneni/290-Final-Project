A Line to Kill by Anthony Horowitz is the third book in the “Hawthorne & Horowitz” series and is narrated in the first person by the fictionalized version of the author, Anthony Horowitz, who is shadowing retired detective‑turned‑writer Daniel Hawthorne on what they suppose will be a routine publicity tour. Instead, the pair land on the tiny Channel Island of Alderney for a small literary festival, where a local power‑line controversy and a pair of brutal murders quickly turn the idyll into a locked‑island mystery. Below is a long, detailed, spoiler‑filled plot synopsis told in chronological order, covering the full story, the killer’s identity, and how the mystery is solved.
The invitation to Alderney

The novel opens with Anthony Horowitz meeting his publisher to discuss a publicity tour for the books he has already written about retired Detective Inspector Daniel Hawthorne. The tour will include a minor literary festival on the island of Alderney, off the south coast of England, hosted by a wealthy local businessman, Charles le Mesurier, and his wife, Helen.

Anthony is lukewarm about the trip; Hawthorne, however, is oddly eager, and it’s only later we learn this is because of a secret connection to the island that he has kept hidden from Anthony. The festival is also being sponsored by Charles’s online gambling company, “Spin‑the‑Wheel,” which is gambling on the festival’s success both figuratively and literally.
The atmosphere on the island

On Alderney, the atmosphere is tense. The island is in fierce dispute over a proposed high‑voltage power line that would cut through its countryside, desecrating a local war cemetery and dividing neighbor from neighbor. The power‑line controversy has turned the community into a powder‑keg of suspicion, protest, and threats.

The literary festival itself is a strange, almost eccentric gathering: attendees include a blind medium, a French performance poet, a celebrity chef, and a handful of crime writers and journalists. Horowitz finds the whole thing faintly absurd, but Hawthorne, as usual, is quietly observant and personally detached.
The murder of Charles le Mesurier

The first murder happens early. After a late‑night party at the le Mesurier home, where Charles has been loudly pushing for the power line and clashing with opponents, the next morning Charles is found dead in his study. He has been stabbed to death, and his body is arranged in a peculiar way: he is taped to a chair, with only his right hand left free, as if he has been immobilized and forced to “sign” something against his will.

The island has no proper police force, so the local assistant‑police‑officer hands over the investigation to Hawthorne, who is the only ex‑DI in the room. The island is effectively “locked down” in the sense that the ferry schedules and the small size of the population make it hard for anyone to leave without being noticed, turning the case into a classic closed‑circumstances mystery.
The second murder and the hunt for suspects

The first victim is soon followed by a second: Helen le Mesurier, Charles’s wife, is found dead in one of the island’s many sea caves, her head smashed with a rock. The brutal double‑death shocks the festival‑guests and the islanders, and the potential pool of suspects is substantial:

    The le Mesuriers’ immediate circle (friends, associates, and employees).

    Angry locals opposed to the power line, including protesters and families with deep ties to the affected land.

    The writers and festival guests, several of whom have hidden agendas or secret connections to the island.

Horowitz, narrating the action, begins to assemble a rogues’ gallery of possible culprits, many of whom he describes in his wry, self‑mocking style. The key suspects include a fired employee, a local opponent of the power line, and members of a couple who have come to the festival under false identities and are running from their own past.
The child‑pornographer subplot and Hawthorne’s private motive

In parallel with the murders, Horowitz slowly uncovers Hawthorne’s secret reason for agreeing to come to Alderney: the island is home to Derek Abbott, a man Hawthorne once investigated years earlier for possessing child pornography and other sex‑related crimes. Abbott had a suspicious fall down a flight of stairs while in custody with Hawthorne, which left him permanently injured, and he has long accused Hawthorne of pushing him.

Horowitz becomes convinced that Hawthorne is here partly to confront Abbott, and possibly even to exact a kind of personal justice, which makes him question his mentor’s professional detachment. The Abbott subplot adds a layer of moral ambiguity: Hawthorne is not purely an impartial detective; he carries his own grudges and ghosts.
The mounting pressure and the “suicide”

The investigation gains momentum when the police arrest Derek Abbott for the murders, and he later appears to commit suicide by leaping off a cliff. A note he leaves behind looks like a confession, and the authorities are inclined to close the case.

Hawthorne, however, is not satisfied. He knows Abbott has a violent past, but the timing and the staging of the supposed confession feel wrong. Using a mix of psychological insight and physical evidence, he begins to pick apart the “suicide” story and the supposed suicide‑letter, deducing that the note was written earlier, under coercion, and then planted.
The real killers and the gambling motive

The full truth turns out to be far more intimate and personal than the power‑line dispute suggests. The real killers are Anne and Kathryn, a mother and daughter local to the island, who are not part of the big‑money, power‑line politics but are deeply damaged by Charles le Mesurier’s online gambling business.​​

Their son, William, was a chronic gambler who poured his life savings into “Spin‑the‑Wheel,” Charles’s online casino. He ultimately killed himself because he was financially ruined and ashamed, and the family blamed Charles and the company for pushing their son over the edge.

Anne and Kathryn decide to make Charles and Helen pay. They kill Charles first, staging the chair‑and‑tape scene to symbolize the way he had metaphorically “trapped” their son with gambling. Then they kill Helen, who had modeled for the online gambling site, as a way of punishing the face of the brand that lured so many young men, including William, into self‑destruction.
How the murders are unraveled

Hawthorne pieces it together by noticing behavioral inconsistencies, financial traces, and the mismatch between the “suicide” narrative and the physical facts. He traces the involvement of Anne and Kathryn through:

    The gambling‑loss records and the family’s desperation.

    The psychological pattern of the killings, which fit a personal vendetta more than a random or political one.

    The coercion of Abbott and the plant of the note, which points toward someone who knew both the island and the business intimately.

When Hawthorne confronts them, the women do not deny the killings. Anne, in fact, presents a letter in which she claims she is dying of a heart condition and minimizes Kathryn’s role, hoping to shield her daughter. Kathryn is eventually tried, but the jury, moved by the tragic backstory of William’s suicide and the family’s suffering, finds her not guilty of the murders.​​
The aftereffects and the closing tone

The case ends with a morally ambiguous sense of “justice.” The real killers are exposed, but because of public sympathy and the tragic circumstances, the legal system does not fully punish them. The wider power‑line dispute continues, unresolved, and the island remains divided between progress and preservation.

For Hawthorne and Horowitz, the experience underscores how entangled personal pain and public spectacle can be: a festival that was supposed to be about books becomes a stage for a family’s revenge. Anthony, narrator and writer, is left wondering how to turn the messy, incomplete outcome of the case into a satisfying narrative—exactly the kind of meta‑commentary on mystery writing that defines the series.
