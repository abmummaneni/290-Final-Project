--- BACKGROUND AND CONTEXT ---

"The Conspirators" is a Season 7 episode of Columbo, airing May 13, 1978.
It is directed by Leo Penn (father of Sean Penn) and stars Clive Revill as
Joe Devlin — the last episode of the classic original run of Columbo before
the series went on its long hiatus and was revived in the 1980s and 1990s.
The episode is set in the world of Irish-American politics and the funding
networks for Irish Republican causes — a topic with particular contemporary
resonance in 1978, as the Troubles in Northern Ireland were at their most
intense.

The episode is notable for its political subject matter — Columbo rarely engaged
directly with contemporary political conflicts — and for the moral complexity
that the Irish Republican context introduces. Devlin is not simply a murderer
protecting personal interests but a man who believes, genuinely and passionately,
in a political cause, and who has committed murder in the service of that cause.

--- THE KILLER, THE VICTIM, AND THE MOTIVE ---

Joe Devlin is a charming, gregarious, celebrated Irish-American poet and public
intellectual — a man whose facility with language and whose Irish heritage make
him a beloved figure at the center of Irish-American cultural life in Los Angeles.
He is also a weapons buyer: secretly, he has been using his public persona and
his networks to raise money for and purchase weapons for IRA-connected groups
in Northern Ireland. The poetry and the charm are real. So is the gun-running.

Devlin's contact in the weapons-supply chain, a man named Vincent Conroy,
has become a liability. Conroy is greedy or unstable or threatening to expose
the operation — the specific nature of his threat to Devlin is that he will
blow open the arms supply network if Devlin does not meet his demands. This
would destroy Devlin's public persona, expose the network, and potentially
result in serious criminal charges.

Devlin kills Conroy. The killing is accomplished in a way that Devlin has
planned to be deniable: he uses a gun connected to the arms supply network,
and he establishes an alibi through a public event — a poetry reading or social
engagement — that places him elsewhere at the time of death.

--- THE ARMS SUPPLY CONTEXT ---

The episode handles its political subject matter with some care. Devlin's belief
in the Irish cause is presented as genuine — he is not cynically exploiting
Irish-American sentiment but is a true believer who has been willing to risk
everything for what he sees as a just war. This does not excuse the murder,
but it gives him a moral complexity that purely self-interested killers lack.

The arms supply network — the specific mechanism by which money raised in the
United States found its way to weapons purchases for the conflict in Northern
Ireland — is the background that Columbo must navigate in investigating the
killing. This is politically sensitive territory, and the episode is aware of it.

--- COLUMBO AND DEVLIN ---

Columbo meets Devlin at an Irish-American cultural event, and the dynamic
between them is one of the series' warmest: Columbo genuinely loves Devlin's
poetry, genuinely responds to the man's wit and warmth, and finds himself in
the position he most dislikes — liking someone he suspects of murder.

Devlin, for his part, is one of the most actively charming of all Columbo's
antagonists. He is a professional verbal performer — he talks for a living,
he knows how to make people feel seen and heard, and he deploys this skill
on Columbo with full awareness of what he is doing. He tells Columbo Irish
stories, recites poetry for him, shares whiskey with him. He is managing
Columbo through charm, which is what he does with everyone.

Columbo allows himself to be charmed while continuing to look for what he knows
is there. The warmth between them is real even as the investigation proceeds.

--- INVESTIGATING THE WEAPONS NETWORK ---

Columbo's investigation requires him to understand the arms supply network —
not simply to identify Devlin as Conroy's killer but to establish the specific
connection between Devlin and the weapons purchase that created the situation
Conroy was threatening to expose.

This investigative thread takes Columbo through the financial networks of the
Irish-American community, the specific money flows that supported the arms
purchases, and the people — some of them well-meaning and politically committed —
who contributed without fully understanding what their money was being used for.

The investigation is delicate: Columbo is not trying to prosecute the Irish
cause but to solve a murder, and the distinction between these two things must
be maintained throughout. His focus is on Devlin and Conroy, not on the broader
political network.

--- THE WHISKEY ---

One of the episode's most celebrated elements is the whiskey motif. Devlin
and Columbo drink whiskey together in their meetings — it is part of the warmth
and the Irish hospitality that characterizes Devlin's management of Columbo.
But the whiskey becomes relevant to the investigation: something about the
specific whiskey, or about how and when it is consumed, or about what it
tells Columbo about Devlin's movements and habits, is part of the evidence
that builds the case.

The specific detail varies in different accounts of the episode, but the whiskey
is both a social lubricant and a clue — the same object serving two completely
different functions simultaneously, which is very characteristic of how Columbo
works.

--- THE ALIBI DISMANTLED ---

Devlin's alibi — the public event at which he was present during Conroy's
killing — is examined by Columbo with the standard investigative tools: the
specific timing, what different witnesses saw, whether the window available
between the event and Conroy's death is consistent with the distance and the
means.

Something in the alibi's construction, or something about Devlin's specific
movements in the critical window, is inconsistent. The discrepancy is small
but Columbo — as always — does not let small discrepancies go.

--- THE FINAL CONFRONTATION ---

The episode's closing confrontation takes place in the context of the Irish-
American world Devlin inhabits — at a cultural event, or at his home, or in
a setting that reflects the specific world the investigation has moved through.
Columbo presents the full case: the connection to Conroy, the arms supply
network, the specific evidence that dismantles the alibi.

Devlin's response is consistent with his character: he does not dissolve or
confess immediately but meets Columbo's revelation with the verbal fluency
that defines him. The confrontation has more dignity on both sides than many
Columbo endings — two men who genuinely like each other, meeting across an
unbridgeable divide.

--- RESOLUTION ---

Joe Devlin is arrested for the murder of Vincent Conroy. The arms supply network
is exposed as part of the investigation's fallout, though the episode's primary
concern is the murder rather than the political prosecution.

The episode ends the original classic run of Columbo on a note that is
characteristic of the series at its best: morally complex, emotionally warm
even in the moment of arrest, and deeply attentive to the human quality of
both the detective and the killer he has caught.

--- NOTES ON THE EPISODE ---

"The Conspirators" is a fitting conclusion to the original Columbo run. Its
political subject matter — Irish Republican gun-running in 1978 — gives it
a specificity and a contemporary urgency that most Columbo episodes avoid.
Clive Revill's Devlin is among the most sympathetically drawn of all Columbo
killers: a man of genuine gifts and genuine conviction who has done a terrible
thing in the service of a cause he believes in absolutely. The warmth between
Columbo and Devlin throughout the episode makes the final arrest feel like a
genuine loss rather than a simple restoration of order.
