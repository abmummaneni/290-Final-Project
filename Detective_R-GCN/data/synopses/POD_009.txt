THE SHOW: CASEFILE

Casefile True Crime is an Australian true crime podcast with a distinctive characteristic:
its host is anonymous. He is known only as "The Host" or "Casey," and his decision not
to reveal his identity has been maintained consistently throughout the show's production.
His voice — Australian-accented, measured, and deliberately unemotional — is immediately
recognizable to regular listeners, and the anonymity has become part of the show's identity
rather than a gimmick.

Casefile's approach is forensic and factual: it does not editorialize heavily, it does
not insert personal narrative, and it presents case details with a level of specificity
and documentation that distinguishes it from more personality-driven true crime shows.
It is one of the most rigorously researched major true crime podcasts, and its episodes
on the Golden State Killer are among its most sustained and detailed productions.

THE GOLDEN STATE KILLER: BACKGROUND


The Scale of the Crimes

The Golden State Killer is the name applied to a serial offender responsible for one
of the most prolific and wide-ranging series of violent crimes in American history.
Between approximately 1973 and 1986, the same individual committed at least 13 murders,
more than 50 rapes, and more than 100 residential burglaries across California. He
operated under multiple names given to him by law enforcement and the public at different
periods: the Visalia Ransacker, the East Area Rapist, and the Original Night Stalker,
before DNA evidence eventually linked all three series of crimes to a single perpetrator,
who was given the composite name Golden State Killer.

The crimes spanned more than a decade and multiple California jurisdictions. Their
geographic range, the time between them, and the pre-DNA-era limitations of forensic
linking meant that investigators in different regions were often working their cases
in isolation without awareness that they were pursuing the same offender.


The Visalia Ransacker (1973-1975)

The earliest crimes attributed to the GSK occurred in Visalia, California, in the San
Joaquin Valley. Over approximately two years, hundreds of residential burglaries were
committed in Visalia, typically at night, by someone who entered homes quietly, rummaged
through personal belongings, stole small items, and left without confrontation. The
burglaries had a quality of personal intrusion — the items stolen were often intimate
(jewelry, photographs, items of personal significance) rather than high-value electronics
or cash — that suggested psychological motivation beyond financial gain.

In September 1975, the Visalia Ransacker murdered Claude Snelling, a journalism professor,
when Snelling confronted him attempting to abduct his daughter. This was the first
attributed murder and marked an escalation in the crimes' seriousness.


The East Area Rapist (1976-1979)

Beginning in 1976, a series of rapes began in the Sacramento area and subsequently spread
across Northern California. The offender — designated the East Area Rapist by Sacramento
investigators — committed at least 50 rapes over approximately three years, with a
highly consistent and distinctive modus operandi:

He conducted extensive pre-offense surveillance of his targets, studying their routines,
mapping their neighborhoods, and learning the specific layout of their homes. He struck
at night, almost always when couples were home together. He bound the male victim
using shoelaces or ligatures he brought with him. He terrorized both victims verbally,
placing dishes or similar objects on the male victim's back and threatening to kill the
female victim if he heard them move. He would then return to assault the female victim
repeatedly over extended periods.

The level of planning, the consistency of method across dozens of attacks, and the
geographical knowledge he demonstrated all pointed to an organized, methodical offender
who lived in or was thoroughly familiar with the areas he targeted. He listened to
police scanners. He called some victims afterward, taunting them or breathing silently.
He evaded an enormous law enforcement effort involving multiple agencies despite being
glimpsed by neighbors and witnesses on several occasions.

Physical descriptions of the suspect were compiled across multiple incidents: white male,
medium build, wearing a ski mask. DNA evidence was collected from multiple scenes.
He was never identified during the active period of these crimes.


The Original Night Stalker (1979-1986)

Beginning in 1979, the crimes shifted southward and in character. In Southern California —
Santa Barbara, Ventura, Goleta, Dana Point, Irvine — a series of murders began. The
offender (designated the Original Night Stalker, later Distinguished from Richard Ramirez
who was also known as the Night Stalker) targeted couples in their homes at night,
killing both the male and female victims. The murders showed the same evidence of
pre-offense surveillance and the same highly organized methodology as the East Area
Rapes.

The victims in Southern California included Robert Offerman and Debra Alexander (1979);
Lyman and Charlene Smith (1980); Keith Harrington and Patrice Harrington (1980);
Manuela Witthuhn (1981); Cheri Domingo and Gregory Sanchez (1981); and Janelle Cruz (1986).

DNA evidence from the Southern California murders eventually matched the DNA from the
East Area Rape cases — a connection made in 2001, after DNA technology had advanced
sufficiently to permit cross-case comparison. The realization that the East Area Rapist
and the Original Night Stalker were the same person was one of the most significant
developments in American cold case history.

THE INVESTIGATION AND PAUL HOLES


Investigators and Their Methods

The investigation of the Golden State Killer — particularly after the DNA link was
established in 2001 — involved multiple investigators across multiple jurisdictions
working to identify the DNA profile that had been collected across dozens of crime
scenes.

Paul Holes, a cold case investigator at the Contra Costa County District Attorney's
office, became one of the most prominent and dedicated investigators pursuing the
case in its later years. His work — along with that of other investigators and the
journalist Michelle McNamara, whose book I'll Be Gone in the Dark significantly raised
public awareness of the case — formed the investigative infrastructure that eventually
produced an identification.


Michelle McNamara

Michelle McNamara was a crime writer and journalist who became deeply focused on the
Golden State Killer case in the years before her death in 2016. Her blog True Crime Diary
and her book I'll Be Gone in the Dark (published posthumously in 2018) brought the GSK
to widespread public attention and are credited with a significant role in generating
the public interest and investigative energy that eventually led to the arrest.


The DNA Technology Breakthrough

The identification of the Golden State Killer was ultimately achieved through genealogical
DNA matching — a technique that, by 2018, had become possible through the accumulation
of large consumer DNA databases (companies like GEDmatch, where people submitted DNA
for genealogy purposes). Investigators uploaded the GSK's DNA profile to GEDmatch and
identified distant relatives whose family trees could be used to narrow down the suspect
pool.

This technique — now known as forensic genealogy or investigative genetic genealogy —
was, at the time of the GSK identification, largely untested in a legal context and
generated significant discussion about privacy, consent, and the proper limits of law
enforcement access to genetic databases.

JOSEPH JAMES DeANGELO


The Arrest

On April 24, 2018, Joseph James DeAngelo Jr. — a 72-year-old former police officer
living in Citrus Heights, California — was arrested and charged as the Golden State Killer.
He had been identified through forensic genealogy: investigators worked from distant
DNA relatives in public databases, constructed a family tree, and narrowed the suspect
list to DeAngelo. They confirmed the match by collecting his discarded DNA — from the
door of his car and a paper bag — which matched the GSK profile.

DeAngelo had served as a police officer in the Exeter Police Department and the Auburn
Police Department during the period of his crimes. He was dismissed from the Auburn
department in 1979 for shoplifting — a detail that, in retrospect, acquired significance.
He had been, for decades after his crimes ended, living an apparently ordinary life:
retired, living in a suburban California neighborhood, known to his neighbors as a
somewhat reclusive but unremarkable elderly man.


The Guilty Plea and Sentence

In August 2020, DeAngelo pleaded guilty to 13 counts of first-degree murder and to
additional charges related to the rapes, using a legal framework that acknowledged
the uncharged rapes (for which the statute of limitations had expired) as relevant
aggravating factors. He was sentenced to life in prison without possibility of parole.

Victims and their families were given the opportunity to address DeAngelo at the
sentencing hearing. The resulting hearings — spread over three days — produced some
of the most powerful victim impact statements in California legal history, as survivors
of his attacks and family members of his murder victims confronted the man who had
destroyed their lives and families across a period of more than a decade.

THE CASEFILE EPISODES

Casefile's multi-episode coverage of the Golden State Killer case covers the full arc
described above: the Visalia Ransacker period, the East Area Rapist attacks in
exhaustive detail (including specific case-by-case accounts of individual incidents),
the shift to Southern California murders, the 2001 DNA linkage, the long investigation
period including McNamara's work, the forensic genealogy breakthrough, DeAngelo's
identification and arrest, and the legal proceedings.

The episodes are notable for their detail and their restraint: the Host presents the
facts of each incident with specificity, including details of victim experiences that
are not sanitized, while maintaining a tone that is respectful of the victims and
unemotional in its narration. The cumulative effect of the episode-by-episode
accumulation of crimes — each one a specific person or couple in their home, each one
a specific act of terror — is one of the most comprehensive and sobering portraits
of a serial offender available in any audio format.
