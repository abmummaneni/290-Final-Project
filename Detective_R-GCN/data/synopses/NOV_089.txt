NOV_089 | The Crooked Hinge | John Dickson Carr | Novel

FULL PLOT SYNOPSIS — SPOILERS INCLUDED
================================================================================

--- BACKGROUND AND SETTING ---

"The Crooked Hinge," published in 1938, is widely considered one of John Dickson
Carr's finest achievements and one of the masterpieces of the "impossible crime"
or locked-room subgenre of detective fiction. It features Dr. Gideon Fell, Carr's
large, boisterous, port-drinking detective who solves apparently supernatural or
physically impossible crimes through the application of extraordinary ingenuity.

The novel is set in and around Farnleigh Close, a country estate in rural Kent,
England. It combines a brilliantly constructed murder mystery with genuine
historical interest — one of the characters claims to be the heir to an estate
on the basis of his identity, and the mechanism of the "impossible" murder
ultimately hinges on a piece of Victorian mechanical ingenuity.

--- THE IDENTITY DISPUTE ---

The novel opens with a legal and personal crisis at Farnleigh Close. The estate
has been for some years in the possession of Sir John Farnleigh, who as a young
man survived the sinking of the Titanic in 1912. He returned from America after
the disaster, eventually inherited the Close, married a woman named Molly, and
has lived there as a somewhat unhappy country gentleman for years.

Now, a stranger has appeared claiming to be the real Sir John Farnleigh. This
man — referred to variously as the "Claimant" and later identified as Patrick
Gore — presents documentation and testimony suggesting that the two young men
(both teenagers when the Titanic sank) were confused in the chaos of the
disaster and its aftermath, and that the man currently living as Sir John
Farnleigh is in fact an impostor — a boy from a lower social class named John
Dodd — while Gore is the true heir.

The evidence on both sides is complicated. A document expert named Kennet
Murray is brought in to examine documents and compare handwriting and other
authenticating details. Both men claim memories of childhood experiences at
Farnleigh Close. Both seem genuine. The household and local community are
deeply unsettled.

The characters connected to Farnleigh Close include: Molly Farnleigh (the wife,
devoted to the man she has always known as John); Nathaniel Burrows (the family
solicitor, cautious and troubled); Betty Harbottle (a local woman with historical
connections to the family); Dr. Fell, who arrives as the crisis deepens; and
Patrick Gore himself, calm, sardonic, and alarmingly self-possessed.

--- WITCHCRAFT AND THE GOLDEN HINGE ---

Complicating the already tense situation is the presence of a local tradition of
interest in witchcraft or occult practice. An old woman in the village — Molly
describes her to Fell — is known locally as someone with connections to folk
magic and old ritual. This backdrop is not mere decoration: it feeds the novel's
atmosphere of hidden, ancient things beneath the polished surface of the English
countryside.

Connected to this is the "golden hinge" of the title — a small, unusual
mechanical artifact with a hidden hinge or joint that is in some way associated
with the Farnleigh family history and the old estate. The nature of this object
becomes central to the murder mechanism.

--- THE FIRST MURDER: SIR JOHN FARNLEIGH ---

Before the identity dispute can be legally resolved, the man known as Sir John
Farnleigh is murdered in a fashion that appears absolutely impossible.

He is found dead in the open grounds of the estate — on the lawn, in full
daylight or early evening, in a position that was visible (or should have been
visible) to multiple witnesses. He has been killed by slashing wounds to the
throat, consistent with a sharp instrument. No one saw the attack. There are no
footprints near the body in the soft ground other than the dead man's own.

No weapon is found near the body or in the immediate area. The witnesses who
were in a position to see the lawn either saw nothing at all or caught only a
strange, fleeting impression — something that some described as a figure,
perhaps not quite human, near the body for an instant. The impression is vague
and frightening.

This is the classic Carr situation: a murder committed in apparently impossible
circumstances, with no physical explanation immediately available.

--- DR. FELL'S INVESTIGATION ---

Dr. Fell arrives and begins his investigation. He is large, wheeze-prone, and
apparently casual in his manner — he drinks well, talks at length, and seems
to be everywhere and nowhere at once. In fact, he misses nothing.

Fell is interested in three things simultaneously: the identity dispute (which
may be connected to the murder), the impossible circumstances of the death, and
the old witchcraft lore of the district, which he suspects may be more than
mere atmosphere.

He interviews all the witnesses carefully. The most important testimony comes
from those who were on or near the terrace or grounds at the time. Each account
is slightly different — different positions, different sightlines, different
impressions of timing. Fell maps these accounts against the physical layout of
the estate with care.

He becomes particularly interested in the area immediately around where the body
was found — the condition of the grass, the fact that certain bushes or garden
features limit sightlines from specific positions, and the geometry of what
could and could not have been seen by each witness from where they stood.

--- THE AUTOMATON ---

The key to the impossible crime — and one of Carr's most inventive murder
mechanisms — is the golden hinge and what it connects to: a Victorian automaton.

An automaton is a mechanical figure constructed in the nineteenth century to
simulate human movement, driven by clockwork or spring mechanisms. The
Victorian era was fascinated by such devices — mechanical figures that could
appear to write, draw, or gesture, seemingly alive but entirely mechanical.
The Farnleigh family had such an automaton in their possession — a figure kept
in storage or disuse for many years.

Dr. Fell determines that the automaton was removed from storage, dressed or
configured to present a human silhouette, wound or set with a mechanism that
allowed it to strike or slash, and placed in a specific position on the grounds
where the victim was lured. The golden hinge — the "crooked hinge" of the title
— was either part of the automaton's mechanism or the device by which the
killing stroke was delivered.

The intended victim walked close enough to the device to be struck. The automaton
— or the mechanical device hidden within or disguised as something — delivered
the slashing wounds that killed him. The killer was nowhere near the victim at
the moment of death, having set the mechanical trap in advance.

The reason no footprints other than the victim's were found is that no living
person was on the lawn near the body at the moment of the killing. The apparent
"figure" glimpsed by witnesses was the automaton itself, or its shadow, before
it fell or was collapsed by the mechanism completing its cycle.

--- THE SECOND DEATH AND THE IDENTITY RESOLUTION ---

As Fell's investigation continues, the identity dispute takes on new significance.
If the man known as Sir John was actually the impostor (John Dodd), then his
death removes one player from the dispute but does not resolve it in Gore's
favor automatically — there are still legal processes and the question of Molly
Farnleigh's position and the estate.

Further examination of the documents and the histories of the two men — with
reference to the Titanic survivors' records, childhood documents from Farnleigh
Close, and testimony from those who knew both boys before 1912 — eventually
clarifies the truth. The resolution of the identity question is intertwined with
the murder: the killer's motive required that one of the two claimants be
eliminated before the legal process could render its verdict.

There may be a second death or further violence as the killer, facing exposure,
acts again. In the compressed crisis of the novel's final sections, Fell brings
the full solution together.

--- THE KILLER'S IDENTITY AND MOTIVE ---

The killer is one of the small circle connected to Farnleigh Close — a person
who had specific knowledge of the automaton and its mechanism, access to the
grounds, and a motive connected to the estate or the outcome of the identity
dispute. The automaton's existence and its mechanical properties were known to
very few, and identifying who had that knowledge narrows the field dramatically.

The motive relates to what the resolution of the identity dispute would mean
for the killer financially or in terms of their position — someone who stood
to lose significantly if the legal process went a specific way, and who
calculated that eliminating one of the claimants (and perhaps further
complicating the situation) would serve their interests.

Fell reveals the solution in his characteristic fashion — a long, somewhat
theatrical explanation that covers both the physical mechanism of the
impossible crime and the human motivations behind it.

--- RESOLUTION ---

The killer is identified and arrested. The identity dispute, freed from the
interference of murder, is resolved by the accumulated evidence. The automaton
is discovered or its remains examined, confirming the physical mechanism of the
impossible crime.

The novel ends with the satisfying dual closure that Carr always provided: the
how of the impossible crime explained in full (removing all remaining sense of
the supernatural), and the who of the murder identified beyond doubt.

--- NOTES ON THE IMPOSSIBLE CRIME ---

"The Crooked Hinge" is notable for the ingenuity and originality of its murder
mechanism — the automaton is not a device Carr used elsewhere, and the specific
use of Victorian mechanical engineering as a murder weapon is unique in the
literature of the period. The novel also handles the atmosphere of occult
suggestion with unusual skill, maintaining genuine unease without ever
committing to a supernatural explanation. Everything that seems uncanny is
explicable; the explanation is simply more imaginative than most readers expect.
Carr is often credited with the observation (through Dr. Fell's famous
"Locked-Room Lecture" in an earlier novel) that impossible crimes are fun
precisely because their solutions are startling rather than merely clever.
"The Crooked Hinge" bears this out.
