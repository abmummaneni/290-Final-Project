A Slow Fire Burning by Paula Hawkins is a London‑set domestic thriller about the murder of a twenty‑three‑year‑old man, Daniel Sutherland, and the three women whose lives intersect around his death. The story is told in rotating perspectives—Laura, Miriam, Carla, and then others—and slowly reveals that the killer is not the obvious suspect, and that the real motive reaches back fifteen years to the death of a toddler. Below is a long, detailed, spoiler‑filled plot synopsis told in clear chronological order, covering the full story, the killer’s identity and motive, and how the case is solved.
Laura’s bloody night and the houseboat murder

The novel opens in the early hours of a Sunday morning. Laura Kilbride, a twenty‑year‑old woman with a traumatic brain injury and a history of erratic behavior, is leaving a houseboat on a London canal, standing in the street and cleaning blood off her body and clothes. She has just had a volatile one‑night stand with Daniel Sutherland, a young man who rents a houseboat nearby.

Laura is confused, physically hurt, and mentally disoriented. She has a fresh cut on her arm, and in her pocket she finds Daniel’s watch, which she does not remember taking. Still half‑dazed, she tries to call her father for help, but her stepmother cuts the call short, leaving Laura alone.

Later that morning, the body of Daniel Sutherland is discovered in his boat, brutally stabbed; his throat is cut, and the scene is bloody and chaotic. The police quickly trace the last person seen near the boat to Laura, whose name emerges from security‑camera footage and her own presence at the boat the night before.
The three women and their separate connections to Daniel

Parallel to Laura’s night, the story follows two other women, each with a strong stake in Daniel’s life:

    Miriam Lewis, a middle‑aged, lonely woman who lives on a neighboring houseboat. She discovers Daniel’s body, calls the police, and becomes obsessed with the case, keeping a meticulous log of comings and goings on the canal. Miriam is also a failed novelist who has a long‑standing grievance with Daniel’s uncle, Theo, whose book she believes plagiarized her own manuscript.

    Carla Myerson, Daniel’s aunt, who has recently lost her sister, Angela (Daniel’s mother), only a few weeks before the murder. Carla is grieving and emotionally fragile, and she is very close to her nephew, even as she is quietly estranged from her ex‑husband, Theo.

A fourth, quieter presence is Irene, an elderly woman who lives near Carla and is good friends with Laura. She is sharp, observant, and quickly becomes suspicious that the official picture of the case does not add up.
The police suspect Laura and the forging of evidence

The police investigate Laura and find that she has a prior record: she was once charged with stabbing someone else with a fork, and the weapon this time—a knife—belongs to Theo, found in Laura’s possession. The circumstantial case against her quickly stacks up:

    She was the last person seen with Daniel.

    She has blood on her clothes and a fresh cut on her arm.

    She has Daniel’s watch.

    The murder weapon is the same knife that Theo admits to owning.

Laura insists she did not kill Daniel, claiming that they had sex, then argued, and that she left in a confused state but did not see him die. The police do not fully believe her, and she is arrested and imprisoned while the case is prepared.
The buried family secret: Ben’s death

As the story progresses, it becomes clear that the murder is tied to the much older death of Ben, the toddler son of Carla and Theo, who died fifteen years earlier after falling from a balcony. The official story is that Angela, Carla’s sister, left the door open while caring for Ben, and the child wandered out and fell to his death.

The real truth is far darker:

    Daniel, as a nine‑year‑old boy, was home with Angela when he became enraged after catching his mother, Angela, having sex with her boyfriend.

    In a fit of jealousy and cruelty, Daniel placed a toy car near the open balcony door so that Ben would crawl after it and fall.

    After the fall, Daniel let everyone believe the door was left open accidentally, allowing Angela to be blamed and emotionally destroyed.

Over the years, Daniel internalized his guilt as power: he would alternately taunt his mother and Carla about what he had done, using the secret as a weapon to control them. The memory of Ben’s death poisoned the entire family.
Carla’s hidden motive and the sexual relationship with Daniel

In the years before his death, Daniel’s behavior toward Carla becomes increasingly twisted. Carla, still grieving Ben and broken by the loss of her nephew, has also had a brief, shameful sexual relationship with Daniel, a nephew‑like figure who exploited her vulnerability.

When Carla finally discovers proof of Daniel’s true role in Ben’s death—likely a notebook or sketch in which he details the toy‑car trick and the fall—she is pushed over the edge. The man who killed her son, destroyed her sister, and later used her sexually now has that evidence in his hands. For Carla, killing Daniel is not just revenge; it is a twisted form of atonement, a way to end the cycle of guilt and shame he has kept alive.
The twist: Theo falsely confesses

The police investigation leans harder and harder on Laura, and it seems she will be prosecuted for Daniel’s murder. The evidence is damning, and even Laura’s many supporters, including Irene, fear she may be convicted.

Unable to bear the thought of Carla going to prison, Theo decides to take the blame. He knows the knife is his and that the police are already close to an arrest; he also knows Carla is unraveling. He goes to the police and confesses to the murder, claiming he killed Daniel out of jealousy and anger over the implied sexual relationship between Daniel and Carla. Theo’s confession is detailed and convincing, and the police drop the case against Laura, releasing her from custody.

Laura is attacked by another inmate before she can fully leave prison, and the assault lands her in the hospital, further destabilizing her already fragile state.
Irene uncovers the truth and Carla’s confession

Behind the scenes, Irene, the elderly neighbor, has been quietly gathering evidence. She is the best friend of Laura and the next‑door neighbor of Carla and Angela. She notices oddities in the family’s behavior and the way Carla and Theo talk past each other about Daniel and Ben.

Irene also discovers drawings and notes that reveal:

    Daniel’s own account of how he lured Ben toward the balcony.

    Evidence that Carla had access to and knew about the drawing.

    The fact that the knife in Laura’s flat is actually the one Theo owned, placed there by Carla to frame Laura.

Using this knowledge, Irene confronts Carla. In a tense, emotionally charged scene, Carla breaks down and admits that she killed Daniel. She argues that he deserved to die for causing Ben’s death and for psychologically torturing Angela and Carla for years. Carla’s confession is defensive, but it is a full acknowledgment of the crime.

Irene secretly records the confession and takes the recording to the police. The authorities reopen the case, charge Carla with murder, and drop the charges against Theo, who had falsely taken the blame. Theo is arrested anyway—not for killing Daniel, but for the false confession and the complexities of the cover‑up.
The Miriam subplot and the additional “burning”

In the background, the story also resolves a second, non‑murder mystery involving Miriam’s kidnapper. Miriam was once abducted years ago by a man named Jeremy, who took her and her friend Lorraine. Jeremy has been resentfully corresponding with Theo, accusing him of stealing details of his life for a novel, and Theo has assumed the letters are from Miriam trying to ruin his reputation.

Eventually Theo confronts Miriam, but the truth is revealed that the letters came from Jeremy, who has been watching and tormenting them from afar. Jeremy is later found dead in a houseboat, completing the theme of secrets that “burn slowly” before they explode. His death is not directly tied to Daniel’s murder but underscores the novel’s broader idea that buried pasts can erupt in violence years later.
The ending and the women’s fates

By the end of the novel:

    Carla is in prison for Daniel’s murder, having admitted that she killed her nephew‑nephew‑like‑figure out of a twisted mix of grief, guilt, and punitive love.

    Theo is also arrested for his role in the false confession and the manipulation of the evidence, though his motive is paternalistic and protective rather than vengeful.

    Laura survives the prison attack and is released. Her life remains fragile, but she is spared a wrongful conviction, and her bond with Irene helps anchor her.

    Irene has redeemed herself by uncovering the truth, and the two women begin to rebuild a stable, supportive life.

    Miriam is left with a sense of vindication about her manuscript and the larger narrative of justice, even though her own trauma has not been fully healed.

The “slow fire burning” of the title refers to the long‑smoldering secrets of the family—Ben’s death, the sexual abuse of Carla by Daniel, Carla’s guilt, and the broader web of guilt and resentment that finally ignites in a single violent act. The book ends with a bitter sense that the capacity for good people to do terrible things has been amply demonstrated, and that the real damage was done long before the houseboat murder.
