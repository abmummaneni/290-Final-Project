Mariana’s grief and the call to Cambridge

Mariana Andros is a successful group therapist in London whose life has been shattered by the recent death of her husband, Sebastian, who drowned in what was deemed a tragic swimming accident about a year before the novel begins. She is still deep in mourning, struggling with guilt and emptiness, and has withdrawn emotionally from much of her work and social circle. Mariana and Sebastian had effectively raised her niece, Zoe, as their own after Zoe’s parents died in a car accident, so Zoe is the closest thing Mariana has to a child.

Mariana’s fragile new normal is broken when Zoe calls from Cambridge University, where she is a student, panicked and distraught: her close friend and roommate, Tara, has been brutally murdered on or near the college grounds. The murder—stabbing, with a ritualistic feel—shocks the campus, and Zoe is terrified both for herself and by the savagery of the crime. Determined to support Zoe and unable to ignore her therapist’s instinct to help, Mariana travels to Cambridge, which is also her own alma mater, returning to a place loaded with memories of her youth and her early relationship with Sebastian.
Edward Fosca, The Maidens, and the first murder

Once in Cambridge, Mariana learns more about Tara’s life and the social circles she moved in. The center of Zoe’s suspicions is Professor Edward Fosca, a handsome, charismatic Greek tragedy scholar who teaches at the college. Fosca is adored by staff and students, known for his engaging lectures on Greek myth and drama and for projecting an aura of cultivated mystery. Most notably, he has a small, intensely devoted group of female students who orbit him like a cult; they are known as “The Maidens,” named in reference to Persephone and associated Eleusinian rites.

Tara had been one of these Maidens, and rumors circulate that she was sleeping with Fosca. Before her death, Tara had confided to Zoe that she was afraid of Fosca, suggesting that he might be dangerous and even saying she feared he might kill her. This confession convinces Zoe that Fosca is the murderer, and her conviction heavily influences Mariana’s initial impressions. Mariana meets with the local police, including an inspector who is cautious but not especially receptive to the idea that a revered professor is responsible; Fosca has a solid alibi, seemingly corroborated by other staff and by his students. Even so, Mariana becomes fixated on Fosca, convinced that his authority and adoration give him cover to do something terrible.

Mariana attends one of Fosca’s lectures and is unsettled by how he weaves in stories of sacrifice, death, and rebirth from Greek mythology, particularly the Eleusinian mysteries and Persephone’s journey to and from the underworld. She also observes the Maidens sitting together, rapt and almost worshipful, reinforcing her sense that this is not just a fan club but something cult‑like. Her professional training tells her not to jump to conclusions, but her instincts and grief make her increasingly willing to see Fosca as a predatory figure hiding in plain sight.
Postcards, more deaths, and Mariana’s obsession

After Tara’s death, the threat at Cambridge escalates. Mariana learns that Tara had received mysterious postcards quoting lines from Greek tragedy about death, sacrifice, and fate; similar cards begin to surface as the story goes on. Soon, another young woman—Veronica, also one of the Maidens—is found murdered in a similar fashion, with ritualistic elements and a link to the Greek quotations. The pattern suggests a serial killer targeting members of Fosca’s inner circle, pulling Mariana deeper into the investigation and intensifying her certainty that Fosca is orchestrating these killings under the guise of academic interest in mythic rituals.

At the same time, Mariana’s own life becomes more tangled and dangerous. One of her male patients from London, who is emotionally unstable and obsessively attached to her, begins appearing around Cambridge, effectively stalking her and adding a thread of menace and distraction. She also meets a younger man named Fred, a somewhat eccentric and earnest Cambridge student who claims to have psychic visions and insists that he and Mariana are destined to be together romantically. While she does not take his prophetic talk seriously, Fred becomes a persistent presence and eventual ally.​

Mariana and Zoe both receive Greek‑quote postcards themselves, which suggests that the killer is not only targeting the Maidens but actively toying with them. Mariana interprets this as further evidence that Fosca is playing a psychological game, using mythic references to taunt and frighten them. Her behavior grows more obsessive and intrusive: she watches Fosca, pushes herself into conversations with his students, and probes the secretive dynamics of The Maidens, even as college authorities and the police begin to see her as an interfering outsider. Her credibility is undermined by the fact that, in their view, she is a grieving widow fixated on a popular professor with an apparently airtight alibi.
Secret rites, Morris, and The Maidens’ world

As Mariana digs deeper, she discovers hints of hidden ceremonies linked to Fosca and The Maidens. Zoe eventually admits that she once attended one of Fosca’s initiation‑style gatherings, held in a secluded, atmospheric setting, involving chanting, drinking, and a charged atmosphere that felt increasingly sexual and uncomfortable. When Fosca turned his attention directly on her—kissing and touching her—Zoe panicked and fled, and afterward she never returned to the group. This experience fuels Mariana’s sense that Fosca exploits his status to blur boundaries with his students, treating them not just as pupils but as quasi‑acolytes in a private cult of personality.​

Mariana’s suspicions widen to include Morris, the college’s head porter, a man with significant access and knowledge of campus goings‑on. She sees Fosca surreptitiously hand Morris an envelope, then later follows Morris and witnesses him having sex with Serena, another Maiden. This suggests some sort of arrangement or blackmail: later it is made clear that Morris has leverage over Fosca because he knows about Fosca’s affairs with students and is being paid off or otherwise compensated. Soon after this encounter, Serena is found murdered as well, in the same ritualistic manner as Tara and Veronica. The pattern confirms that The Maidens themselves are the specific targets of the killer.

Mariana brings what she has seen to the police, and they arrest Morris based on his connections to the victims and his link to Fosca’s secretive behavior. However, while authorities lean toward Morris as a plausible suspect, Mariana remains convinced that Fosca is the mastermind and that Morris is at most an accomplice or a tool. Each new death heightens the stakes, yet also deepens the puzzle of Fosca’s continuing apparent alibi.
The knife, Sebastian’s secret, and the reveal of the killer

With bodies mounting and fear rising, Mariana finally decides that she and Zoe need to leave Cambridge and return to London for safety. Before they go, Zoe insists that they must retrieve a ceremonial knife used in one of Fosca’s rites, believing it may be the murder weapon and could serve as decisive evidence against him. Reluctantly, Mariana agrees, and the two women go to locate the knife, entering an isolated, charged setting that echoes the earlier rituals.

When they find the knife, the situation abruptly turns. Zoe reveals she has no intention of using it as evidence; instead, she intends to use it to kill Mariana. In a chilling confrontation, Zoe confesses a long‑hidden truth: she and Sebastian had been lovers since she was about fifteen, years before Sebastian married Mariana. According to Zoe, Sebastian married Mariana primarily for her father’s money and family wealth, not for love. Together, Sebastian and Zoe had developed a twisted plan to kill Mariana and inherit her fortune—Sebastian as the husband, Zoe as the secret partner who would ultimately share the spoils.

Sebastian’s death in the swimming accident derailed this scheme, but after his death, Zoe decided to carry out his plan on her own. She orchestrated the entire sequence of murders at Cambridge, killing Tara, Veronica, and Serena herself, crafting them as ritualistic Greek‑themed killings tied to The Maidens and Fosca’s lectures. The Greek postcards were also her work, designed both to terrorize the victims and to lure Mariana into fixating on Fosca as the obvious suspect. Zoe’s ultimate goal was to involve Mariana in a dramatic, psychologically fraught investigation, frame Fosca as the serial killer, and then finally murder Mariana in a way that would make her appear the last victim in the series. In this way, Zoe could eliminate Mariana, collect the inheritance, and leave Fosca and the whole Cambridge mythos as a convenient narrative cover.

Zoe’s confession flips the entire story: the seemingly vulnerable niece calling her aunt for help was, in reality, the architect of the nightmare, pulling Mariana back into Cambridge precisely so she could be destroyed on a stage rich with symbolic meaning and personal memories. Zoe’s capacity for deception—maintaining a façade of fragility and dependency while killing her peers—exposes a deeply disturbed psyche and a longstanding, warped influence from Sebastian.
Final struggle, aftermath, and epilogue

In the climactic struggle, Zoe attacks Mariana with the knife, intending to complete her plan. Mariana fights back, and Fred—the earnest young man who has been hovering around her, attracted to her and protective of her—arrives in time to intervene. Together, they manage to overpower Zoe, who is injured in the confrontation and subsequently subdued. The authorities arrive, and Zoe is arrested, her role in the murders uncovered along with her confession about Sebastian and the plotted killing of Mariana.

In the aftermath, Edward Fosca is cleared of the murders but not of serious misconduct. It emerges that he has indeed been sleeping with multiple students, including members of The Maidens, behavior that is ethically and professionally unacceptable. Morris had been blackmailing him over these affairs, which explains the envelope and their clandestine connections, but neither Fosca nor Morris turns out to be the ritual killer. Fosca is ultimately fired from the university for his predatory relationships, even if he avoids being branded a murderer.

Zoe is sent to a psychiatric institution, her mental state and history of abuse and manipulation presented as part of her evaluation and treatment. The novel’s epilogue shows Mariana going to visit Zoe there, suggesting that, despite everything, she still feels a sense of responsibility and a need for closure with the young woman she practically raised and who nearly killed her. Mariana’s own grief over Sebastian is reframed; she must now process not only his death but also the revelation that he exploited and betrayed her, and that his influence helped twist Zoe into a killer.

The story closes on a note of psychological unease rather than neat comfort: the murders have been solved and the killer identified, but the deeper wounds—Mariana’s trauma, the betrayal at the heart of her marriage, and the question of how love and obsession can warp into violence—remain raw.
