Missing Joseph centers on the death of a village vicar, Robin Sage, who appears to have been accidentally poisoned by an herbalist, only for Lynley, St. James, and Havers to uncover a far darker tangle of parentage, loss, and revenge in a Lancashire village. The mystery turns on who really killed Sage, why he was targeted, and how an old child‑protection case and a desperate mother’s love finally come together in a deliberate poisoning.
Deborah Meets the Vicar and the Trip to Winslough

Deborah St. James, struggling with repeated miscarriages and her inability to have a child, ducks into London’s National Gallery to escape the rain. She finds herself staring at Leonardo da Vinci’s study of the Virgin and Child, noticing that Joseph is absent from the composition. A man sits beside her and comments that “Joseph is missing” from so many depictions of the Holy Family, a remark that pierces Deborah’s grief about her own missing child and absent motherhood.

The man is Robin Sage, vicar of the remote northern village of Winslough in Lancashire. He speaks gently and offers a picture of his parish as a place of solitude and reflection, somewhere people can think and heal; his kindness comforts Deborah, who feels an immediate connection to him.

Inspired by his description and yearning for peace, Deborah persuades her husband, forensic scientist Simon St. James, to take a winter holiday in Winslough so she can see the vicar again and try to get some emotional equilibrium back. Their marriage is under severe strain from her miscarriages and their disagreement about how (or whether) to become parents, and both hope the trip will help them reconnect.

When they arrive in Winslough, however, they are stunned to learn that Robin Sage is dead.
The “Accidental” Poisoning and Local Relationships

Robin Sage has recently died after eating a meal at the home of Juliet Spence, a reclusive local herbalist. The inquest has ruled his death “death by misadventure,” concluding that Juliet mistook water hemlock—one of the most deadly native plants—for wild parsnip and cooked it for supper. Sage ate the dish, suffered violent symptoms, and died.

Juliet Spence lives on the margins of the village: she is seen as a “witch,” a woman who knows herbs, keeps to herself, and has an intense, difficult relationship with her teenage daughter Maggie. Maggie is thirteen, restless and sexually awakening, with a local boyfriend; Juliet’s fierce, possessive mother‑love and fear about Maggie’s sexuality and future are constant undercurrents.

Constable Colin Shepherd, the local policeman, conducted the original investigation and inquest. He is also Juliet Spence’s secret lover, which makes his quick acceptance of the “accident” verdict highly suspect: he has every reason to protect her from scrutiny and charges. Colin is himself emotionally fragile, depressed, and increasingly unstable; he is a widowed father with burdens he cannot fully cope with, and his affair with Juliet combines passion, guilt, and desperation.

Deborah and Simon are immediately uneasy with the idea of such an “accident.” Simon, as a forensic scientist, knows how unlikely it is to confuse deadly water hemlock with wild parsnip, and how improbable it is that a knowledgeable herbalist like Juliet would make that mistake. Suspicious of both the conclusion and the constable’s objectivity, Simon contacts his friend Inspector Thomas Lynley at New Scotland Yard and asks him to come to Winslough to look again at the case.
Lynley, Havers, and the Reopened Investigation

Lynley, already burdened by tensions in his relationship with Lady Helen Clyde, decides to go north, bringing Detective Sergeant Barbara Havers with him. He reopens the investigation into Sage’s death, immediately challenging the “accidental poisoning” verdict and Colin Shepherd’s handling of the case.

Lynley discovers that water hemlock is notoriously poisonous and extremely unlikely to be gathered and cooked by mistake. Juliet, as a skilled herbalist, knows plants intimately, which makes “I confused it with wild parsnip” a very weak explanation. The physical evidence and the botanic realities strongly suggest that the herb in Sage’s dinner was used deliberately.

Lynley also begins to explore the complex web of relationships in Winslough:

    Colin Shepherd and Juliet Spence’s illicit affair.

    Juliet’s fraught bond with Maggie, who is pushing against her mother’s control, experimenting with sex, and drawn to her boyfriend.

    Polly, the vicarage housekeeper, who “dabbles in witchcraft” and may have stronger feelings about Sage than she shows.

    A wealthy, “spoiled” local wife and her roving‑eye husband, whose marriage hides resentment and infidelity.

    Robin Sage’s own shadowed past: his infant son died of SIDS, and his wife Suzanna apparently committed suicide by jumping from a ferry fifteen years earlier.

The more Lynley and Havers look at Sage’s last days, the clearer it becomes that almost everyone around the vicar has some grievance, secret, or need tied up with him.
Robin Sage’s Hidden Past

As Lynley, Simon, and Havers dig deeper, they peel back “layer after layer of personal history” in Sage’s life. Many villagers saw him as compassionate and thoughtful, but he carried heavy personal loss:

    His infant son died suddenly, apparently of cot death (SIDS).

    His wife Suzanna vanished after supposedly throwing herself from a ferry and was declared dead.

This double tragedy shrouded Sage in an aura of suffering and guilt, and there are hints he may have made grave mistakes connected to his family and to a past child‑protection case.

Investigating that older case, Lynley and St. James uncover a history involving social services, accusations of abuse or neglect, and decisions that tore a mother and child apart. The specifics differ by reader summary, but the core is consistent: Sage’s involvement—either as priest or as someone who testified or reported concerns—contributed to a mother losing her child, with catastrophic consequences.

This earlier case connects directly to Winslough and to someone now living there; the emotional scars from that event never healed, and resentment toward Sage has been festering for years. Sage’s theology of suffering and his attempts at compassion could not erase the fact that his actions, however well‑intended, led to enormous pain.
Juliet, Maggie, Colin, and the Village’s Ugliness

While the past is being reconstructed, the present tense of the village grows more volatile.

    Juliet Spence struggles to control Maggie, whose burgeoning sexuality and relationship with her boyfriend threaten Juliet’s sense of safety and moral order. Juliet’s entire identity revolves around being Maggie’s mother and protector, and the thought of losing her daughter—literally or emotionally—is unbearable.

    Colin Shepherd, emotionally fragile and in love with Juliet, tries to manage the official inquiry even as he feels his world slipping, fearing she will be charged. His judgment deteriorates, and his behavior becomes more erratic as pressure mounts.

    Polly, the “good witch” housekeeper at the vicarage, is suspected, then half‑cleared, then suspected again: did she tamper with the herbs? Did she hold her own resentment of Sage, or see something she has not admitted?​

    Other villagers show the “ugliness” Elizabeth George taps into: teenage drama, casual cruelty, indifference to religion despite a vicar in their midst, and a willingness to scapegoat whomever is most convenient once suspicion rises.

As Lynley and Havers question people, Colin grows defensive and hostile. His mishandling of the original investigation, compounded by his emotional entanglement with Juliet, makes him look like he is searching for a scapegoat rather than truth.

Around three‑quarters of the way through, Colin’s mental state breaks further: in scenes that reviewers describe as “stupendously portrayed,” he spirals into violent behavior, including a rape, underscoring how damaged and dangerous he has become. This breakdown both complicates and clarifies matters: he is capable of terrible acts but did not create the poison; he is an obstructive force, not the original poisoner.​
How the Poisoning Is Exposed as Murder

Scientifically, Simon confirms that water hemlock is extraordinarily lethal and very unlikely to be gathered by mistake; he also demonstrates how it would be nearly impossible to confuse it with wild parsnip if one has even basic herbal knowledge. This directly contradicts Juliet’s claim of a tragic misidentification.

Lynley and Havers track Sage’s last movements and conversations:

    Who invited him to dinner that night and why.

    Whether he knew something new or threatening about Maggie’s paternity or about an old child‑protection decision.

    Whether he had recently tried to make amends or reveal a secret.

The investigation shows that Sage had begun to revisit his past decisions and had knowledge about the identity of a child’s father—knowledge that could shatter someone’s current life and sense of self. There are suggestions that he knew who Maggie’s father really was, or that he knew damning details about Juliet’s past that she desperately wanted kept buried.

Gradually, Lynley discerns that the poisoning was not a random act or a spur‑of‑the‑moment error but the endpoint of long‑nursed fury. The dinner at Juliet’s house was the chosen occasion because:

    It provided an intimate, controllable setting.

    Juliet could plausibly claim an herbal mistake.

    Colin’s emotional involvement could be relied on to push the “accident” verdict through.

With this frame, the question becomes: whose vengeance is being enacted through that bowl of poisoned food?
The Killer’s Motive: A Mother for the Sake of Her Child

In the final stretch, the narrative reveals that the core of the crime lies in motherhood, loss, and the lengths to which a mother will go to protect or avenge a child. The earlier social‑services case in which Sage played a role involved removing a child from a mother (or destroying a family) under official auspices; the mother’s life was ruined, and she never forgave him.

The novel exposes that the “accidental” poisoning was in fact a carefully planned act of revenge by someone whose life had been reshaped by Sage’s choices about a child. Whether through Juliet’s hands or another’s, the motive is essentially:

    You took my child, my motherhood, or my family from me.

    I will take your life in return.

This motive, when fully explained, makes the use of water hemlock and the cloak of herbal “misadventure” chillingly logical: the killer knew the plant, knew its effects, and counted on the village’s willingness to believe in a witchy blunder rather than face the possibility of a long‑concealed injustice.

Lynley ultimately identifies the responsible party and is forced to decide how to act: enforcing the law strictly would mean punishing someone whose crime sprang from torment that the system itself—and Sage’s past actions—helped create. The decision he reaches is one “he does not want to do but has no choice,” underscoring the moral weight of his role and the impossibility of a solution that feels wholly just.
Deborah, Simon, Lynley, and the Aftermath

Running parallel to the murder plot is the disintegration and partial repair of Deborah and Simon’s marriage. Their conflict over children—Deborah’s desperate desire; Simon’s mix of love, caution, and fear—permeates everything in Winslough, coloring how Deborah sees the mothers around her and how she responds to the revelations about Sage and the past.

Confronting a crime rooted in motherhood and the loss or destruction of a child forces Deborah to examine her own grief and rage. She must reckon with the idea that “for the sake of her child” can drive someone to murder, and she recognizes in that extremity a distorted mirror of her own willingness to do almost anything to become a mother. This recognition becomes part of how she and Simon start to bridge the rift between them.

For Lynley, the case in Winslough adds another layer to his ongoing relationship with Helen and to his view of family and responsibility. The moral compromise or painful choice he makes at the end leaves him carrying a heavy burden of ambiguity: he has upheld the law in one sense and violated it in another, all in a setting where nearly everyone’s suffering traces back to decisions about children—who gets them, who loses them, and who is allowed to be called a parent.

Once the culprit is exposed and the official work is done, Lynley and his team leave Winslough, but the village’s future remains unsettled. The final focus on the villagers after the detectives depart suggests that they must now live with the fallout of what has been revealed: broken relationships, shattered illusions, and a community forced to acknowledge that its vicar’s death was not a tragic accident but the “suitable” vengeance of someone whose life he irreparably damaged.
