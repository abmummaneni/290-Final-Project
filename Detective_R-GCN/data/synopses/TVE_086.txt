--- BACKGROUND AND CONTEXT ---

"The Fifth Woman" is an episode of the BBC's English-language Wallander series,
first broadcast in November 2010 as part of Series 2. It stars Kenneth Branagh
as Kurt Wallander and is directed by Philip Martin — the same director who
helmed the Poirot "Murder on the Orient Express" and "Three Act Tragedy" in the
same period. The BBC Wallander series is a British production filmed in Sweden,
using Swedish locations but with an English-speaking cast, and it represents
a somewhat different interpretation of the character from the Swedish-language
Krister Henriksson version: Branagh's Wallander is more explicitly suffering,
more visibly on the edge of breakdown, and in some respects more theatrical
in his melancholy.

The episode is based on Henning Mankell's 1996 novel "The Fifth Woman" — one
of the longest Wallander novels and one of the most explicitly political in
its subject matter. The novel's central concern is violence against women —
specifically the specific forms of male violence that are enabled by the
assumption of female compliance, and the specific forms of female response
that accumulate when that violence is sustained without accountability. The
title refers to the four women in the novel's backstory who were murdered before
the investigation begins, and to the fifth woman who becomes the novel's active
figure.

--- THE SETTING AND THE CASE'S ORIGIN ---

The investigation begins with the death of Holger Eriksson, a retired businessman
and amateur ornithologist in the Ystad area. Eriksson is found dead at night
near a ditch on his property — he has fallen or been placed in a trench in
which sharpened stakes have been embedded, and has been impaled. The killing
is extraordinary in its specific preparation: someone dug the trench, sharpened
the stakes, and then waited for Eriksson to walk into it. This is a killing
that required significant advance planning and specific knowledge of the victim's
movements and habits.

Wallander investigates with his team — Ann-Britt Höglund and the other Ystad
detectives — and is immediately struck by the specific quality of the violence:
not the explosive passion of an angry killing or the efficiency of a professional
assassination, but something more deliberate, more patient, and in a specific
sense more personal. This killing required a specific investment of time and
effort that tells Wallander something about the killer's relationship to the
victim.

--- THE SECOND VICTIM ---

A second murder follows, and the specific similarity of method — again elaborate,
again clearly planned in advance, again requiring specific knowledge of the
victim — establishes a pattern. The second victim is a man named Gösta Runfeldt,
who has disappeared from his home and is found dead in the woods in circumstances
as carefully prepared as Eriksson's.

The two victims' apparent connection is not immediately obvious: they are from
different social and professional worlds, they have no visible common acquaintance,
and the specific method of their deaths is different in its physical details
while being consistent in its psychological character. Someone is killing
specific men, slowly and deliberately, in ways that suggest the killings have
been planned over considerable time.

Wallander builds the investigation around the specific question of what connects
these two men — what they share that made them targets for the same killer's
specific and sustained attention.

--- THE AFRICAN CONNECTION ---

The investigation of both victims' histories eventually uncovers an African
connection: both men were in Africa, at different times, in circumstances that
connect them to the same specific event or the same specific people. The nature
of this connection — what they did, or witnessed, or participated in, during
their time in Africa — is the investigation's central thread.

The African backstory involves a group of women who were the victims of specific
violence in a context where that violence was enabled by the specific power
dynamics of the situation. What was done to these women, and the specific
identity of the men responsible, is what the investigation must establish.

--- THE FIFTH WOMAN: ELEANOR ---

The killer is a woman named Eleanor — a figure who was connected to the four
women killed in Africa, who may have been present for or have specific knowledge
of what was done to them, and who has returned to Sweden specifically to kill
the men responsible.

Eleanor is the "fifth woman" of the title: the one who survived, or who came
after, or who remained to act where the four could not. Her killing campaign
is organized around a specific list of men — the people whose specific acts
in Africa she holds responsible for what happened to the four women — and she
has been planning and executing it with the specific patience of someone who
has spent years preparing to do exactly this.

Her psychology is one of the novel's and episode's central concerns. She is
not mad. She is not simply violent. She is a woman who has been carrying
specific knowledge of specific crimes for a long time and who has decided,
in the specific absence of any official accountability for those crimes, to
provide accountability herself. Her methods are elaborate and violent, but
they are directed at specific people for specific reasons, and the investigation's
gradual understanding of those reasons is one of the episode's most morally
challenging threads.

--- WALLANDER AND THE MORAL QUESTION ---

Wallander, as he assembles the picture of what Eleanor is doing and why,
faces the specific moral question that "The Fifth Woman" poses most directly:
what is the investigator's relationship to the specific justice being administered
by the killer?

Eleanor is killing men who committed serious violence against women and were
never held accountable. The violence she is addressing was real, the impunity
of those responsible was real, and the specific failure of official mechanisms —
the absence of any investigation or prosecution of what happened in Africa —
created the conditions that she is responding to.

Wallander does not find Eleanor sympathetic in the simple sense — she has killed
people, and he is a police officer who believes in the law — but he finds her
comprehensible in a way that complicates the investigation. His specific
discomfort with what he is learning, and what it means for how he conducts
the investigation, is rendered with Branagh's specific quality of visible
internal conflict: a man who is doing his job and thinking very hard about
the cost of doing it.

--- ANN-BRITT HÖGLUND AND THE FEMALE PERSPECTIVE ---

Ann-Britt Höglund's role in the investigation is particularly significant in
"The Fifth Woman" given the case's subject matter. Her specific perspective —
as a woman, as a police officer, as someone whose professional experience has
given her specific understanding of how violence against women is treated
by institutions — gives the investigation a dimension that Wallander's own
perspective cannot fully cover.

Her specific responses to what the investigation reveals — the evidence of
what was done to the four women in Africa, the specific ways in which their
deaths were ignored or suppressed, and the specific nature of Eleanor's
response — are given space in the episode, and they are not simply confirmatory
of Wallander's own position. She brings a different emotional and analytical
intelligence to the material.

--- THE INVESTIGATION CLOSES IN ---

Wallander's investigation of the victims' African histories, combined with
the forensic and procedural work on the specific murders, eventually identifies
Eleanor and establishes her specific connection to both the victims and to
the four women whose deaths she is avenging.

The specific evidence that establishes Eleanor's identity and her method is
assembled through the combination of Wallander's persistent investigative
work and Ann-Britt's specific contributions. The closing in on Eleanor — the
physical investigation that narrows her location and establishes when she will
strike next — creates the episode's climactic tension.

--- THE CONFRONTATION ---

The confrontation between Wallander and Eleanor is the episode's most emotionally
complex sequence. She is not denying what she has done — she has no interest
in denial. She is not remorseful — she has done what she intended to do. What
she has is a specific clarity about the justice she was serving and the
inadequacy of the systems that failed to serve it before she did.

Wallander listens to her with the specific quality of a man who hears what she
is saying and cannot simply dismiss it, even while he understands that he must
arrest her and that the law will process her as a killer. The specific exchange
between them — Eleanor's account of what happened in Africa and why she did
what she did, and Wallander's response — is the episode's most powerful scene.

--- RESOLUTION ---

Eleanor is arrested. The investigation is complete. The men who were her
intended victims who survived are alive but will live with the specific knowledge
of what was done in Africa and what it produced.

The episode ends with Wallander carrying the specific weight that "The Fifth
Woman" puts on him: he has done his job, and doing it required arresting a
woman whose actions were a response to specific, real, unaddressed violence.
The justice system will now process her as a murderer. Whether it will ever
address what she was responding to is not the system's immediate concern.

This gap — between the justice the system administers and the justice the
situation demanded — is what "The Fifth Woman" is about, and the episode
leaves Wallander sitting in it without the comfort of a resolution that
closes it.

--- BRANAGH'S WALLANDER ---

Kenneth Branagh's interpretation of Wallander differs from Krister Henriksson's
in specific ways that are visible in "The Fifth Woman." Branagh plays a man
in more evident psychological distress — the burden of the work more visibly
carried, the specific cost of investigating these cases more explicitly displayed.
His Wallander is less ordinary than Henriksson's, which has the effect of
making the suffering more operatic but perhaps less specifically true to
Mankell's conception of a detective whose ordinariness is essential to his
character. Both are valid interpretations; they represent different choices
about what to foreground in the same material.

--- NOTES ON THE EPISODE ---

"The Fifth Woman" is one of the most explicitly political episodes of the BBC
Wallander series and one of its most morally demanding. The case it investigates —
the specific intersection of male violence, female response, and institutional
failure — is handled with the seriousness it deserves. Philip Martin's direction
is assured, and Branagh's performance in the confrontation scene is among the
best he gives in the series. The episode's refusal to resolve the moral question
it poses — whether what Eleanor did is simply wrong or whether the context of
what produced it changes the calculation — is its most honest quality.
