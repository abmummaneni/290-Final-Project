NOV_376 — Last to Die
Tess Gerritsen, 2012
Full Plot Synopsis (Rizzoli & Isles #10)

THE SURVIVORS


Three Children, Three Massacres

The novel opens with a disturbing pattern that no one can yet explain. Over the course of
several months, three separate, apparently unconnected families have been massacred —
every member of each household killed in brutal incidents that look like targeted attacks
or home invasions. In each case, every person in the house died. Except one: a child,
who survived. Three families destroyed, three child survivors.

The three surviving children have no known connection to each other. Their families were
not linked by geography, profession, social circle, or apparent circumstance. And yet the
pattern is undeniable — each time a family was killed, one child was left. It is as if
the killer deliberately spared the children, or as if the children were somehow the point.

Detective Jane Rizzoli and Dr. Maura Isles are drawn into the investigation as the
pattern becomes clear and the fear grows that whatever produced it is not finished.


The Children

The three survivors are Teddy Clock, a teenage boy; Rat (real name not initially given),
a teenage girl; and Will Yablonski, another teenager. Each carries the specific trauma of
being the only person left alive in their household. Each survived in slightly different
circumstances, and the differences in how they survived become significant.

None of them fully understands why they are alive. Some of them have theories they have
not shared. And at least one of them is not telling the full truth about what they
remember.

EVENSONG


The School

Given the danger to the surviving children — whoever killed their families has demonstrated
a pattern of behavior suggesting ongoing threat — a decision is made to place the three
of them at Evensong, a private boarding school in rural Maine. Evensong is specifically
designed for children with difficult or dangerous backgrounds: it is a school with
enhanced security, a staff trained to work with traumatized young people, and a philosophy
of rehabilitation and protection.

The school's founder and headmistress is a woman named Lily Saul, who built Evensong
precisely for children like Teddy, Rat, and Will — children who have survived violence
and need both safety and therapeutic support. She is presented initially as wholly
dedicated to her students' welfare.


Something Wrong at Evensong

As Rizzoli and Maura investigate the massacres and pursue the question of why these three
children were spared, their attention increasingly turns toward Evensong. Several Evensong
students have died in the past — in ways that seemed like accidents or illness but which,
when examined in the context of the current pattern, begin to look like something else
entirely.

The school is not what it appears. Its history of student deaths, its specific selection
of students with certain backgrounds, and the nature of Lily Saul's interest in her
charges all become areas of investigation. The place built to protect children is itself
a source of danger, though the exact nature of that danger requires careful unraveling.

THE INVESTIGATION


Rizzoli's Approach

Jane Rizzoli pursues the case from the Boston end — investigating the massacred families,
building profiles of the victims, trying to find the connection between three apparently
unrelated households. She is working in her characteristic mode: tenacious, direct,
skeptical of received explanations, and personally invested in protecting the children
who have already lost everything.

Her investigation involves the forensic analysis of the three crime scenes, interviews
with anyone connected to the dead families, and attempts to construct a picture of what
the victims had in common that they didn't know they had in common.


Maura's Role

Maura works the forensic side — both the massacre victims and the Evensong students whose
past deaths are now being re-examined. Her work involves determining whether deaths
previously attributed to natural causes or accidents have forensic signatures suggesting
something else. The reexamination of old deaths is one of the novel's most technically
interesting threads, as Maura applies current forensic methodology to cases that were
closed without that scrutiny.


The Children as Witnesses

The three surviving children are not simply victims to be protected — they are also the
key to understanding what happened. Each of them knows or observed something that
contributed to their survival, and extracting that information without re-traumatizing them
or tipping off whoever may be watching requires care.

The relationships between the three teenagers are complicated by the extreme circumstances
of their shared situation. They have been brought together by the worst thing that has
ever happened to them, and Gerritsen develops the dynamic between them with attention —
the way trauma creates intimacy between people who might otherwise have nothing in common.

THE WIDER CONSPIRACY


Connecting the Families

The common thread between the three murdered families — the thing that made them targets —
is eventually established. It is not immediately obvious and requires the investigation to
dig beneath the surface presentations of three unremarkable households. What they shared
was not a crime or a business relationship but a connection to a specific event in the
past, or a specific person, or a specific knowledge — something that made them collectively
dangerous to whoever ordered the killings.

The killer, or the person who directed the killings, had a specific motivation for choosing
these families and a specific reason for leaving the children alive. The children were not
spared out of mercy. They were spared deliberately, and understanding why is the key to
understanding the whole operation.


Evensong's Real Purpose

Lily Saul and Evensong's role in the conspiracy is the novel's most disturbing revelation.
The school was built with genuine therapeutic intent but has been compromised — or was
always serving a second purpose alongside its stated one — and the connection between
Evensong, its selection of specific students, and the pattern of family massacres is
the architecture of the central crime.

THE CLIMAX AND RESOLUTION

As the investigation closes in on the truth and the conspirators behind the family
massacres understand that exposure is imminent, the danger to the three children at
Evensong becomes acute and immediate. The school, supposed to be their sanctuary, becomes
the site of the novel's climactic confrontation.

Rizzoli and Maura converge on Evensong as the crisis peaks. The confrontation involves
direct physical danger to the children and to the investigators. The conspiracy is exposed
and the people responsible for the massacres — and for the manipulation of Evensong — are
identified and brought to account.

The three surviving children — Teddy, Rat, and Will — come through the climax with their
lives, and with something that might eventually become the beginning of recovery: the
knowledge of why they were spared, and the fact that the people who killed their families
have been stopped.

The novel ends with Rizzoli reflecting on the cost and meaning of this case — the three
families annihilated, the children who carry that annihilation, and the specific darkness
that produces people willing to destroy entire households to protect a secret.
