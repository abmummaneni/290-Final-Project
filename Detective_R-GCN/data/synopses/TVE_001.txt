TVE_001 | Columbo: Murder by the Book | Director: Steven Spielberg | TV Episode

FULL EPISODE SYNOPSIS — SPOILERS INCLUDED
================================================================================

--- BACKGROUND AND CONTEXT ---

"Murder by the Book" is the first regular episode of the Columbo series proper,
airing September 15, 1971, as part of the NBC Mystery Movie wheel. It was
directed by Steven Spielberg, then twenty-four years old and already
demonstrating the visual fluency that would define his feature career. The
episode is widely considered one of the finest in the entire Columbo canon —
its opening is among the most precisely observed in the series, and the
cat-and-mouse dynamic between Columbo and the killer is established here with
a confidence and precision that the show would sustain for decades.

The episode stars Jack Cassidy as Ken Franklin, in the first of his three
Columbo appearances. Cassidy is one of the most effective of all Columbo
killers: charming, witty, self-regarding, and ultimately exposed by exactly
the quality — his vanity — that made him think he could not be caught.

--- THE KILLER AND THE VICTIM ---

Ken Franklin and Jim Ferris are co-authors of the "Mrs. Melville" mystery novel
series — an enormously successful franchise that has made both men wealthy.
The partnership, however, is deeply unequal: Jim Ferris is the actual writer,
the one with the creative gift, the one who constructs the plots and does the
work. Ken Franklin is the front man — charming, socially skilled, good at
publicity and appearances — but entirely without the ability to write a word
on his own.

Jim has decided to dissolve the partnership. He wants to write something
different, something more serious, without Ken. He tells Ken of this decision.
Ken immediately understands what this means: without Jim, Ken has nothing.
His income, his lifestyle, his public persona as a successful mystery author —
all of it depends on Jim continuing to write under their shared name. Without
Jim he is exposed as a fraud and financially ruined.

Ken decides to kill Jim.

--- THE MURDER ---

Ken invites Jim on a drive to Ken's cabin, ostensibly to talk things through.
The drive, the arrival at the cabin, and the killing are shown in the episode's
opening without ambiguity: Ken shoots Jim at the cabin, then stages the scene
to establish his alibi. He has arranged for Jim to make a phone call earlier
in the day that will establish a timeline inconsistent with Ken being present
at the time of death. He plants evidence suggesting Jim went elsewhere. He
drives back and establishes himself publicly in Los Angeles.

The scheme is carefully constructed. Ken is smart, and he has spent years
writing (or at least reading) murder plots. He applies the craft of the mystery
novel to the real crime.

--- COLUMBO ENTERS ---

Lieutenant Columbo is assigned to investigate Jim Ferris's death. He meets
Ken at the publishing house and the dynamic is established immediately: Columbo
appears distracted, disheveled, self-deprecating. He forgets things, seems
confused, compliments Ken on his success. Ken is charmed by and contemptuous
of Columbo in equal measure — he sees a bumbling detective whose incompetence
is reassuring.

Columbo is, of course, neither bumbling nor confused. From his first moments
at the scene and his first conversation with Ken, he is attending to everything.

--- THE COMPLICATIONS: LILY LA SANKA ---

The episode's second significant plot element involves a woman named Lily La
Sanka, who lives near Ken's cabin and who has a specific piece of information
that could damage Ken's alibi. Ken realizes this and decides he must kill her
too. This second murder — more improvised, more desperate than the first —
introduces the element that all the best Columbo episodes use: the killer
creating additional problems in the attempt to shore up their original scheme.

Lily's death, while Ken's work, is harder to conceal than Jim's. The improvised
nature of it leaves traces that Columbo finds.

--- COLUMBO'S INVESTIGATION ---

Columbo works through Ken's alibi with apparent casualness and actual precision.
The timeline Ken established — the phone calls, the movements, the witnesses —
is examined detail by detail. Each element holds on its own but, assembled
together, contains something that doesn't quite fit.

The specific evidence Columbo finds varies in its nature: some of it is physical
(something about the cabin, something about Ken's car or its mileage), some of
it is testimonial (what witnesses actually saw versus what Ken claims they saw),
and some of it is logical (the specific sequence of events Ken describes requires
something impossible or implausible to be true).

Columbo keeps returning to Ken. Always apologetically, always with "just one
more thing," always giving the impression that this is the last visit. Ken
becomes gradually more anxious beneath the surface charm. The irony — a man
who wrote murder mysteries for a living being investigated for murder — is
something Columbo notes with genuine apparent admiration.

--- THE TRAP ---

The episode's final act involves Columbo engineering a situation in which Ken
effectively confirms his guilt. The specific mechanism — a staged confrontation,
a piece of evidence that forces Ken into an impossible position, or a revelation
that collapses his alibi — is deployed with Spielberg's characteristic visual
clarity.

The trap works on Ken's vanity and his underestimation of Columbo. He has been
treating Columbo as an obstacle to be managed rather than an intelligence to
be respected, and this specific failure of perception is what undoes him.

--- RESOLUTION ---

Ken Franklin is arrested. The episode ends with Columbo having established
the truth of both murders — Jim Ferris's death at the cabin and Lily La Sanka's
improvised killing — through the accumulated evidence and Ken's inability to
sustain his alibi under the specific pressure Columbo applied.

--- NOTES ON THE EPISODE ---

Spielberg's direction is notably more visually ambitious than later Columbo
episodes. The opening is long and almost wordless — the camera watching Ken
and Jim's conversation, the drive, the killing — with a confidence in what
the image alone can communicate. The episode establishes the series' tonal
formula: the killer is shown fully, the viewer knows what happened, and the
pleasure is entirely in watching Columbo find the thread and pull. Cassidy's
Ken Franklin is a template for the Columbo killer — charming, intelligent,
underestimating, ultimately betrayed by his own self-regard.
