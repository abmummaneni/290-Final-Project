Post‑pandemic Three Pines and the lecture

The novel is set just after the end of the COVID‑19 pandemic, as Quebec is cautiously returning to gatherings and a sense of normal life. Chief Inspector Armand Gamache is in Three Pines over the holidays with Reine‑Marie, Jean‑Guy Beauvoir, their extended family, and the village’s usual cast, savoring peace after years of turmoil. The quiet is interrupted when Gamache is assigned a seemingly odd task for a homicide chief: providing security at a New Year’s season lecture at a nearby university, given by Professor Abigail Robinson, a statistician whose talks have gone viral.

Gamache digs into Robinson’s work and is horrified by what he finds. Her post‑pandemic analysis argues that society would be safer and more prosperous if it systematically “removed” its most vulnerable members: the elderly, chronically ill, and children born with disabilities. She frames this eugenic idea as logical public policy, using statistics from the pandemic to claim that letting the “weak” die spared resources, and that deliberately preventing or ending such lives would protect the “greater good.” Her slogan, widely shared on social media, is a deceptively reassuring “All will be well” (in French, “ça va bien aller”), which Gamache hears as chillingly ironic given her proposals.

Gamache begs the university’s president and chancellor, Colette Roberge, to cancel the lecture, warning it could incite violence from furious opponents or embolden dangerous supporters. Colette refuses, citing academic freedom and accusing him of cowardice for even suggesting it. Meanwhile, Three Pines hosts another controversial guest: Haniya Daoud, a Sudanese human‑rights hero and Nobel Peace Prize nominee whose past includes brutal torture and escape from sexual enslavement. She despises Robinson’s ideas, having seen vulnerable people targeted and murdered in real life, and her presence will later collide with the lecture’s fallout.
The lecture, the shooting attempt, and rising tension

Despite Gamache’s hope that the timing and winter weather will keep attendance low, the lecture hall is packed with a volatile mix of supporters and outraged protesters, many wearing “All will be well” buttons and gear. Gamache, Jean‑Guy, and their team set up security, acutely aware that Robinson’s message threatens people like Jean‑Guy’s daughter Idola, who has Down syndrome and, by Robinson’s logic, should never have been born. This personal stake creates deep inner conflict for both men: Gamache questions whether he can or should risk his life to protect someone advocating for children like Idola to be “weeded out,” while Jean‑Guy struggles with the disturbing thought that, on a purely utilitarian level, her argument could seem rational.

Robinson takes the stage with a calm, reasonable demeanor that sharpens the horror of her content. As the crowd grows more agitated—shouting, pushing, and reacting to her proposals—the atmosphere edges toward riot. Firecrackers go off in the auditorium, causing panic; in the chaos, someone manages to get a gun inside and shots are fired at Robinson on stage. In an instinctive act of duty, Gamache throws himself in front of her, shielding her with his own body and preventing her assassination. The police restore order, and Robinson survives, but Gamache is left shaken by the paradox of having saved a life whose work he abhors, while Jean‑Guy is wracked with guilt for momentarily abandoning his post at the door, a lapse that may have allowed the shooter’s weapon inside.

The shooter, a local man named Édouard Tardif, is arrested and claims he only meant to scare Robinson into reconsidering her message, not actually kill her. Gamache does not fully believe the surface explanation and senses that Robinson may still be in danger from others—either devout followers willing to martyr her or opponents driven beyond restraint. The lecture and near‑assassination ignite fierce public debate and personal anguish in Three Pines, as residents grapple with Robinson’s ideas and their own feelings about vulnerability, disability, and sacrifice.
New Year’s Eve in Three Pines and the murder

After the lecture, Robinson continues to gain notoriety and supporters, even as many find her claims repulsive. Her growing fame brings her physically closer to Three Pines when Colette Roberge invites Robinson and her assistant, Debbie Schneider, to stay and attend the village’s New Year’s Eve party at the Auberge. Haniya Daoud is also present, as is Vincent Gilbert, the irascible local doctor and “Asshole Saint,” a humanitarian known for his work with people with disabilities who finds Robinson’s proposals morally intolerable. At the party, Robinson has several tense confrontations with Haniya, Gilbert, and others who challenge her eugenic agenda, while fireworks and televised countdowns provide a deceptively festive background.

Just after midnight, a body is discovered in the snow near the inn, face‑down and struck in the back of the head. Given the circumstances and threats, Gamache initially assumes Professor Robinson herself has been murdered. When the body is turned over, however, it is revealed to be Debbie Schneider, Robinson’s assistant, not Robinson. The killing instantly turns the already fraught ideological conflict into a concrete homicide investigation, with a web of potential motives: resentment of Robinson’s message, anger at her growing influence, or secrets tied to her past.​

Gamache and his team must now juggle two linked crises: the attempted shooting at the lecture and the murder in Three Pines. They interrogate the guests and examine the interpersonal tensions exposed at the party. Haniya’s traumatic history and simmering rage, Gilbert’s protective advocacy for disabled people, Colette’s institutional loyalty and ambitions, Robinson’s controversial fame, and Debbie’s close proximity to Robinson all become strands in a complex tapestry of possible guilt. Gamache senses that the killing is not simply a spontaneous act of outrage; there are deeper, older wounds involved.
Past atrocities, Vincent Gilbert, and Abigail’s history

As Gamache digs into Debbie and Robinson’s backgrounds, he discovers connections to a dark chapter in medical and psychiatric history. Investigations into Vincent Gilbert reveal that, decades earlier when he was a young medical student, he worked under Ewen Cameron, a real‑life psychiatrist notorious for conducting non‑consensual, brutal experiments on patients, including so‑called “depatterning” therapies. Gamache uncovers evidence that Abigail Robinson’s mother was one of Cameron’s victims, subjected to these cruel treatments, which contributed to her psychological collapse and eventual suicide. Abigail grew up in the shadow of this trauma, with a mother shattered by institutional abuse and a legacy of suffering and stigma.​

Furthermore, Abigail appears to know that Gilbert was involved, however junior, in Cameron’s program. Gamache comes to believe that Robinson may have planned to blackmail Gilbert, threatening to expose his past complicity to destroy his reputation as a revered humanitarian doctor. This revelation reframes some of Gilbert’s hostility toward Robinson: it is not only moral outrage at her current proposals but also fear and shame linked to his own history. The interplay of victims and perpetrators becomes murky—Robinson is both the daughter of a victim of medical atrocities and the public advocate of new atrocities dressed up as rational policy.​

At the same time, Gamache continues to probe the shooting at the lecture. Édouard Tardif’s story does not fully satisfy him, and the team looks for connections between Tardif, Robinson, and anyone in her orbit, including Debbie and Colette. The ongoing presence of Haniya Daoud adds another moral dimension: someone who has survived atrocities and dedicated her life to protecting the vulnerable finds herself sharing space with a woman promoting a sanitized vision of genocide. This clash of lived experience and abstract theory underscores the stakes of Robinson’s ideas far beyond academic debate.
Debbie’s secret, the killer’s motive, and the climax

As the investigation proceeds, Gamache focuses more closely on Debbie Schneider’s role. Debbie had been Abigail’s assistant and confidante, privy to her private life, research, and personal history. Over time, Gamache realizes that the key to the murder lies not only in who wanted to kill Robinson, but in who had discovered something dangerous about Abigail herself. Through painstaking interviews and reconstruction of New Year’s Eve events, he uncovers that Debbie had stumbled on a critical secret Robinson was hiding.​

That secret concerns the authenticity of Robinson’s family story and how she has leveraged her mother’s suffering and her statistical expertise to build her platform. Once Debbie pieced together the truth, Abigail became terrified that her carefully constructed public persona and her growing influence would collapse if the secret came out. Debbie’s knowledge turned her from a trusted subordinate into a liability. On New Year’s Eve, as tensions mounted and everyone’s attention was split between celebrations and the looming ideological storm, Abigail confronted Debbie and, in a panic, struck and killed her, staging the scene in the snow near the inn.

When Gamache eventually identifies Abigail as the murderer and confronts her, she unravels under the pressure. In a final, disturbing attempt to maintain control of the narrative and her status as a martyr to the cause, she turns her cruelty directly on Jean‑Guy Beauvoir. Knowing that Jean‑Guy is the father of a child with Down syndrome—one of the very lives her proposals would prevent—she taunts him viciously, trying to provoke him into losing control and shooting her in anger in front of witnesses. If he did, she would die as a victim of the very “madness of crowds” she has been stoking, her movement possibly strengthened by her martyrdom.​

Jean‑Guy, despite his rage and anguish, refuses to take the bait. He holds his ground, restrains himself, and lets due process proceed. Abigail is arrested, ending both her personal campaign to spread euthanasia of the vulnerable and the immediate wave of violence circling her lectures. Gamache, seeing Jean‑Guy’s restraint, recognizes it as an act of profound courage: the choice not to respond to escalation with violence, even when personally attacked at the deepest level.​
Aftermath and themes

With Abigail in custody, the murder of Debbie Schneider and the attempted shooting at the lecture are finally resolved, though not without lingering scars. Vincent Gilbert must continue to live with the exposure of his early connection to Ewen Cameron’s abuses, stripping some of the shine from his saintly reputation and forcing him to confront his own complicity. Haniya Daoud returns to her global advocacy, having witnessed once again how easily people can be swayed by seemingly rational arguments for sacrificing the defenseless.

For Gamache and Jean‑Guy, the case leaves lasting moral questions. Gamache must live with having protected a woman whose ideas would have erased children like his beloved granddaughter, and with his decision to uphold the rule of law even when it meant saving someone he believes is profoundly wrong. Jean‑Guy grapples with the fleeting moments when Robinson’s cold logic nearly made sense to him, and with the knowledge that he chose restraint over vengeance when she tried to weaponize his love for Idola. In Three Pines, holiday life resumes—snow, shared meals, community rituals—but the villagers carry a sharper awareness of how fear, statistics, and “the greater good” can be twisted into justification for cruelty.

The title, The Madness of Crowds, reflects the novel’s central concern: how societies, shaken by catastrophe and grief, can be seduced into abandoning individual human worth in favor of dangerous collective “solutions.” Through the murder mystery and the clash between Abigail Robinson’s followers and her opponents, Penny explores cowardice, responsibility, and the fragile line between protecting the vulnerable and sacrificing them in the name of safety.
