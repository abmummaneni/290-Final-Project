--- BACKGROUND AND CONTEXT ---

"Unbelievable" is a Netflix limited series of eight episodes, first released
in September 2019. It is written by Susannah Grant, Ayelet Waldman, and Michael
Chabon, and directed by Lisa Cholodenko and Michael Dinner. It stars Kaitlyn
Dever as Marie Adler, Toni Collette as Detective Karen Duvall, and Merritt
Wever as Detective Grace Rasmussen.

The series is based on "An Unbelievable Story of Rape," a 2015 Pulitzer
Prize-winning article by T. Christian Miller and Ken Armstrong published in
ProPublica and The Marshall Project, which itself was the basis for the book
"A False Report." The events depicted are drawn directly from real events that
occurred in Colorado and Washington State between 2008 and 2011. The names of
most people involved have been changed.

"Unbelievable" is one of the most significant pieces of crime drama produced
in the 2010s — significant not primarily as entertainment but as a sustained,
accurate, and deeply serious account of systemic institutional failure in the
investigation of rape, and of what that failure costs specific women. It is
the most direct and most carefully researched treatment of this subject in
television drama.

--- MARIE ADLER ---

Marie Adler is eighteen years old. She has grown up in the foster care system,
has aged out of it, and is living independently for the first time in a
supervised apartment building near Lynnwood, Washington. She reports to the
police that she was raped in her apartment by a man who broke in, tied her up,
and assaulted her.

The investigation of her report is conducted by the Lynnwood police. It goes
wrong from the beginning.

The specific failure of the Lynnwood investigation of Marie's case is the
most painful thread in the series, and it is rendered with complete accuracy
to the actual events:

Two detectives — one male, one female — interview Marie multiple times. Each
time, her account has small inconsistencies — the kind of inconsistencies
that are entirely normal in trauma-affected memory but that the detectives treat
as indicators of fabrication. Two of her former foster mothers, contacted by
the police, express doubts about Marie's account — doubts rooted in their
specific knowledge of Marie's history of seeking attention, which they interpret
as providing a motive for false reporting.

Under sustained pressure from the detectives — who make clear, through their
manner and their questions, that they do not believe her — Marie recants. She
tells them she made the report up. She is then charged with filing a false
police report and fined.

She did not make it up. She was raped. And the institutional failure that
produced her recantation — the specific pressure, the specific doubt, the
specific way that a traumatised young woman with no institutional advocates
was managed by the people who were supposed to investigate her case — is the
series' central indictment of how rape investigations can fail.

--- THE GOLDEN INVESTIGATION: COLORADO ---

Two years after Marie's recantation, in Colorado, Detective Karen Duvall
investigates a rape in Golden. The victim is a woman who was attacked in
her apartment by a man who broke in, tied her up, photographed her, and
assaulted her. The method is specific, methodical, and suggests someone who
has done this before.

Duvall (Toni Collette) is a careful, experienced detective whose specific
approach — taking the victim's account seriously, investigating it thoroughly,
not making assumptions — is in direct contrast to the Lynnwood failure. Her
investigation of the Golden case is conducted with the procedural rigour that
the subject demands and with the specific respect for the victim that the
Lynnwood investigation denied Marie.

In Westminster, Colorado, Detective Grace Rasmussen (Merritt Wever) is
investigating a separate rape case whose method matches the Golden case exactly.
The two detectives make contact and establish that they are investigating the
same perpetrator — a serial rapist who has been operating across multiple
jurisdictions, whose specific method is consistent and whose specific operational
care (he uses a rape kit, he takes photographs, he binds his victims in a
specific way) suggests a level of preparation and repeatedness that the
investigation must account for.

--- DUVALL AND RASMUSSEN ---

The partnership between Karen Duvall and Grace Rasmussen is the series' central
positive relationship — two experienced detectives whose specific qualities
complement each other and whose specific professional commitment to doing the
investigation properly is the show's most sustained expression of what the
system can be when it works.

Toni Collette and Merritt Wever are both extraordinary in their roles. The
specific quality of their partnership — warm, productive, mutually respectful,
and entirely focused on finding the perpetrator and serving the victims — is
deliberately contrasted with the Lynnwood investigation's failure. These are
the same professional context — women who have been raped, police investigators —
producing entirely different outcomes based on entirely different approaches.

Their investigation is also, the series is careful to note, based on a specific
set of conditions that the Lynnwood investigation did not have: multiple victims
across multiple jurisdictions whose cases, connected, provide the kind of
evidence base that a single case cannot generate. The series does not simply
attribute the Colorado investigation's success to the detectives' superior
approach — it acknowledges that luck and circumstance played a role alongside
professional excellence.

--- THE INVESTIGATION: TRACING THE PERPETRATOR ---

Duvall and Rasmussen's investigation of the serial rapist proceeds with the
specific procedural thoroughness that the series renders with unusual accuracy.
The specific work — canvassing the areas around the attack scenes, analysing
the specific operational patterns, working with forensic evidence, and the
specific digital forensics that eventually cracks the case — is shown in detail.

A crucial development is the discovery of a specific car seen near multiple
attack scenes. The investigation of vehicle records, combined with the digital
forensics from the victims' own photographs (several victims had cameras in
their homes whose metadata provided specific location and time information that
the perpetrator was unaware of), eventually produces a specific lead.

The perpetrator is a man named Marc O'Leary — a former US Army soldier living
in Colorado whose specific operational discipline (the rape kit, the specific
binding methods, the photographs) reflects military training and careful
planning. His specific pattern of operation — identifying victims, planning
the approach, executing with methodical precision — is established through the
investigation.

O'Leary is identified and arrested. His home contains extensive documentation
of his assaults — photographs of his victims, the specific material that
constitutes the most comprehensive evidence of serial sexual violence that the
detectives have ever seen.

--- MARIE ADLER AND THE INVESTIGATION'S CONVERGENCE ---

The investigation of O'Leary's crimes leads back to Marie Adler. Among O'Leary's
photographs is a photograph of Marie — the assault in Lynnwood that she reported,
was disbelieved about, and was charged for fabricating. The photograph is
incontrovertible evidence that she was raped, that she told the truth, and
that the Lynnwood investigation failed her in every specific way that the series
has shown.

The specific moment when Detective Duvall makes contact with Marie — when
Marie learns that the investigation has found the proof of what she was not
believed to have experienced, that the person who raped her has been caught,
and that she did not lie — is the series' most significant emotional sequence.

The charge against Marie for filing a false police report is dropped. She
receives a financial settlement from the City of Lynnwood. The detectives
responsible for the original investigation face consequences.

--- THE COST TO MARIE ---

The series is careful and honest about what the investigation's eventual success
means for Marie. The truth was found. Justice — partial, late, imperfect —
was done. Marc O'Leary is prosecuted and receives a substantial prison sentence.

And none of this undoes what was done to Marie by the system that was supposed
to help her. The specific cost — of having been raped, of having reported it,
of having been disbelieved and pressured into recantation, of having been charged,
of having carried the specific stigma of "false reporter" for two years —
is real and is not erased by the eventual resolution.

Kaitlyn Dever's performance of Marie across the series — from the report through
the recantation through the years of carrying what happened to her — is among
the finest individual performances in the miniseries format in recent years.
The specific quality of someone managing an experience that has no adequate
language, who has been forced to disown the truth of what happened to her, and
who must then process the eventual confirmation that she was right all along —
is rendered with total commitment and total specificity.

--- THE SERIES' ARGUMENT ---

"Unbelievable" is not primarily about Marc O'Leary. It is about Marie Adler
and about the system that failed her, and about the specific conditions —
institutional, cultural, and procedural — that make that failure possible
and common.

The specific conditions the series identifies: the specific training (or lack
of training) that produces scepticism about rape reports rather than appropriate
investigation; the specific way that a victim's credibility is assessed through
criteria — consistency under repeated questioning, absence of visible distress —
that have no evidential basis; the specific vulnerability of women without
institutional advocates to pressure from the very institutions that should
be protecting them; and the specific way that the culture around rape reporting
makes recantation both likely and then used as evidence of false reporting.

The Lynnwood detectives are not monsters. They are people who operated within
a specific professional culture and with specific training that produced a
specific outcome. The series is not primarily interested in assigning personal
blame: it is interested in the system, and in what the system requires people
to do to function within it, and in what those requirements cost.

--- NOTES ON THE SERIES ---

"Unbelievable" is one of the most important crime dramas produced in the decade.
Its specific achievement — maintaining the truth and the seriousness of the
real events on which it is based while constructing a dramatic work that is
also compelling and human — is rare. The performances of Kaitlyn Dever, Toni
Collette, and Merritt Wever are all exceptional. The series' specific argument —
about institutional failure, about the specific conditions that produce that
failure, and about what it costs specific women — is made with the precision
and the sustained attention that the subject deserves.
