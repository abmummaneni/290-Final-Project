THE CASE: FOUR UNIDENTIFIED MURDER VICTIMS


The 1985 Discovery

In November 1985, a hunter in Bear Brook State Park in Allenstown, New Hampshire, came
across a 55-gallon steel drum near a trail in the park. Inside the drum were the
skeletal remains of two people: an adult woman and a young child, estimated to be between
five and eleven years old. Both had been murdered. Neither was identified. They had
evidently been dead for some years before their discovery.

The case was investigated by New Hampshire state police. The victims could not be
identified through any standard means available in 1985: no one reported them missing,
no matching missing persons reports were found, and forensic technology at the time
was insufficient to determine identity from skeletal remains alone. The case went cold.
The woman and child were buried in a Allenstown cemetery, their identities unknown.


The 2000 Discovery

In May 2000, metal detector enthusiasts searching in Bear Brook State Park — in an
area approximately 100 feet from where the first drum was found in 1985 — discovered
a second 55-gallon steel drum. Inside were two more sets of remains: a second woman
and a second young child. The same disposal method — the same kind of drum, in the
same park — made it immediately clear that the two discoveries were connected. All four
victims had been killed by the same person or persons.

The additional remains were also unidentified. Law enforcement now had four murder
victims, all unidentified, connected by their disposal but with no known identity for
any of them. The case remained open and cold through the 2000s and 2010s.

THE INVESTIGATION: FORENSIC EVOLUTION


The Problem of Identification

The Bear Brook case sat in cold storage for decades not because investigators were
uninterested but because the tools to solve it did not exist. Identifying human remains
from skeletal evidence alone, without DNA databases and without forensic genealogy
technology, requires either a match to a specific missing persons report or a specific
known individual's DNA — neither of which was available for these victims.

The case became a testing ground and a beneficiary of the successive waves of forensic
technological development that transformed cold case investigation in the 2000s and 2010s.


The Isotope Analysis

In the early 2010s, advances in isotopic analysis — the examination of chemical isotopes
in bone and tooth enamel that reflect the geographical origins of food and water consumed
during childhood — were applied to the Bear Brook remains. The analysis suggested that
at least one of the victims had origins in the American Midwest or Pacific Northwest,
narrowing the geographic range for missing persons investigations.


The DNA Profiles

DNA was eventually extracted from the Bear Brook remains, providing genetic profiles for
the four victims. The profiles could not produce identifications without a database match,
but they could establish relationships: the DNA confirmed that the adult woman found in
the second drum was not the mother of the two children found with the first adult woman.
The family relationships among the four victims were more complex than a simple mother-
and-children scenario.


The Second Adult Woman's Identification

In 2017, the second adult woman — found in the 2000 discovery — was identified through
DNA and genealogical research as Marlyse Elizabeth Honeychurch, a Massachusetts woman
who had been missing since the late 1970s. Her identification was a significant
breakthrough and opened new investigative avenues.


Forensic Genealogy and the First Victim

The identification of Marlyse Honeychurch used early versions of the forensic genealogy
methods that would later identify the Golden State Killer. The same methods were applied
to the first adult woman — the woman found in 1985 — and eventually to the children,
with the goal of identifying all four victims.

Bear Brook is one of the earliest and most prominent cases in which forensic genealogy
was used for victim identification (as opposed to suspect identification), and the podcast
documents this process in detail.

BOB EVANS / TERRY PEDER RASMUSSEN


The Suspect

As the identities of the victims were being established, investigators also developed
a suspect profile and eventually an identification. The man responsible for the Bear
Brook murders is believed to be Terry Peder Rasmussen, a California man who died in
prison in 2010 while serving time for an unrelated domestic violence conviction.

Rasmussen operated under multiple aliases throughout his life, including "Bob Evans" —
the name under which he had relationships with women and under which he appears in
several of the investigative threads. He was a transient, a man who moved frequently,
who had relationships with women that sometimes involved their children, and who has
been connected to multiple suspicious deaths beyond the Bear Brook victims.


His Background and Other Possible Crimes

Rasmussen's history, assembled from records and witnesses across multiple states, paints
a picture of a man who preyed on vulnerable women, often with children, and who had
a pattern of making women disappear. The Bear Brook victims were women and children
who had been in his life and who were then found in barrels in a New Hampshire state
park.

Beyond Bear Brook, investigators and journalists who followed the case identified
additional suspicious deaths and disappearances associated with Rasmussen across
California and elsewhere. He is believed to have been responsible for more murders
than the four confirmed Bear Brook victims.


Rasmussen's Death Before Resolution

The specific difficulty of the Bear Brook case's resolution is that Rasmussen died in
2010 — before the forensic genealogy breakthroughs that produced the identifications
of his victims. He was never arrested or charged for the Bear Brook murders, and the
case never went to trial. The identification of him as the perpetrator is based on
forensic evidence — DNA comparison between his remains (obtained from prison records)
and the victims — rather than on a conviction.

THE CHILDREN AND THEIR IDENTITIES

The identification of the four children found in the drums — particularly their specific
identities and relationships to the adult women and to Rasmussen — was an ongoing
investigation at the time of the podcast's production. Forensic genealogy continued
to be applied to the children's DNA profiles in the years following the podcast's
release.

The specific identities of the children and the full picture of their relationships —
which children were Honeychurch's, which were Rasmussen's biological children, what
their family histories were — is the most emotionally urgent unresolved dimension of
the case. These were small children, discarded in steel barrels in a New Hampshire
forest, whose names the world did not know for decades.

JASON MOON'S PRODUCTION

Bear Brook is distinguished not only by the extraordinary nature of its subject but
by the quality of its production. Jason Moon's reporting is precise and thorough, and
the podcast's use of audio from investigators, forensic scientists, and genealogists
makes it an unusually rich documentary of how modern cold case investigation actually
works. It is less about a single journalist's investigation and more about the collective
effort of scientists, detectives, and genealogists working across years and technologies
to give four murder victims back their names.

The podcast won the Overseas Press Club of America Award for Outstanding Audio
Journalism and is widely cited as one of the finest short-form documentary podcasts
ever produced.
