--- BACKGROUND AND CONTEXT ---

"Death on the Nile" is a feature-length episode of ITV's Agatha Christie's
Poirot, first broadcast in April 2004. It is directed by Andy Wilson and stars
David Suchet as Hercule Poirot. It is based on Christie's 1937 novel of the
same name — one of the most beloved and most widely read of all the Poirot
novels, and one that has been adapted for film and television multiple times
(most famously the 1978 theatrical film with Peter Ustinov). The 2004 Suchet
version is widely regarded as the finest screen adaptation of the novel, more
faithful to Christie's text and more psychologically rich than the Ustinov
version, which prioritised the glamour of the Egyptian setting over the
emotional depth of the characters.

The novel is set on a paddle steamer cruising the Nile from Aswan to Abu Simbel,
with a cast of characters whose complicated personal histories and financial
and romantic entanglements provide multiple layers of motive for the murder
at the story's center. The Egyptian setting — the temples, the desert, the
ancient river — functions as a kind of moral backdrop: a landscape in which
human passions seem both vast and negligible against the scale of history.

--- LINNET RIDGEWAY AND SIMON DOYLE ---

The story begins not on the Nile but in England, where Poirot first encounters
the situation that will culminate in murder. Linnet Ridgeway is extraordinarily
wealthy, extraordinarily beautiful, and accustomed to getting everything she
wants with very little friction. She is about to marry Simon Doyle, a charming
young man with excellent prospects who has — until very recently — been engaged
to Linnet's best friend, Jacqueline de Bellefort.

Jacqueline is the abandoned fiancée: she introduced Simon to Linnet, never
suspecting that her best friend would take him from her. The betrayal is double —
both the man she loved and the woman she trusted have conspired, however
unconsciously, to destroy her. Jacqueline confronts Poirot in London and tells
him, with the specific clarity of someone at the end of their resources, that
she will follow Simon and Linnet wherever they go. She will not be put aside.
She will not disappear. They will not have their happiness while she has nothing.

Poirot is disturbed by this conversation — he sees in Jacqueline's passion
something that can only end badly — and attempts to dissuade her from her
course. She is not to be dissuaded.

--- THE CRUISE AND THE PASSENGERS ---

Linnet and Simon are honeymooning in Egypt. And Jacqueline de Bellefort is
there, on every boat, at every hotel — following them as she promised, a
constant, haunting presence that poisons the honeymoon and that Linnet finds
both infuriating and frightening.

When the party boards the Karnak, the paddle steamer on which the main action
takes place, the passenger list is a collection of people many of whom have
some connection to or grievance against the fabulously wealthy Linnet Ridgeway.
The cast is extensive and is one of the pleasures of the novel and the
adaptation — Christie populates the Karnak with tremendous variety and
specificity.

Among the passengers:

Colonel Race — a British intelligence officer who is acquainted with Poirot
and who is on the cruise in an unofficial capacity. He becomes Poirot's partner
in the investigation.

Mrs. Allerton and her son Tim — English tourists, old money, pleasant company.
Tim Allerton has a connection to the criminal world that is not immediately
apparent.

Miss Van Schuyler and her companion Miss Bowers, and her niece Cornelia Robson —
a wealthy and difficult American woman and her entourage.

Dr. Bessner — a German physician on holiday with his patient.

Linnet's maid Louise Bourget — a French woman with her own agenda.

Mr. Pennington — Linnet's American trustee, who is present on the cruise and
who, it will emerge, has been systematically defrauding her estate.

Mr. Ferguson — a young man with radical political views who is contemptuous
of the wealthy passengers and particularly hostile to Linnet.

Andrew Pennington's business associate Fleetwood.

Salome Otterbourne — a writer of lurid romantic fiction, accompanied by her
daughter Rosalie, who is embarrassed by her mother and by the family's
precarious finances.

And Jacqueline de Bellefort — who is, to Linnet and Simon's horror, aboard
the same vessel.

--- LINNET'S DEATH ---

During the night, while the Karnak is moored at Abu Simbel, Linnet Ridgeway
Doyle is shot through the head in her cabin. She is found dead in the morning.
The murder weapon — a small pistol — is missing. On the wall of the cabin,
written in what appears to be Linnet's own blood but is actually lipstick, is
the letter J.

The apparent implication is immediate and obvious: Jacqueline de Bellefort,
the spurned ex-fiancée who followed the couple to Egypt and threatened them,
who carries a pistol of the relevant caliber and has been visible and hostile
throughout the cruise.

But Jacqueline has an alibi. The previous evening, in a public scene witnessed
by many of the passengers, she shot and wounded Simon Doyle in an apparent
fit of passion — a dramatic incident that left Simon injured and in the care
of Dr. Bessner for the rest of the night. After the shooting, Jacqueline was
observed, intoxicated and distressed, in the saloon. She was never alone and
unobserved during the period when Linnet was killed. She cannot have committed
the murder.

--- THE INVESTIGATION ---

Poirot and Colonel Race investigate the murder in the specific conditions of
a crime committed on a vessel: all the passengers are confined to the ship,
the crime scene is intact, and the pool of suspects is limited. But the
investigation is complicated by two further deaths that occur during its course.

Louise Bourget, Linnet's maid, is found murdered — she has been killed before
she could tell Poirot something she knew, something she was apparently going
to sell rather than simply report. Her death establishes that someone on the
ship is willing to kill multiple times to suppress evidence.

Mrs. Salome Otterbourne is shot while in the middle of making a statement to
Poirot — she had seen something, or had information about something she
witnessed, and was killed at the exact moment of disclosure. This killing,
committed in front of Poirot and Race, is particularly disturbing precisely
because the killer was willing to act even under these specific circumstances.

--- THE MULTIPLE MOTIVES ---

Poirot's investigation maps an extraordinary range of motives for Linnet's
death, which is one of the novel's and episode's great structural pleasures:

Jacqueline de Bellefort — the obvious suspect, the woman with the most personal
reason to hate Linnet, who cannot have done it.

Andrew Pennington — the trustee who has been defrauding Linnet's estate and
who would be ruined by the full accounting that Linnet was apparently about
to demand.

Tim Allerton — who, it emerges, is connected to a ring of jewel thieves, and
whose presence on the cruise is not coincidental.

Mr. Ferguson — whose contempt for the wealthy is more personal and more specific
than his political posturing suggests.

Simon Doyle himself — the widowed husband, now heir to an enormous fortune.

The apparent jealousy and passion of the Jacqueline situation has provided
such a vivid and immediate framework for the murder that almost every passenger
accepts it as the obvious explanation and has barely considered whether anyone
else might have had reason to want Linnet dead.

--- THE SOLUTION: SIMON AND JACQUELINE ---

The solution to "Death on the Nile" is one of Christie's most audacious
constructions and one of her most emotionally devastating. The killer is Simon
Doyle. And Jacqueline de Bellefort is his accomplice.

They planned the murder together. Their apparent relationship — the jilted
fiancée following the happy couple, the dramatic confrontation, the shooting
of Simon in the saloon — was performed. Jacqueline never stopped loving Simon.
Simon, for all his apparent passion for Linnet, was primarily in love with
her money. Their plan was for Simon to marry Linnet, for Jacqueline to follow
them and play the role of the obsessed ex-lover (which required the public
display of hostility and the dramatic shooting), and for Simon — once Linnet
was dead and he was her heir — to have both the fortune and Jacqueline.

The shooting in the saloon was staged. Jacqueline shot Simon in a carefully
planned and non-fatal way. The wound bled convincingly and gave Simon an
excuse to be in Dr. Bessner's care, establishing an alibi that was even better
than Jacqueline's: he was injured, attended by a physician, and physically
incapacitated during the time of the murder. He slipped out, killed Linnet,
wrote the J in lipstick to direct suspicion at Jacqueline, and returned before
his absence was noticed.

Louise Bourget saw Simon leave or return and was blackmailing him — she was
killed to prevent her testimony. Mrs. Otterbourne also saw something relevant
and was killed before she could report it.

--- POIROT'S REVELATION ---

The specific evidence Poirot finds is technical but characteristic: the pistol
used to kill Linnet was later found in Jacqueline's possession — but it could
not have been the same pistol she used to shoot Simon, because she never had
time to recover and clean it during the night. There must be two pistols. Two
pistols means a conspiracy. The conspiracy involves Simon.

Poirot confronts Simon and Jacqueline together with the full account of what
he has deduced. Their responses — the specific quality of how each of them
behaves when faced with the complete truth — confirm what Poirot has assembled.

Jacqueline, when the case is fully established, kills Simon and then herself.
She cannot allow the plan they made together, which has now destroyed everything —
Linnet, Louise, Mrs. Otterbourne, their own futures — to end in a prison dock.
If Simon is taken, she will not live without him, and she will not let him
live to face it alone. It is a terrible, specific act of love.

--- THE EMOTIONAL WEIGHT OF THE RESOLUTION ---

The ending of "Death on the Nile" is not triumphant. Three people who were
all alive at the beginning of the cruise are dead by its end, plus the two
additional murder victims. The specific tragedy is not simply the original
crime but what the crime did to Jacqueline: a woman who was, by most accounts,
genuinely good and genuinely loving, who was betrayed by her best friend and
her lover, and who then allowed that betrayal to consume her entirely. Her
complicity in Linnet's murder is not presented as incomprehensible — it is
the specific outcome of a specific destruction, of what happens when someone
whose capacity for love is very great is treated as expendable.

Poirot is characteristically affected. He told Jacqueline in London, before
any of this happened, that her course could only end badly. He was right.
Being right does not comfort him.

--- NOTES ON THE EPISODE ---

The 2004 "Death on the Nile" is a remarkably faithful and emotionally rich
adaptation. Andy Wilson's direction makes full use of the Egyptian setting —
the temples of Abu Simbel, the desert, the Nile itself — without allowing
the visual splendor to overwhelm the character work. The performances are
uniformly strong, and the central relationship between Linnet, Simon, and
Jacqueline — the specific triangle that drives the tragedy — is rendered with
real emotional intelligence. This is one of the episodes that demonstrates
most clearly what the Suchet series could achieve at its best: Christie's
story told at its full emotional weight, without sentimentality and without
reduction.
