--- BACKGROUND AND CONTEXT ---

"Unforgotten" is a British ITV crime drama created by Chris Lang, each series
of which investigates a single cold case — a death that occurred many years
in the past whose circumstances are revealed only when new evidence surfaces
in the present day. The show is notable for its dual structure: the cold case
investigation is conducted in the present by the police, while the past is
gradually revealed through the lives of the people connected to it, showing
how the original crime shaped the decades that followed.

Series 4, broadcast in 2021, is the first series following the death of the
show's original lead detective, DCI Cassie Stuart (played by Nicola Walker),
who died at the end of Series 3 in a road accident. Series 4 introduces a new
lead detective, DCI Jessie James (played by Sinéad Keenan), partnered with
Cassie's established partner DS Sunny Khan (Sanjeev Bhaskar). The series
addresses both a new cold case and the emotional weight of Sunny's grief for
his lost colleague and friend.

--- THE COLD CASE: MATTHEW READ ---

Series 4's cold case concerns the death of Matthew Read, a young man who
died thirty years earlier under circumstances that were never fully explained.
Matthew's remains are discovered in the present day, bringing the old case
back to life.

Matthew was a young, gay man living in London in the early 1990s — a period
of intense social and institutional hostility toward gay men, during the AIDS
crisis and under Section 28, when homophobia was both socially normalised and
legally sanctioned in various forms. His death occurred in this specific
context, and the investigation of what happened to him must reckon with the
world in which it occurred.

--- THE FOUR SUSPECTS ---

As in all Unforgotten series, the discovery of the cold case body leads the
investigation to a small group of people from the past who were connected to
the victim. Series 4's four main suspects are:

Dean Barton — now a respected prison officer, a family man, carrying the
weight of something from his youth that he has never disclosed. He is a decent
person in his present life whose past contains a specific act that is the
subject of the investigation.

Liz Baildon — a senior local government official, a woman of evident
competence and public service, whose connection to Matthew's death is buried
deep in a past she has spent thirty years trying not to revisit.

Ram Sidhu — a university academic whose career has been built on his public
commitment to social justice and equality, and whose private history contains
a specific contradiction with those stated values.

Fiona Grayson — a former teacher whose proximity to the events of thirty
years ago was not voluntary and who has been living with the knowledge of
what she saw or knew ever since.

Each of these four people has spent thirty years building lives that are, in
their present forms, genuinely good or at least ordinary. Each of them carries
something from the past. The investigation's pressure on the present lives
of these four — the specific costs of revelation for their families, careers,
and self-understandings — is the series' central dramatic territory.

--- THE WORLD OF THE EARLY 1990S ---

The flashback sequences — showing Matthew's world in the early 1990s and
the circumstances that connected him to the four suspects — are rendered with
attention to the specific conditions of that period for young gay men in
Britain. The hostility, both institutional and social; the specific dangers
of being out or visible; the AIDS crisis and its effects on community and
self-perception; and the ways in which violence against gay men was routinely
dismissed or inadequately investigated by the police of the period.

Matthew's death is not separate from this context. The investigation must
understand what happened to him in the light of the world he was living in —
a world in which his specific vulnerability was created and sustained by
the society around him.

--- JESSIE JAMES ---

DCI Jessie James is introduced as a character with her own complications:
she has a difficult family situation, specific personal history that affects
her professional approach, and the particular challenge of stepping into a
role — leading this unit, working with Sunny — that was defined by someone
whose death is still recent and raw.

Her relationship with Sunny is initially uneasy. Sunny is grieving for Cassie
and is not fully present in the new partnership. Jessie is trying to establish
herself in a role that carries the weight of her predecessor's reputation.
The development of their working relationship — from wariness to mutual respect
to something approaching genuine partnership — is the series' central
character arc alongside the cold case investigation.

--- SUNNY'S GRIEF ---

Sunny Khan's grief for Cassie Stuart is one of Series 4's most carefully
handled elements. He is a man who is professionally functional but personally
hollowed out — going through the motions of the job while carrying the absence
of someone who was his closest colleague for years. His management of this
grief, and the specific ways it affects his engagement with the new partnership
and with the investigation, is rendered with Sanjeev Bhaskar's characteristic
quiet precision.

--- WHAT HAPPENED TO MATTHEW ---

The investigation establishes the circumstances of Matthew's death:

Matthew was killed as the result of a confrontation that escalated into
violence. The specific people involved, and the specific sequence of events,
is assembled through both the physical evidence and the testimony — eventually —
of those who were present or knew what happened.

The killing was connected to homophobia — to the specific contempt and fear
of Matthew's identity that the early 1990s context produced in certain people.
It was not a premeditated murder but a violent act whose perpetrators were
never held accountable, partly because the investigation at the time was
inadequate, partly because the specific social context made Matthew a victim
whose death was not urgently pursued by the institutions that should have
investigated it.

The four suspects' various levels of involvement range from direct participation
to witness to complicity in the subsequent cover-up. None of them are simply
innocent. None of them are simply monsters. The series' characteristic moral
texture — people who did terrible things and then lived good lives, and the
question of what justice means in that context — is fully present.

--- THE REVELATION AND ITS CONSEQUENCES ---

The investigation closes in on the truth over the series' six episodes. Each
of the four suspects is confronted with what the investigation knows — and
what it still needs from them. Their responses, as the pressure builds, are
psychologically varied and convincing: some confess, some deflect, some try
to protect others, some collapse under the weight of thirty years of carried
guilt.

The person most directly responsible for Matthew Read's death is Dean Barton —
whose specific act of homophobic violence in the early 1990s, in a group
context that also implicated Ram Sidhu and left Liz Baildon and Fiona Grayson
as witnesses and subsequent concealers, is established through the investigation's
accumulation of testimonial and physical evidence. The specific distribution
of culpability among the four — Dean as the primary actor, the others complicit
in varying degrees in both the original violence and the thirty-year cover-up —
is the investigation's most morally demanding determination.

The series' emotional climax involves the specific cost of revelation for
the people whose present lives are destroyed by the truth of their past — not
simply the person most guilty but the people around them, who had no knowledge
of what they were living alongside.

--- THE ENDING: JESSIE JAMES ---

Series 4 ends with a development that mirrors the conclusion of Series 3 for
Cassie Stuart: Jessie James is seriously injured in a road accident near the
series' close. She survives, but the echo of Cassie's death — the same kind
of event, the same shattering of a detective in the middle of her work —
is clearly deliberate. The show is making a statement about the costs of
the work and about the continuity of loss.

--- RESOLUTION ---

The cold case of Matthew Read's death is resolved: the responsible parties
are identified, the truth of what happened thirty years ago is established,
and the inadequacy of the original investigation — rooted in the homophobia
of the period — is acknowledged.

The justice achieved is partial and painful, as it always is in Unforgotten:
the people responsible are held accountable, but the thirty years cannot be
undone, Matthew cannot be brought back, and the lives of the people connected
to the case — the guilty and the innocent — are permanently altered by the
investigation's conclusion.

--- NOTES ON THE SERIES ---

Series 4 of Unforgotten is a strong entry in one of British television's most
consistent crime drama franchises. The cold case of Matthew Read is both a
compelling mystery and a considered examination of a specific historical
injustice — the routine dismissal of violence against gay men in early 1990s
Britain. The introduction of Jessie James is handled with care, and the
continuity with Sunny's grief for Cassie gives the new partnership an
emotional depth that a clean break would not have had.
