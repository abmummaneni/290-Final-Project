NOV_383 | The Cuckoo's Calling | Robert Galbraith | Novel
================================================================================
FULL PLOT SYNOPSIS — SPOILERS INCLUDED
================================================================================

--- BACKGROUND AND SETTING ---

"The Cuckoo's Calling," published in 2013 under the pseudonym Robert Galbraith,
is the first novel in J.K. Rowling's Cormoran Strike series. The pseudonym was
maintained for a short period before Rowling's authorship was revealed, at which
point the novel became a bestseller. It introduces two central characters:
Cormoran Strike, a private detective and war veteran (he lost the lower part of
one leg in Afghanistan, wears a prosthesis, and is physically and personally
battered at the novel's opening), and Robin Ellacott, his temporary receptionist
who becomes his indispensable partner.

The novel is set in London, predominantly in the Soho and Mayfair worlds of
celebrity, fashion, music, and the superrich. It engages with the intersection
of extreme wealth, celebrity culture, and the specific vulnerabilities of people
who live their lives entirely in the public eye.

--- CORMORAN STRIKE AT HIS LOWEST ---

The novel opens with Strike in a state of profound personal and professional
crisis. His detective agency is failing — he has almost no clients and is
deeply in debt. He has recently ended his long, turbulent relationship with
Charlotte Campbell, a beautiful and deeply troubled woman whose relationship
with Strike has been one of the central dramas of his adult life. He is sleeping
on a camp bed in his own office.

Into this wreckage arrives Robin Ellacott, sent by a temp agency to fill the
receptionist position. Robin is engaged to be married to Matthew, has a
conventional life ahead of her, and absolutely loves detective work — a passion
she has harbored since a traumatic event in her past. The collision between her
proper, conventional existence and the chaotic reality of Strike's agency
begins immediately, and the novel traces the development of a professional
partnership that is one of contemporary crime fiction's most satisfying
relationships.

--- THE DEATH OF LULA LANDRY ---

The case is brought to Strike by John Bristow — a man who was, by adoption,
the brother of Lula Landry. Lula Landry was one of the most famous models
in the world — extraordinarily beautiful, troubled, mixed-race (her Black birth
father was never identified), and enormously famous. She died three months
before the novel's opening by falling from the balcony of her luxury Mayfair
apartment in the early hours of a winter morning.

The official verdict was suicide. Lula was known to have had mental health
problems; she had been in a psychiatric facility. She was alone in her flat
that night (her boyfriend Evan Duffield had left in the hours before her death
and was not with her when she fell). She left no note. The police investigation
concluded it was suicide, and this verdict was accepted.

John Bristow refuses to accept it. He is convinced his sister was murdered.
He hires Strike, paying him a retainer that Strike desperately needs, to
investigate.

Strike's initial skepticism — suicides are far more common than murders made
to look like suicides — gives way as he begins to look at the evidence.

--- THE INVESTIGATION: LULA'S WORLD ---

The investigation takes Strike and Robin into Lula Landry's world: the London
fashion and celebrity circuit, with its extraordinary wealth, its hangers-on,
its drugs, and its peculiar emotional economy. Lula moved among people who
were famous, very rich, or both, and the investigation requires interviewing
a cast of characters who are all, in various ways, not entirely straightforward
about what they know.

The key figures connected to Lula at the time of her death include:

Evan Duffield — Lula's boyfriend, a troubled young actor with a drug problem.
He was with Lula on the night of her death, left after an argument, and was
absent when she fell. He is the obvious suspect but has an alibi for the time
of death.

Ciara Porter — a model friend of Lula's, young, somewhat vapid but not without
genuine feeling for Lula. She was with Duffield after he left Lula's apartment.

Deeby Macc — a famous American rapper who was supposed to be staying in the
apartment directly below Lula's. He arrived late and is connected to the
investigation tangentially.

Rochelle Onifade — a young Black woman who was Lula's friend, someone she
had met during her psychiatric hospitalization. Rochelle is from a very
different social world than Lula's celebrity circle. She is killed during the
investigation — a murder that Strike takes as evidence that someone is actively
suppressing information about Lula's death.

Guy Somé — Lula's flamboyant fashion designer and close friend, who was
devoted to Lula and who has information he is reluctant to share.

Tansy Bestigui — a neighbor in the apartment building who claims to have heard
Lula's voice crying out just before her fall, and who was a witness whose
testimony was crucial to the original investigation.

John Bristow himself — the client. His devotion to his sister's memory is
genuine, but Strike's habit of not taking anything at face value means he
does not simply accept Bristow's version of events.

--- LULA'S SEARCH FOR HER BIRTH FATHER ---

A thread that Strike identifies as crucially important is Lula's search for
her biological father in the months before her death. Lula, mixed-race and
adopted, was deeply interested in her Black heritage and was specifically trying
to discover who her birth father was. She apparently made significant progress
in this search shortly before her death — she learned something, or found
someone.

The discovery of her birth father's identity — and more importantly, the
discovery that she had a biological half-brother — is the investigation's
most important thread. Lula's will was recently revised, and the revision has
financial significance that depends on who is included in it.

--- FINANCIAL MOTIVE ---

Lula Landry was extremely wealthy. The revision of her will, made shortly before
her death, left a very substantial sum to her newly discovered half-brother —
a young Black man from a completely different background, someone she had met
and connected with in the weeks before her death.

John Bristow, by contrast — her adoptive brother, who has presented himself
as devoted to her memory — would have expected to be a significant beneficiary
of Lula's estate. The revised will changes this substantially.

Strike realizes that Bristow's stated motive for hiring him (love for his
sister, refusal to accept the suicide verdict) may coexist with, or be
overridden by, a financial motive of his own.

--- THE TRUTH ABOUT THAT NIGHT ---

Strike's investigation reconstructs the night of Lula's death with increasing
precision. The key facts:

Lula was visited that night by her newly discovered half-brother, Jonah Agyeman —
a man whose existence was not widely known and whose visit was secret. CCTV
footage that Strike examines carefully shows a young Black man entering and
leaving the building. This person was not Deeby Macc (who was in the downstairs
apartment) but someone else.

Tansy Bestigui's testimony about hearing Lula's voice was genuine — she did
hear a voice, but Strike determines that what she heard was not a struggle but
something else, and her account was shaped by the circumstances of where she
was (she was engaged in activity she preferred not to admit to).

Lula did not jump from her balcony in despair. She was pushed. The killer
entered through the apartment, confronted her, and threw her from the balcony.

--- JOHN BRISTOW AS THE KILLER ---

The killer is John Bristow.

Bristow killed his adoptive sister Lula because he stood to inherit from her
previous will — a will that the revised version substantially altered in favor
of Jonah Agyeman. Bristow knew about the revision and understood what it meant
for his financial future.

He hired Strike not out of grief or devotion but as part of a calculated plan:
he anticipated that an investigation would uncover the existence of Jonah
Agyeman as Lula's half-brother, and he wanted Strike to be the one to expose
this publicly, since Jonah (as someone with a secret connection to Lula and
a financial motive through the revised will) could then be cast as the most
plausible suspect in Lula's death. Bristow would be presenting himself as the
grieving brother seeking justice while actually directing suspicion at the
man he feared.

The murder of Rochelle Onifade was committed by Bristow to prevent her from
telling Strike what she knew about Jonah Agyeman's existence and Lula's
relationship with him.

--- THE CONFRONTATION ---

Strike works out the full truth and confronts Bristow. The confrontation is
tense and dangerous — Bristow, cornered, is capable of further violence, and
Strike's physical disability (his prosthetic leg) is a real limitation in
a physical confrontation.

Strike survives. Bristow is arrested and faces charges for both Lula's murder
and Rochelle's murder.

--- ROBIN AND STRIKE ---

Throughout the investigation, Robin has proved herself repeatedly. She is not
simply a receptionist but an investigator by temperament and intelligence —
perceptive, resourceful, and utterly committed to the work. The novel ends
with her temp assignment technically complete and her engagement to Matthew
continuing, but it is entirely clear to both the reader and (on some level) to
Robin that she has found her vocation.

The professional partnership between Strike and Robin is the series' great
pleasure: two people who are, in their different ways, extremely good at what
they do and who bring out the best in each other.
