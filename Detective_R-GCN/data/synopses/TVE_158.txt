--- BACKGROUND AND CONTEXT ---

"Only Murders in the Building" is an American mystery comedy series created
by Steve Martin and John Hoffman, streaming on Hulu from 2021. The show
follows three residents of the Arconia, a grand apartment building on the
Upper West Side of Manhattan, who become amateur true crime podcasters when
a murder occurs in their building. The three leads are Charles-Haden Savage
(Steve Martin), a faded television actor; Oliver Putnam (Martin Short), a
theatre director of diminished reputation; and Mabel Mora (Selena Gomez),
a young woman with a mysterious past and a sharp eye for the significant detail.

Season 1 solved the murder of Tim Kono. Season 2, released in 2022, picks
up immediately after Season 1's ending and centers on the murder of Bunny
Folger, the formidable president of the Arconia's board.

--- THE BODY AT THE END OF SEASON 1 ---

Season 2 opens with the three podcasters in immediate jeopardy: at the end
of Season 1, Bunny Folger was found stabbed in Mabel's apartment, dying,
with Mabel holding the murder weapon. This makes the three — who spent Season
1 as amateur detectives — the primary suspects in Season 2's murder.

Bunny Folger was the imperious, difficult, and privately rather lonely president
of the Arconia board — a woman who ran the building with iron authority and
who was not universally beloved. Her death in Mabel's apartment, in circumstances
that implicate the podcasters, sets Season 2's central investigation: who
killed Bunny, and why?

--- THE PODCASTERS AS SUSPECTS ---

The three amateur detectives are now the suspects, which creates the season's
central comedic and dramatic irony. They must investigate the murder of which
they are accused while simultaneously attempting to clear their own names —
and while the new true crime podcaster in their midst, the formidably successful
Cinda Canning (Tina Fey), is making a podcast about them.

The specific investigation of Bunny's death involves her life — her connections,
her secrets, her history in the Arconia — that the three must reconstruct while
managing their own legal exposure.

--- THE ART WORLD AND THE PAINTING ---

Season 2's central mystery thread involves a valuable painting. Bunny possessed
a painting that was believed to be a copy of a famous work but which Bunny
knew or suspected to be the original — a genuine artistic masterpiece that had
been replaced by a copy at some point in the past, with the original hidden
in the Arconia.

The painting's value and its history connect Bunny's death to the art world —
specifically to people whose interest in the painting's existence was not
innocent. The specific painting is connected to Bunny's family history and
to the Arconia's own history: the building's past, and the people who have
lived in it, are woven into the mystery.

The art forgery / genuine masterpiece thread takes the investigation into the
New York art world, with characters connected to galleries, collectors, and
the specific criminal ecosystem of high-value art.

--- ALICE BANKS ---

Mabel develops a romantic relationship with Alice Banks (Cara Delevingne),
a conceptual artist whose work uses other people's traumas as material. Alice
is intelligent, charismatic, and connected to the art world that is relevant
to the painting investigation. Her relationship with Mabel is genuine but also
complicated by the question of whether Alice's interest in Mabel is partly
professional — Mabel's involvement in true crime and murder is exactly the
kind of material Alice's art exploits.

The specific question of whether Alice can be trusted — whether her connection
to the Arconia's world and the painting is innocent or more direct — is one
of the season's sustained ambiguities.

--- BUNNY'S LAST WORDS ---

Bunny, dying in Mabel's apartment, whispered something to Mabel — a fragment
of a message that Mabel could not fully understand. As the investigation
develops, the meaning of what Bunny said becomes increasingly important: it
was pointing toward something, naming someone, or communicating something about
the painting that Bunny knew was the reason she was being killed.

The decoding of Bunny's last words — arriving at the correct interpretation
of a fragmentary dying communication — is one of the season's central puzzle
elements, in the tradition of the dying clue that Christie and others used
extensively.

--- JAN AND THE COMPLICATIONS ---

Charles's romantic life is complicated by the revelation (from Season 1) that
Jan, his bassoonist girlfriend, was involved in the Season 1 murder. She is
now in prison. The specific emotional fallout of this — a man who trusted
someone who turned out to be a murderer — gives Charles an arc about the
difficulty of trusting his own judgment and the specific loneliness of someone
who chose wrong and knows it.

--- THE KILLER: GLEN CHARLES WINSHIP AND THE ART CONSPIRACY ---

The investigation of Bunny's death establishes that she was killed because
of the painting. Specifically, Bunny had discovered that the Arconia's copy
of the famous work was actually the original — and she knew who had made the
substitution and where the original had been hidden.

The killer is connected to the original theft and substitution of the painting —
a person whose specific connection to the Arconia and its history gave them
both the knowledge of the painting and the motive to kill Bunny when she
threatened to expose what happened to it.

The painting itself, the history of its concealment in the Arconia, and the
specific family and institutional connections that tie together Bunny's history,
the building's history, and the killer's motive are assembled through the
final episodes' investigation.

--- CINDA CANNING ---

Cinda Canning's podcast about the Arconia trio — "All Is Not OK in Oklahoma,"
her hit show, has pivoted to covering them — is both a comedic element and
a plot-relevant one. Her investigation of them intersects with their investigation
of Bunny's murder, and the specific information she has or is pursuing turns
out to be connected to the case in ways that become significant in the finale.

Her assistant Poppy White (Adina Verson) is a figure of particular interest
as the season progresses — her specific role in Cinda's operation and her
own connection to the case are revealed in the season's concluding episodes.

--- RESOLUTION ---

The killer is identified and the full picture of the painting conspiracy is
exposed. Bunny's death is explained — she was killed to prevent her from
exposing the theft and substitution of the masterpiece — and the three
podcasters are cleared of suspicion.

The season ends with the specific twist that has become the show's signature:
a final revelation that opens the next season's mystery. The closing moments
of Season 2 establish the murder that will drive Season 3.

--- NOTES ON THE SERIES ---

Season 2 of "Only Murders in the Building" expands the show's world while
maintaining the qualities that made Season 1 successful: the warmth of the
central trio, the genuinely constructed mystery, the New York setting used
with real specificity and affection, and the self-aware relationship between
the podcasters' amateur investigation and the genre conventions they are
simultaneously inhabiting and commenting on.
