--- BACKGROUND AND CONTEXT ---

"Five Little Pigs" is a feature-length episode of ITV's Agatha Christie's
Poirot, first broadcast in January 2003. It is directed by Paul Unwin and
stars David Suchet as Hercule Poirot. It is based on Christie's 1942 novel
of the same name — widely regarded as one of the finest of all the Poirot
novels, and one of Christie's most psychologically sophisticated works. The
novel and its adaptation are distinguished by a specific structural feature
that sets them apart from nearly all other entries in the Christie canon:
the murder being investigated happened sixteen years before the story's present
day, and Poirot investigates it not by examining a fresh crime scene but
entirely by interviewing the surviving witnesses and reading their written
accounts of what they remember.

This retrospective investigation method gives "Five Little Pigs" an unusual
literary quality — it is, in effect, a story about memory, about how the same
events look different from different perspectives, and about how the truth of
what happened can be assembled from the partial, subjective, and sometimes
consciously distorted accounts of people who all witnessed the same events
but understood them differently. Christie was fifty-two when she wrote the
novel, and it has the psychological maturity and structural confidence of a
writer at the absolute peak of her powers. The adaptation captures much of
this quality in a remarkably faithful production.

--- THE COMMISSION: CARLA LEMARCHANT ---

Poirot is approached by Carla Lemarchant, a young woman in her mid-twenties
who was born Carla Crale — the daughter of the two people at the center of
the sixteen-year-old case. Carla was a small child when the events she wants
Poirot to investigate took place, and she has grown up in Canada, adopted by
a new family and given a new name, shielded as much as possible from the
shadow of what happened to her parents.

Carla's mother, Caroline Crale, was convicted of the murder of her husband
Amyas Crale sixteen years earlier and died in prison. Caroline never denied
the killing — she accepted the verdict with a specific, resigned dignity that
the people who knew her found both convincing and inexplicable. Carla, now
old enough to investigate her own history, is convinced that her mother was
innocent — that Caroline did not kill Amyas, and that whoever did allowed
an innocent woman to be convicted and to die in prison for a crime she did
not commit.

Carla presents Poirot with a letter that her mother wrote to her, to be given
to her when she was grown. In this letter, Caroline writes with the specific
clarity of someone who knows she is going to die without being believed: she
says, plainly and simply, that she did not kill her husband. She tells Carla
not to let the matter rest.

Poirot is moved by the letter and by Carla's situation. He agrees to investigate.

--- THE FIVE LITTLE PIGS ---

The "five little pigs" of the title are the five people who were present at
Alderbury, the Crale family estate in the countryside, on the day that Amyas
Crale died — the five witnesses to the circumstances that led to his death.
The nursery rhyme framework (this little piggy went to market, this little
piggy stayed home, and so on) is used by Christie to characterize each of the
five, which sounds more whimsical than it is: each designation is a precise
psychological shorthand.

Poirot contacts all five surviving witnesses and asks each of them to write
a full account of their memories of that day and the days leading up to it,
and then interviews each of them in person. The episode's structure follows
this method: we hear each account, we see the events as each person remembers
them, and we watch Poirot synthesizing the five different versions of the truth
into the single truth that underlies them all.

The five witnesses are:

PHILIP BLAKE — Amyas Crale's closest friend, a stockbroker, a solid and
conventional man who was deeply in love with Caroline and who has never forgiven
her for what he believes she did to Amyas. His account is the most hostile to
Caroline, the most certain of her guilt, and the most shaped by his specific
emotional investment in the story — his love for Caroline and his grief for
Amyas are intertwined in ways that distort his account in specific and
identifiable ways.

MEREDITH BLAKE — Philip's elder brother, a gentler and more introspective
man whose passion is the preparation of herbal remedies and potions, and who
owned the specific laboratory from which the poison that killed Amyas was
taken. Meredith is consumed by guilt — his poison, taken from his laboratory
by someone who knew it was there, was the instrument of death — and his account
is shaped by his need to understand what happened without fully examining the
most troubling possibilities.

ELSA GREER (now Lady Dittisham) — a young woman who was Amyas's model at the
time and his mistress. Amyas had been painting Elsa when he was killed — the
painting was unfinished on the easel, Elsa's portrait interrupted by his death,
which is one of the episode's most potent images. Elsa was passionately in
love with Amyas and believed he would leave Caroline for her. She has spent
sixteen years in a kind of suspended animation around the event, moving through
a successful life in terms of status and marriage but never fully leaving
the summer of Amyas's death behind. Her account of Caroline is the most
contemptuous and the most certain.

CECILIA WILLIAMS — the Crale children's governess, a woman of strong principles
and genuine intelligence who was devoted to Caroline and who is the most
likely witness to give Caroline the benefit of the doubt. Her account is the
most carefully reasoned of the five and the most attentive to specific details
of behavior and character. She is also the only witness whose account contains
the specific anomalies that Poirot finds most significant.

ANGELA WARREN — Caroline's much younger half-sister, who was living at
Alderbury as a teenager at the time of the events. Angela is now a distinguished
archaeologist of international reputation, a woman who has built a remarkable
life and career. She has a specific disfigurement — a scar from an incident
that happened before the summer in question — that is relevant to the case
in ways that will only become apparent at the end of the investigation.

--- AMYAS CRALE AND HIS WORLD ---

The flashback sequences — reconstructing the summer at Alderbury through the
five accounts — establish Amyas Crale as a vivid and specific character. He
was a painter of genuine genius — the kind of talent that is both a gift and
a curse, that demands everything of the person who possesses it and justifies
(in his own mind) treating everything and everyone as subordinate to the work.

He was serially unfaithful to Caroline, not because he was indifferent to her
but because his specific character — his greed for experience, his inability
to prioritize any obligation over his own desires — made fidelity impossible.
He always came back to Caroline. He always would come back to Caroline. The
affairs were terrible but they would end, as they always had.

The affair with Elsa Greer was different. Elsa was not simply an infatuation
— she was determined, serious, and genuinely believed that Amyas would leave
Caroline for her. Whether she was right — whether this affair was the one that
would have broken the marriage rather than simply strained it — is one of the
questions that the five accounts circle around without resolving.

Caroline Crale, as the five accounts reconstruct her, was a woman of deep
feeling and specific dignity — someone who absorbed Amyas's infidelities with
a patience that looked to some like resignation and to others like complicity,
who loved him in the specific way of someone who knows exactly who a person
is and loves them anyway, without illusion.

--- THE EVIDENCE AGAINST CAROLINE ---

The case against Caroline at the original trial was built on several elements:

She had motive. Amyas was conducting a flagrant affair with Elsa, under
Caroline's nose, in their own home. Elsa was present at Alderbury, being
painted every day, and was making no secret of her expectations. The humiliation
was public and sustained.

She had access to the poison. The coniine — a herbal poison derived from
hemlock — was taken from Meredith Blake's amateur laboratory. Caroline was
known to have visited the laboratory on the day before Amyas's death.

She was heard, by multiple witnesses, to say things on the day of the murder
that sounded like threats: heated language about what she might do, statements
that were interpreted as expressing a murderous intent.

She behaved, after the death, in a way that was consistent with guilt rather
than grief: she was too calm, too accepting, too resigned. She did not fight
the conviction. She accepted it.

And yet — and this is what Poirot keeps returning to — she wrote, in the letter
to Carla, that she did not do it. She was a woman of specific character and
dignity. She did not lie. Whatever she was protecting by accepting the
conviction, it was not her own skin, because she died in prison.

--- POIROT'S SYNTHESIS ---

Poirot reads the five accounts with the grey cells engaged on a specific
question: not what each person says happened, but what each person's account
reveals about them and about what they knew or didn't know, were hiding or
weren't aware they were revealing.

The specific discrepancies between the accounts — the things that one person
mentions that another doesn't, the moments where someone's account is oddly
precise or oddly vague, the specific things that the governess Miss Williams
describes that don't quite fit the narrative of Caroline's guilt — accumulate
into a picture that Poirot assembles with characteristic patience.

The key insight is about Angela Warren.

Angela, as a teenager at Alderbury that summer, was the person closest to
Caroline in the way that only a much-loved younger sibling can be. She
worshipped her. She would have done anything for her. And she had a specific
reason — rooted in an event from before that summer — to have a particular
kind of feeling about Amyas Crale.

The scar on Angela's face was caused by something Amyas did — not intentionally,
but through recklessness or carelessness, a piece of casual damage that left
a permanent mark on a child's face and that Caroline, characteristically,
absorbed without fully confronting Amyas about.

--- THE TRUTH ---

Angela Warren killed Amyas Crale.

She was the one who took the poison from Meredith's laboratory. She was the
one who put it in Amyas's beer. She did it because she loved Caroline and
because she could not bear to watch what he was doing to her — the humiliation
of the Elsa affair, conducted so openly, so brutally. She did it in the specific
impulsive moral absolutism of a teenager who loves someone with total devotion
and who cannot accept the damage being done to them.

Caroline knew. Not before — she did not plan it, did not collaborate, was
not part of the decision. But she knew afterward. She found out, or she worked
it out, and she made the specific decision that defines her: she would not
allow Angela to be destroyed by what she had done. Angela was seventeen.
Her whole life was ahead of her. She had a specific brilliant future that
the world would benefit from — the international archaeologist, the woman
of distinguished career and genuine achievement. Caroline accepted the
conviction to protect her sister. She died in prison to protect her sister.

This is what Poirot finds. This is what he tells Carla.

--- THE FINAL SCENE ---

The episode's final scene is among the most powerful in the Suchet Poirot
series. Poirot confronts Angela Warren — now a woman in her forties, decorated,
respected, accomplished — with the truth of what she did sixty years ago and
what Caroline did for her. Angela does not deny it. She has been carrying it
for sixteen years. The specific quality of what Suchet does in this scene —
the gentleness of Poirot's manner, the absence of triumph, the specific sadness
of a man who has found the truth and knows exactly what it cost — is one of
the finest moments in the entire series.

There is no arrest. Angela Warren is old, distinguished, and the crime is
decades past. The legal system cannot be meaningfully engaged. What Poirot
can do — and does — is restore Caroline's name. Carla gets the truth her
mother promised her. Caroline Crale did not kill her husband. She died to
protect the person she loved most. The truth is given back to the daughter
who never knew her mother.

--- NOTES ON THE EPISODE ---

"Five Little Pigs" is by the consensus of both Christie scholars and Poirot
series viewers one of the finest things the Suchet production achieved. The
retrospective investigation structure — entirely faithful to the novel —
requires a different kind of dramatic construction from the usual Poirot episode,
and Paul Unwin's direction handles it with real intelligence. The multiple
flashback sequences, each presenting the same summer from a different subjective
perspective, are beautifully integrated. The performances of the five actors
playing the witnesses are uniformly strong. And Suchet's Poirot — older in
this episode, more melancholy, more aware of the costs of truth — is among
his most affecting. The episode earned Suchet a BAFTA nomination.
