TVE_002 – Columbo: Any Old Port in a Storm

The story centers on the murder of wine‑estate owner Rick Carsini, who is found dead in a manner that is supposed to look like a tragic accident involving a bottle of wine. Rick owns a large, prestigious Napa‑style vineyard, and the estate is famous for its rare vintages. The crime is hidden in the ritualized world of wine tasting, and the killer uses the cellar itself as a crime scene.

The prime suspect at first is a junkie who had broken into the estate and was caught on the grounds. The police assume the intruder got into a scuffle with Rick, leading to a violent struggle and the man’s accidental death. However, Columbo is suspicious from the start because the body shows no signs of a struggle on the hands, and the position of the death looks more staged than spontaneous.

The real killer is Adrian Carsini, Rick’s younger half‑brother. Adrian lives on the estate and runs the winery operations. He is a meticulous, almost obsessive wine connoisseur who sees wine as art. Rick, in contrast, is a businessman only interested in profit. The conflict is simple: Rick has decided to sell the estate to a large conglomerate that will turn the vineyard into a mass‑market label, destroying the boutique, artisanal character Adrian loves.

Adrian has tried to talk Rick out of the sale, but the argument only deepens the rift. When Rick announces that the sale is going through, Adrian realizes there is no legal or financial way to stop it. So he turns to a more extreme, cold‑blooded solution: murder. His plan is not just to kill Rick, but to make the death look like an accident related to the wine or the exercise equipment, so that the sale can be renegotiated, delayed, or even halted by the confusion.

Adrian’s scheme involves three elements:

    The wine‑cork device,

    The exercise‑room weight,

    And the carefully staged timing.

The wine‑cork setup is this:

    A bottle of particularly old, valuable wine has a stubborn cork.

    Wine enthusiasts often use special tools or techniques to open such bottles, usually involving some pressure.

    Adrian carefully primes a bottle with a cork that will behave in a problematic way—too tight, or too fragile, depending on when it’s opened.

He then waits for Rick to open the bottle in the cellar, knowing that the stress of the difficult cork and the heat of the room will contribute to a physical strain. The story is that Rick, exerting himself while wrestling with the cork, suffers a heart attack and dies. The wine is merely the trigger.

But the wine is only the cover. The actual murder happens in the exercise room. Earlier that day, Adrian has tampered with a weight machine so that a heavy barbell will fall onto Rick at a precise moment if he steps into a certain position. The setup is very specific: a small misalignment in the safety won’t be noticeable to an untrained user, but it will guarantee that the bar drops.

The sequence of events Adrian plans is:

    Rick will work out in the exercise room,

    The tampered weight will fall, striking Rick and causing a real fatal injury,

    Then, later, Rick will be found in the cellar, grappling with the stubborn cork,

    The heart‑attack story will be applied to the cork‑related strain,
    hiding the fact that the damage was already done by the weight.

The timeline is carefully choreographed so that the fall of the weight happens first, and Rick’s “strenuous” attempt to open the wine happens after the heart damage is done, making the death look like it was caused by the cork.

Columbo’s first oddity is the location of the body. The wine‑cork story is emotionally compelling, but the forensic details don’t match: the strain on the heart would have killed Rick more quickly than he is reported to have lived after touching the cork. Columbo quietly tests the cork himself, using the same bottle and the same tools, and finds that the cork is not as difficult as the story suggests. The exertion would not have been enough to kill someone in good health without prior trauma.

The second oddity is the state of the exercise machine. Columbo, poking around, notices that the safety bar is misaligned in a way that would have caused a collapse only if someone stepped into a very precise spot. The spot is not the natural position most people would use, so it would have to be positioned deliberately.

The final clue is a bottle of rare wine that Columbo later “borrows” from the cellar and uses in a restaurant. He deliberately exposes the wine to a rapid temperature change, similar to the conditions of the cellar, and then shows it to Adrian. When Adrian reacts with horror—because the sudden change will ruin the wine—Columbo realizes that the combination of physical strain and the wine‑cork ritual could only have killed Rick if he had already been weakened by the weight‑drop. The cork is the glamorous, dramatic cause; the weight is the real one.

In the final confrontation, Columbo walks through the sequence:

    The tampered weight,

    The timing of the fall,

    And the way the wine‑cork scene was staged afterward.

Adrian, cornered by the logic of the wine‑lover’s obsession, admits that he killed Rick to protect the vineyard and the quality of the wine. The episode ends with Columbo expressing a kind of mournful understanding for Adrian’s twisted devotion to the vintages, while still acknowledging that the murder cannot be allowed to stand. The rare bottle of wine that Columbo holds becomes a symbol of the case: a beautiful thing, ruined by violence.
