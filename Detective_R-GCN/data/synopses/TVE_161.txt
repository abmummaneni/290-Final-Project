FULL SERIES SYNOPSIS — SPOILERS INCLUDED
================================================================================

NOTE: This synopsis covers all three parts of the Netflix French series Lupin
(2021–2023) as a single complete narrative, since the story of Assane Diop
runs continuously through all three and reaches its conclusion in Part 3.

--- BACKGROUND AND CONTEXT ---

"Lupin" is a French Netflix series created by George Kay and François Uzan,
starring Omar Sy as Assane Diop. It is not a direct adaptation of the classic
Maurice Leblanc "Arsène Lupin" novels but rather a modern reimagining in which
a contemporary man named Assane, inspired by and deeply knowledgeable about
the Lupin books, uses the fictional gentleman thief's techniques and philosophy
to pursue his own mission: to avenge his father and expose the powerful family
that destroyed him.

The show is a heist thriller, a revenge narrative, and a love letter to the
Leblanc originals simultaneously. It moves at considerable speed, is enormously
entertaining, and is grounded by Omar Sy's performance of Assane as a man
whose charisma and intelligence are matched by his emotional wounds.

--- ASSANE'S FATHER: THE ORIGIN ---

The story's foundation is established in flashback sequences that run through
all three parts:

Babakar Diop was a Senegalese immigrant who worked as a chauffeur for the
Pellegrini family, one of France's most powerful and wealthy dynasties. When
Assane was a teenager, a necklace — the Countess of Cagliostro's diamond
necklace, a historically significant jewel — was stolen from the Pellegrini
family. Babakar was framed for the theft by Hubert Pellegrini (Hervé Pierre),
the family patriarch, who used his wealth and political connections to ensure
that the poor immigrant chauffeur was convicted.

Babakar, unable to bear the shame of a conviction for a crime he did not commit
and the separation from his son that imprisonment represented, hanged himself
in his prison cell.

Assane was fourteen years old. He grew up in the care system, reading the
Arsène Lupin books that his father had given him, and he built his entire adult
life around the project of exposing Hubert Pellegrini and restoring his father's
name.

--- PART 1: THE NECKLACE ---

Part 1 (five episodes, released January 2021) opens with Assane in motion —
already in the middle of a complex plan. He has engineered himself a position
as a museum cleaner specifically to steal the Countess of Cagliostro's necklace
when it goes on display at the Louvre. The theft is the inciting public event
of the story: a high-profile heist that immediately draws media attention and
that Assane uses to announce, publicly and dramatically, that the necklace's
history is not what the Pellegrini family claims.

The theft is accomplished with the elaborate misdirection and disguise that
the Lupin books celebrate — Assane is a master of costume, of reading people,
of creating attention in one place while acting in another.

Assane's plan is not simply to steal the necklace but to use it as leverage:
he wants to expose Hubert Pellegrini and prove his father's innocence. His
investigative work — into the original theft, into what really happened with
the necklace twenty-five years ago, and into Pellegrini's continuing criminal
activities — drives Part 1's plot.

--- CLAIRE AND RAOUL ---

Claire (Ludivine Sagnier) is Assane's ex-partner and the mother of his teenage
son Raoul. Assane and Claire have been separated for years — partly because
Assane's obsession with his father's case has made sustained normal life
impossible. Claire loves Assane but cannot live with the consequences of what
he does and who he is.

Raoul is approximately fifteen in Part 1 — old enough to idolize his father,
not old enough to fully understand the risks Assane is running. The specific
danger to Raoul, which increases as Assane's confrontation with Pellegrini
escalates, is the most important emotional lever the antagonists use against
him.

--- PELLEGRINI AND THE OPPOSITION ---

Hubert Pellegrini is the primary antagonist — a man of vast wealth and deep
political connections who has operated for decades with impunity. He is not
simply a corrupt businessman but a man who has systematically used his power
to crush anyone who threatens him, and whose specific destruction of Babakar
Diop was entirely characteristic of how he operates.

Pellegrini's lawyer and fixer, Dumont, is his operational instrument for
dealing with Assane. As Assane's activities become more public and more
threatening, Pellegrini escalates his response.

The police detective assigned to investigate the necklace theft — Commissaire
Laugier and later Guedira (Soufiane Guerrab) — plays an increasingly important
role. Guedira is a Lupin enthusiast who correctly identifies Assane as the
thief and who, uniquely, understands what Assane is doing and why — a connection
that complicates the simple cop-chasing-criminal dynamic.

--- PART 1'S RESOLUTION ---

Part 1 ends without the full confrontation with Pellegrini achieved — Assane
has made his opening moves, has established public attention on the necklace
and its history, and has survived initial attempts to stop him. But Pellegrini
is not yet fully exposed, and the danger to Assane and his family is escalating.

The final episodes of Part 1 establish the specific threat that will drive
Part 2: Raoul's kidnapping.

--- PART 2: RAOUL IN DANGER ---

Part 2 (five episodes, released June 2021) opens with Assane's world in crisis.
Raoul has been taken. The kidnapping of his son — the most effective way to
control Assane, as Pellegrini's operation correctly identified — forces Assane
to operate under a different kind of pressure: not the controlled, methodical
pressure of a planned heist but the desperate urgency of a father whose child
is in danger.

Part 2 is darker and more emotionally intense than Part 1. Assane must find
Raoul while continuing to pursue Pellegrini — and the two objectives are in
direct tension: Pellegrini's people will harm Raoul if Assane pushes the
Pellegrini case, but failing to push it means accepting that Pellegrini wins.

--- THE INVESTIGATION DEEPENS ---

Part 2 also deepens the investigation of what happened twenty-five years ago.
New evidence about the original theft, about the specific mechanism by which
Pellegrini framed Babakar, and about the people who were involved or who know
the truth is assembled through Assane's investigation and through the work of
his old friend Benjamin (Antoine Gouy), who functions as his researcher,
accomplice, and moral anchor.

A journalist, Fabienne Beriot, who was investigating Pellegrini is killed —
another casualty of his determination to suppress the truth. Her death raises
the stakes and establishes that Pellegrini is willing to kill to protect himself.

--- ASSANE'S APPARENT DEATH ---

Part 2 contains a significant narrative turn: Assane is apparently killed.
In classic Lupin fashion, he engineers his own death — faking it convincingly
enough that Pellegrini and his associates believe the threat is removed.

This faked death gives Assane freedom of action — he can operate without being
hunted — but at enormous personal cost: Claire and Raoul believe he is dead.
The grief this causes them, and the question of whether Assane's obsession
has finally consumed everything he loves, is Part 2's emotional center.

--- RAOUL RESCUED ---

Raoul is eventually found and rescued through a combination of Assane's planning
and the specific trust that develops between Assane's situation and the one
police detective — Guedira — who understands enough of what Assane is doing
to make a moral choice about which side to support.

Part 2 ends with Raoul safe but with Pellegrini not yet fully exposed. Assane's
"death" is maintained — he is in hiding, presumed dead, planning his final
move.

--- PART 3: THE RECKONING ---

Part 3 (seven episodes, released October 2023) is the conclusion of the story.
Assane, still officially dead, is now free to operate with full commitment to
the final confrontation with Pellegrini — the complete exposure of what he
did to Babakar Diop and the restoration of his father's name.

Part 3 expands the investigation to include the full picture of Pellegrini's
crimes: not just the framing of Babakar but the wider pattern of corruption,
political manipulation, and murder that has defined his career. Building the
complete case against a man this powerful — one with the political connections
to suppress investigation and the resources to fight any legal challenge —
requires a different kind of operation than the earlier heists.

--- THE POLITICAL DIMENSION ---

Part 3 reveals the full extent of Pellegrini's political protection: connections
to politicians, to media figures, to the specific machinery of French institutional
power that has allowed him to operate with impunity. Exposing him requires
not simply presenting evidence but doing so in a way that his political
connections cannot suppress — a public, undeniable exposure.

Assane's plan for Part 3 is his most elaborate: a scheme that draws on
everything he has learned from the Lupin books and from his years of planning,
designed to expose Pellegrini publicly in a way that cannot be managed or
contained.

--- CLAIRE AND ASSANE ---

Part 3 addresses the relationship between Assane and Claire with more directness
than earlier parts. Claire has believed Assane dead. When she learns the truth,
her response — anger, relief, exhaustion — is one of the most emotionally
honest sequences in the series. The question of what, if anything, remains
possible between them after everything is one of the series' final emotional
questions.

--- THE FINAL CONFRONTATION ---

The climax of Part 3 involves Assane engineering a public confrontation that
exposes Hubert Pellegrini before an audience that cannot be silenced or
controlled. The specific mechanism — drawing on Assane's knowledge of Lupin,
his understanding of misdirection and spectacle, and the specific evidence
he has assembled — is the series' showpiece heist, designed not to steal
anything but to reveal everything.

Pellegrini is exposed. The evidence of his framing of Babakar Diop — and of
his wider crimes — is made public in a way that his connections cannot contain.
He faces legal and reputational consequences proportionate to what he has done.

Babakar Diop is posthumously exonerated. His innocence is established publicly.
His name is restored.

--- RESOLUTION ---

Assane Diop achieves what he set out to achieve: his father's name is cleared,
Pellegrini is exposed and faces justice, and the specific injustice that destroyed
the Diop family is publicly acknowledged and corrected.

The cost is significant: years of his life, his relationship with Claire
(partially restored, uncertain), the specific shape of Raoul's childhood shaped
by a father who was always in pursuit of something.

Assane is alive, free, and in possession of the thing he has been working
toward for twenty years. Whether that is enough — whether justice for his
father compensates for what pursuing it cost — is left as a genuinely open
question. The series respects the ambiguity.

--- NOTES ON THE SERIES ---

"Lupin" is one of Netflix's most successful French productions and one of
the most entertaining heist narratives in recent television. Omar Sy's
performance of Assane is consistently electric: a man of extraordinary gifts
who is simultaneously a master planner and an emotional wreck, whose
relationship to the Lupin books is both inspiration and instruction manual.
The show's pacing is relentless, its set pieces are inventive, and its
emotional core — a son's love for a wronged father, and the cost of making
that love the centre of your life — is treated with genuine seriousness amid
the entertainment.
