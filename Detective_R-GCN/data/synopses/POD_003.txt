THE SHOW AND ITS FORMAT

Somebody Knows Something (SKS) is a Canadian true crime podcast produced by CBC and
hosted by investigative journalist David Ridgen. Its format is distinctive within the
true crime genre: Ridgen works directly and actively with the families of missing or
murdered people, re-investigating cold cases in real time, using public appeals and
direct community engagement to surface new information. He is not a detached narrator
reporting on an investigation conducted by others — he is an active participant in the
investigation, making calls, knocking on doors, meeting witnesses, and pursuing leads
alongside the victim's family members.

The result is a podcast that feels genuinely live in a way that most true crime does
not. The investigation is actually happening as the episodes are recorded and released.
Leads are pursued in real time. Witnesses who have not spoken before agree (or refuse)
to speak on the podcast. The possibility of a breakthrough is real rather than merely
narrative.

Ridgen's approach is calm, methodical, and deeply humane. He is clearly motivated by
genuine commitment to the families he works with and genuine belief in the value of
public accountability in cold cases. His manner with grieving families — patient,
respectful, never exploitative — distinguishes the show from the more sensationalist
end of true crime.

THE CASE: THE DISAPPEARANCE OF ADRIEN McNAUGHTON


Background

Season 1 of Somebody Knows Something investigates the disappearance of Adrien McNaughton,
a five-year-old boy who vanished on July 12, 1972, from a fishing trip at Olmstead Pond
in Lanark County, Ontario, Canada. Adrien was at the pond with his family — his father,
his brothers, and others — when he simply vanished. He was there, and then he was not.
No trace of him was ever found. No body was recovered. No credible explanation for his
disappearance was ever established.

The case was investigated at the time and in subsequent years, but remained open and
unresolved for more than four decades. Adrien's family never received answers.

Season 1 was made in collaboration with Adrien's surviving family members, particularly
his brother Don McNaughton, who has spent decades trying to understand what happened
to his little brother on that July day in 1972.


The Day of the Disappearance

The circumstances of Adrien's disappearance are both simple and baffling. He was at
the pond, in the company of family members. He was five years old — not an infant, but
a small child, fully dependent on the adults around him. At some point during the outing
he was not where he was expected to be. A search began. He was never found.

The specific window of time in which he could have gone missing — how long he was
unobserved, exactly where the family members were, what they saw and did not see — is
reconstructed by Ridgen through interviews with people who were present and through
the available documentation from the original investigation.

The pond and its surroundings present several possible explanations: he could have
drowned (the pond was searched multiple times and his body was not found, which argues
against drowning or at least against the pond as the location); he could have wandered
into the surrounding forest (extensive searches found no trace of him); or he could
have been taken.

THE INVESTIGATION


Witnesses and Memories

Ridgen interviews people who were present at or near Olmstead Pond on the day of
Adrien's disappearance, as well as people from the surrounding community who may have
information about what happened. Many of these witnesses are elderly — the events
occurred more than forty years before the podcast's production — and their memories
are what memories are after four decades: imperfect, sometimes contradictory, shaped
by subsequent events and by decades of thinking and rethinking about what they saw.

The challenge of working with very old memories is one of the podcast's recurring
methodological concerns. Ridgen is careful about this: he does not treat elderly
witnesses' recollections as fact, but he also does not dismiss them. He is looking for
the consistency across accounts that suggests genuine memory rather than reconstructed
narrative.


A Specific Person of Interest

As the investigation develops, attention focuses on a specific individual — a man who
was in the area of Olmstead Pond on the day of the disappearance and whose behavior
and subsequent history have made him a persistent person of interest in the community's
memory of the case. This man is now elderly or deceased, but his proximity to the events
and the specific nature of what witnesses remember about him are examined in detail.

Ridgen pursues this thread carefully, presenting the evidence that points toward this
individual while being scrupulous about the distinction between suspicion and proof.
He is not trying to convict anyone in a podcast; he is trying to generate sufficient
attention and new information to potentially move the case forward.


The Public Appeal

A central element of the SKS format is the public appeal for information — the use of
the podcast's broadcast platform to reach people who may have relevant knowledge and
who have never come forward because no one has asked them directly or publicly. Ridgen
makes explicit appeals throughout the season: if you know something, if you remember
something, if someone told you something about this case, please come forward.

These appeals generate responses. People contact the show with information, with memories,
with fragments of knowledge they have been carrying for years. Not all of it is useful,
but some of it advances the investigation in ways that conventional cold case work could
not achieve.


Don McNaughton's Participation

Don McNaughton — Adrien's surviving brother — is present throughout the season,
participating in the investigation actively alongside Ridgen. His emotional involvement
in the case, and the specific quality of what it means to have spent four decades without
knowing what happened to your little brother, is one of the season's most affecting
dimensions.

Don is not simply a grieving relative providing emotional weight to the story. He is
an intelligent, observant man who has been thinking about his brother's disappearance
for forty years and who has specific memories, specific theories, and specific
information that contribute to the investigation. Ridgen treats him as a partner rather
than a subject.

WHAT THE INVESTIGATION ESTABLISHES AND WHAT REMAINS UNRESOLVED

By the season's conclusion, the investigation has surfaced significant new information
about the day of Adrien's disappearance and about the person of interest who has been
in the community's memory. The picture of what may have happened is clearer than it
was before the podcast — the preponderance of the available evidence, as assembled and
presented by Ridgen, suggests strongly that Adrien was taken rather than that he
drowned or wandered, and that the specific individual identified by witnesses deserves
serious investigative attention.

However — and this is characteristic of the SKS format and of cold case investigations
in general — the season does not resolve the case. No charges were laid as a direct
result of Season 1. Adrien McNaughton's fate remains officially unknown. The case
is technically still open.

What the season achieves is a public record of the investigation, a surfacing of
information that had not previously been publicly assembled, and a renewed public
awareness of a case that had gone cold. Whether that public awareness produces further
developments — tips, new witnesses, official investigative activity — is beyond the
podcast's control.

SIGNIFICANCE OF SEASON 1

SKS Season 1 established the show's format and its ethical approach. The combination
of active investigation, family participation, and public appeal proved to be both
journalistically effective and emotionally compelling. Ridgen's specific manner — his
lack of exploitation, his genuine partnership with the families, his willingness to
acknowledge uncertainty — set the show apart from most true crime.

The Adrien McNaughton case is the kind of cold case that conventional journalism
struggles with: old, physically remote, without a dramatic criminal narrative that
sells itself. SKS's willingness to engage with this kind of case — a five-year-old boy
who vanished from a pond in rural Ontario — and to treat it with the same seriousness
as higher-profile crimes is one of the show's defining qualities.
