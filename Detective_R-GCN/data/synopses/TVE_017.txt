--- BACKGROUND AND CONTEXT ---

"Nemesis" is a BBC Miss Marple adaptation first broadcast in April 1987,
directed by David Tucker. It stars Joan Hickson as Miss Marple and is based
on Christie's 1971 novel — one of the last Marple novels and one of the most
unusual, in which Miss Marple is invited on a coach tour of English gardens
by the posthumous instructions of a man she met briefly in a previous case
("A Caribbean Mystery"). She does not know the purpose of the tour or what
she is expected to do, only that she will "know injustice when she sees it"
and that she should act upon it.

The episode has a particular atmosphere among the BBC Marples — more meditative,
more elegiac, dealing with events from the past rather than a crime committed
in the present, and with a central concern about the nature of justice for
crimes that official channels failed to address.

--- THE POSTHUMOUS COMMISSION ---

Miss Marple receives a letter informing her that Jason Rafiel, a very wealthy
man she encountered during a holiday in the Caribbean (the events of "A
Caribbean Mystery"), has died and left her a legacy of twenty thousand pounds —
on the condition that she undertakes "a certain task." The task is not specified
in legal terms. She is simply asked to join a coach tour of famous English
gardens and houses, to observe what she finds, and to act on her conscience.

This is an extraordinary commission: Miss Marple is asked to investigate
something without being told what that something is, by a man who is already
dead. She accepts, trusting that the situation will make itself apparent.

--- THE COACH TOUR ---

Miss Marple joins the garden tour, operated by a company called "Famous Houses
and Gardens of Great Britain." The other passengers are a mixed group of
tourists, mostly elderly, with various personal histories. Among them are
three women who are sisters: Clothilde, Lavinia, and Anthea Bradbury-Scott.
These three women live together at a house called Jocelyn St. Mary, which is
on the tour's itinerary. Their house and their lives will prove central to
everything.

Also on the tour: various other passengers including Professor Wanstead, a
forensic psychologist, who seems to be observing the group with professional
attention.

--- MICHAEL RAFIEL ---

The connection that gradually emerges is Michael Rafiel — Jason Rafiel's son.
Michael was convicted some years earlier of the murder of a young woman named
Verity Hunt. He was sentenced and imprisoned. Jason Rafiel, his father, spent
years and considerable money trying to establish his son's innocence without
success. He died believing Michael was innocent and hoping that Miss Marple —
with her specific gift for seeing human truth that formal investigation misses —
might find what he could not.

Verity Hunt had a connection to the Bradbury-Scott sisters. She lived with
them for a period, or was closely associated with them, before her death. Her
body was found, and Michael Rafiel was convicted largely on circumstantial
evidence combined with the fact of their relationship.

--- THE BRADBURY-SCOTT SISTERS ---

As the tour visits Jocelyn St. Mary and Miss Marple meets the three sisters,
she observes them with the careful attention that the tour was designed to
provide. The sisters present a surface of cultivated gentility — their house
is beautiful, their hospitality is warm, their manner is distinguished. Beneath
this surface, Miss Marple perceives the specific quality that her village
experience has taught her to recognize: something is wrong here, something
has been wrong for a long time, and the surface has been maintained with great
effort specifically to conceal it.

The sisters' relationships with each other are not warm. The specific dynamic
between Clothilde — the eldest, dominant, possessive — and the others contains
something that Miss Marple identifies as a specific kind of distorted love:
a controlling attachment that would rather destroy the object of its love than
share it.

--- WHAT HAPPENED TO VERITY HUNT ---

Verity Hunt was in love with Michael Rafiel, and he with her. She was living
in the orbit of the Bradbury-Scott sisters — specifically under the powerful
influence of Clothilde, who had adopted a maternal or quasi-maternal relationship
with her. When Verity's relationship with Michael became serious and she prepared
to leave with him, Clothilde's reaction was one of the consuming possessiveness
that Miss Marple has identified in her manner.

Clothilde killed Verity Hunt. She could not bear to lose her. She killed the
girl she had claimed to love and arranged the evidence to point toward Michael
Rafiel — a young man with a troubled history who was an easy target for
suspicion.

Michael Rafiel went to prison for a murder he did not commit, convicted on
evidence that was circumstantially damning but that had been arranged by the
actual killer.

--- A SECOND BODY ---

During the course of the tour and the investigation, a second body is discovered
— a passenger or person connected to the tour whose death is connected to the
Verity Hunt case and to someone's awareness that Miss Marple is getting close
to the truth. This second death confirms that the danger is real and that the
killer is still present and still capable of violence to protect the secret.

--- MISS MARPLE'S CONCLUSION ---

Miss Marple's evidence against Clothilde is not forensic but human: the
accumulated observations of the tour, the specific character of Clothilde's
possessive love, the behavior of the three sisters when the subject of Verity
is raised, and the village parallel — a woman in St. Mary Mead whose love
for someone she regarded as her property led to exactly this kind of destructive
violence.

She presents her conclusions to Professor Wanstead — whose forensic psychological
expertise is now revealed to have been brought on the tour for exactly this
purpose, as part of Jason Rafiel's posthumous arrangement — and to the relevant
police authority.

--- RESOLUTION ---

Clothilde Bradbury-Scott is identified as Verity Hunt's killer. The evidence,
combined with the reopening of the original case, is sufficient to establish
Michael Rafiel's innocence. He is released from prison.

Jason Rafiel's posthumous commission is fulfilled: Miss Marple did know injustice
when she saw it, and she acted upon it. The twenty thousand pounds are hers.
Michael Rafiel, freed, can begin to live the life that was taken from him by
his father's lover's possessiveness and a court's failure to see past
circumstantial evidence.

The episode ends with Miss Marple, having served as the instrument of belated
justice for both the dead girl and the wrongly convicted young man, returning
to her ordinary life — carrying, as she always does, the knowledge of what
human beings are capable of, both in their destructiveness and in their eventual
accountability.

--- NOTES ON THE ADAPTATION ---

"Nemesis" is one of the most emotionally complex of the BBC Marple adaptations.
Its concern with a past injustice — a conviction that was wrong, a killer who
escaped accountability for years — gives it a moral weight that the present-
tense murder investigations don't always have. The meditative quality of the
garden tour, the elegiac atmosphere of late Christie, and Hickson's performance
of a Miss Marple who is older, slightly more tired, and doing something she
has been specifically trusted to do by a dead man she respected — all combine
to make this one of the series' most affecting entries.
