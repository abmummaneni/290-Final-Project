TVE_018 | Morse: The Dead of Jericho | Director: Alastair Reid | TV Episode
================================================================================
FULL EPISODE SYNOPSIS — SPOILERS INCLUDED
================================================================================

--- BACKGROUND AND CONTEXT ---

"The Dead of Jericho" is the first episode of Inspector Morse, first broadcast
on ITV on January 6, 1987. It is directed by Alastair Reid and is based on
Colin Dexter's 1981 novel of the same name — the fifth Morse novel but chosen
as the first television adaptation, apparently because it was considered the
best introduction to the character and his world.

The episode stars John Thaw as Chief Inspector Endeavour Morse and Kevin Whately
as Sergeant Robert Lewis. This first episode establishes what would become one
of British television's most beloved partnerships: the complex, brilliant,
emotionally guarded Morse and the straightforward, loyal, often underestimated
Lewis. It also establishes the series' characteristic Oxford setting —
Morse's Oxford is a city of dreaming spires, canal paths, real ale pubs,
and bodies.

--- MORSE AND ANNE STAVELEY ---

The episode opens with Morse attending a party in the Jericho neighborhood of
Oxford — a quietly unfashionable area of small terraced houses near the canal.
At the party he meets Anne Staveley, a woman he finds immediately attractive.
They talk, they connect, and Morse — characteristically awkward about personal
matters — fails to pursue the connection as directly as he might.

He has, however, noted her address. He returns to Jericho some weeks later
to visit her, only to find that Anne has apparently hanged herself. He finds
the body.

Because Morse has a personal connection to the dead woman — he met her, he
liked her, he came to see her — he is in an awkward position: he is the
detective who will investigate her death, but he is also someone who knew her,
however briefly.

--- THE APPARENT SUICIDE AND MORSE'S DOUBT ---

The official initial reading of Anne Staveley's death is suicide. She was found
hanging in her own home, there is no obvious sign of intrusion or struggle,
and there is no immediately obvious reason to suspect murder.

Morse is certain it was not suicide. His certainty is partly professional —
something about the scene does not read correctly to him — and partly personal.
He knew Anne Staveley for one evening, but he is a reader of people, and the
woman he met did not strike him as someone who was about to take her own life.

Lewis, newly paired with Morse in this first episode, is introduced as a man
learning the Morse method: following where Morse's intuition and irritable
intelligence leads while occasionally providing the practical, methodical
groundwork that makes Morse's leaps mean something.

--- THE JERICHO COMMUNITY ---

The investigation moves through the small community of Jericho — the neighborhood's
specific social world of canal dwellers, small householders, and Oxford
periphery people. Anne Staveley's life, as the investigation reconstructs it,
was connected to several people in the area.

Her neighbor George Jackson is an important figure: a man who has been
conducting a blackmail operation, apparently collecting information about people
in the Jericho community and using it to extract money. Jackson's operation
is amateur in its way — not a professional criminal operation but the opportunistic
exploitation of a nosy man who noticed things about his neighbors that they
preferred kept private.

Anne Staveley had something in her background — something Jackson knew about —
that he had been using to extract money from her or that gave him leverage over
her. The specific nature of what Jackson knew about Anne is part of what Morse
must establish.

--- ANNE'S BACKGROUND AND THE ABORTION ---

Anne Staveley's history involves a past pregnancy and a termination — an event
from her life that she had kept private and that, in the social world of the
late 1970s or early 1980s, carried enough stigma that its exposure would have
been damaging. Jackson had apparently learned of this and had been using it.

The investigation also reveals that Anne had a significant relationship in her
past — a man who was connected both to the circumstances of the pregnancy and
to events that led to his own death. The thread of this past relationship,
and what it means for the present-day deaths, is central to the plot.

--- GEORGE JACKSON'S MURDER ---

George Jackson is himself found dead — murdered. His death is directly connected
to his blackmail operation: someone he was blackmailing, or someone with a
connection to what he knew, has killed him to silence him. The murder of the
blackmailer raises the stakes and expands the investigation: the same person
who killed Jackson may have killed Anne Staveley, or Jackson's murder may
have been committed by someone different who had their own reason to want
him gone.

The specific connection between Anne's death and Jackson's death — whether
the same person committed both — is the central investigative question.

--- MORSE'S INVESTIGATION ---

Morse investigates with his characteristic combination of classical music
listening, crossword-solving intellectual process, real ale consumption, and
brilliant, irritable deduction. He is not a comfortable man to work with:
he is dismissive of Lewis's suggestions, he pursues leads that Lewis cannot
follow until Morse explains them, and he is personally invested in Anne
Staveley's case in a way that affects his judgment.

The specific evidence Morse assembles includes: the physical details of Anne's
death (something about how she was found that is inconsistent with suicide),
the financial records of Jackson's blackmail operation (which implicate a
specific set of people), and the relationship between Anne's past and a
specific person in her present who had reason to fear what Jackson knew.

--- THE KILLER IDENTIFIED ---

The killer of both Anne Staveley and George Jackson is a person whose connection
to Anne's past — and specifically to the pregnancy and its circumstances —
gave them a specific motive for both deaths. Anne's death was not the first:
the past event that Jackson was blackmailing Anne about involved a death that
was not accidental, and the killer has been protecting that secret for years.

When Jackson's blackmail threatened to expose the truth, the killer acted:
murdering Anne (staging it as suicide to suggest that the weight of her secret
drove her to death), and then murdering Jackson to eliminate the source of
the threat.

The specific identity of the killer, and the specific past event they have been
protecting, are the final revelations of the episode.

--- RESOLUTION ---

The killer is identified and arrested. Anne Staveley's death is confirmed as
murder. The blackmail operation that Jackson ran is exposed, with all the
damage that involves to the people who were being blackmailed.

Morse closes the case with the mixed satisfaction that characterizes his
resolutions: he has found the truth and done his job, but Anne Staveley is
still dead and the personal loss of someone he might have known better stays
with him.

--- ESTABLISHING MORSE AND LEWIS ---

The episode's secondary achievement — equally important to the series' future —
is the establishment of the Morse-Lewis partnership. Lewis is shown as someone
who does not yet understand Morse but who is patient, loyal, and capable.
Morse's treatment of Lewis is gruff and often dismissive, but the mutual
respect that will develop over the series' run is already visible in seed form.
The dynamic is established: Morse is the mind, Lewis is the method, and together
they are more effective than either would be alone.

--- NOTES ON THE EPISODE ---

"The Dead of Jericho" is a confident and atmospheric series opener. John Thaw's
Morse is fully formed from the first scene: opinionated, brilliant, melancholy,
and devoted to a specific idea of Oxford and English culture that the series
will develop across many episodes. The Jericho setting — unfashionable Oxford,
canal paths, terraced houses — is a deliberate counterpoint to the dreaming
spires, and the episode's concern with what people do to each other in private,
what they hide, and what those hiddennesses eventually cost is the series'
central preoccupation from the start.
