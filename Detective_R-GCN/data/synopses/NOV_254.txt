*Tess Gerritsen’s The Surgeon is the first novel in the Rizzoli & Isles series and centers on a surgically precise serial killer in Boston, a trauma surgeon haunted by a past attack, and a determined young detective who suspects that the new murders are a terrifying echo of an older crime. Below is a long, detailed, spoiler‑filled plot synopsis told in clear chronological order, covering the full story, including the ending, the killer’s identity, and how the mystery is solved.
The killer’s opening murder

The novel opens in Atlanta several years in the past, with a surgeon named Andrew Capra breaking into the home of a Boston‑based trauma surgeon, Dr. Catherine Cordell. He chloroforms her, ties her up, and begins to perform a surgical‑style hysterectomy on her while she is conscious, planning to kill her in the same way he has killed several women before.
Cordell manages to break free, stabs Capra, and then shoots him dead, saving her own life but being left with deep psychological trauma. She retires from surgery for a time, struggles with PTSD, and eventually moves to Boston to work in the ER at a major hospital, trying to rebuild her career and her sense of safety.
Diana Sterling’s murder in Boston

The main timeline of the novel opens roughly two years later in Boston. A young woman named Diana Sterling, employed at a travel agency, is found in her apartment with her uterus surgically removed and her throat cut. The precision and medical nature of the wounds are so distinctive that the press quickly dubs the perpetrator “The Surgeon.”
Detective Thomas Moore of the Boston Police Department is assigned to the case along with Detective Jane Rizzoli, a junior but sharp‑witted officer who is still working hard to prove herself in a male‑dominated homicide unit.
The connection to Andrew Capra

While investigating the Sterling murder, Rizzoli discovers that the modus operandi closely resembles a series of killings committed years earlier in Atlanta and Savannah by Andrew Capra, a surgeon who hunted women and performed hysterectomies on them before murdering them.
Records show that Capra was killed by his last surviving victim, Dr. Catherine Cordell, who had shot him in self‑defense. Rizzoli and Moore track Cordell down at the Boston hospital where she now works as an ER surgeon and ask her for insights. She is initially reluctant to talk about the past, but she confirms that the Boston victims’ injuries do resemble Capra’s work.
Elena Ortiz’s murder and the copycat pattern

The case gains urgency when a second woman, Elena Ortiz, is found murdered in the same way: hysterectomy, surgical‑grade incisions, and throat slashed. The two victims have no apparent connection to each other, and there is no clear link to Cordell, yet the similarity to Capra’s crimes is unmistakable.
Rizzoli and Moore begin to suspect that someone is either copying Capra’s methods or was inspired by him. The fact that the new “Surgeon” is active in Boston, the same city where Cordell now lives, raises the possibility that Cordell herself is being targeted or is somehow connected to the killer.
Nina Peyton, the third victim

The killer’s next victim is a young woman named Nina Peyton, a successful office worker who had been sexually assaulted two months earlier after being drugged at a bar. She went to get blood tests for STDs but refused a rape kit, so her attacker’s identity was never recorded.
The Surgeon targets her, performs a hysterectomy in her home, and leaves her for dead, writing “Happy birthday. Do you like my gift?” on her leg. The birthday line is a chilling message to Cordell, because Peyton is brought into the ER on Cordell’s birthday; Cordell operates on her, realizes she has lost enormous amounts of blood, and manages to stabilize her, saving her life.

This is a major turning point: for the first time, the Surgeon does not actually succeed in killing his victim, and the case suddenly has a survivor who can potentially describe him.
The investigation into the rape kit

Forensic work eventually yields a rape‑kit DNA match from Peyton’s earlier assault. The DNA points to a man named Karl Pacheco, a blue‑collar worker with a history of violence. Rizzoli goes to his apartment block and confronts him on the roof. Pacheco raises his hands, but Rizzoli, judging that he is still moving toward her, shoots and kills him.
However, when Moore and Cordell help examine the case more closely, they realize Pacheco is not the Surgeon: his shoe size does not match the prints at the scenes, he has no training or evidence of surgical knowledge, and there are no medical tools in his home. He was guilty of raping Peyton, but he is not the serial killer.

Rizzoli is traumatized by having killed a man she now knows was not the person she was hunting, and the case is back to square one.
The hair clue and the “bamboo hair” disease

The forensic team notices something unusual about a hair found on one of the victims: it has tiny nodules running along it, a sign of a rare genetic condition called “bamboo hair” (also known as Monilethrix).
This rare trait becomes a key identifier. The detectives begin to search for men in Boston who have this condition, narrowing the pool of suspects in a way that ordinary physical descriptions alone would not allow.
Introducing the real killer: Warren Hoyt

The person who is stalking and killing the women is a shy, unassuming phlebotomist named Warren Hoyt, who works at a lab attached to the hospital where Cordell operates. He is slight, androgynous, and almost invisible in a hospital setting, which makes him the perfect person to move through clinical spaces unnoticed.
Hoyt has a pathological obsession with Cordell. He knows that she survived Andrew Capra’s attack and that she fought back and killed him. He is both fascinated by her and envious of her power; in his twisted mind, he wants to capture her and subject her to the same ordeal she once survived, but this time she will not be able to kill him.

To find victims, he uses his access to lab‑run blood tests. He cross‑checks results that show signs of a recent sexual assault (such as abnormal STD markers) and then tracks down the women who never reported the rape, reasoning that they will be less likely to be protected by police involvement.
The Surgeon manipulates the investigation

As the case progresses, Hoyt continues to insert himself into the investigation indirectly. He sabotages a patient’s IV bag in the hospital, causing a cardiac arrest, so he can watch Cordell struggle to save the man’s life and demonstrate her skill under pressure.
He also begins to escalate his focus on Cordell, watching her more closely, studying her routines, and planning to abduct her once he feels he has built up enough “proof” that he can out‑will her.

Moore and Rizzoli, meanwhile, grow increasingly uncertain about Cordell’s role. Rizzoli, in particular, suspects that Cordell may be hiding something or that she could even be involved in the crimes, given how central she is to the case and how calm she often appears under pressure.
Romance and misplaced trust

Moore begins to fall in love with Cordell. They grow close as she shares more of her trauma with him, and he becomes emotionally invested in protecting her.
From Rizzoli’s perspective, Moore’s feelings for Cordell cloud his judgment; she believes he is downplaying evidence that might point toward Cordell and is too ready to accept her explanations at face value.

This emotional tension underlines the story’s psychological element: the team is hunting a surgeon‑like killer while also interrogating trust, intimacy, and the way trauma can distort behavior.
The real target: Cordell is kidnapped

Eventually Hoyt makes his move. He learns that Cordell plans to meet with the son of a recently deceased patient, Herman Gwadowski, and sends her a call luring her back to the hospital parking lot. She gets into her car, and Hoyt chloroforms her from the back seat, kidnapping her.
The hospital reports her missing, and police rush to Hoyt’s apartment, but they find no sign of him or his surgical tools. They realize he must have taken Cordell to a remote location.

As part of the search, Rizzoli and Moore re‑examine all the evidence and the tiny leads they have: the “bamboo hair,” access to lab records, and the fact that one of the victims’ cars had a vanity plate spelling “POSEY5,” which Cordell recognized as a chat‑room code for a women’s PTSD support group she moderates online.
Rizzoli’s breakthrough and the farm

Rizzoli, working partly on her own and partly against the grain of the official investigation, follows a hunch. She notices that an Asian hair has also been found in Hoyt’s apartment and traces this to a woman who runs a small farm or rental property outside the city.
She drives out to the property and discovers a hidden cellar beneath the farmhouse. Inside, she finds Cordell tied up, still alive, but clearly in the early stages of what Hoyt intends to be a surgical hysterectomy.

Rizzoli confronts Hoyt, but they struggle, and she is overpowered. He knocks her down, then impales her hands with medical instruments, planning to torture both women simultaneously.
The final confrontation and Hoyt’s capture

In the cellar, Cordell, despite her restraints and shock, manages to grab Rizzoli’s fallen gun and shoots Hoyt, seriously wounding him.
The gunfire alerts the police, who arrive just in time to arrest the incapacitated Hoythim and free both Cordell and Rizzoli. Hoyt survives the shooting but is taken into custody, where he will face trial for the murders of Diana Sterling, Elena Ortiz, and the attempted murder of Nina Peyton and Catherine Cordell.
Aftermath and the characters’ fates

In the aftermath, the investigation is officially closed. The Surgeon’s pattern of operating on women who had been raped but who never reported the crime is exposed, and the DNA and hair‑disease evidence is used to cement the case against Hoyt.
Cordell, emotionally and physically scarred, must reckon again with the fact that she has survived another surgical‑style attack. However, this time she is not alone; she has allies in Rizzoli and Moore, and the experience deepens her bond with Moore. The novel ends with the suggestion that she and Moore will eventually marry, moving beyond the trauma toward a more stable future.

Rizzoli, for her part, has proven herself in a brutal, high‑stakes case. She survives a near‑fatal confrontation with the killer and emerges as a tougher, more confident detective, albeit one deeply marked by the violence she has witnessed.
