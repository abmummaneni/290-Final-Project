BACKGROUND: TAKAKURA AND HIS NEW LIFE


A Former Detective Steps Back

Koichi Takakura is a former police detective who left active service after a traumatic
incident during an arrest: a suspect took a hostage and Takakura's hesitation — or arguably
his excessive empathy for the situation — resulted in near disaster. Shaken by how close
things came to catastrophe and by the behavior of the suspect, who seemed utterly devoid of
normal human responses, Takakura has moved into academia. He now teaches criminology at a
university, studying criminal psychology at a safer remove from actual crime.

He and his wife Yasuko have moved into a new suburban neighborhood, a quiet residential
street of ordinary middle-class houses. They are settling in, meeting neighbors, building a
new life.


The Neighbor

Almost immediately upon moving in, the Takakuras encounter their neighbor: a man named
Nishino. Nishino is superficially charming — smooth, attentive, immediately friendly in a
way that feels slightly excessive. He is the kind of person who makes an effort that is just
a degree too deliberate to be entirely comfortable. Takakura, with his criminologist's
trained attention, notices something off about Nishino from the first meeting but cannot
articulate it.

Nishino lives with a woman and a teenage girl who seem oddly subdued in his presence.

THE COLD CASE


A Six-Year-Old Disappearance

Simultaneously, Takakura's former colleague — a detective named Nogami — approaches him
for help with a cold case. Six years ago, an entire family disappeared from a suburban
house: a husband, a wife, and their child. Only the eldest daughter, then a teenager, was
not home and survived. The daughter, now grown, is named Saki Takagi and she has never
gotten over what happened. She has no idea what became of her family.

The case was never solved. No bodies were ever found. The family simply ceased to exist one
day, leaving behind their home and all their possessions.


Saki Takagi

Takakura meets Saki, who has been quietly investigating on her own for years. She is haunted
and driven. Her memory of the time leading up to the disappearance is the only evidence the
investigators have: her recollections of her family's behavior in the weeks before they
vanished, anything unusual, anyone new in their lives.

When she describes what she remembers — a new neighbor who inserted himself into her family's
life with particular smoothness and care — Takakura feels the first cold recognition.

NISHINO


Parallel Behavior

The connection that forms between the cold case neighbor and Takakura's own neighbor Nishino
is not immediately confirmed as the same person, but the behavioral signatures are identical.
In both cases: a man who moved into a neighborhood, made himself unusually available and
helpful to a nearby family, cultivated the family's trust with patient charm, and then —
in the cold case — was connected to their disappearance.

Takakura begins to observe Nishino with new eyes. The charm that seemed merely excessive
now reads as something more calculated. The woman and girl in Nishino's house seem not
like a family but like people held in a specific kind of fear — the fear of someone who
has thoroughly dismantled their ability to resist.


A New Kind of Predator

The novel's central psychological proposition, which Takakura develops through his academic
framework, is that Nishino represents a type of criminal not well accounted for in standard
psychological profiling: not driven by rage, not impulsive, not obviously disordered in ways
that register on standard measures. He is simply, at his core, without the internal
constraints that prevent normal people from treating other human beings as objects to be
used and discarded. His charm is not a mask over a seething inner violence — it is itself
the weapon, deployed with cold patience.

This is what made Takakura's instincts fire from the beginning and what makes Nishino so
difficult to pin down. He does nothing overtly wrong. He is just wrong.

YASUKO


The Wife's Relationship with Nishino

As Takakura becomes increasingly focused on Nishino as a threat, he realizes with horror
that his wife Yasuko has been drawn into Nishino's orbit. Nishino has been cultivating a
relationship with her — nothing overt, nothing she would identify as threatening — but a
gradual, careful accumulation of small attentions, confidences, and dependency. By the time
Takakura fully understands what is happening, Yasuko is in a psychological position that
makes it very difficult to simply warn her. She would not entirely believe him. Nishino has
been too careful.

This element of the novel — the way Nishino has simultaneously made himself a figure of
trust for Yasuko and a figure of obsession for Takakura — is deeply unsettling. Takakura
is the criminologist who sees the monster next door and cannot make his own wife see it.

RESOLUTION AND ENDING


The Investigation Converges

Takakura and Nogami assemble enough to understand who Nishino is and what he has done —
including, eventually, the fate of Saki's family. The resolution of the cold case is
provided, though the details of what Nishino did to the disappeared family are presented
with restraint rather than graphic detail. The horror is in the implication.

The ending of the novel is genuinely dark. The confrontation with Nishino does not resolve
cleanly in the way a conventional thriller might provide. Maekawa is interested in the
psychological and social reality of people like Nishino — the fact that they exist among
us, recognized too late, and that our ordinary social instincts (to be polite, to assume
good faith, to not make a scene) are precisely the vulnerabilities they exploit.

Takakura is left in a place of hard-won knowledge: he understands now what he should have
understood in his career, and the cost of that understanding has been paid partly by the
people around him.

The novel was adapted into a well-regarded Japanese film by Kiyoshi Kurosawa in 2016, which
emphasized the suburban horror elements and Nishino's psychopathic charm.
