TVE_003 – Columbo: A Friend in Deed

The story opens with the murder of Janice Caldwell, the wife of wealthy businessman Hugh Caldwell, in their family home. The crime is staged as a burglary‑gone‑wrong: the house is ransacked, money and valuables are taken, and Janice is found killed in a violent struggle. At first, the LAPD assumes a random intruder, possibly a junkie burglar, and focuses on the trail of petty criminals who took the loot.

Enter Lieutenant Columbo, who is quietly suspicious from the start. The burglary is too neatly set up. The amount of the stolen cash matches almost exactly the amount Hugh had previously reported missing: a large sum of embezzled money he’d siphoned from a company account. Columbo picks up on the coincidence and starts asking about the embezzlement and who might have known the precise figure.

As he digs, he uncovers that Hugh Caldwell has a powerful friend in the police department: Deputy Police Commissioner Mark Halperin, a high‑ranking, charismatic top‑cop and Hugh’s longtime pal. The two men have a secret arrangement: Hugh is being investigated for embezzlement, and Halperin has offered to “help” him by burying parts of the case if Hugh cuts him in for a portion of the stolen money.

The plan is to stage a false burglary:

    Hugh will stage the robbery of his own house,

    Plant the missing embezzled cash among the “stolen” loot,

    And then later have the burglars apprehended,
    so the account imbalance can be explained away as the burglary having taken the money, rather than Hugh taking it himself.

The only person who knows this is a fraud is Janice Caldwell, who has seen the books and realized the “missing money” never existed as a burglary loss. She threatens to expose the scheme, not only to the police but also to the press and the board. If she talks, Hugh is ruined, and Halperin’s career will be destroyed for covering it up.

Halperin decides to kill Janice and make it appear part of the burglary. On the night of the “robbery,” Hugh is sent out of the house on a pretext while the staged burglary is carried out. Halperin sneaks over to the Caldwell residence, enters discreetly, and waits for the chaos. Once the staged burglars are in, he kills Janice during the “break‑in,” then arranges the scene so that the fatal attack looks like a result of a violent struggle with the intruders.

The burglars leave, taking the set‑up loot; the embezzled amount remains among the “stolen” cash, and the case is positioned to look like a tragic but ordinary burglary‑homicide.

However, Columbo notices two key inconsistencies:

    The timing of the crime – the official time of death doesn’t quite match the sequence of calls and comings and goings.

    The state of the house compared with what the “burglars” took – the theft is too targeted and precise for a random junkie‑style robbery.

Columbo begins to look at who benefits from the embezzlement and the cover‑up. He soon learns of the Caldwell–Halperin connection and the plan to stage the robbery. The real breakthrough, however, comes from a minor, almost throwaway detail involving Halperin’s wife, Margaret, and her garden.

Earlier that night, Columbo had visited the Halperin house on routine business and noticed that Mrs. Halperin was in the garden, wearing a cardigan that had been snagged on a rosebush. Columbo absentmindedly commented that she’d done that once before, and Columbo later recalls that detail when he is reconstructing the timeline of the murder. He realizes that the snagged sweater proves Halperin left his house at a very specific time—a time that conflicts with the story he told about when he allegedly saw the “burglar” fleeing the Caldwell house.

If Halperin had been home at the time he describes seeing the burglar, his wife’s sweater would not have been snagged in the way it was, because she wouldn’t have been outside yet. The only way for the sweater to be caught on the rosebush is if Halperin and his wife had already been outside, before the time he claims to have seen the burglar. That means he must have left the house earlier, committed the murder, and then returned, fabricating the sighting.

Columbo lays out the chain:

    Halperin left his own house,

    Went to the Caldwell home,

    Killed Janice during the staged burglary,

    Returned home in time to create the alibi of seeing the burglar,

    And then used the staged robbery and the embezzlement as a smokescreen.

In the classic final scene, Columbo calmly walks through these logical steps, pointing out the discrepancy between the snagged sweater and the time line, and the fact that the “burglary” benefited Halperin financially while also removing the one person (Janice) who could expose the embezzlement. The motive—keeping the embezzled money hidden and protecting his own position in the department—is laid bare.

Halperin, cornered, can no longer maintain his story. Columbo politely arrests him, and the episode ends with Halperin realizing that his own sense of superior control and manipulation has been outmaneuvered by a seemingly bumbling detective who simply asked the wrong‑seeming question about a sweater at the right time.
