--- BACKGROUND AND CONTEXT ---

"At Bertram's Hotel" is a BBC Miss Marple adaptation first broadcast in January
1987, directed by Mary McMurray. It stars Joan Hickson as Miss Marple and is
based on Christie's 1965 novel. The production is notable for its evocation
of a specific kind of Edwardian-inflected London hotel — meticulously, almost
too perfectly preserved in its traditions — and for a plot that involves large-
scale organized crime operating beneath the surface of apparent respectability.
This is one of the darker and more structurally complex of the BBC Marple
adaptations, with Miss Marple's role being partly that of investigator and
partly that of someone processing a kind of disillusionment: the England she
loved was not, perhaps, what it appeared to be.

--- BERTRAM'S HOTEL ---

Miss Marple has come to Bertram's Hotel in London for a holiday — a treat she
has been looking forward to for years. Bertram's is a famous establishment
in the Mayfair area: it preserves an Edwardian atmosphere of impeccable service,
traditional English food, log fires, and the sense that no time has passed since
1909. Its clientele are elderly aristocrats, well-bred country visitors, and
people who value the kind of England that Bertram's preserves.

Miss Marple, arriving and settling in with great pleasure, notices something
almost immediately that she cannot quite articulate: Bertram's is too perfect.
Everything is precisely as it should be, every tradition is maintained with
extraordinary care, every member of staff is exactly right — and this excess
of perfection gives her a faint unease, as if a stage set has been arranged
rather than a real place discovered.

Among the hotel's other guests are:

Bess Sedgwick — a famous adventuress, a daring woman known for her exploits,
who seems an unlikely guest at the sedate Bertram's.

Elvira Blake — a beautiful young woman who is Bess Sedgwick's daughter, though
the relationship between them is distant and complicated.

Ladislaus Malinowski — a racing driver, handsome and dangerous, connected to
both Sedgwick women.

Canon Pennyfather — an absent-minded clergyman who is one of Bertram's regular
guests.

--- CANON PENNYFATHER'S DISAPPEARANCE ---

The episode's first strange event is the disappearance of Canon Pennyfather.
He was at the hotel, went out one evening, and has not returned. His absence
is puzzling — he is a mild, absent-minded man with no obvious enemies and no
reason to vanish. The circumstances of his departure from the hotel are unclear.

The disappearance is the first visible sign that Bertram's serene surface is
covering something dangerous. Chief Inspector Davy, the Scotland Yard detective
assigned to the case, has been watching Bertram's for some time — he already
suspects the hotel is connected to a series of highly organized crimes.

--- THE ORGANIZED CRIME NETWORK ---

Chief Inspector Davy's investigation reveals that Bertram's Hotel is the
operational center of a sophisticated criminal organization. The hotel's
appearance of traditional respectability is precisely its value: no one suspects
that the gentle anachronism of Bertram's is a front for criminals operating
with extraordinary efficiency.

The specific criminal operation involves a series of spectacular thefts and
robberies across Britain. The crimes share a characteristic: they are committed
with exact knowledge of timing and opportunity, they use disguise and misdirection
with professional skill, and the perpetrators seem to vanish completely. Scotland
Yard has been baffled.

The criminal organization operates through Bertram's as a coordination point:
people arrive, plans are made, criminals depart and return. The hotel's tradition
of discretion and its staff's professional incuriosity make it ideal cover.

--- BESS SEDGWICK ---

Bess Sedgwick's presence at the hotel turns out to be connected to her specific
role in the criminal organization. She is one of its key operators — her
adventurous life, her international travels, her ability to move in many social
worlds are all qualities that have been turned to criminal use. She came to
Bertram's with a specific purpose connected to the organization's current
operations.

Her relationship with her daughter Elvira is complex and laden with guilt:
Bess abandoned Elvira as a child, and Elvira has grown up without her. Their
encounter at the hotel carries the weight of this history, and Elvira's own
actions are partly driven by things she knows or has discovered about her mother.

--- ELVIRA'S INVESTIGATION AND DANGER ---

Elvira Blake is conducting her own private investigation — she has come to
suspect that she has a claim on a specific fortune or legal entitlement, and
she is looking for evidence to support or deny this. Her investigation brings
her into contact with dangerous people and dangerous knowledge connected to
the criminal operation.

A shooting occurs: someone attempts to kill Elvira. The shot is fired in the
vicinity of Bertram's, and the question of who fired it — and whether the
intended target was Elvira or someone else who was nearby — is part of the
murder investigation that follows.

--- THE MURDER ---

A murder does occur in the course of the investigation. The specific victim
and the specific circumstances are connected to the criminal organization's
internal tensions: someone who knows too much, or whose silence can no longer
be relied on, is killed. The murder is connected to both the large-scale criminal
operation and the more personal drama of Bess Sedgwick, Elvira, and Malinowski.

--- MISS MARPLE'S ROLE ---

Miss Marple's specific contribution to the resolution is her identification,
from the beginning, that Bertram's is wrong — that its perfection is performed
rather than genuine. This insight, which she brings to Chief Inspector Davy's
attention, provides the emotional and observational confirmation he needs for
what he already technically suspects.

Her village parallel involves the specific quality of an imitation: she recalls
a person in St. Mary Mead who kept their house exactly as a certain kind of
house should be kept, with such deliberate, careful maintenance of the
appearance of the thing, that the appearance itself became the tell. When
something is done too correctly, too consistently, it reveals that it is being
done consciously rather than naturally.

--- RESOLUTION ---

The criminal organization is dismantled. Chief Inspector Davy's investigation,
confirmed and complemented by Miss Marple's observations, produces the evidence
needed to establish Bertram's as the center of the operation and to identify
the key players.

Bess Sedgwick's specific role and her fate are resolved in connection with the
shooting and its aftermath. Elvira's situation is clarified. Canon Pennyfather
is eventually found — unharmed but bewildered, having been used or confused
as part of one of the organization's operations.

Miss Marple leaves Bertram's with the specific melancholy of someone who came
for a holiday and found instead a disillusionment: the England she loved and
that Bertram's preserved was not real. It was a stage set for crime. The places
that seem most quintessentially to represent what one loves can be exactly the
places that have been most thoroughly hollowed out.

--- NOTES ON THE ADAPTATION ---

"At Bertram's Hotel" is one of the darker BBC Marple adaptations, and its mood —
the corruption behind the beautiful surface — suits the later period of Christie's
writing, when her social observation had grown more pessimistic. Mary McMurray's
direction captures both the hotel's genuine visual appeal and the wrongness
beneath it. The episode is also notable for giving Miss Marple a more explicitly
emotional arc than most adaptations: her disillusionment at what Bertram's
really is gives Hickson some of her most inward and moving moments in the series.
