The Adventure of the Empty House is the first story in The Return of Sherlock Holmes and brings Sherlock Holmes back from his supposed death at the Reichenbach Falls to solve a locked‑room‑style murder and unmask a dangerous assassin. The story is told in two tightly woven strands: the “Park Lane Mystery” of Ronald Adair’s death and Holmes’s return from the dead. Below is a long, detailed, spoiler‑filled plot synopsis laid out in clear chronological order, covering the full story, the locked‑room trick, the killer’s motive, and how Holmes is able to fake his death and trap his would‑be murderer.
Watson still grieving Holmes’s “death”

The story is set in March 1894, three years after the events of The Final Problem, in which Holmes apparently fell to his death during a struggle with Professor Moriarty at the Reichenbach Falls. Watson has never fully recovered from the loss and still thinks of Holmes constantly, especially when he reads about unusual crimes in the newspaper.
One evening he comes across an account of a baffling murder: the honorable Ronald Adair, son of a British general and a member of the aristocracy, has been shot dead in his locked room at his mother’s Park Lane house, with no weapon found and no clear means of entry or escape. The case is being called the “Park Lane Mystery.”
The Ronald Adair case

Ronald Adair is a young man of good family, wealthy, well liked, and said to have no known enemies. He plays whist at several clubs and is known as a quiet, straightforward man.
On the night of his death, sometime between 10 p.m. and 11:20 p.m., he was sitting in his room going over his card‑game accounts when he was shot in the head. The room was locked from the inside, the door was undisturbed, and there was no sign of a forced entry. The window was open, but there was no trace of anyone climbing in or out, and no weapon was found in the room. The shot was virtually silent, and no one in the house heard it.

The police and the public are baffled. The case looks like a perfect locked‑room mystery, and Scotland Yard has made no real progress. Watson is riveted by the details, not only because of the puzzle itself but because the sort of elaborate, seemingly impossible crime is exactly the kind that would have attracted Holmes.
Watson’s visit to the scene

Watson decides to satisfy his own curiosity and visits the house on Park Lane, though he has no official role in the investigation. He is walking near the scene when he collides with a dirty, elderly, deformed book collector who is carrying a bundle of books. The man is short‑tempered and shoves past Watson, muttering about his own troubles.
Watson thinks nothing of the incident until later that evening, when the same man appears at his own rooms on 221B Baker Street, apologizing for his earlier rudeness and offering a few volumes for sale.

As Watson turns to look at the empty space on his bookshelf that the man points out, he turns back—and sees Sherlock Holmes standing before him, transformed out of the old‑book‑seller disguise. Watson is so shocked that he faints.
Holmes explains his survival

After Watson recovers, Holmes calmly explains how he survived the fall at the Reichenbach Falls. He had noticed a small ledge and clung to it, letting Moriarty drop to his death, while Watson and the Swiss guides believed both men had perished.
Holmes then vanished for three years, traveling under aliases and systematically dismantling the rest of Moriarty’s criminal network across Europe and the world. He allowed himself to be believed dead so that Moriarty’s allies would relax their guard and be easier to trap.

However, one man—Colonel Sebastian Moran—knew the truth or at least strongly suspected it: Holmes was still alive and in hiding. Moran, a former Indian Army officer and now a professional gambler, was Moriarty’s right‑hand man and “the second most dangerous man in London” next to the Professor.
Moran’s unique silent air‑gun

Holmes reveals that Moran has developed a special weapon: a compressed‑air rifle fitted with a silencer, capable of firing a small, poisoned dart or bullet over a considerable distance without making a loud report.
Because Holmes had once written a monograph on the “air‑gun and its possibilities,” he is uniquely equipped to recognize the kind of weapon that could have killed Ronald Adair in such a quiet, seemingly impossible way.

Holmes also explains that Moran is not only a dangerous marksman but a ruthless man who has already killed before and would happily kill Holmes if he got the chance. That is why Holmes has stayed in hiding so long: Moran is still at large and actively hunting him.
The connection between Adair’s murder and Moran

Holmes has pieced together that Moran is not only a potential assassin of Holmes but also the murderer of Ronald Adair. The key link is the whist‑playing circles: Moran and Adair had been frequent card partners.
Adair, a careful and honest man, had begun to notice that Moran was cheating at cards—using skillful but dishonest techniques to win consistently. Adair decided he would return the money he had won from Moran, intending to do the honorable thing and free himself from the debt.

From Moran’s point of view, exposure as a card cheat would be disastrous. He would be blackballed from the gentlemen’s clubs, lose his livelihood as a gambler, and be ruined socially. He could not allow Adair to make his confession or repayment public.

So Moran chose to murder him. He used his compressed‑air rifle, shooting through the open window of Adair’s room from a nearby house, timed so that he could observe the window and take the shot without being seen himself. The near‑silent discharge explained why no one in the house heard the shot, and the lack of a gun in the room completed the illusion of a locked‑room impossibility.
Holmes’s plan to trap Moran

Holmes realizes that Moran wants to kill him as well, both to finish the job Moriarty started and to prevent Holmes from exposing the rest of the gang. The only way to get Moran into the open is to lure him into taking a shot at Holmes himself.

Holmes therefore sets a trap. He arranges for a highly realistic waxwork bust of his own head and shoulders to be placed in a window of 221B Baker Street, where it can be seen from the outside. The figure is posed in profile, just as Holmes might sit reading by the light, and he leaves the curtains slightly open so the silhouette is clear.

Then, on the night of the trap, Holmes and Watson slip across the street into an empty house facing Baker Street whose front room overlooks Holmes’s window. They hide in that room, waiting for Moran to appear and take the shot.
The capture at the “empty house”

As expected, Moran arrives at the empty house across the street, uses his compressed‑air rifle, and fires at the silhouette in the Baker Street window. The shot strikes the waxwork bust, and at the same moment Holmes and Watson leap forward, overpowering Moran in the room before he can get off a second shot.
Holmes whistles, and Inspector Lestrade, who has been lying in wait with two constables, bursts in and arrests the would‑be assassin. The murder weapon—the unusual silent air‑gun—is found in Moran’s possession, and he is charged not only with attempting to murder Holmes (by shooting at the waxwork) but also with the murder of Ronald Adair, thanks to Holmes’s reconstruction of the earlier crime.
Motive and twist wrapped up

Holmes explains to Watson and Lestrade that Moran killed Adair because the young man was about to expose his cheating, and he targeted Holmes because he feared Holmes would eventually identify and dismantle the last remnants of Moriarty’s organization.
The locked‑room aspect of Adair’s murder is fully resolved: there was no magic, only a carefully chosen line of fire from a nearby building, an almost soundless weapon, and a window left open by Adair himself, which made the shot possible while preserving the appearance of an unsolvable mystery.
Holmes’s return to Baker Street

With Moran captured and Holmes’s survival publicly revealed, Holmes is now safe to resume his old life. He returns to 221B Baker Street, and Watson—having recovered from his initial shock—rejoins him as his chronicler and companion.
The story ends on a note of quiet triumph: Holmes is back in London, his greatest remaining enemy among Moriarty’s circle is behind bars, and the “Park Lane Mystery” is solved, restoring order to a case that had seemed impossible to the police.
