THE SHOW AND ITS APPROACH

In the Dark is a podcast produced by APM Reports, the investigative journalism arm of
American Public Media. It is one of the most rigorously produced and ethically serious
true crime podcasts in existence — it functions more as investigative journalism that
uses the podcast format than as true crime entertainment. Its first season examined
the case of a blind man, Curtis Lovelace, and the system that failed him; its second
season, described here, is widely considered one of the finest works of audio journalism
ever produced.

Madeleine Baran is the lead reporter and the series' anchor. Her approach is that of a
beat journalist rather than a true crime narrator: she is interested in systems and
institutions as much as individuals, and her investigation of Curtis Flowers is as much
an investigation of the criminal justice system of Winona, Mississippi as it is of any
specific crime.

In the Dark Season 2 won the Peabody Award, the duPont-Columbia Award, and the Investigative
Reporters and Editors Award, among others. It is one of the most decorated works in the
history of audio journalism.

THE CASE: CURTIS FLOWERS AND THE TARDY FURNITURE MURDERS


The Crime

On July 16, 1996, four people were shot and killed at Tardy Furniture store in Winona,
Mississippi. The victims were Bertha Tardy, the store's owner, and three employees:
Robert Golden, Derrick Stewart, and Carmen Rigby. All four were shot in the head at
close range. The store was a small-town furniture business, and the killings left Winona —
a town of approximately 5,000 people, deeply divided by race — in shock.

The crime was investigated by local police and the Mississippi Bureau of Investigation.
Physical evidence from the scene included shell casings and what appeared to be bloody
shoe prints. A gun was never recovered. No surveillance footage existed.


Curtis Flowers Is Charged

Curtis Flowers, a Black man who had briefly worked at Tardy Furniture before being fired,
was charged with all four murders. The prosecution's case rested on circumstantial evidence:
a witness who claimed to have seen Flowers near the store that morning, alleged physical
evidence connecting Flowers to the crime, and the employment history giving him a motive.

What followed is one of the most extraordinary series of trials in American legal history.

SIX TRIALS


The Unprecedented Record

Curtis Flowers was tried six times for the Tardy Furniture murders between 1997 and 2010.
The specific history of these six trials is the structural spine of In the Dark Season 2:

Trial 1 (1997): Flowers is convicted and sentenced to death. The conviction is reversed
on appeal because of prosecutorial misconduct — specifically, the prosecution's suppression
of evidence favorable to the defense.

Trial 2 (1999): Flowers is again convicted and sentenced to death. This conviction is
also reversed on appeal due to prosecutorial misconduct.

Trial 3 (2004): Flowers is convicted for a third time. The Mississippi Supreme Court
reverses this conviction as well, citing extensive prosecutorial misconduct including
the improper exclusion of Black jurors.

Trial 4 (2007): Ends in a mistrial — the jury cannot reach a unanimous verdict.

Trial 5 (2008): Another mistrial.

Trial 6 (2010): Flowers is convicted for the fourth time and sentenced to death.

The fact of six trials for the same defendant, in the same court, prosecuted by the same
prosecutor, over thirteen years is itself extraordinary. The pattern of convictions reversed
for prosecutorial misconduct, followed by more prosecutorial misconduct, followed by more
reversals, represents either the most persistent legitimate prosecution in Mississippi history
or a system determined to convict a specific man regardless of the rules.


Doug Evans

The prosecutor in all six of Curtis Flowers's trials — and in virtually every major
criminal case in his district for decades — is Doug Evans. Evans is the District Attorney
for Mississippi's Fifth Circuit Court District, an elected office he has held since 1992.
He is the most important figure in the systemic failure that In the Dark Season 2 documents.

Baran and her team spent years investigating Evans — his trial record, his use of
peremptory challenges (the strikes used to remove potential jurors without stated cause),
his evidentiary decisions, and his behavior during trials. What they found is documented
with extraordinary specificity.

RACE AND JURY SELECTION


The Batson Issue

One of In the Dark's most important investigative contributions is its analysis of
Doug Evans's use of peremptory challenges to exclude Black jurors from Curtis Flowers's
trials. Under Batson v. Kentucky (1986), prosecutors cannot strike potential jurors
on the basis of race. But the enforcement of Batson in practice is notoriously weak:
prosecutors can offer race-neutral explanations for their strikes that courts will
accept even when the pattern of strikes is racially disparate.

In the Dark commissioned an analysis of Evans's strike patterns across not just Flowers's
trials but his entire prosecutorial career — hundreds of cases, thousands of jury
strikes. The findings were stark: Evans struck Black jurors at a dramatically higher
rate than white jurors across his entire career, and the pattern in the Flowers trials
was the most extreme expression of a practice that ran through his entire record.

In Flowers's sixth trial, Evans used five of his six peremptory strikes to exclude
Black jurors. The pattern prompted the Supreme Court of the United States to take up
the case.


The Supreme Court Decision

In June 2019 — during the podcast's run — the Supreme Court issued its decision in
Flowers v. Mississippi. The Court found, in a 7-2 decision authored by Justice Brett
Kavanaugh, that Doug Evans had violated the Constitution by discriminating against Black
jurors. The decision reversed Flowers's sixth conviction.

The Supreme Court decision came in part because of the documentation that In the Dark
Season 2 had assembled and made public. The podcast's investigative work, presented in
a form accessible to the general public, created pressure that contributed to the legal
outcome.

THE EVIDENCE AGAINST FLOWERS: WHAT THE PODCAST FINDS


The Key Witness

A central pillar of the prosecution's case across multiple trials was a witness named
Odell Hallmon, a jailhouse informant who claimed that Flowers had confessed to him
while both were incarcerated. Jailhouse informant testimony is one of the most
unreliable categories of evidence in the criminal justice system, and In the Dark
examines Hallmon's account and his record in detail.

Baran tracks down Hallmon and interviews him — an interview in which he recants his
testimony, stating that he made it up. This recantation is not legally binding (he
cannot simply undo sworn testimony) but it is documented on audio and is a significant
part of the podcast's examination of the prosecution's evidentiary foundations.


The Physical Evidence

The physical evidence connecting Flowers to the crime is examined in detail: what it
consists of, what forensic analysis has been conducted on it, and what legitimate
questions exist about its reliability and its chain of custody. Baran and her team
work through the forensic elements of the case with the same rigor they apply to
the legal and testimonial dimensions.


The Alternative Suspects

In the Dark also examines whether there were alternative suspects — people with more
direct connections to the Tardy Furniture murders who were not adequately investigated.
The podcast does not definitively identify an alternative perpetrator, but it documents
specific individuals whose connections to the case warranted more investigative attention
than they received.

FLOWERS'S RELEASE AND THE AFTERMATH

Following the Supreme Court's reversal of his sixth conviction, Curtis Flowers was
released on bail in December 2019 after spending more than 22 years in prison, including
significant time on death row. In September 2020, District Attorney Doug Evans announced
that the state was dropping all charges against Flowers. He is a free man.

He has not been exonerated in the formal sense — the charges were dropped rather than
a finding of innocence being made — but he is free after six trials, four convictions,
and 22 years of imprisonment. Whether he committed the Tardy Furniture murders is
not legally resolved; whether the process that kept him imprisoned for 22 years was
fair is clearly answered by the Supreme Court's decision.


Doug Evans

Doug Evans remains, as of the podcast's conclusion, the District Attorney of the Fifth
Circuit Court District. He was not charged with misconduct. The Supreme Court found
that he violated the Constitution; there have been no professional consequences.

SIGNIFICANCE

In the Dark Season 2 is not simply a story about one man's wrongful prosecution. It is
a sustained, documented examination of how racial bias operates in the American criminal
justice system — not as individual prejudice but as institutional practice, embedded in
prosecutorial discretion, in jury selection procedures, in the mechanisms of appeals
courts, and in the election of local officials who face no accountability for systematic
misconduct.

Madeleine Baran and her team's willingness to spend years on a single case, to go to
Winona repeatedly, to analyze thousands of court records, to find and interview Odell
Hallmon, and to document the full history of six trials and their reversals produced one
of the most important pieces of investigative journalism of the 2010s in any medium.
