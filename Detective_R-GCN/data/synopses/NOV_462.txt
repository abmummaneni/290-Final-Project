1. Case timeline (Avery’s disappearance)
Before the disappearance (same day, morning–afternoon)

    William Wooler, a respected doctor in Stanhope, NY, is having an affair with his neighbor Nora Blanchard. Their affair ends abruptly in a motel room when Nora breaks it off, leaving William humiliated, angry, and emotionally volatile.

    He drives home to “cool off,” expecting an empty house. Instead, he finds his nine‑year‑old daughter, Avery, unexpectedly at home. She’s been sent home early or kicked out of school for behavioral issues related to her ADHD and oppositional behavior.

    Avery is mouthy and defiant. Already upset over the breakup, William loses his temper and hits/slaps Avery hard enough to knock her to the ground. He immediately regrets it, apologizes, and begs her not to tell her mother, Erin.

    Overwhelmed with guilt and panic, William leaves the house again instead of staying with Avery, telling himself he needs to calm down and pretending, even to himself, that the incident “barely happened.”

Late afternoon–early evening: Avery goes missing

    At some point after William leaves, Avery disappears from the house. When Erin and William later realize she’s gone and can’t find her, they call the police and report her missing.

    Initially, William lies to the police and to Erin, saying he didn’t see Avery at all when he came home after the motel. He hides both the affair and the fact that he hit Avery shortly before she vanished.

    Detectives begin canvassing the neighborhood. Stanhope is a quiet, “safe” suburban area, so Avery’s disappearance quickly becomes major local news. Everyone on the street is questioned, and neighbors begin to panic and gossip.

Early investigation: turning on William and Ryan

    As hours pass with no sign of Avery, suspicion focuses on William:

        He was the last known person to have access to the house.

        Avery has documented behavior problems, and a neighbor boy (Michael) tells police that William hits Avery regularly.

    The police discover William’s burner phone, which exposes his cheating. Under pressure, William admits he was having an affair but refuses to name Nora. He also finally confesses that he did see Avery and slapped her that afternoon.

    This makes him look much worse: violent toward his child, lying from the start, and with a strong motive to hide what happened that day. He becomes the prime suspect in Avery’s possible murder. Erin is devastated and furious as each new lie comes out.

    Meanwhile, Avery’s friend Jenna tells police that Avery mentioned an older “boyfriend” who touched her inappropriately. This introduces the possibility of sexual abuse and broadens the suspect list.​

    Police then receive an anonymous tip that someone saw Avery getting into a car driven by Ryan Blanchard, Nora’s teenage son. Ryan had been out looking for Avery with others after she was reported missing, which makes this detail more sinister.

Ryan’s arrest

    Based on the tip, Ryan is arrested and questioned. He insists he did nothing wrong, but the image of a teen boy, connected to Nora (who’s linked to William), picking up a missing nine‑year‑old looks damning.

    Nora and her husband Al are pulled into the maelstrom. Nora is terrified her son might be implicated and fears that her affair with William will be exposed. Al is furious and confused.

What actually happened to Avery (hidden timeline)

Behind all this, there is the “true” chain of events the book slowly reveals:

    Avery, used to being hit by her father and enraged by this latest slap, decides to punish him. She goes to see a neighbor, Marion – an odd, lonely woman living nearby – with a plan: she will “disappear” for a few days to scare her father and focus suspicion on him.

    Marion, who is emotionally unstable and nursing resentments and obsessions of her own, agrees to hide Avery in her basement. Avery wants her father to suffer public shame, to be suspected, and to feel as powerless as she has felt.​

    Avery stays in Marion’s basement, watching TV, eating, and following the media coverage. She is not actually restrained at first; she has some free rein in the basement, which makes her later “kidnapping” story more complicated.

    The anonymous “witness” who claimed to see Avery get into Ryan Blanchard’s car is Marion herself, lying to the police to deflect suspicion and to stir trouble between families.

2. Main characters and their real roles
The Wooler family

    William Wooler – doctor, husband to Erin, father to Avery and her brother. On the surface, a respectable family man; secretly having an affair with Nora and physically abusive to Avery. His lies about the affair and about hitting Avery make him the natural suspect.

    Erin Wooler – William’s wife, mother of Avery. Initially shocked and paralyzed by Avery’s disappearance, she becomes increasingly proactive, questioning neighbors herself and pushing past what the police are doing. Her trust in William shatters as his lies come out.

    Avery Wooler – nine years old, diagnosed with ADHD and significant behavioral problems. Smart, manipulative, and angry; she has been hit by her father before. She ultimately orchestrates her own disappearance to punish him and then spins out far darker lies to protect herself when things spiral out of control.

The Blanchard family

    Nora Blanchard – neighbor and William’s mistress. Married, with two children. She ends the affair at the start of the book, prompting William’s emotional spiral. She tries to keep the affair secret for self‑protection, even when honesty might help the investigation.

    Al Blanchard – Nora’s husband. He joins the search for Avery and is drawn into suspicion via Ryan, all while slowly realizing his wife has secrets.

    Ryan Blanchard – Nora and Al’s teenage son. He genuinely goes out searching for Avery with others, but Marion falsely claims to have seen Avery get into his car, leading to his arrest. He is innocent of harming Avery but becomes a scapegoat for some time.

Marion (key neighbor)

    Marion – a lonely, unstable neighbor who becomes central to the real explanation. She takes Avery in, hides her in her basement, and then lies to police by saying she saw Avery get into Ryan’s car.

    Motivations include resentment, jealousy, and a desire for power in a neighborhood where she’s otherwise invisible. She’s also infatuated with the idea of being at the center of drama and possibly harbors obsessive feelings toward William and/or his family.​

Other important figures

    Jenna and Alice – Jenna is Avery’s friend, and Alice is her mother. Jenna’s comments about Avery’s older “boyfriend” help steer the investigation toward a possible abuser and broaden suspicion in the neighborhood.​

    Michael – a neighborhood boy who tells police that William hits Avery regularly, supporting the abuse narrative.​

    Detectives – the investigating officers (often referred to collectively; the book isn’t a procedural in the classic sense). They juggle conflicting witness statements as everyone lies, half‑lies, or withholds information.

3. Motives: who wants what?
William

    Hide the affair with Nora from Erin, the kids, and the community.

    Hide the fact that he hit Avery right before she disappeared, to avoid arrest, divorce, and public disgrace.

    Find Avery – genuine parental fear and guilt – but constantly undermined by his own earlier lies.

Erin

    Find Avery at any cost.

    Discover the truth about what William has done, including his cheating and violence.

    Protect her children and regain a sense of control when the official investigation seems slow or misdirected.

Nora

    Keep the affair with William secret to protect her marriage and family.

    Protect Ryan when he is accused, even if that means revealing more of her own secrets.

    Escape the fallout of the affair once it becomes entangled with a missing child case.

Marion

    At first, to be needed: taking Avery in gives her a sense of importance and connection.

    To hurt or embarrass William (and possibly other families), aligning with Avery’s initial plan to make him suffer.

    As things get more dangerous, to avoid being exposed as the one hiding Avery. This leads her to escalate and consider killing Avery to remove the witness.

Avery

    Punish her father for his abuse by making him the focus of suspicion and public hatred.

    Enjoy the attention and power of being at the center of a missing child story, once she realizes how huge the reaction is.

    Protect herself from consequences once Marion’s involvement and her own role risk coming to light, even if that means telling much bigger lies.

4. Villain(s) and what actually happened

There are two “villain” layers: the adult neighbor who turns deadly, and the child’s extreme manipulation.
Marion’s escalation: attempted murder

    While Avery is in Marion’s basement, she watches the news and learns Ryan has been arrested. Avery doesn’t want Ryan to suffer for something he didn’t do, even though she wanted her father to be blamed.​

    Avery confronts Marion about her false statement identifying Ryan’s car. She tells Marion she has to recant because Avery doesn’t want Ryan punished.

    Marion realizes Avery could expose her as the one who:

        hid a missing child,

        lied to the police about Ryan.

    To avoid that, Marion decides she will kill Avery the next day. She plans to stage things so she’s not implicated, a “final solution” to her problem.

Avery’s lethal counter‑move

    Avery picks up on Marion’s intention and decides to strike first. She kills Marion (the reviews summarize this as Avery “beating her to it”) to avoid being murdered.

    Having killed Marion, Avery now needs a story that explains everything while keeping herself safe and still punishing the adults she resents.

Avery’s final lie (public story)

Avery constructs a narrative in which:

    Marion kidnapped her because Marion was obsessed with William and jealous that William chose Nora, not her, as a mistress.​

    Marion supposedly kept Avery drugged, trapped in the basement, and threatened, which explains why Avery didn’t escape even though she had some freedom earlier.

    Marion planned to frame both families—the Woolers and the Blanchards—out of twisted revenge and jealousy. Avery portrays herself as a terrified, passive victim.

To the outside world, Marion becomes the sole obvious villain: a deranged neighbor who abducted a child, lied to police, and then died in a confrontation. But the reader knows Avery killed her and that Avery is shaping the story to control blame.
5. Key interactions that crack the case
Erin vs the neighborhood (especially Marion)

    Erin doesn’t just sit back; she goes door to door, asking who saw what on the day Avery disappeared.

    When she visits Marion, Erin senses Marion is lying or withholding something. Under pressure, Marion admits she was the “anonymous” witness who claimed to see Avery get into Ryan’s car.

    This admission proves that key testimony was false and that Marion is more involved than anyone realized. Erin leaves, deeply suspicious. Avery overhears this confession from the basement.​

Detectives vs William

    Police initially accept William’s “I didn’t see her” story, but Michael’s claim of regular abuse and the burner phone force them to push harder.

    William eventually confesses both the affair and that he hit Avery that day and left her alone, pushing him from “concerned father” into “serious suspect.”

    These interrogations help surface multiple layers of lies but don’t, by themselves, solve the disappearance; they mostly show how thoroughly everyone is lying.

Avery vs Marion

    Avery persuades Marion to shelter her as part of a revenge plan against William: let everyone think Avery has vanished so her father is blamed and humiliated.​

    Once Ryan is arrested and Marion’s lie about seeing Avery in his car is revealed, Avery turns on Marion, insisting she must recant to save Ryan.

    Marion’s decision to kill Avery, and Avery’s preemptive killing of Marion, happen in this tension point. This is the true climactic “crime” sequence, though much of it is reconstructed later rather than shown live.

6. Resolution and final twist
Avery’s return and media spotlight

    Avery is eventually “found” and returned, telling a story of kidnapping, drugs, and threats in Marion’s basement, with Marion as the unambiguous villain.

    Erin and William are relieved but unsettled: over the next weeks, they notice Avery seems bizarrely happy, not traumatized, and eager to talk to the media.​

    Avery wants TV interviews and public attention, which doesn’t fit the image of a terrified child victim. Erin senses something is off but can’t quite pin it down.​

The televised slip

    Erin finally allows Avery to appear on national television to tell her story.

    During the interview, Avery slips and says she was angry at Marion for “double‑crossing” her—an odd phrase for a kidnap victim describing her captor.​

    The interviewer catches it and asks Avery to start from the beginning and tell the whole story of what really happened. This moment strongly implies that Avery’s carefully constructed narrative is about to collapse and that she will have to reveal her own role in orchestrating the disappearance and in Marion’s death.​

Thematic “villain” and end state

    On the surface: Marion is the obvious villain—she hid Avery and lied to the police. But she’s dead, and the community can safely blame her.

    Underneath: Avery is revealed as deeply manipulative and capable of lethal violence and sustained lying, even at nine years old. William’s abuse and the adults’ lies have shaped her into someone who weaponizes lies and public sympathy.

    The title, Everyone Here Is Lying, is literal:

        William lies about the affair and hitting Avery.

        Nora lies about the affair.

        Marion lies about the car and her involvement.

        Neighbors shade the truth or omit things.

        Avery tells the biggest lies of all, rewriting herself as a pure victim.

By the end, the case is “solved” for the public—Avery is home, Marion is dead, the community has a villain—but the truth is darker and messier. The book closes on the chilling suggestion that Avery, not Marion, may be the most dangerous person in Stanhope, and that her pattern of lying and manipulation has only just begun.
