The Red-Headed League sees Sherlock Holmes unravel a bizarre job scam targeting pawnbroker Jabez Wilson’s fiery red hair, exposing master criminal John Clay’s tunnel-digging bank heist masked by the fake league’s daily distractions. The tale unfolds from Wilson’s puzzled visit to Baker Street, through Holmes’s scrutiny of assistant Vincent Spaulding’s cellar antics, to a midnight bank vault stakeout where Holmes and police nab Clay as he breaches the gold stash.
Wilson’s Strange Job Offer

Red-headed pawnbroker Jabez Wilson visits Holmes and Watson, distraught over losing his dream gig: copying the Encyclopaedia Britannica for four hours daily at £4 weekly, paid by the mysterious Red-Headed League. His assistant Vincent Spaulding spotted the ad; at the league’s cramped Fleet Street office, one-eyed manager Duncan Ross hired Wilson alone from a redhead crowd, enforcing strict no-leaving rules under threat of job loss.

After eight weeks, Wilson arrives to find the office vanished—landlord denies its existence, note declares league “dissolved.” Spaulding, unperturbed, spends hours daily in Wilson’s Saxe-Coburg Square cellar “developing photos,” his acid-scarred forehead and cheap wages puzzling Holmes.
Holmes Spots the Criminal Connection

Holmes examines Spaulding (pierced ears betraying his disguise) and deduces his true identity: John Clay, London’s top cracksman, murderer, and forger, with a junior partner. The league’s absurdity—a hair-color charity paying lavish wages?—screams ruse to lure Wilson away afternoons, freeing the cellar for Clay’s real work.

Holmes taps Wilson’s pavement, confirming the cellar abuts City and Suburban Bank; worn knees on Spaulding’s trousers and coal dust betray tunnel-digging to the gold vault holding French Napoléons. League dissolution signals tunnel completion—robbery imminent that night, before gold shipment moves.
Stakeout and Tunnel Breach

Holmes recruits bank manager Mr. Merryweather and Scotland Yard’s Inspector Jones for a 10 p.m. vault vigil amid wooden-boxed gold bars; they douse lights, extinguish furnace glow to feign emptiness. After an hour’s wait, a lantern cracks the floor—Clay (clean-shaven, “bright-looking”) emerges, hauling red-headed accomplice Archie (alias Duncan Ross).

Holmes tackles Clay; Jones grabs Archie, who flees down-tunnel only to meet three more cops at the pawnshop end. Clay compliments Holmes’s thoroughness; Holmes praises the “ingenious” league ploy keeping Wilson absent precisely when needed.
Clay’s Motive and Holmes’s Wrap-Up

Clay needed Wilson out daily for uninterrupted digging from pawnshop cellar to bank—league ensured four empty hours, Ross (Archie) herded redhead applicants as cover. Spaulding’s photo ruse masked dirt removal; Wilson’s hiring over twelve rivals was rigged—Clay’s own ad placement.

Back at Baker Street, Holmes muses criminals overlook mundane solutions; Watson marvels at deduction from hair, assistant, and geography. Case underscores Holmes’s disdain for idleness—“my mind rebels at stagnation”—Clay’s flair earning wry respect.
