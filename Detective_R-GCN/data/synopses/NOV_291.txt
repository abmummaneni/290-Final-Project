DETAILED SPOILER SYNOPSIS

The novel interweaves two timelines: a present-day investigation in Fjällbacka, Sweden, and a historical narrative set during the Second World War concerning Swedish Nazi collaboration.

PRESENT-DAY PLOT

Crime writer Erica Falck (now married to Detective Patrik Hedström and the mother of their infant daughter Maja) discovers among her late mother Elsy's belongings a Nazi medallion and a diary, hinting at a wartime secret her mother never spoke of. This discovery draws Erica into an investigation of her own family's hidden past.

Meanwhile, a local neo-Nazi named Per Ringholm is found murdered. Patrik and his colleagues investigate while also dealing with a separate, apparently unrelated case: Erik Frankel, an elderly historian and expert on Nazi memorabilia, is also found dead in his home. He appears to have been murdered months earlier but the body went undiscovered until summer visitors returned. The investigation into Frankel's death takes on added urgency when it emerges that a neo-Nazi youth gang — including troubled teenager Adam, son of a local family — may be connected.

The two threads converge when it becomes clear that Frankel had been researching Swedish Nazi collaboration during the war and had uncovered information about specific individuals, including the ancestors of people still living in Fjällbacka.

WARTIME NARRATIVE

The historical sections follow a group of young friends in Fjällbacka during the German occupation of Norway and the broader Nazi period, including Elsy (Erica's mother as a teenager) and several male companions. One of them, Frans Ringholm (father of the murdered Per), was a genuine Nazi sympathizer. The group becomes caught up in events involving Norwegian refugees and a tragic death that was covered up.

A young Norwegian Jewish child was hidden by the group — the "hidden child" (Tyskungen, literally "the German child" in Swedish, though referring to a child hidden from the Germans) of the title. This child's fate, and the guilt surrounding what happened, becomes the buried secret that echoes through subsequent generations.

THE RESOLUTION

It emerges that Erik Frankel was killed not by neo-Nazis but by someone protecting a family secret rooted in the wartime events. The killer is revealed to be a member of the older generation — someone who feared that Frankel's research would expose what really happened to the hidden child and the roles various Fjällbacka residents played.

Frans Ringholm, elderly and dying of cancer, is connected to the wartime events and to his son Per's death as well, though his direct culpability is complicated. The neo-Nazi youth gang subplot is resolved with the exposure and arrest of the ringleader.

Erica's research into her mother's diary reveals that Elsy had been brave and morally upright during the war, attempting to protect the hidden Jewish child, and that the guilt Elsy carried was survivor's guilt rather than complicity. This provides Erica with a more complete, ultimately redemptive picture of her difficult, emotionally distant mother.

PERSONAL SUBPLOT

Throughout, Erica and Patrik navigate the stresses of new parenthood — sleep deprivation, shifting relationship dynamics, Erica's restlessness while on maternity leave. These domestic realities are characteristic of the series' blend of crime investigation with detailed family and community life.

THEMES AND NOTES

The novel examines Sweden's complex and often uncomfortable relationship with its own wartime history — officially neutral but complicit in various ways with Nazi Germany, a topic that became more openly discussed in Swedish public life in the 1990s and 2000s. The generational transmission of guilt, silence, and unexamined history is central. The "hidden child" functions both literally and metaphorically: the concealed Jewish child, but also the hidden truths within families and nations.
