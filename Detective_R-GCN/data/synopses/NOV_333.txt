NOV_333 — Mercy
(Original Danish title: Kvinden i buret — The Woman in the Cage)
Jussi Adler-Olsen, 2007 (English translation 2011)
Full Plot Synopsis (Department Q #1)

SERIES CONTEXT AND DEPARTMENT Q


Carl Mørck

Carl Mørck is a Detective Inspector with the Copenhagen police who is, at the novel's
opening, in a state of significant professional and personal collapse. He was involved in
an ambush a short time before the novel begins — an ambush in which two of his colleagues
were shot, one killed and one left paralyzed, while Carl himself escaped largely unharmed.
The circumstances of why Carl survived while his colleagues fell have never been fully
explained, and the implication — never quite spoken aloud but permeating everything —
is that Carl may have failed to act when action would have saved them.

Carl is consumed by survivor's guilt, uninterested in his work, borderline dysfunctional
in his personal life, and broadly resistant to rehabilitation. He drinks. He sits at his
desk. He does as little as possible and is as difficult as possible with anyone who tries
to engage him.


Department Q Is Created

Rather than fire Carl — which would require going through a politically difficult process
given his injury and status — the Copenhagen police solve the problem of him by creating
a new unit: Department Q, dedicated to cold cases of "special national importance." Carl
is placed in charge of it. It is, transparently, a way of putting him somewhere he cannot
cause trouble or consume departmental resources on active investigations.

Department Q is given office space in the basement of police headquarters — literally
underground. It is allotted minimal budget, minimal staff, and minimal institutional
respect. The entire thing is designed to be a graveyard for a difficult detective.


Assad

The one staff member allocated to Department Q is Hafez el-Assad — simply known as Assad —
a Syrian-born man who works as Carl's assistant. Assad is one of Adler-Olsen's most beloved
creations: apparently unsophisticated, cheerful in a way that resists Carl's misery, maker
of deeply questionable tea that he is inexplicably proud of, and gradually revealed to be
a person of considerable hidden depth, skill, and past. His background is initially opaque —
it is unclear exactly what Assad's history is or why he was assigned to Department Q, and
the series spends multiple books slowly revealing who he really is.

Assad's warmth and practicality are the perfect counterpoint to Carl's depression and
obstruction. They are, immediately, a wonderfully mismatched team.

THE COLD CASE: MERETE LYNGGAARD


A Danish Politician Disappeared

The case that Carl and Assad pick up — almost against Carl's will; Assad pushes it into
his attention — is the five-year-old disappearance of Merete Lynggaard, a prominent and
well-regarded Danish politician. Merete was a rising figure in Danish politics — smart,
principled, and popular — who vanished without explanation five years ago. She was traveling
on a ferry, was seen by other passengers, and then simply ceased to exist. No body was
ever found. The official conclusion was that she fell overboard and drowned.

The case was closed. Most people believe she is dead. A few do not.


Why This Case?

Assad's instinct for the Lynggaard case is not initially explained. Carl resists engaging
with it. But as he is forced — by Assad's persistence and his own grudging professional
instincts — to begin looking at the file, things begin to surface that make him sit up.
Small inconsistencies. Things that were not properly followed up. The shape of an investigation
that stopped too soon.

Carl begins, slowly, to care.

MERETE IS ALIVE


The Alternating Narrative

One of the novel's most powerful structural choices is that the reader is shown Merete's
perspective throughout. She is not dead. She has been held captive for five years in a
sealed pressure chamber — a tank-like enclosure where the atmospheric pressure is gradually
being increased as a form of torture. She has been in this chamber for the entirety of her
disappearance, kept alive but barely, isolated completely from the outside world, with no
sense of time, fed minimally, knowing nothing of why she is there or what her captors want.

The pressure is both literal and metaphorical. Every few months, it is increased slightly,
the physical strain on her body intensifying with it. She has developed means of surviving
mentally — routines, memories, conversations with herself — but five years of solitary
confinement in a pressurized tank has taken an enormous toll.

Reading Merete's chapters is genuinely agonizing. Adler-Olsen sustains the horror of her
situation — the claustrophobia, the physical deterioration, the psychological cost — with
unflinching precision. She is one of crime fiction's most compelling captive characters
because she is fully drawn as a person, not simply as a victim.


The Captors and Their Motive

Merete's captors are eventually revealed, as Carl's investigation proceeds, to be connected
to a specific tragedy in Merete's past — an event involving her brother, Uffe, who
survived an accident that killed their parents but was left with profound mental disability.
Merete became Uffe's caretaker and devoted her life to him alongside her political career.

The people holding Merete have a specific grievance connected to that accident and to what
they believe Merete or her family was responsible for. Their act of abduction was not
random; it was premeditated revenge, extended across years into something beyond revenge
into something altogether more twisted.

CARL'S INVESTIGATION


Resurrection of Interest

Carl's investigation into Merete's disappearance revives him — not quickly, not cleanly,
but in the way that a genuinely interesting problem revives a genuinely good detective even
when he is trying to remain inert. He interviews witnesses from the ferry. He revisits the
physical evidence. He and Assad track down people who knew Merete, people who worked with
her, and gradually begin to assemble a picture of what was happening in her life in the
weeks before she vanished.

What they find is a woman who had begun to notice she was being followed — who had become
frightened in ways she didn't fully understand and hadn't yet told anyone about. The
disappearance was not random opportunity. She was hunted.


The Secret Admirer and the Stalking

A specific thread involves an obsessive admirer whose behavior in the weeks before her
disappearance was alarming and whose connection to her captors is a significant part of
the investigation's later stages. The investigation traces the network of people connected
to Merete and to the specific grievance that produced her abduction.


Personal Stakes for Carl

As Carl invests more deeply in the case, it also becomes a vehicle for his own partial
recovery. Assad becomes genuinely important to him — a friendship forms, though Carl
would resist the word. The case of Merete, and the possibility that she might still be
alive if they move fast enough, pulls Carl back from the edge of the professional
non-existence he had been cultivating.

THE CLIMAX AND RESOLUTION

Carl and Assad identify Merete's captors and location. The race to find her before the
pressure in the chamber reaches a lethal level — the captors' apparent intention once they
learned that the investigation had been reopened — provides the novel's final sustained
tension. The clock is literal and physical: Merete's body cannot survive much more.

The confrontation with the captors and the rescue of Merete — after five years of
imprisonment — is the novel's emotional peak. Her survival is not clean; her body and
mind carry the full weight of five years of captivity and progressive physical abuse. She
lives, but what she has been through cannot be undone.

The captors are identified and brought to account. Their motive — the specific grievance
that drove them to create and sustain a five-year torture chamber — is fully revealed and
is as sad as it is monstrous.


THE ESTABLISHMENT OF DEPARTMENT Q

The resolution of the Lynggaard case establishes Department Q as something other than a
graveyard. Carl and Assad, in solving a five-year cold case that active investigators
failed to close, have demonstrated that the department — however it was created and whatever
its intended purpose — is capable of doing what the name implies.

Carl is not fixed. His guilt about the ambush, his drinking, his general difficulty are all
still present. But he is a detective again, which is more than he was at the novel's opening.
Assad remains Assad — warm, mysterious, impeccably strange — and their partnership, fully
formed by the Lynggaard case, will define both of them going forward.
