POD_032 | Your Own Backyard | Chris Lambert | Podcast
================================================================================
FULL PODCAST SYNOPSIS — SPOILERS INCLUDED
================================================================================

--- BACKGROUND AND CONTEXT ---

"Your Own Backyard" is an investigative true crime podcast created and hosted
by Chris Lambert, a filmmaker and content creator based in California who grew
up in San Luis Obispo County and had a personal connection to the case he
investigates. The podcast launched in 2019 and ran across multiple seasons of
episodes, with Lambert continuing to update the investigation as new developments
occurred.

The case at the center of the podcast is the disappearance of Kristin Smart,
a nineteen-year-old freshman at California Polytechnic State University (Cal
Poly) in San Luis Obispo, who vanished in May 1996 after attending an off-campus
party. She was never seen again. For twenty-five years the case went unsolved,
with no arrest, no charges, and no remains found. The podcast is credited as
a significant factor in generating the renewed public attention and investigative
pressure that ultimately led to the arrest, prosecution, and conviction of her
killer.

The title refers both to the literal geography of the case — Kristin's remains
were believed to be buried on or near property connected to the Flores family
in San Luis Obispo — and to the broader truth the podcast keeps returning to:
that violent crime against young women often originates not with strangers in
the dark but with people known to the victim, in the most ordinary and familiar
of settings.

--- KRISTIN SMART: WHO SHE WAS ---

Kristin Smart was born in 1977 and grew up in Stockton, California. She was
the daughter of Denise and Stan Smart, and she had two siblings. She was
described by everyone who knew her as friendly, outgoing, and enthusiastic —
the kind of person who talked to everyone, who made friends easily, and who
had the specific social openness of someone who genuinely liked people. She
had chosen Cal Poly for its strong agricultural and environmental programs and
arrived on campus in the fall of 1995 full of the energy of a young person
starting a new chapter.

She was in her second semester when she disappeared. She had not yet fully
found her footing at Cal Poly — she was still making friends, still figuring
out the social landscape of university life — which would later become relevant
to the investigation: she was someone who was still new enough to the community
that her social network was not yet deep, and whose absence, in the immediate
aftermath of her disappearance, could be attributed by some to her simply
having moved on or gone home.

--- THE NIGHT OF MAY 25, 1996 ---

On the evening of Saturday, May 25, 1996, Kristin Smart attended an off-campus
party in the Crandall Way neighborhood near the Cal Poly campus, along with
other students. The party was the kind of event that is entirely ordinary in
college life: a gathering at a private home, students drinking, socializing,
moving between rooms and between the house and the street outside.

At some point in the evening, Kristin had consumed alcohol and was visibly
intoxicated. As the party wound down, she was seen outside the house, in a
condition that concerned at least one witness — she was unsteady and appeared
to need assistance getting home.

Three students left the party together heading toward campus: Kristin Smart,
a young man named Paul Flores, and another student named Cheryl Anderson.
The three walked together for a portion of the route. At a certain point,
Cheryl Anderson turned off toward her own residence. She last saw Kristin
walking with Paul Flores, who said he would walk her the rest of the way home.
Kristin Smart was never seen again.

Paul Flores was the last known person to see Kristin Smart alive.

--- PAUL FLORES ---

Paul Flores was also a Cal Poly student and was nineteen at the time of
Kristin's disappearance. He was from the San Luis Obispo area — his family,
including his father Ruben Flores, lived locally. He was known to Kristin
in the general way of students who move in overlapping social circles on a
college campus: they were not close friends, but they knew each other and
had encountered each other on previous occasions.

In the immediate aftermath of Kristin's disappearance, Paul Flores was noted
as a person of interest by investigators. He was questioned multiple times.
His behavior in these early interviews was evasive and inconsistent. He had
a scratch on his face that he could not satisfactorily explain. He appeared
nervous and uncomfortable in ways that the investigators noticed.

Despite these early flags, Paul Flores was never charged. The investigation
could not produce sufficient evidence for a prosecution. No body was found.
No physical evidence directly connecting Flores to a crime was established
to the prosecutorial standard. The case remained open but inactive.

--- THE TWENTY-FIVE YEAR COLD CASE ---

The years between 1996 and the podcast's launch in 2019 represent one of the
longest sustained cold case periods in a high-profile disappearance in California
history. The Smart family — particularly Kristin's parents Denise and Stan,
whose public advocacy for the case was sustained across two and a half decades —
never stopped pushing for investigation and resolution. They gave interviews,
attended forums, maintained a public profile for the case, and worked with
law enforcement to keep it from being completely forgotten.

Various investigative developments occurred over the years: periodic searches
of properties associated with Flores, renewed media coverage at significant
anniversaries, and continuing attention from the San Luis Obispo County
Sheriff's Office, which maintained the case as an open investigation. A 2002
civil suit brought by the Smart family against Paul Flores established, through
the civil standard of evidence, that he was responsible for Kristin's
disappearance — though this was a civil finding, not a criminal conviction,
and did not result in prosecution.

Paul Flores himself continued to live in California, working various jobs and
maintaining a relatively ordinary life. He was not in hiding. He was known
in the community as the person who was last with Kristin Smart, and he lived
with that knowledge while no criminal prosecution materialized.

--- CHRIS LAMBERT'S PERSONAL CONNECTION ---

Chris Lambert grew up in San Luis Obispo County, and Kristin Smart's
disappearance was part of the fabric of his childhood and adolescence. The
case was locally well known — a college student missing from the campus that
was central to the community's identity — and it occupied a specific place
in the consciousness of people who grew up in the area.

Lambert's motivation for creating the podcast was both personal and journalistic.
He believed the case had been inadequately investigated and that the accumulated
evidence against Paul Flores, while perhaps insufficient for a 1990s-era
prosecution, was compelling enough that a modern investigative treatment might
generate new information, new witnesses, or new pressure on the case.

He was also motivated by a sense of what the case meant more broadly: a young
woman who disappeared during the vulnerable first year of college, whose
disappearance was facilitated by the specific circumstances of college party
culture and the specific social dynamics of a community where everyone assumed
everyone else was safe. The podcast is partly a personal reckoning with what
it means to grow up in a place where something like this happened and was not
resolved for a generation.

--- THE PODCAST'S INVESTIGATIVE METHOD ---

Lambert's approach to the investigation combined several strategies:

ARCHIVAL RESEARCH: He assembled the most comprehensive available record of
the original 1996 investigation — police reports, witness statements, media
coverage, court documents from the civil proceedings — and presented it to
his audience with detailed analysis of what each element showed and what
it left unexplained.

NEW WITNESS INTERVIEWS: He identified and interviewed people connected to the
case who had not previously spoken publicly — fellow students from 1996 who
had been at the party or who had information about Paul Flores's behavior before
or after the disappearance, former neighbors and acquaintances of the Flores
family, and people connected to the various properties that had been the subject
of previous searches.

COMMUNITY ENGAGEMENT: The podcast generated substantial listener engagement,
including tips and information from people in the San Luis Obispo community
who had held information for years without knowing whether it was relevant or
where to direct it. Some of this listener-generated information was significant
to the developing investigation.

DIRECT INVESTIGATION: Lambert conducted physical investigation of locations
relevant to the case, including properties associated with the Flores family,
and documented evidence of what those locations showed.

--- RUBEN FLORES AND THE PROPERTY ---

One of the podcast's most significant investigative contributions was its
sustained attention to Ruben Flores, Paul's father, and specifically to the
property at Ruben's home in Arroyo Grande, California.

Lambert's research into the history of the Flores property — the specific
changes made to the landscaping and ground over the years since 1996, the
accounts of people who had been on the property at various times, and the
specific geography of the location relative to where the Smart family believed
Kristin might be buried — directed investigative attention to Ruben's home
in a way that would prove directly relevant to the subsequent law enforcement
investigation.

The theory that Kristin Smart's remains were buried somewhere on or connected
to Flores family property was not new — law enforcement had considered it
previously — but Lambert's detailed treatment of the evidence supporting this
theory, and his specific focus on Ruben's Arroyo Grande property, gave the
investigation new traction.

--- THE SMART FAMILY ---

Denise and Stan Smart are central presences throughout the podcast. Their
two and a half decades of advocacy for their daughter — attending every hearing,
every law enforcement briefing, every press conference; giving interviews year
after year to keep the case in public consciousness; refusing to accept the
effective dormancy of the official investigation — is one of the podcast's
most sustained and affecting threads.

Lambert treats the Smart family with considerable care. He is aware that the
podcast is engaging with their grief as well as with a crime investigation,
and the specific pain of parents who have spent twenty-five years not knowing
where their daughter is or exactly what happened to her is rendered with real
attention and humanity.

The relationship between the podcast's investigation and the Smart family's
own advocacy is collaborative: the family was supportive of Lambert's work and
provided access to information and their own memories of Kristin as a person —
not simply as a missing person but as the daughter and sister they knew before
she went to college.

--- RENEWED LAW ENFORCEMENT INVESTIGATION ---

The combination of Lambert's podcast, the sustained Smart family advocacy, and
improved investigative tools and techniques led to a renewed, intensive
investigation by the San Luis Obispo County Sheriff's Office and the California
Department of Justice in the years after the podcast's launch.

In April 2021 — twenty-five years after Kristin's disappearance — Paul Flores
was arrested and charged with the first-degree murder of Kristin Smart. On the
same day, his father Ruben Flores was arrested and charged with accessory after
the fact for allegedly helping his son conceal Kristin's remains.

The arrests were the result of a years-long investigation that included:

Surveillance of Paul and Ruben Flores, in which investigators observed and
documented behavior consistent with the concealment of evidence.

Ground-penetrating radar examination of the Ruben Flores property in Arroyo
Grande, which detected anomalies beneath the surface of the property's yard
consistent with disturbed earth and the presence of remains.

DNA evidence obtained from the examination of the property and from items
connected to the case.

Testimony from new witnesses who had come forward — some directly as a result
of the podcast's audience — with information about Paul Flores's behavior and
statements in the years since 1996.

Digital forensic evidence related to Paul Flores's online activity, including
the discovery on his devices of material of an extremely disturbing nature
that was consistent with a specific profile of predatory behavior toward women.

--- THE TRIAL ---

Paul Flores stood trial in 2022. The trial was held in Santa Barbara County
(venue was changed due to the difficulty of finding an impartial jury in San
Luis Obispo County after the extensive publicity). The prosecution presented
a circumstantial case — no body had been found, and the physical evidence
of the murder itself was limited — but the circumstantial evidence was extensive
and compelling:

Paul Flores was the last person seen with Kristin Smart. He had no credible
account of what happened after Cheryl Anderson left them on the night of
May 25, 1996.

He had a scratch on his face the following morning that he could not explain.

His behavior in the days after the disappearance was consistent with guilty
knowledge: he was evasive, he changed his story in multiple interviews, and
his affect was described by investigators as inappropriate given the circumstances.

The ground-penetrating radar results from Ruben's property, combined with
evidence from the property consistent with disturbed earth and with Kristin's
DNA, supported the theory that her remains were buried there — and subsequently
moved when investigators' attention focused on the property.

Expert testimony established that the specific injuries Paul Flores had
sustained (the scratch) were consistent with the kind of resistance a victim
of assault would offer, and inconsistent with the explanations he provided.

Digital evidence from Paul Flores's devices established a pattern of behavior
and interest that was consistent with the prosecution's characterization of
him as someone whose predatory conduct toward women was not an isolated incident.

The defense challenged the circumstantial nature of the evidence and argued
that the prosecution had not established beyond reasonable doubt that Paul
Flores murdered Kristin Smart in the absence of a body, a confirmed cause of
death, or direct physical evidence of the killing.

In October 2022, the jury found Paul Flores guilty of first-degree murder.
He was sentenced to twenty-five years to life in state prison.

--- RUBEN FLORES'S TRIAL ---

Ruben Flores was tried separately for his role as accessory after the fact.
The prosecution's case was that Ruben helped his son conceal Kristin Smart's
remains — specifically, that the body was initially buried at Ruben's Arroyo
Grande property and that Ruben participated in or facilitated its subsequent
removal when investigation intensified.

Ruben Flores was convicted of the accessory charge and sentenced to three
years in state prison.

--- KRISTIN'S REMAINS ---

As of the completion of the trials, Kristin Smart's remains had not been found
in their entirety. Partial DNA evidence from the Flores property was part of
the prosecution's case, but no complete remains were recovered. This means
that despite the conviction, Kristin's family has not had the specific
resolution of being able to bury their daughter. The specific location of her
remains — whether they were permanently destroyed, scattered, or moved to a
location that has not been identified — is not known.

This absence of remains, and the specific grief it represents for the Smart
family, is one of the most painful elements of a case that is otherwise
resolved in legal terms.

--- THE PODCAST'S LEGACY ---

"Your Own Backyard" is one of the clearest examples of true crime podcasting
as genuine investigative journalism with direct real-world consequence. Lambert's
work — his assembly of the historical record, his new witness interviews, his
sustained focus on the Flores family and their properties, and his generation
of a large and engaged listener community that fed information back into the
investigation — contributed directly to the renewed law enforcement effort
that produced the 2021 arrests.

The San Luis Obispo County Sheriff's Office and the prosecution's team publicly
acknowledged the role of public attention in keeping the case alive and in
generating new information. Several of the witnesses whose testimony was used
in the trial came forward as a result of the podcast.

Lambert himself was clear throughout the podcast's run about the limits of
his role: he was not conducting an official investigation, he was not a law
enforcement officer, and the determination of guilt or innocence was properly
a matter for the courts. His stated purpose was to document the case as fully
as possible, to keep it in public consciousness, and to ensure that information
that existed in the community had a channel through which it could reach the
appropriate authorities.

--- THE MEANING OF THE TITLE ---

"Your Own Backyard" is a title that works on multiple levels. Literally, it
refers to the theory — ultimately confirmed by the evidence — that Kristin
Smart's remains were buried in Ruben Flores's backyard in Arroyo Grande, visible
and accessible to anyone who knew where to look. Figuratively, it refers to
the broader truth the case illustrates: that violence against young women most
often comes from the people and places closest to them, not from the stranger-
danger frameworks that parents and communities tend to focus on. Kristin Smart
was not attacked by a stranger. She was attacked by a person she knew, on a
walk home from a party that could have been any college party on any campus
in America, in circumstances that were entirely ordinary until they were not.

The title also has a quality of reproach — "your own backyard" is a phrase
that implies something hidden in plain sight, something that should have been
visible and was not, something that a community should have found and addressed
and didn't for twenty-five years. The people of San Luis Obispo grew up,
went to school, lived their lives, and drove past the Flores property in Arroyo
Grande without knowing what was there. The podcast's title holds that fact
quietly and asks its audience to sit with what it means.

--- SUMMARY ---

"Your Own Backyard" is one of the most consequential true crime podcasts ever
made, and one of the most complete in terms of narrative arc: it covers a
twenty-five-year cold case from its origins through the podcast's investigative
work to the arrest, trial, and conviction of the killer. Chris Lambert's
sustained, meticulous, and deeply personal engagement with the Kristin Smart
case produced something that was simultaneously journalism, advocacy, and
community service — a document of a specific harm done to a specific young
woman, and of the specific institutional failures that allowed her killer to
live freely for two and a half decades. The Smart family's long fight for
justice, Paul Flores's conviction, and the partial resolution of a case that
will never be fully resolved — because Kristin has not been found — are all
part of a story that the podcast tells with the full weight it deserves.
