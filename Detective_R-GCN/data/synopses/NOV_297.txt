Salvation of a Saint presents an inverted mystery where readers know from the opening scene that Ayane Mashiba poisoned her unfaithful husband Yoshitaka, but the police must unravel the "how" of an impossible crime committed from hundreds of miles away. The narrative follows Tokyo detectives Kusanagi and Utsumi, aided by physicist Yukawa (Detective Galileo), as they probe motives tied to infertility, betrayal, and a dead friend's arsenic gift, culminating in the revelation of a year-long water filter trap that forces Yoshitaka to brew his own death.
Marital Collapse and the Poisoned Coffee

The story opens with Yoshitaka Mashiba confronting his wife Ayane in their Tokyo home: after a childless year of marriage—per their explicit prenup agreement—he demands divorce to pursue her young quilting student, the pregnant Hiromi Suguro. Devastated but outwardly calm, Ayane leaves for a long weekend in distant Sapporo, handing house keys to Hiromi for plant-watering duties.

Days later, Hiromi finds Yoshitaka dead in the living room from arsenic poisoning, a half-empty coffee cup beside him; the house is locked from inside, no signs of forced entry, and forensics confirm the arsenic leached slowly into his morning brew from an impossible source. Ayane's alibi is ironclad—verified Sapporo hotel stay, no travel records—but her motive screams: Yoshitaka's infidelity shatters her hope of motherhood and stability.
Kusanagi's Investigation and Suspect Pool

Veteran Detective Kusanagi leads the case, drawn to Ayane's quiet grace and convinced of her innocence despite the glaring opportunity (Hiromi as proxy?). He canvasses suspects:

    Hiromi, the mistress, whose pregnancy and access raise jealousy angles.

    Ayane's quilting circle, including bitter acquaintances.

    Yoshitaka's business associates, hinting at financial grudges.

Utsumi, Kusanagi's sharp junior partner, senses his growing infatuation with Ayane clouds judgment and pushes forensic angles: how did arsenic enter a fresh coffee pot when Ayane was absent? Toxicology shows arsenous acid, rare and traceable, but no container or delivery method surfaces.
Yukawa's Physics and the Dead Friend Twist

Utsumi recruits Kusanagi's old friend, physicist Manabu Yukawa (Galileo), for objective insight; Yukawa ponders contamination vectors like air, pipes, or timed devices, rejecting implausible "locked-room" tricks. Digging into Ayane's past reveals her friendship with artist Mina Tsukui, who died by "suicide" (actually arsenic overdose) a year prior, leaving Ayane postcards from London and—crucially—a hidden vial of arsenous acid acquired there.

Flashbacks expose Ayane's guilt: she and Mina vied for Yoshitaka at a singles party (staged as "fateful meeting"); Mina lost, spiraled into depression, mailed Ayane the poison as twisted parting gift before killing herself. Ayane hoarded it, her "saintly" facade masking festering resentment over Yoshitaka's reproductive ultimatum and betrayal.
The Water Filter Trap Revealed

Yukawa cracks the method: a year earlier, Ayane laced their home water filter cartridge with the powdered arsenic, calculating slow dissolution over time. She knew Yoshitaka's routine—always brewed coffee via that filter each morning—and timed her Sapporo exit precisely so the cumulative dose hit lethal peak on his solo day, self-administered without her presence.

Traces in the discarded filter confirm it; the house's locked state and Hiromi's irrelevant key-drop seal the alibi's perfection. Kusanagi's team overlooked the filter amid coffee-focused forensics, but Yukawa's physics (dissolution rates, dosage math) proves irrefutable.
Confession, Motive Depth, and Bittersweet Close

Confronted with filter evidence and Tsukui's arsenic link, Ayane confesses in a tearful wine-bar finale: her "salvation" stems from shedding saintly endurance, using Mina's poison to "save" herself from loveless marriage while ironically fulfilling Yoshitaka's childless pact by proxy (Hiromi's pregnancy). Kusanagi's affections curdle into professional resolve; Utsumi grows as detective; Yukawa departs, musing on science exposing human fragility.

Ayane faces trial, her guilt absolved in narrative poetry—she "saved" the saintly wife by killing the user—but legally damned; the novel ends on thematic ambiguity, pondering if premeditated patience redeems or damns.
