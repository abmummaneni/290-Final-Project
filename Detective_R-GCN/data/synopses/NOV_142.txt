--- BACKGROUND AND CONTEXT ---

"Before Midnight," published in 1955, is one of the most formally inventive
novels in Rex Stout's Nero Wolfe series — and the most formally inventive in
a series that is characterised throughout by formal invention. The Nero Wolfe
series, begun in 1934, follows the routines and cases of Nero Wolfe: an
enormously fat, almost pathologically sedentary genius who grows orchids on
the roof of his West 35th Street brownstone, drinks between two and four quarts
of beer daily, rarely leaves the house, and is one of the finest private
detectives in the world. His legman and narrator is Archie Goodwin — younger,
physically active, socially adept, sharp-tongued, and in possession of a
photographic memory that serves as the investigation's recording instrument.

The specific flagging for this novel — "NO_VILLAIN, NO_VICTIM_EITHER" — reflects
a genuinely unusual situation in mystery fiction: the novel involves a murder,
but the conventional categories of victim and villain are substantially inverted
from the reader's initial understanding of them, and the moral weight of the
resolution is distributed in a way that makes those categories almost inapplicable
in their conventional sense.

--- THE PERFUME CONTEST ---

"Before Midnight" opens in the world of advertising and consumer contest culture —
specifically, a major national advertising campaign for a perfume company,
Pour Amour, that has constructed a puzzle-contest for the American public.
The contest requires contestants to match a set of five perfumes to five
poetic clues, and has generated enormous public participation. The five correct
answers — the names of the five perfumes that match the five clues — are known
only to the five judges who designed the contest, and the contest is about to
enter its final phase.

The five judges of the Pour Amour contest — five advertising, literary, and
cultural figures whose specific combination of expertise was assembled to
guarantee the contest's integrity — are the novel's central characters. They
are:

Louis Dahlmann, the contest's chief creative figure and the primary architect
of the puzzle.
Assa, an advertising executive.
Buff, a literary figure.
Younger, a cultural critic.
O'Garro, a corporate representative.

Each of the five judges holds one of the five correct answers. The contest's
design requires that all five answers be present together for the winning entry
to be verified — no single judge can confirm a winner alone, and no single
judge knows the others' answers.

This is the mechanism. Understanding the mechanism is essential to understanding
the murders.

--- THE MURDER OF LOUIS DAHLMANN ---

Louis Dahlmann is murdered. He is killed in circumstances that are violent and
deliberate, and his death is clearly connected to the contest — specifically
to the specific answer he held, and to the specific financial stakes of knowing
all five answers before the contest closes.

The contest's prize is very large. A person who knew all five answers could
construct a perfect winning entry — or could sell that knowledge to a contestant
who would share the winnings. The specific financial incentive for obtaining
all five answers is substantial, and Dahlmann's murder — with its specific
connection to his role as a judge — immediately suggests that someone is trying
to obtain the answers by eliminating the judges and searching their possessions.

The four surviving judges come to Nero Wolfe. They are frightened — each of
them holds one of the remaining answers, each of them is potentially the next
target, and the specific mechanism of the contest's design means that they
cannot simply surrender the answers to the police without destroying the contest's
integrity.

--- WOLFE'S INVESTIGATION ---

Wolfe's investigation is characteristically conducted from his brownstone,
with Archie as his mobile instrument — interviewing the judges, managing the
police relationship (specifically with Inspector Cramer of the NYPD, Wolfe's
regular institutional adversary and occasional collaborator), and assembling
the specific physical and testimonial evidence that Wolfe processes from his
chair.

The investigation must simultaneously establish who killed Dahlmann and protect
the four surviving judges from further harm while the contest approaches its
resolution. The time pressure — the "before midnight" of the title refers to
the contest's closing deadline — creates urgency alongside the murder investigation.

A second murder occurs during the investigation. Another judge is killed —
again in circumstances connected to their specific position as a holder of
one of the answers. The pattern is clear: someone is systematically killing
the judges and, presumably, searching their effects for the answers they held.

--- THE INVERSION OF VICTIM AND VILLAIN ---

The specific formal achievement of "Before Midnight" — what the "NO_VILLAIN,
NO_VICTIM_EITHER" flag points toward — is the resolution's specific moral
inversion.

The killer is identified as one of the four judges — specifically, as the person
among the surviving judges who has been presenting as one of the potential
next victims while actually being the person systematically eliminating the
others to acquire their answers.

The killer is Buff — the literary figure among the judges. Buff killed Dahlmann
and the second judge for the specific purpose of acquiring their answers, adding
them to his own, and constructing a complete winning entry or selling the
complete information.

But the moral inversion that makes "Before Midnight" unusual is what the
investigation establishes about the victims. Dahlmann — the primary victim,
the man whose murder initiated the investigation — was himself engaged in the
same scheme as Buff, but from the other direction. He was selling the contest
answers, or had been negotiating to sell them, to a specific party outside
the contest. He was not an innocent victim of a criminal scheme: he was a
participant in a competing criminal scheme, killed by a competitor.

The second judge was also not entirely innocent — their specific relationship
to the contest's integrity was, the investigation establishes, compromised
in their own specific way.

This produces the novel's central moral situation: a murder investigation in
which the victims were themselves criminals, killed by a fellow criminal in
the specific context of competition for the fruits of their shared crime. The
conventional distinction between victim and perpetrator has not simply blurred —
it has essentially dissolved.

Wolfe's specific response to this situation is characteristic of the series'
moral framework. He does not simply hand Buff to the police and declare the
case closed. His specific management of the resolution — what he tells the
police, what he withholds, and what specific outcome he engineers — reflects
the novel's understanding that conventional justice cannot straightforwardly
be applied to a situation in which its categories do not straightforwardly apply.

--- ARCHIE'S ROLE ---

Archie Goodwin's role in "Before Midnight" is one of the series' most active —
the specific time pressure of the contest deadline requires him to be
continuously mobile, moving between the judges, the contest organisers, and
the police in ways that are both procedurally essential and personally demanding.
Stout uses the time pressure to give Archie's narration an urgency that the
more leisurely Wolfe novels sometimes lack.

His specific observations about the judges — the specific ways in which their
individual presentations of alarm and cooperation are either genuine or
performed — contribute directly to Wolfe's identification of the killer. Archie's
photographic memory and his specific social intelligence, reading people in
the way that Wolfe cannot from his chair, are the investigation's primary
field instruments.

--- THE CONTEST RESOLVED ---

The Pour Amour contest is resolved — not in the way its organisers intended,
and with the specific legal and commercial complications that the murders and
the investigation have created. The specific outcome for the contest itself
reflects the novel's mordant sense of how commercial enterprises interact with
criminal events.

--- NOTES ON THE NOVEL ---

"Before Midnight" is one of the more formally inventive Wolfe novels and one
of the most interesting in its specific moral territory. The contest setting
is ingeniously constructed — the specific mechanism by which five separate
answers create a situation of mutual dependence and mutual vulnerability is
both the puzzle's cleverness and its specific criminal opportunity. The
dissolution of the victim/villain distinction — the investigation finding that
the murdered are themselves murderous in their intentions — gives the novel
a moral complexity that the mystery genre rarely achieves without melodrama.
Stout handles it with the light touch and the specific irony that his best
work consistently demonstrates.
