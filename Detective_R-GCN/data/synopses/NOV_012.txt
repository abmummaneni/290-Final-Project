NOV_012 | The Red House Mystery | A.A. Milne | Novel
================================================================================
FULL PLOT SYNOPSIS — SPOILERS INCLUDED
================================================================================

--- BACKGROUND AND SETTING ---

"The Red House Mystery," published in 1922, is A.A. Milne's only detective
novel — a light, witty, and elegantly constructed country-house mystery written
three years before Winnie-the-Pooh would make him famous as a children's author.
The novel is set almost entirely at the Red House, a comfortable English country
estate belonging to one Mark Ablett, over the course of a single summer weekend.
It is a self-aware, somewhat playful contribution to the genre, with the author
clearly enjoying the conventions of the Golden Age mystery even as he constructs
a perfectly sound one.

The story features two central characters who do most of the detective work:
Antony Gillingham, a young man of independent means and charming intelligence
who happens to be visiting the neighborhood, and his friend Bill Beverley,
a more ordinary fellow who serves as a genial Watson-figure, asking useful
questions and supplying companionable enthusiasm.

--- THE HOUSE PARTY AND THE ARRIVAL ---

Mark Ablett is a pleasant, somewhat self-important bachelor who has invited a
houseful of friends for an extended country weekend. His guests include a varied
collection of county types — well-drawn in Milne's deft, slightly satirical
style. The mood is light and sociable.

The weekend is disrupted by news that Mark's brother Robert is arriving
unexpectedly. Robert Ablett is the family black sheep: a man with a notorious
past, rumored to have spent years in Australia, possibly under some kind of
legal cloud. Mark has not seen him for many years. Robert's imminent appearance
causes visible anxiety in Mark, who had apparently hoped to keep Robert's
existence forgotten among his new social circle.

Robert arrives. He is rough, coarse, and clearly out of place among the
country-house guests. He and Mark go into the study together for a private
conversation. The other guests hear what sounds like a heated argument — raised
voices — and then a shot.

--- THE DISCOVERY IN THE STUDY ---

When the study door is opened by concerned guests and staff, a horrible scene
is found. A man is dead on the floor, shot in the head. The pistol is nearby.
But there is immediate confusion: the dead man is Robert Ablett, not Mark.
Mark himself is nowhere to be found.

The window of the study is open, and there are traces suggesting someone left
through it. The guests search the house and grounds. Mark Ablett has vanished
completely.

The local police are summoned. They assume one of two scenarios: either Mark
shot his brother and fled (murder and flight), or Robert shot himself (suicide)
and Mark, finding the body and panicking, ran away. The police lean toward the
former but acknowledge the physical evidence is ambiguous.

--- ANTONY GILLINGHAM TAKES AN INTEREST ---

Antony Gillingham happens to arrive at the Red House in this exact moment of
chaos — he is visiting a friend nearby and has walked over. He is struck
immediately by the fascination of the situation and begins his own quiet
investigation, with Bill Beverley eagerly attached as his assistant.

Antony is no hard-boiled detective; he is an amateur of intelligence and
curiosity, proceeding with a kind of cheerful seriousness. The novel
deliberately evokes the Sherlock Holmes model — Antony even explicitly
compares his methods to Holmes's at certain points — while keeping the tone
light. He and Bill move through the house, talking to guests and servants,
examining the study, and exploring the grounds.

Antony is particularly interested in a series of details that don't quite fit
the simple "Mark shot Robert and ran" theory:

First, the body is that of Robert, who has been living rough and is evidently
much coarser and more weathered than the well-kept Mark — yet the dead man,
on closer examination, has hands that are in better condition than one would
expect for a man of Robert's described lifestyle.

Second, the study door was locked from the inside when the guests first tried
to open it, and only a spare key was used to enter — but who locked it, and
why?

Third, a small item of clothing or personal effect near the body seems more
consistent with Mark than with Robert.

--- THE COTTAGE AND THE POND ---

Antony expands his investigation to the grounds of the estate. Near the Red
House is a small ornamental pond and an old gamekeeper's cottage that has been
disused for some time. Antony and Bill discover that someone has been using the
cottage recently — there are signs of very recent habitation: food, bedding,
disturbance in the dust.

This is significant. Whoever has been hiding in the cottage was doing so
deliberately, close to the main house but concealed. Antony infers that
whoever is in the cottage is connected to the crime.

An investigation of the pond reveals a bundle of clothes — submerged and
weighted. When retrieved, these prove to be the rough, coarse clothing of
the type Robert Ablett would have been wearing.

--- THE THEORY TAKES SHAPE ---

Antony now begins to construct his theory of the crime, sharing it with Bill
in the novel's characteristic fashion — dialogue-driven, with Antony working
through the logic aloud and Bill responding with useful incredulity or
enthusiasm.

His theory, assembled piece by piece, is as follows:

Mark Ablett did not kill his brother Robert. Instead, Mark Ablett IS his
brother Robert — or more precisely, Robert Ablett has been impersonating Mark
for years. The real Mark Ablett died some time ago (or was killed by Robert),
and Robert, who bore enough family resemblance to his brother and had been
absent long enough that no one who knew the real Mark was around, assumed
Mark's identity, his estate, his money, and his social position.

Robert — now living as Mark — knew that the real investigation into who he was
would inevitably arise if his actual brother ever appeared. The arrival of
"Robert" — actually the real surviving man who knew the truth — threatened
everything. When they met in the study, Robert (as Mark) killed the man who
could expose him, dressing him in Robert's rough clothes to make the corpse
look like Robert. He then took his own clothing off, changed into something that
would allow him to escape without being identified as the man everyone knew as
"Mark," and slipped out through the window.

The man hiding in the cottage is "Mark" — i.e., Robert — who has been hiding
on the grounds in his new disguise while deciding what to do next, monitoring
the investigation.

--- THE IDENTITY QUESTION ---

The novel now investigates the history of the Ablett brothers to confirm this
theory. Antony and the investigators trace Mark/Robert's past. They find that
the real Mark Ablett and his brother Robert were estranged many years ago and
that Robert went abroad. During Robert's time in Australia, the real Mark also
seems to have had an obscure period during which he was not in contact with his
English friends and family.

The substitution took place during this gap. Robert returned to England not
as himself but as his dead (or killed) brother, having acquired enough
knowledge of Mark's life and enough social distance from Mark's earlier
acquaintances to carry off the deception for years.

The man who arrived as "Robert" at the house party weekend was not Robert at
all, but someone with knowledge of the real situation — perhaps an old
associate from Australia who knew the truth — and his arrival forced the
impostor's hand.

--- CAPTURE OF THE KILLER ---

The cottage where the fugitive has been hiding is staked out by Antony and
eventually by the police. The man calling himself Mark Ablett — now stripped
of that identity, revealed as Robert who murdered his own brother years ago
and then murdered again to protect his secret — is found and arrested.

The full case is laid before the police and subsequently before the courts.
The physical evidence — the switched clothing in the pond, the cottage hideout,
the condition of the dead man's hands, and records from Australia establishing
Robert's real history — is assembled into a complete picture.

--- RESOLUTION ---

The killer is taken into custody and faces justice for two murders: the killing
of the real Mark Ablett years earlier, and the killing of whoever the man was
who arrived that weekend knowing the truth. The comfortable world of the Red
House house party is permanently shattered.

The guests disperse. Antony and Bill depart, their amateur investigation
complete. The tone at the end remains relatively light — the novel never
becomes truly dark despite its double-murder plot — which is consistent with
Milne's design: this is a puzzle story, an exercise in elegant construction,
more interested in the mechanics of the mystery than in its psychological weight.

--- NOTES ON STRUCTURE ---

"The Red House Mystery" is constructed with considerable care. Milne plants
clues fairly — the reader who pays close attention to the condition of the
dead man's hands, the locked door, the pond, and certain comments about the
Ablett family history has everything needed to solve the mystery before Antony
reveals his solution. Raymond Chandler famously criticized the novel in "The
Simple Art of Murder" (1944), arguing that the plot had a logical flaw regarding
whether the imposture could really have been sustained. This critique remains
debated among readers. What is not debated is the charm and wit of Milne's
prose and the elegance of the central puzzle construction.
