FULL PLOT SYNOPSIS — SPOILERS INCLUDED

--- BACKGROUND AND SETTING ---

"Vanish," published in 2005, is the fifth novel in Tess Gerritsen's Rizzoli &
Isles series. It is one of the series' most propulsive and structurally
unusual entries — the novel opens with a sequence from the perspective of a
woman other than Rizzoli or Isles, and it incorporates a hostage situation
that drives much of the first half of the book. The novel deepens the series'
ongoing engagement with human trafficking, Eastern European criminal networks,
and the exploitation of women — themes Gerritsen handles with unusual seriousness
and moral weight.

Jane Rizzoli is now pregnant in this novel, a development that adds a specific
vulnerability to her situation and that Gerritsen uses to complicate the usual
thriller dynamics: the detective is not simply putting herself at risk but
the child she is carrying.

--- THE CORPSE THAT WASN'T ---

The novel opens with Dr. Maura Isles in the morgue, conducting an examination
of a woman brought in as a presumed corpse. The woman — found at a scene and
assumed to be dead — is not dead. She revives on the autopsy table.

This is an extraordinary scene, and Gerritsen handles it with real skill: the
woman's return to consciousness is both medically grounded and profoundly
disorienting. She is alive. She is terrified. And she will not or cannot speak
in a way that is immediately comprehensible.

The woman, it will emerge, is connected to a trafficking network — she is a
victim who was being transported, perhaps as a slave laborer or for sexual
exploitation, and she is in the United States without legal status and without
anyone who can immediately explain who she is or where she came from.

--- THE HOSTAGE SITUATION ---

Before investigators can fully establish who the revived woman is and what she
knows, an extraordinary and violent event occurs. The woman — whose name and
history will be gradually revealed as Olena — is resourceful and desperate
enough to take action when she perceives an opportunity. She seizes a gun and
takes hostages in the hospital where she has been taken for care.

The hostage situation draws in law enforcement, including Jane Rizzoli, who is
the detective assigned to connect with the woman and negotiate. Rizzoli, visibly
pregnant, is in a building where an armed woman is holding hostages — a situation
that puts both her and her unborn child at risk.

The hostage crisis is resolved, but not before it has established Olena as a
character of considerable agency and desperation. She is not simply a victim —
she is a woman who has survived extreme circumstances through will and
adaptability, and who took violent action because she had reason to believe
that the people coming for her were not necessarily on her side.

--- OLENA'S STORY ---

As the crisis resolves and Olena is taken into custody, her story begins to
emerge. She was trafficked — brought from Eastern Europe (Ukraine or a nearby
country) through a network that moves women into the United States under false
pretenses and then exploits them. She has witnessed things she should not have
witnessed. She has knowledge of the trafficking network's operations that makes
her extremely dangerous to the people running it.

The trafficking network is not a simple criminal enterprise: it has connections
to individuals who are powerful, who have cover and protection, and who are
willing to kill to prevent Olena from talking to authorities. The people who
tried to kill her and who failed — leaving her as a "corpse" in Maura Isles's
morgue — will try again.

The investigation must simultaneously protect Olena, extract her testimony, and
dismantle the trafficking operation before the people running it can eliminate
the threat she poses.

--- THE INVESTIGATION INTO THE NETWORK ---

Rizzoli and the investigative team, working with FBI involvement (including
Gabriel Dean, now Rizzoli's husband), trace the trafficking network from Olena's
account and from the physical evidence of the operation's Boston-area activities.

The network's American end is managed by people with legitimate cover — business
figures, possibly officials or law enforcement personnel whose involvement makes
the trafficking both easier to run and much harder to prosecute. This corruption
angle is one of the novel's most disturbing elements: the women being trafficked
are not simply at the mercy of obvious criminals but of people who have the
appearance of respectability and the resources to suppress investigation.

Gerritsen is explicit about the mechanics of trafficking: the false promises
made to women in economically desperate situations in Eastern Europe, the
conditions in which they are kept once they arrive, the violence used to maintain
control, and the near-impossibility for victims to access help in a system
where they may distrust any authority figure.

--- MAURA ISLES AND THE DEAD ---

Running through the investigation is the question of other women connected to
the trafficking network who did not survive. Bodies are found — women who were
killed either as enforcement or as the result of the conditions of their
exploitation. Maura Isles's forensic work on these bodies provides crucial
evidence about the network's operations and the specific individuals responsible.

Isles is particularly affected by this case — the forensic evidence she handles
is of women who were entirely vulnerable and entirely abandoned, and Gerritsen
allows Isles's characteristic composure to be strained by the weight of what
the evidence reveals.

--- RIZZOLI'S PREGNANCY ---

Jane Rizzoli's pregnancy is not merely a biographical detail but an active
element of the novel's tension. She is carrying a child while working a case
involving the extreme exploitation of women's bodies. She is physically
vulnerable in ways she is not accustomed to. She is also acutely aware of the
contrast between her situation — a woman with a husband, a career, protection —
and the situation of the women being trafficked.

The pregnancy also affects her relationships: Gabriel Dean's concern for her
is genuine and sometimes frustrating to Rizzoli, who does not want to be
protected or managed. The tension between her determination to do her job and
the physical and emotional reality of pregnancy is handled with realism.

--- THE CONSPIRACY EXPOSED ---

The trafficking network's American connections — the people with legitimate
cover who are facilitating and profiting from the exploitation of trafficked
women — are identified through Olena's testimony, the forensic evidence from
the bodies, and the investigative work of Rizzoli and the FBI.

The exposure of these individuals — who have relied on their respectability and
their connections to suppress investigation — is the culmination of the case.
Some of them are connected to law enforcement or political life in ways that
make their exposure particularly significant.

--- OLENA'S FATE ---

Olena herself survives the novel. Her fate is complicated: as an undocumented
person who committed a crime (the hostage-taking), she exists in a legal grey
zone. The investigation of the trafficking network, and her cooperation as a
witness, are the primary factors in determining what happens to her. Gerritsen
does not offer a simple resolution to Olena's situation — her survival is real,
but her future is uncertain in ways that are consistent with the actual situation
of trafficking survivors.

--- RESOLUTION ---

The immediate criminal threat is resolved: the trafficking network is disrupted,
the American facilitators are exposed and face legal consequence, and Olena is
safe. Rizzoli and Isles have navigated one of their most morally complex cases.

The novel ends with Rizzoli's pregnancy advancing — the child she will have, and
the specific character that baby will grow into, is a thread that runs through
subsequent books in the series.

--- THEMES ---

"Vanish" is one of the Rizzoli & Isles novels most explicitly engaged with
systemic crime against women. Gerritsen uses the thriller format to make the
realities of human trafficking viscerally present — not as a distant news story
but as something that leaves specific bodies in a specific morgue, examined by
a specific doctor, investigated by a specific detective. The moral weight of
the novel is considerable, and it is one of the series' strongest entries.
