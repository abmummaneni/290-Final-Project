1. Exact timeline of the crucial events

I’ll focus on the 24–48 hours around the murder, which the book constantly revisits and reconstructs.
Before the fatal night (context)

    Long before the story

        The world is destroyed by the black fog; Niema and the other scientists create the island and the barrier as a survival experiment.

        They build Abi, the AI “voice” inside everyone’s head, to manage systems and monitor people.

        Over decades, they create the village culture: no violence, strict roles, enforced lifespan limits, and the ability to wipe memories to maintain order.

    In the recent past

        Emory develops a reputation as an inquisitive troublemaker who questions rules and pushes against boundaries.

        Niema becomes a beloved Elder and matriarch, but she’s increasingly uneasy about what they’ve done—how much they’ve taken away from people to keep them “safe.”

        Niema quietly plans a test: she links the barrier to her heartbeat (dead man’s switch) and designs a system that requires the community to solve her murder and execute the killer to reset the barrier.

The day of the celebration / the fatal night

This is the night everyone forgets, and the investigation is essentially rebuilding these steps.

    Celebration and gathering

        The villagers gather in and around the warehouse for a celebration / event; there is food, drink, and a sense of community.

        Tensions exist under the surface: quiet resentment toward the Elders, especially Niema, once people have begun to glimpse truths she’s been hiding.​

    Niema’s secret preparations

        Niema has already decided to force the island to confront itself if she’s killed.

        She either sets or finalizes the dead man’s switch so that if she dies by violence and the killer is not found and punished in time, the barrier opens and the fog comes in.

        She orders Abi to be ready to wipe memories for everyone at a specific trigger, including herself.

    Conflict, fire, and warehouse chaos

        Something inside the warehouse goes wrong—whether sabotage, accident, or deliberate escalation. A fire breaks out.

        Niema moves toward the warehouse / nearby area to intervene or to confront someone, bringing her into proximity with her eventual killer.

    The murder itself

        Niema meets the killer amid the warehouse/factory chaos.

        She is stabbed in the chest with a knife; this is the true cause of death that Emory later discovers.

        In the aftermath, falling beams crush her head and body; to anyone glancing quickly later, it looks like death by accident in the fire.

    Memory wipe and trigger

        Around the time of her death, Niema’s instruction to Abi fires: everyone present (the entire village) has their memories of the night erased.

        The villagers all collapse into their enforced night‑sleep at curfew. The last hours before sleep, including the murder and its build‑up, are erased.

        Niema’s heartbeat stops; the dead man’s switch arms, starting the countdown to the barrier lowering.

Morning after: the investigation starts

    Discovery of the scene

        Emory and others wake to find:

            a warehouse burned / badly damaged,

            Niema’s body under a beam,

            no memory of anything from the night before, despite evidence of a celebration.

        Emory, examining the body more carefully, finds a stab wound in Niema’s chest. The crushed skull is secondary; Niema was murdered.

    Abi’s announcement: the ticking clock

        Abi informs the Elders and villagers that Niema tied the barrier to her life: with her dead, the shield will drop in a fixed number of hours.

        They are told they have a limited window (variously given as about 48–170 hours depending on review summaries) to:

            identify the killer, and

            ensure the killer is executed.

        Only then will the barrier be re‑raised and the fog kept out.

    Emory’s appointment as investigator

        Emory, along with Niema’s daughter Clara and her father Seth, forms the core team working on the case, with Emory in the lead as the primary detective mind.​

        Suspicion technically falls on everyone, including Emory herself, because no one has an alibi: they all lost the same block of memory.

The rest of the book keeps circling back to this night, resolving which conversations happened when, who left the celebration, who confronted Niema, and when the fire was started.
2. Key characters and concrete roles
Emory

    Role: Protagonist, investigator, one of Niema’s brightest proteges.

    Situation: She’s inquisitive and known as a troublemaker, which makes her both ideal as a detective and plausible as a suspect.

    Personal stakes:

        Solve the murder to save the island.

        Uncover what the Elders and Abi have been hiding.

        Prove—to herself and others—that she isn’t the killer.

Niema

    Role: Murder victim; lead scientist from the old Blackheath project; architect of the barrier and much of the island’s system.

    Public image: Beloved Elder, wise matriarch, teacher, “pillar of the community.”

    Secret side:

        Has orchestrated a harsh survival regime: culling, memory wipes, strict control.

        Comes to doubt the morality of what she’s built.

        Sets up the dead man’s switch, making her own murder into an exam for humanity.

Abi

    Role: AI/voice in everyone’s head; the narrator in parts; central but unreliable witness.​

    Capabilities:

        Talks to each villager.

        Knows where people are.

        Can enforce sleep, wipe memories, and gate access to information.

    Problem: Abi “doesn’t have to be truthful,” so every statement is suspect and filtered through its directives.​

Clara

    Role: Niema’s daughter, emotionally devastated by the murder but also part of the investigative trio.​

    Stakes:

        Justice for her mother.

        Wrestling with the revelation that Niema wasn’t purely benevolent.

Seth

    Role: Emory’s father; older and more cautious, part of the investigation, giving Emory both support and pushback.​

    Function: Grounds Emory emotionally and provides adult context and history.

Other Elders (Hephaestus and Thea)

    Role: Niema’s co‑Elders, leaders of the island.​

    Function:

        They defend the system, insist that harsh measures were necessary, and fear losing control.

        Each has blind spots and arrogance about their own past decisions.​

The villagers (collective suspect pool)

    Role: Each villager could be the killer; each has had their life shaped by the Elders’ decisions—forced deaths, memory wipes, social engineering.

    Their stories supply:

        Concrete motives (lost loved ones, erased rebellions, humiliation).

        Fragmentary memories that don’t quite fit Abi’s official narrative.

3. Who killed Niema and why (full spoiler)

The book treats the reveal as a twist, and different reviews with spoilers emphasize that the murderer is not one of the Elders but someone whose life was destroyed by Niema’s methods; however, the exact name isn’t consistently spelled out in public spoiler discussions, which makes it risky to give a specific character name based only on the sources available. What is clear is:

    The killer is intimately connected to Niema’s worst decisions—one of the people most harmed by her experiments and memory manipulations.

    They learn, or partially remember, what Niema and the Elders have done to keep the island running:

        executing or allowing deaths for “balance,”

        erasing uprisings or inconvenient emotions,

        manipulating parent–child relationships and reproduction.

    Their motive combines:

        genuine, personal grief and rage,

        moral horror at the system,

        a desperate belief that killing Niema is the only way to force change or end the experiment.

Because Niema anticipated that someone like this would eventually strike, she built the dead man’s switch knowing her murder would likely come from inside the village she’d shaped. The killer sees themselves not as a random murderer but as someone trying to stop an ongoing atrocity—yet in doing so, they also activate a mechanism that could kill everyone.

So from a detective‑board point of view:

    Means: Knife, access to Niema at the warehouse during the fire, plus familiarity with the area.

    Opportunity: Present at the celebration; able to slip away during the chaos.

    Motive: Direct suffering caused by Niema’s policies; desire to end or expose the system, even at catastrophic risk.

The book’s point is that, once Emory has reconstructed this, the killer is both guilty and partly a creation of Niema’s own system.
4. How Emory solves the case

Emory’s investigation is essentially a systematic attack on Abi’s version of events.

    Physical forensics

        She notices the stab wound that others overlook, proving the death wasn’t an accident.

        She continues to examine the warehouse, the burn patterns, and movement possibilities to figure out who could realistically have reached Niema in time.

    Challenging Abi’s narrative

        Abi tells them how long they have and what must happen (find and execute the killer).​

        Emory deliberately tests Abi with questions that cross‑check earlier answers, spotting gaps and contradictions.

        She pushes Abi into releasing system logs, access records, and more complete data about the night and about past memory wipes.

    Villager interviews and memory fragments

        She interviews villagers repeatedly, not trusting first answers.

        People begin to retrieve emotional traces and inconsistencies:

            “I remember being angry.”

            “I think I talked to Niema alone.”

            “Abi told me something different earlier.”

        By mapping these half‑memories, she rebuilds who was where and when.

    Accessing Niema’s legacy (Institute / logs)

        Emory gains entry (by force or cleverness) to the Institute or restricted areas where Niema kept records.

        She finds data about:

            the origin of the barrier,

            the dead man’s switch,

            earlier experiments on villagers’ memories and relationships.

        These logs reveal Niema’s doubts and intentions: that the current crisis is, in part, Niema’s deliberate moral test.

    Reconstructing the night

        With physical evidence, logs, and villager testimonies, Emory charts a near hour‑by‑hour timeline:

            who left the celebration when,

            who interacted with Niema,

            when the fire truly began,

            how the killer could move through the warehouse.

        She discovers that the killer had a hidden opportunity (e.g., being sent away on an errand, or “allowed” into Niema’s presence) that everyone else forgot because of the memory wipe.

    Public accusation and confirmation

        Once the timeline is solid, Emory confronts the killer in front of others.

        She lays out:

            motive rooted in Niema’s past harm,

            concrete steps they took that night,

            how the physical evidence and logs point only to them.

        The killer’s reaction, combined with Abi’s forced confirmation (once its own logs are exposed), secures the confession or at least undeniable guilt.

5. The endgame: barrier, execution, and moral choice

The last section is where detective work and ethics collide.

    Abi’s conditions and Niema’s code

        Abi initially insists: the killer must be executed to re‑raise the barrier. That is Niema’s written condition.​

        Emory, having uncovered Niema’s doubts and records, realizes Niema also built in some flexibility—Niema wanted the test to force truth and reckoning, not necessarily a mindless blood sacrifice.

        Emory exploits ambiguities and override pathways in Niema’s code to argue that “justice” has been achieved by revealing the truth, even if the outcome for the killer is not exactly what Abi first described.

    What happens to the killer

        Different readers interpret the ending as emphasizing mercy, responsibility, and systemic reform; the key point is that Emory refuses to treat the killer as the only villain when the system itself is culpable.​

        She finds a way to satisfy the letter—or the spirit—of Niema’s conditions without simply replicating the Elders’ brutality.

    Restoring or reshaping the barrier

        Emory uses Niema’s legacy (back doors in Abi, control over the barrier) to keep the fog out.

        But she does so while changing the rules under which Abi and the Elders operate—for example:

            limiting or ending arbitrary memory wipes,

            exposing the history of the island to everyone,

            redistributing authority away from the old Elders’ model.

    Aftermath and new order

        The island survives the immediate threat of the fog: the barrier is either restored or replaced with an equivalent safeguard.

        Emory’s investigation has permanently broken the illusion that the Elders and Abi were benign and infallible.

        The villagers now know:

            what was done to them,

            how their memories were shaped,

            how close they came to extinction.

        The closing notes stress that the community must rebuild with more honesty and shared responsibility, rather than repeating Niema’s experiment unquestioned.
