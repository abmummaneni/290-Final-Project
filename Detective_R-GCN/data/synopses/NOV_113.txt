Cadogan, the corpse, and the vanishing shop

Middle‑aged poet Richard Cadogan is bored and restless, stuck in uninspiring suburban life and suffering a mild mid‑life crisis, so he decides to go back to his old university city, Oxford, in search of adventure and inspiration. After a troublesome train journey that leaves him stranded outside town late at night, he hitchhikes in and starts walking toward the center, luggage in hand, looking for his lodgings.

On Iffley Road he notices a toyshop with its awning still up and, crucially, the front door unlocked, despite the late hour. Curious and needing shelter, Cadogan slips inside. The ground floor is full of toys—trains, dolls, bricks, and soldiers—but no people, so he wanders upstairs into the dark living quarters above the shop. There he stumbles over the strangled body of an elderly woman lying on the floor. Before he can react or escape, someone lurking nearby knocks him unconscious and locks him in a small storeroom or cleaning closet.

Cadogan wakes in the morning with a headache and forces his way out through a window, then goes straight to the police to report the murder and the toyshop. The police are skeptical but agree to accompany him back. When they arrive on Iffley Road, Cadogan is stunned to find that the toyshop is gone; in its place is a perfectly ordinary grocery store that looks as if it has been there for years, with no sign of toys or a dead body. The officers, seeing only a prosaic grocer’s and an apparently confused poet with a bump on his head, conclude he must be suffering from concussion or imagination and dismiss his story. Bewildered but still convinced of what he saw, Cadogan turns to someone who might believe him.
Enter Gervase Fen and a missing heiress

Cadogan’s old Oxford friend, Gervase Fen, is an eccentric professor of English at St. Christopher’s College, with a side‑line in amateur detection and a taste for wild driving and self‑consciously literary jokes. Fen not only takes Cadogan seriously but is delighted by the sheer oddity of a disappearing toyshop and a corpse no one else has seen. He declares the matter “gloriously” mysterious and insists they investigate themselves rather than let the police muddle it.

Fen and Cadogan soon learn that an elderly woman named Mrs. Helen Lewis, a wealthy spinster and owner of valuable property, including shops, has disappeared from Oxford. Her relatives are awaiting the reading of her will and the division of her estate. It becomes clear that the dead woman Cadogan saw is almost certainly Mrs. Lewis, and that the “moving toyshop” is bound up with attempts to conceal her murder while manipulating her property and inheritance.

Fen discovers that Mrs. Lewis owned several shops, including the premises on Iffley Road, and that her heirs and business associates were in the process of drawing lots to divide some of these properties among themselves. The possibility emerges that someone with a financial stake in a particular building had reason both to kill Mrs. Lewis and to obscure the location of the crime scene.
Chases, capers, and the “moving” itself

From this point, the plot becomes a rapid series of episodes: Fen, Cadogan, and a growing circle of helpers dash around Oxford—by car, on foot, even commandeering vehicles—pursuing leads, dodging attacks, and repeatedly getting knocked out or locked up. Fen delights in keeping the police at arm’s length, often refusing to explain himself until he has a complete solution, and he frequently breaks minor laws in the name of justice, much to Cadogan’s alarm.

They pick up a key ally in Sally Carstairs, a young shopgirl with commonsense and courage, who becomes part of the investigative trio. Along the way, they uncover:​

    A kidnap plot and several related murders tied to control of Mrs. Lewis’s fortune and properties.

    A network of relatives and associates, some venal, some foolish, all with partial information or motives.

    A seemingly “impossible” murder in which four people are together in one room while a fifth person is killed elsewhere in the house, yet one of the four must somehow be the killer.​

The titular puzzle—how the toyshop “moved”—turns out not to be the central mystery so much as an early hook. Crispin eventually reveals that there is a straightforward, if laborious, explanation: under cover of night, those involved in the crime swapped the interiors (and signage) of two shops, stripping out the contents of the toyshop and a nearby grocery and exchanging them so that, by morning, the premises Cadogan remembered as a toyshop looked like a long‑standing grocer’s. This physical switch‑over, though implausibly energetic for a single night, accounts for why the same building presents as a different shop with no trace of toys or a body when the police arrive.
The “impossible” murder and the mastermind

More crucial than the moving shop is the later “impossible” crime: four people are indisputably together in a room at the time a fifth person is killed elsewhere in the house, and yet the circumstances suggest the killer must be one of the four. Fen analyzes their statements, the layout of the house, and the timings, noting that the impossibility is partly a matter of testimony—people who have strong incentives to lie—and partly a matter of misdirection. Once he peels away the falsehoods and re‑times certain movements, the “locked‑room‑like” problem collapses into something logically solvable.​

As Fen and Cadogan close in, it becomes clear that the entire pattern of events—the murder of Mrs. Lewis, the moving of the toyshop interior, the kidnappings, and the further killings—has been orchestrated by a single mastermind seeking to maximize his share of Mrs. Lewis’s estate while eliminating witnesses and rivals. One key conspirator is Havering, an heir who, after losing out when the lots were drawn, found himself with an unwanted property and the immediate problem of disposing of a body, as well as disguising the original scene. His involvement in the overnight shop‑swap and subsequent attempts at cover‑up make him a linchpin of the plot.

Fen’s deduction, revealed late in the book, shows how the inheritance arrangements, property ownership, and the physical possibility of moving two shops in one night line up with the personalities and opportunities of the suspects. The mastermind’s identity, while not a wildly outlandish twist, is unveiled in a relatively low‑key manner; Crispin doesn’t stage an elaborate drawing‑room denouement so much as allow Fen’s reasoning to click into place as the action culminates.
Climax and aftermath

The climax involves a frenetic pursuit and confrontation, including a famous fairground / merry‑go‑round sequence in which violence on a spinning ride produces a near‑disaster and a desperate attempt to stop it—set‑pieces that later influenced Alfred Hitchcock’s Strangers on a Train without official credit to Crispin. Amid this chaos, Fen and Cadogan prevent further deaths, expose the conspiracy, and ensure that the main conspirators are caught or killed, bringing the underlying inheritance plot into the open.

With the case solved, the explanation of the moving toyshop, the impossible murder, and the financial scheme is laid out. Cadogan, having found more “adventure” than he bargained for, has material for poetry and a renewed sense of purpose, while Fen returns to his post as mercurial don, having once again proved that logic, literary allusions, and a tolerance for chaos can untangle even the most boisterously contrived puzzle.

The book closes on a light, slightly absurdist tone: the murders are solved and justice more or less done, but what lingers is the blend of high‑spirited chase scenes, witty dialogue, and a central mystery that starts with a poet, a corpse, and a shop that shouldn’t be able to disappear—yet does.
