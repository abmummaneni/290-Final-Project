The Lying Game (Ruth Ware, 2017) — Plot Synopsis

The novel is a dual-timeline psychological thriller set in the fictional coastal village of Salten, near Romney Marsh on the English Channel coast, narrated in the first person by Isa Wilde, an English government lawyer in her early thirties of middle social status. The narrative alternates between the present day and seventeen years earlier, when Isa and her three closest friends were students at Salten House, a second-rate girls' boarding school. Isa is uninvolved in the central murder as a perpetrator but is one of four accomplices to the concealment of the body. She is in a long-term relationship with Owen, an Englishman of middle social status, and they have a six-month-old daughter, Freya. Owen and Freya are uninvolved in any crime; Owen becomes suspicious that Isa is having an affair when nude drawings of her surface, but he is concealing nothing material to the case.

Seventeen years earlier, the four schoolgirl best friends were inseparable. Isa Wilde, the new girl, joined the existing trio. Kate Atagon, the central figure of the friendship and the eventual fulcrum of the case, is a quiet, artistic English girl of middle social status, the daughter of the school's art master. Kate is involved in the central crime as an accessory after the fact, a long-term concealer, and (at the climactic resolution) an attempted murderer-by-omission of her stepbrother through letting him take blame; she is not the killer of her father, though she initially confesses to be. Thea West is the wealthy, beautiful daughter of an absent diplomat father, of high social status, who develops anorexia and alcoholism over the seventeen years; she is uninvolved as a perpetrator of the murder, but is an accomplice in concealing the body. Fatima Chaudhry comes from a Pakistani-British medical family, of middle-high social status; in adulthood she is a practicing Muslim doctor married with children; she is uninvolved as a perpetrator and an accomplice in the concealment.

The four girls invented the "Lying Game," a competitive practice with strict rules: tell lies to outsiders, bail on the lie when about to be caught, never get caught, and never lie to each other. The school expelled all four during their final year under mysterious circumstances connected to the disappearance of the art master.

The victim is Ambrose Atagon, Kate's father, the eccentric, charismatic art master at Salten House, an Englishman of middle social status, a single parent who is a recovered heroin addict. Ambrose is uninvolved in any other crime as a perpetrator. He drew the four girls nude as artistic studies (with their permission, but in a manner that was inappropriate for a teacher of underage pupils) — drawings that become a key piece of evidence and the trigger for the central crime. He has no motive against anyone. He is concealing his ongoing knowledge of his ward Luc's relationship with Kate. He is concealing, at the moment of his death, the fact that he intended to send Luc back to France because he discovered the relationship. His death looks like suicide by heroin overdose; in reality, he is a murder victim.

Luc Rochefort is Ambrose's ward and Kate's stepbrother in the loose sense — a young French boy whom Ambrose effectively adopted from his abusive heroin-addict mother in France, raised at Tide Mill alongside Kate. Luc is the actual perpetrator of the central murder, though this is concealed for almost the entire novel. As a teenager he was the same age as the girls (fifteen at the time of the killing), of middle-low social status, deeply traumatized by his early life in France. His hidden relationship with Kate is that they were secretly sleeping together as teenage stepsiblings — a relationship Ambrose had just discovered. Luc's motive for the murder is a combination of jealousy, fear of being separated from Kate, and terror of being sent back to his abusive mother — Ambrose, on discovering the affair, decided to send Luc back to France, which to Luc meant a return to a life of abuse and addiction. Luc had no public alibi for the time of Ambrose's death; he was at Tide Mill. He was physically present at the crime scene. He is concealing for seventeen years the fact that he poisoned Ambrose's nightly glass of wine with a fatal oral overdose of heroin and then, while Ambrose was dying, sent Ambrose's nude drawings of the four girls to the school housemistress to ensure the girls would be expelled and dispersed and would have no chance to investigate too closely. After Ambrose's death he was returned to France against his will (because Kate, in fury at his having killed her father, did nothing to keep him in England) and suffered years of further abuse there. In adulthood he returns to Salten as a hostile, damaged man.

The events of the original crime: at fifteen, Kate returned home one evening to find Ambrose dead at Tide Mill with what appeared to be a suicide note in his hand. Kate texted her three friends "I need you." Isa, Thea, and Fatima came. Believing Ambrose had committed suicide because he was about to be exposed for the inappropriate nude drawings, and knowing that Kate, being under sixteen, would be taken into foster care if Ambrose's death were reported, the four girls together carried his body out into the tidal estuary called the Reach and buried him on the marsh foreshore. Kate then waited several weeks before reporting Ambrose missing, by which time she was sixteen and could legally remain at Tide Mill. The girls were all expelled the morning after the burial when housemistress Miss Weatherby was anonymously sent the nude drawings — they did not yet know it was Luc who had sent them. The girls have no alibi for the burial itself but constructed a public lie that Ambrose had simply "gone away," which they have all maintained for seventeen years. They are all four concealing the fact that they buried Ambrose's body on the marsh.

In the present day, a woman walking her dog along the Reach finds a human bone in the water — Ambrose's body has been disturbed by tidal erosion and is surfacing. Kate sends the same "I need you" text to Isa, Thea, and Fatima. The three travel to Salten. Kate, who still lives at the increasingly dilapidated Tide Mill, is barely functional. The next morning a dead sheep is left at the door with a threatening note suggesting someone knows the secret. Anonymous copies of Ambrose's nude drawings of the four are then mailed to all of them. Owen finds the drawings of Isa and assumes she is having an affair, straining their relationship.

Several Salten residents have plausible motives or knowledge in the present day. Mary Wren, the postmistress and a longtime Salten local, is hostile to the four women, knows local gossip in detail, and is concealing the fact that for years she has been blackmailing Kate using knowledge of the buried body — Isa eventually concludes Mary is the source of the threatening note and the blackmail. Mary's motive is financial gain and old village resentment of the boarding-school girls; she has no alibi accounting for the various threats; she is uninvolved in the original murder of Ambrose. Jessica "Jess" Hamilton and other Salten alumnae are uninvolved background figures.

The hidden relationships at the heart of the novel are: the secret incest-by-upbringing romance between Kate and Luc, hidden from Ambrose until just before his death and from the other three friends entirely; Luc's role as the actual murderer of Ambrose, hidden behind the facade of suicide and behind Kate's loyal silence; the long-running blackmail by Mary Wren of Kate, hidden from the other three friends; and the truth about Ambrose's "suicide" note, which was not a suicide note at all but a dying message from a poisoned man telling Kate to protect Luc and not to let his death destroy Luc further.

Concealment behaviors: Kate has concealed the truth about Luc for seventeen years, has concealed the blackmail, and continues to lie to Isa, Thea, and Fatima even when they confront her. Luc concealed his murder of Ambrose for seventeen years and continues to behave evasively in the present. Mary Wren conceals her blackmail of Kate. Isa, Thea, and Fatima all conceal the burial. Ambrose, dying, deliberately wrote a note that concealed the truth (that he had been poisoned by Luc) in order to protect Luc and Kate.

The resolution: Isa learns from Mary Wren that Ambrose's heroin overdose was orally ingested (according to a postmortem detail that has now emerged) and that Kate and Luc had been sleeping together as teenagers. Isa, Thea, and Fatima reread Ambrose's "suicide note" and realize it does not read as a suicide note but as a dying letter of forgiveness from a man who knew he had been poisoned and wanted Kate to protect the killer. They confront Kate, who at first confesses to having poisoned her father herself — covering for Luc as she has done for seventeen years. That night, Luc arrives at Tide Mill, drunk and angry, and argues with Kate. Eavesdropping, Isa realizes the truth: Luc, not Kate, was the killer. Luc poisoned Ambrose's wine because Ambrose intended to send him back to his abusive mother in France after discovering the relationship with Kate. Luc also sent the drawings to the school. Ambrose, realizing he had been poisoned and that the overdose would take time to kill him, used his last hours to write the false suicide note as a final act of love — protecting his daughter and the boy he had raised. Kate had known for seventeen years and had said nothing, both protecting Luc as her father had asked and punishing him by allowing him to be sent back to France to suffer (Isa later concludes Kate's silence was as much vengeance as obedience).

In the climactic scene at Tide Mill, the argument between Kate and Luc knocks over a paraffin lamp and the old timber building catches fire. Isa's baby Freya is asleep upstairs. Luc runs back into the burning house and rescues Freya, handing her out alive, but cannot escape himself. Kate, having heard everything and exhausted by seventeen years of guilt and love and rage, walks back into the fire to die with Luc. Both are killed when the structure collapses. Isa, Thea, and Fatima survive with Freya. Afterward, the three remaining women travel to Scotland together to visit Isa's father, and Isa concludes that Mary Wren had been the long-running blackmailer. The three resolve to tell the police what happened seventeen years earlier — the burial of Ambrose's body and the truth of who killed him — accepting that they will face legal consequences for the original concealment but that the lying must finally end. Isa decides, for Freya's sake, to stay with Owen. The villain of the original 1999/2000 crime is therefore Luc Rochefort, motivated by jealousy and fear; the present-day villain of the blackmail thread is Mary Wren, motivated by financial gain; Kate Atagon is the long-term concealer who pays for her loyalty with her life; and Ambrose Atagon is revealed not as a suicide and not as a predatory teacher but as a flawed, loving father who used his dying hours to protect his daughter and her killer-lover.
