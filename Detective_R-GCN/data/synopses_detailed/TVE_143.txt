Unbelievable (Netflix miniseries, 2019) — Plot Synopsis

Note on attribution and framing: Unbelievable is an eight-episode American limited series released on Netflix on September 13, 2019. It was co-created by Susannah Grant, Michael Chabon, and Ayelet Waldman; Susannah Grant served as showrunner and is credited as a director on at least one episode, but the directing was divided primarily between Lisa Cholodenko (who directed the opening block, including the pilot), Susannah Grant, and Michael Dinner. If your dataset attributes the series to Grant alone, that compresses a co-creator/showrunner credit with a directing credit; the more accurate label is "co-created and run by Susannah Grant," not "directed by Susannah Grant." The series is based on the 2015 Pulitzer Prize–winning ProPublica/Marshall Project article "An Unbelievable Story of Rape" by T. Christian Miller and Ken Armstrong, and on their 2018 book A False Report. The case is real; names of victims and law enforcement are dramatized but the events are essentially documentary.

The series alternates between two timelines and two jurisdictions. The 2008 timeline is set in Lynnwood, Washington. The 2011 timeline is set in suburban Denver, Colorado, principally Golden and Westminster.

The series's central wronged-survivor and viewpoint character is Marie Adler, an eighteen-year-old white woman of low social status who has aged out of the Washington state foster-care system, lives in a transitional housing program for at-risk youth at the Oakdale Apartments, and works at a big-box store. Marie Adler is uninvolved in any crime as a perpetrator. She is the first identifiable victim of the serial rapist who is the series's villain. In August 2008, a masked man wielding a knife breaks into her apartment in the early morning, ties her with her own shoelaces, blindfolds her, photographs her, rapes her for hours, and warns her that he will release the photographs if she tells anyone. Marie reports the assault immediately. She has no motive against anyone, has a clear alibi for any other crime (she has committed none), and is concealing nothing about her assault — she is, on the contrary, telling the truth.

The principal antagonists in the 2008 Washington timeline are not the rapist directly but the Lynnwood Police detectives who fail Marie. Detective Robert Parker (inspired by real-life Sergeant Jeffrey Mason) and Detective Pruitt of Lynnwood PD are uninvolved in the rape as perpetrators but are functional antagonists in the Marie storyline. Their motives in disbelieving Marie are a combination of cognitive bias, professional impatience, and uncritical reliance on doubts expressed by people in Marie's life — particularly her former foster mothers Judith and Colleen, both of whom are uninvolved in any crime but who voiced suspicions to police that Marie's behavior post-assault did not match what they expected from a "real" rape victim. Parker and Pruitt re-interview Marie repeatedly, deploy coercive interrogation techniques, force her to repeat the trauma narrative again and again, and ultimately pressure her into recanting. They then charge Marie with filing a false report of rape — a misdemeanor for which she takes a plea deal that imposes a $500 fine, mandatory counseling, and probation. Their concealment behavior is institutional rather than criminal: they do not properly process the rape kit's photographic evidence, do not flag the case to neighboring jurisdictions, and effectively close the investigation without serious follow-up. They have, in the show's framing, no romantic, financial, or vengeance motive — only the institutional motive of moving a difficult case off their desks. Their decision frees the rapist to attack at least five more women.

The fallout for Marie: she loses her housing placement at Oakdale (her counselors Becca and Ty turn against her); she loses her job; she is shamed by her social network; she becomes suicidal; and she lives for years under the false-report conviction. She is uninvolved in any further crime. The Oakdale counselors and the foster mothers are uninvolved as perpetrators of any crime; Judith and Colleen are concealing nothing material but are the unwitting catalysts of the police's wrongful disbelief.

The 2011 Colorado timeline is led by two detectives, both uninvolved in any crime as perpetrators and serving as the series's heroes. Detective Karen Duvall of the Golden Police Department (inspired by Stacy Galbraith) is a young white woman, married to fellow officer Max Duvall (also a detective, uninvolved), a devout Christian, of middle social status. She investigates a home-invasion rape in Golden in which the assailant — masked, armed with a knife, gloved, photographing the victim, ordering her to bathe afterward to destroy evidence — has used virtually identical methods to those in the Lynnwood case three years earlier. The Golden victim, Amber Stevenson, is a graduate student of middle social status, uninvolved in any crime, the second on-screen victim. Detective Grace Rasmussen of the Westminster Police Department (inspired by Edna Hendershot) is an older, twice-divorced, hard-edged white woman of middle social status, currently in a relationship with Steve Rasmussen (uninvolved), and is investigating a separate home-invasion rape in Westminster. The two detectives discover via an offhand conversation between their husbands that they are working strikingly similar cases, and they decide to pool resources. FBI Special Agent Billy Taggart joins their task force and is uninvolved in any crime. Detective Elias of Westminster and Officer Selig of Golden assist; both uninvolved.

The other Colorado victims include Sarah, an older woman in Aurora attacked in her home; Doris, an elderly woman attacked in Lakewood; and Lilly (played by Annaleigh Ashford), a younger woman whose case helps build the pattern. All are victims, uninvolved in any crime as perpetrators, with no motives against anyone, no relevant alibi questions (they were assaulted in their own homes), and concealing nothing — though several initially withhold details out of trauma and shame. Grace Rasmussen identifies the type the rapist seeks: women who live alone. The rapist is patient, methodical, leaves almost no DNA, photographs his victims, and uses the photographs as a tool of control.

The villain of the entire series is Christopher "Chris" McCarthy, a fictionalized version of the real-life Marc Patrick O'Leary. Chris McCarthy is a white American man in his early thirties, an ex–U.S. Army soldier, of middle social status, with a girlfriend (uninvolved). He is the perpetrator of all the rapes — Marie's in Washington in 2008 and the multiple Colorado attacks in 2009–2011. His motive is psychopathic predatory tendencies, sexual violence, and an explicit ideological framework: he tells investigators after his arrest that he subscribes to a self-invented worldview in which humanity is divided into "alphas" and "bravos," and that as an "alpha" he is entitled to take what he wants from "bravos." His motive type is therefore a combination of psychopathic tendencies, entitlement, and a self-rationalized ideology of dominance. Chris McCarthy has no genuine alibi for any of the rapes; he was physically present at every crime scene; he uses gloves, masks, and meticulous post-attack cleanup (forcing victims to bathe, taking bedding) to leave minimal forensic traces. He is concealing his criminal life entirely from his girlfriend, his employer, and the public; he is concealing the photographs of his victims on encrypted hard drives in his apartment.

The hidden relationship at the heart of the series is the connection between Chris McCarthy and his brother Curtis McCarthy (inspired by the real Michael O'Leary), who lives with him. Curtis is initially a suspect when investigators trace a vehicle near one of the crime scenes to him. Curtis is uninvolved in the rapes as a perpetrator; his motive in any sense is none; his alibi is that he was not at the crime scenes; he was not physically present at the rapes. He is, however, briefly concealing his living arrangement with Chris in a way that complicates the investigation. The vehicle and address connection initially points the detectives at Curtis but ultimately leads them to Chris, who lives in the same house. The brothers' relationship is therefore the misdirection that resolves into the actual culprit.

Concealment behaviors: Chris McCarthy conceals everything — the photographs, the identity, the methodology. The Lynnwood detectives concealed (institutionally) their failures with Marie until the case was reopened. Marie was forced into a false confession that became a kind of state-coerced concealment of the truth. The foster mothers and Oakdale counselors are concealing nothing criminal but are concealing their doubts about Marie from her until those doubts surface in police interviews.

The resolution: Karen Duvall and Grace Rasmussen build the case piece by piece. They compile partial DNA from multiple Colorado scenes into a composite profile. They notice that the rapist's distinctive methodology — extended attacks, forced bathing, photography, the use of victims' own shoelaces or zip-ties — links a string of attacks across jurisdictions. They eventually identify a white pickup truck spotted near one of the crime scenes; the plates trace to Curtis McCarthy. Surveillance of the McCarthy address shows Chris driving the same truck. The detectives obtain a warrant, arrest Chris McCarthy, and search the home. They find his digital archive of photographs of his victims — including, to their shock, photographs of Marie Adler from her 2008 assault in Washington — confirming definitively that Marie was telling the truth all along and that the Lynnwood police had wrongly charged a real rape victim with making a false report. Chris McCarthy refuses to give the password to his encrypted drives but the existing physical and photographic evidence is overwhelming. He is convicted on twenty-eight counts of rape, kidnapping, and burglary across Colorado and Washington and is sentenced to 327 and a half years in prison in the dramatization (the real Marc O'Leary received an aggregate sentence of approximately 327.5 years in Colorado, with an additional sentence in Washington bringing the total to a parole-impossible figure).

In the closing arc, Detective Parker — the Lynnwood detective who broke Marie — is informed of the Colorado arrest and the photographic evidence, and he visits Marie to apologize, an apology Marie demands he speak fully and on her terms. Marie sues the City of Lynnwood and settles for $150,000; her false-report conviction is expunged; her $500 plea-deal payment is returned. Marie leaves Washington in her packed car at the close of the series, the future open. The Lynnwood detectives face no formal disciplinary action despite an external review describing their conduct as coercive and unprofessional. Karen Duvall and Grace Rasmussen are vindicated as investigators. The villain is conclusively Chris McCarthy / Marc O'Leary, motivated by psychopathic and entitled sexual violence, captured by the dogged collaboration between two female detectives whose investigative methods stand in deliberate contrast to the institutional failures that allowed the rapes to continue for three years after Marie first told the truth.
