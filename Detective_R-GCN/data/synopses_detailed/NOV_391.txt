The Silkworm (Robert Galbraith / J.K. Rowling, 2014) — Plot Synopsis

The novel is the second in the Cormoran Strike series, set in London a few months after the events of The Cuckoo's Calling. The detective protagonist is Cormoran Strike, a thirty-something English private investigator, of middle social status, the illegitimate son of a famous (off-page) rock star, an Afghanistan war veteran who lost the lower half of one leg to an IED, and the ex-fiancé of the volatile Charlotte Campbell. Strike is uninvolved in the crime as a perpetrator and is the lead investigator. His business partner and assistant is Robin Ellacott, a young Yorkshirewoman of middle social status, formally engaged to Matthew Cunliffe (an accountant who disapproves of her detective work and is uninvolved in any crime). Robin is uninvolved in any crime as a perpetrator and is increasingly Strike's investigative partner rather than secretary. Detective Inspector Richard Anstis of the Metropolitan Police, an old army friend of Strike, is uninvolved as a perpetrator and serves as Strike's contact within the formal police investigation; he wrongly believes Leonora Quine to be the killer.

The case begins when Leonora Quine, a frumpy, blunt, working-class English woman in her late forties, of low social status, asks Strike to find her missing husband. Leonora is uninvolved in any crime as a perpetrator. She is the mother of Orlando Quine, an adult daughter with intellectual disabilities (uninvolved, a dependent witness whose welfare drives much of the case's urgency). Leonora is later arrested for the murder of her husband based on circumstantial evidence and her flat affect; her motive (financial gain, freedom from a difficult marriage) appears plausible to police but Strike believes she is innocent. Leonora has a partial alibi for the time of the murder (she was at home caring for Orlando) and was not physically present at the crime scene. She is concealing nothing material; her apparent oddness is simply her personality and the demands of caring for Orlando.

The victim is Owen Quine, a once-promising English novelist of middle social status, married to Leonora, who has spent decades trying and failing to recreate the success of his debut novel Hobart's Sin. Quine is a victim, not a perpetrator of the murder — but he is morally implicated as the perpetrator of a long-running blackmail (revealed in the resolution) and as the author of a vicious manuscript called Bombyx Mori (Latin for "silkworm") which contains slanderous, grotesque caricatures of nearly everyone in his personal and professional life. Quine has been missing for several days when Leonora hires Strike. He is found brutally murdered in a derelict house on Talgarth Road that he co-owned with the writer Michael Fancourt. The body has been bound, disemboweled, partially dissolved with hydrochloric acid, and arranged to mirror precisely the death of the protagonist Bombyx in the final scene of Bombyx Mori — a ritualized staging that points the killer to someone who has read the manuscript intimately.

The principal suspects are all caricatured in the leaked manuscript. Elizabeth "Liz" Tassel is Owen Quine's literary agent, an English woman in her fifties of middle-to-high social status, unmarried, suffering from emphysema and a chronic cough, and the eventual villain of the novel. She runs her agency on the fringe of the London literary establishment, which she deeply resents. Liz Tassel is concealing several facts: that she was once a novelist herself, a university classmate of Michael Fancourt; that she wrote the parody of Fancourt's wife Ellie Fancourt's novel — a parody that drove Ellie to suicide and that the literary world has wrongly blamed on Owen Quine for over twenty years; that Owen Quine discovered her authorship of the parody and has been blackmailing her ever since by demanding she represent and tolerate him as a client at no real benefit to her; that she rewrote the final manuscript of Bombyx Mori to alter Quine's intended meaning and incorporate the gruesome death scene that became the template for the murder; and that she leaked the rewritten manuscript to multiple parties to stage Quine's "disappearance" and create a wide field of suspects. Liz Tassel's motive is revenge for two decades of financial and reputational extortion, combined with hatred and a desire to silence Quine permanently before he could make good on his threat to expose the parody's true authorship. Her motive type is therefore primarily revenge with strong elements of self-protection and concealed glory (she has long resented being a failed novelist and a mere agent). She has no firm alibi for the time of the murder; she has presented herself as a busy literary professional, but Strike establishes that she had access, opportunity, and motive. She was physically present at the crime scene to commit the murder, having lured Quine to the Talgarth Road house under the pretext of a planned publicity stunt. Liz Tassel appears in Bombyx Mori as "The Tick," a parasitic woman.

Michael Fancourt is a celebrated, misogynistic English novelist of high social status, a former friend and university contemporary of both Owen Quine and Liz Tassel, widowed by Ellie Fancourt's suicide. Fancourt is uninvolved in the murder as a perpetrator. He has a powerful motive of revenge against Quine — he has hated Quine for over twenty years because he believed Quine wrote the parody that destroyed his wife — and he has a partial alibi (his public movements as a literary celebrity). He is concealing his ongoing animosity but is not concealing any criminal act. He appears in Bombyx Mori as "Vainglorious." Strike's interview with Fancourt is the key conversation that unlocks the case: Fancourt mentions a detail about the parody's authorship that contradicts the assumption Quine wrote it.

Daniel Chard is the president of Roper Chard, the publishing house that had a contract with Quine. He is a wealthy English man of high social status, awkward, with poor social skills, implied to be a closeted homosexual. Chard is uninvolved in the murder as a perpetrator. His motive against Quine is professional and reputational — Bombyx Mori depicts him as "Phallus Impudicus," a man who murders a beautiful young writer to steal his talent and posthumously violates the corpse — but he is not the killer. He has an alibi (a corporate retreat) and was not physically present at the crime scene. He is concealing his sexuality and his fury at the manuscript but no criminal act.

Jerry Waldegrave is Owen Quine's longsuffering editor at Roper Chard, an Englishman of middle-high social status, married, an alcoholic. Waldegrave is uninvolved in the murder as a perpetrator. His motive against Quine would be professional humiliation (Bombyx Mori depicts him as a cuckold). He is concealing the fact that his daughter Joanna may not biologically be his — a hidden family-relationship secret that the manuscript hints at — but he is not the killer. He has an alibi for the murder window and was not at the crime scene.

Kathryn Kent is Owen Quine's mistress, a self-published erotic novelist, of middle-low social status, unmarried, who hosted Quine in her flat during his disappearances. Kathryn Kent is uninvolved in the murder as a perpetrator. Her motive against Quine would be jealousy and humiliation; she appears in Bombyx Mori as "Harpy." She has an alibi (she was visiting her dying sister) and was not at the crime scene during the murder. She is concealing her affair with Quine and her resentment at being publicly mocked in the manuscript. The hidden relationship between Owen Quine and Kathryn Kent (a secret affair conducted alongside his marriage to Leonora) is one of the novel's reveals.

Pippa Midgley is Kathryn Kent's young protégée, a transgender woman (birth-named Phillip) preparing for gender reassignment surgery, rejected by her birth mother, of low social status, unmarried. Pippa is uninvolved in the murder as a perpetrator, though she physically attacked Strike with a knife in a moment of panic earlier in the investigation, mistakenly believing him to be threatening her. Her motive against Quine is humiliation: she appears in Bombyx Mori as "Epicoene." She has only a partial alibi and was not at the crime scene during the murder. She is concealing her involvement in the leak of pages of Bombyx Mori and her physical assault on Strike, but she is not the killer.

The hidden relationships of the novel are several. The central one is the twenty-year blackmail relationship between Owen Quine and Liz Tassel, presented to the world as a normal author-agent relationship but in fact a coerced arrangement in which Tassel had to keep representing Quine to keep her secret. The literary world wrongly believed for twenty years that Quine wrote the parody of Ellie Fancourt's novel; the truth is that Liz Tassel wrote it — a hidden authorship at the heart of the case. Owen Quine and Kathryn Kent's secret affair is a second hidden relationship. Daniel Chard's concealed sexuality is a third, though immaterial to the murder. The leaked early version of Bombyx Mori read by the suspects is itself a hidden falsification: Liz Tassel rewrote the ending to insert the elaborate ritual murder scene before circulating the manuscript, so that when Quine was killed in that exact way, every other person in the manuscript would become a plausible suspect.

Concealment behaviors are extensive. Liz Tassel is concealing the parody authorship, the blackmail, the rewriting of the manuscript, the staging of Quine's disappearance, and the murder itself. Kathryn Kent is concealing the affair. Pippa Midgley is concealing the assault and the leak. Owen Quine concealed for twenty years the blackmail he was running against his agent. Michael Fancourt is concealing the depth of his hatred for Quine. Daniel Chard is concealing his sexuality. Leonora Quine is concealing nothing material.

The resolution: Cormoran Strike solves the case by working with Robin Ellacott to establish the timeline of the manuscript's circulation, the precise nature of the staged death scene, and the literary history of the Ellie Fancourt parody. The interview with Michael Fancourt is the turning point: Fancourt reveals a detail (around stylistic markers and a specific vocabulary in the parody) that points to Liz Tassel rather than Owen Quine as its true author. Strike realizes that the only person with both the motive and the manuscript-level knowledge to stage Quine's death as a precise reenactment of the Bombyx Mori finale was the person who had rewritten that finale — Liz Tassel. He works out that Tassel lured Quine to the Talgarth Road house under the pretext of orchestrating a publicity stunt around his "disappearance," that she killed him there in the manner she had inserted into the manuscript, that she then circulated the manuscript widely so that the many people Quine had slandered would all become suspects, and that she attempted to frame Leonora Quine. Strike and Robin set a trap: they confront Tassel in conditions where she cannot escape, and she is arrested by Anstis and the police. The genuine, unaltered manuscript of Bombyx Mori — Owen Quine's actual final draft, without Tassel's inserted murder scene — is recovered and confirms the rewriting. Leonora Quine is released from custody, returns to Orlando, and life continues. Strike, in the closing scene, gives Robin a Christmas gift — a paid place on a professional surveillance and investigative training course — formalizing her transition from secretary to investigator-in-training and setting up the ongoing arc of their partnership in subsequent novels.
