He Who Whispers (John Dickson Carr, 1946) — Plot Synopsis

Note on framing: He Who Whispers is one of John Dickson Carr's most admired Dr. Gideon Fell novels, structured as both an "impossible crime" puzzle (a man stabbed atop an isolated tower under continuous observation, with no one apparently able to reach him) and a character mystery (whether the suspected woman, Fay Seton, is a literal vampire, an immoral femme fatale, or an innocent victim of slander). The crime is solved; the synopsis below names the perpetrator and method.

The novel opens in London a few months after the end of the Second World War. Miles Hammond, a recently demobilized English historian of middle-to-high social status who has just inherited his uncle's New Forest estate and library, accepts an invitation to attend the first post-war meeting of the Murder Club. Miles is uninvolved in any of the crimes and serves as the principal viewpoint character, narrating much of what is dramatized. He has a younger sister, Marion Hammond, an unflappable Englishwoman of middle social status engaged to a quiet ex-soldier named Stephen "Steve" Curtis. Marion is uninvolved in any crime as a perpetrator and becomes a victim of an attempted murder. Stephen Curtis appears, on the surface, to be Marion's pleasant fiancé; he is in fact concealing his entire identity, and is the novel's ultimate villain (see resolution).

At the Murder Club dinner, Miles finds only two other people present: Barbara Morell, a young Englishwoman who has presented herself under a slightly false identity, and Professor Georges Antoine Rigaud, a high-status French academic and member of the club. Barbara Morell is concealing the fact that she is the sister of an artist, Jim Morell, who killed himself years earlier after his fiancée Fay Seton broke their engagement; she has come to the dinner specifically to disrupt the discussion of the Brooke case and to defend or investigate Fay's reputation. Barbara has a motive that resembles revenge or family-protection but is not the killer in either crime; she is uninvolved as a perpetrator. Professor Rigaud is uninvolved in any crime, has no personal motive, and serves as a witness who narrates the back-story.

Rigaud relates the events of a pre-war summer near Chartres, France, at the country villa of Howard Brooke, a wealthy English expatriate businessman of high social status, married to Mrs. Brooke (uninvolved, dies later off-page). Howard Brooke had hired Fay Seton, a beautiful, reserved, red-haired Englishwoman in her twenties of middle social status, as his secretary. Fay is the central enigma of the novel: she is rumored throughout the local French village to be sexually promiscuous and even to be a vampire, and she is publicly stoned and ostracized by the villagers. Fay is initially set up as the principal suspect with apparently the only motive, and she has no firm alibi for the murder of Howard Brooke. She is, however, not the killer of Howard Brooke; she is concealing something else entirely (revealed at the climax).

Howard Brooke's son, Harry Brooke, fell in love with Fay and became engaged to her against his father's wishes. Howard, who had heard the village whispers and received anonymous letters about Fay's character, arranged to meet Fay alone on the roof of a ruined riverside tower (the tower of Henry IV) on the family property to pay her off — he had a briefcase containing £2,000 in banknotes intended to bribe her into breaking the engagement. Harry Brooke and Professor Rigaud walked Howard to the tower, left him at the base at ten minutes to four, and returned fifteen minutes later to find Howard stabbed with his own sword-cane on the roof, the weapon broken in two beside him, the briefcase of money strangely still bulging. A picnicking family at the base of the tower swore that no one entered or left in those fifteen minutes; no boat approached; the lowest window was fifteen feet off the ground; the only access was the single staircase under continuous observation. Howard Brooke is therefore a victim, uninvolved except as the murdered man, whose motive against the killer was disapproval of his son's engagement. Mrs. Brooke is uninvolved as a perpetrator and dies during the war years of natural causes, as does (apparently) Harry Brooke himself, reported killed in action in occupied France.

Years later, in 1945, Miles Hammond — still preoccupied by Rigaud's tale — independently and by an extraordinary coincidence hires Fay Seton as the librarian to catalogue his late uncle's collection at the New Forest estate. Fay travels down with Miles and Marion. That night, Marion is found in her first-floor bedroom in a state of shock approaching death, having fired a revolver at something or someone outside her window, fifteen feet above the ground; she will not say what she saw. This second crime — the attempted murder of Marion Hammond — is the "whispering" crime of the title. Marion is the intended victim. The attacker did not enter the room.

Dr. Gideon Fell, the lexicographer-detective, an immensely fat, jovial, and brilliant elderly Englishman of high intellectual status, arrives the same night and takes over the investigation. Gideon Fell is uninvolved in any crime. Superintendent Hadley of Scotland Yard joins him; also uninvolved. Local doctor Dr. Lawrence Gilpin treats Marion and is uninvolved.

The hidden relationships at the heart of the novel are several. Stephen Curtis, Marion's fiancé, is Harry Brooke. Harry Brooke faked his own wartime death in France, returned to England under the name Stephen Curtis (using the identity of a real soldier killed in France), and engineered his engagement to Marion Hammond. Stephen Curtis / Harry Brooke is therefore concealing his true identity, his prior engagement to Fay Seton, and his role in his own father's murder. Fay Seton, on meeting "Stephen," would inevitably recognize him as Harry, which is why Stephen has begun a campaign to either silence or destroy her. Barbara Morell and Jim Morell are the sister-and-brother whose tragedy is tangled with Fay's past: Jim Morell had been Fay's fiancé before the Brookes and committed suicide when she ended things, and Barbara has come to the Murder Club partly to learn the truth and partly because she had been corresponding with Harry Brooke years earlier and possesses letters he wrote that incriminate him. Fay Seton's own concealed truth is that she is not a vampire and not a casual seductress, but a sexual masochist whose physical condition (a serious heart ailment combined with a particular sexual nature) made her unable to live a conventional married life; the village rumors had a kernel of psychosexual truth that she would not defend herself against, and she allowed herself to be slandered rather than expose her private life. Fay also suffers from a heart condition that proves fatal by the novel's end.

Motives, alibis, and presence: Howard Brooke's murderer had a motive of financial gain (the £2,000), entitlement, and the desire to be free of an unwanted father. Harry Brooke / Stephen Curtis had this motive against his father and later acquired motives of self-preservation against Fay and against Marion's family discovering his impersonation; he eventually develops a motive of jealousy and rage toward Fay as well. Harry Brooke had the apparent alibi of being with Professor Rigaud at the time his father was stabbed at the top of the tower, but this alibi proves to be the entire trick of the case. Fay Seton had no alibi for either crime and was physically near both crime scenes, but is uninvolved as a perpetrator. Marion Hammond and Miles Hammond are uninvolved and have no motive. Barbara Morell has a possible revenge motive against Fay but is uninvolved. Professor Rigaud was the witness providing Harry's alibi and is uninvolved as a perpetrator.

The resolution: Dr. Gideon Fell solves both the impossible-tower murder and the bedroom-window attack and identifies the perpetrator as Harry Brooke, now living as Stephen Curtis.

For the tower murder: Harry Brooke killed his own father, Howard Brooke. His motive was a combination of financial entitlement (Howard had threatened to disinherit Harry if he married Fay, and Howard was carrying £2,000 in cash to the tower), psychopathic tendencies, and a calculated cruelty that allowed him to frame his fiancée. Harry committed the crime not by entering the tower in the fifteen-minute window but by attacking his father before he and Rigaud left, on the staircase, with the sword-cane — Howard was already mortally wounded when Harry and Rigaud "left him in the best of health." Howard, dying, walked to the top of the tower under his own power and collapsed there. The picnicking family observed no one entering because no one needed to enter; the murder had already happened. The sword-cane was broken to suggest a struggle that had not occurred. Harry, throughout, had been the architect of the whispering campaign against Fay in the village — anonymous letters, planted rumors of vampirism — designed precisely to make her the only suspect with any motive. Harry intended that Fay be hanged for his crime; she escaped because no one could explain how she had reached the tower roof.

For the New Forest attack: Stephen Curtis / Harry Brooke had recognized that Fay Seton's reappearance threatened to expose him. He attempted to murder Fay using a method designed once again to look impossible and supernatural — appearing as a whispering, floating face outside an upstairs window using a contrivance involving a ladder, a mask, and stagecraft, with the goal of frightening her to death (Fay's heart condition was known to him). The attempt went wrong: he targeted the wrong window and terrified Marion, his own fiancée, instead, leaving her in a state of collapse but alive. Harry's motive in this second crime was self-preservation and the silencing of the one person who could identify him.

Dr. Fell, working from clues including the bulging briefcase of un-stolen money (which proved Howard had reached the roof already wounded and never had a confrontation there), the unbroken alibis being too neat, the pattern of the whispering campaigns, and Barbara Morell's letters from Harry, exposes Stephen Curtis as Harry Brooke. Confronted in London, Harry is arrested. Fay Seton, finally cleared of the false reputation that has shadowed her for years, dies shortly after of her heart condition, the whisper campaign having helped destroy her health long before any final scene. Marion Hammond recovers. Miles Hammond is left having lost the woman he had become fascinated by, and the supernatural framing — the levitating vampire — is revealed as the wholly human invention of a calculating, patricidal villain who weaponized the rumor of the supernatural to hide an ordinary, sordid murder of a father for money.
