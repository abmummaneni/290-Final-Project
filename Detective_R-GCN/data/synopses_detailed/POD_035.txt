In the Dark, Season 2 — Curtis Flowers Case Synopsis

Note on labeling: The case of Curtis Flowers is the subject of In the Dark Season 2 (released 2018, with follow-up episodes through 2020), not Season 3. In the Dark Season 3 (2024, under New Yorker stewardship) is a separate investigation into the 2005 Haditha massacre in Iraq. If your dataset entry says "Season 3" for the Flowers case, that is also a labeling error in addition to the villain mislabel. Synopsis below treats Season 2 and corrects the villain assignment as you described.

On the morning of July 16, 1996, four employees of Tardy Furniture, a small store in downtown Winona, Mississippi, were shot in the head at close range. The four victims of the crime were Bertha Tardy, the white middle-aged owner of the store; Carmen Rigby, the white female bookkeeper; Robert Golden, a Black male employee; and Derrick "Bobo" Stewart, a teenage Black male part-time worker who was still alive when discovered but died shortly after. All four are unambiguous victims, uninvolved in the crime except as the people killed, and none had any motive against the others. The investigation that followed, dramatized by host Madeleine Baran and producer Samara Freemark across roughly twenty episodes, is the subject of the season.

Curtis Flowers, a Black man from Winona who had briefly worked at Tardy Furniture days before the murders, is in the wrongly-accused / exonerated innocent role — explicitly not a villain. The podcast's framing, and the legal outcome, is that Curtis Flowers did not commit the Tardy Furniture murders. He has no established motive beyond a thin prosecutorial theory that he was angry about being fired and docked pay; the podcast systematically dismantles even this. Curtis Flowers has an alibi for the time of the crime: he was at home and in his neighborhood that morning, accounted for by family members and other witnesses whose testimony was never fairly weighed. Curtis Flowers was not physically present at the crime scene at the time of the murders. Curtis Flowers concealed nothing material; he consistently maintained his innocence through six trials and twenty-three years of incarceration, much of it on death row. Socially, Curtis Flowers is a working-class Black man, a singer in a gospel group, son of Archie Flowers and Lola Flowers, with siblings including Priscilla Flowers, all of whom stood by him throughout.

The actual antagonist and villain of the story, in the podcast's framing, is Doug Evans, the white District Attorney of Mississippi's Fifth Circuit Court District. Doug Evans is the perpetrator of the prosecutorial misconduct that is the real crime the season investigates. Doug Evans's motive is a combination of power, entitlement, and racial bias: he became personally invested in convicting Curtis Flowers and tried him six separate times for the same murders, refusing to relent even after repeated reversals. Doug Evans has a documented pattern, established by an APM Reports statistical analysis, of striking Black prospective jurors at roughly 4.4 times the rate he struck white jurors over his 26-year tenure. Doug Evans is concealing exculpatory evidence — including the existence of alternate suspects — from the defense, in apparent violation of Brady v. Maryland. Doug Evans is concealing the fact that his star witness's testimony was bought with leniency. Doug Evans is a high-status white male public official, married, a former police officer turned prosecutor.

Doug Evans's accomplice in the wrongful prosecution is John Johnson, the lead investigator for the District Attorney's office. John Johnson has a motive of professional loyalty to Doug Evans and institutional self-protection; his role was to build and defend the narrow case against Curtis Flowers and to downplay other suspects under oath. John Johnson is concealing the fact that other viable suspects were investigated and discarded.

Odell Hallmon is a key witness whose testimony the podcast exposes as fabricated. Odell Hallmon, a Black man with a long criminal record now serving three consecutive life sentences for unrelated murders, testified in four of Flowers's trials that Flowers had confessed to the Tardy murders in jail. Odell Hallmon has a motive of self-interest — he received leniency from Doug Evans on his own crimes in exchange for the testimony — and he ultimately recanted on tape to In the Dark, admitting the confession story was a lie. Odell Hallmon is therefore a perjurer and a junior accomplice to the wrongful conviction, though not a perpetrator of the Tardy murders themselves. Other jailhouse-informant witnesses similarly changed their stories over time and are concealing the fact that their testimony was unreliable.

The season also surfaces alternate suspects whom Doug Evans's office is concealing. Willie James Hemphill, a Black man with a violent criminal history including robbery and assault on women, was actually arrested and held in jail for eleven days as an early suspect immediately after the murders. Willie James Hemphill lived near Tardy Furniture and near where a possible murder weapon was later found, wore Fila Grant Hill sneakers matching a bloody shoeprint at the scene, and gave a false alibi (claiming to be at a Memphis mall with a woman named Twanda Woods, who told In the Dark she was actually in Winona that morning). Willie James Hemphill has a possible motive of robbery and possible psychopathic/violent tendencies based on his record, no verified alibi, and concealment behavior in the form of refusing to account for his whereabouts and reacting with hostility to reporters Madeleine Baran and Parker Yesko. The podcast does not assert that Willie James Hemphill committed the murders, only that he is a plausible alternate suspect whose existence Doug Evans hid from the defense. Marcus Presley and LaSamuel Gamble, the perpetrators of an Alabama crime spree the same summer of 1996 involving execution-style shootings of store employees with a possibly-jammed .380 handgun and Fila shoes, are likewise alternate suspects whose presence in a Tardy investigative photo lineup Doug Evans's office concealed from the defense. These men are uninvolved in the wrongful-prosecution storyline but are possible-but-unproven perpetrators of the original Tardy murders. The actual perpetrator of the 1996 Tardy Furniture murders is, as of the season's conclusion, formally unidentified.

Madeleine Baran, the host and lead reporter, and her team — senior producer Samara Freemark, reporter Parker Yesko, data reporter Will Craft, producer Natalie Jablonski, and editor Catherine Winter — function as the investigators in this story. They are uninvolved in any crime, have no motives beyond journalistic ones, and are the analogues of detectives. Katie Ali, one of Curtis Flowers's appellate defense lawyers, is also uninvolved and is the figure who in 2015 spotted Marcus Presley's photo in the original Tardy investigative file — the discovery that opened the alternate-suspects thread.

The resolution: The "crime" that the season actually solves is not the 1996 quadruple homicide but the wrongful prosecution. In the Dark's reporting documented that there was no direct evidence linking Curtis Flowers to the murders, that Odell Hallmon's confession testimony was fabricated, that Doug Evans had systematically struck Black jurors across decades, and that exculpatory evidence about alternate suspects had been withheld. The reporting was cited in legal filings to the U.S. Supreme Court. On June 21, 2019, the Supreme Court ruled 7–2 in Flowers v. Mississippi to overturn his sixth conviction on the grounds that Doug Evans had unconstitutionally excluded Black jurors. In December 2019, Curtis Flowers was released on bail. In September 2020, Mississippi Attorney General Lynn Fitch moved to dismiss all charges for lack of evidence, ending the case after 24 years. Doug Evans recused himself rather than try Flowers a seventh time and was never criminally charged for his conduct, though his reputation was destroyed and his pattern of racial discrimination in jury selection was permanently entered into the legal and public record. Curtis Flowers was exonerated; Doug Evans is the figure the podcast identifies, in effect, as the wrongdoer who escaped accountability.
