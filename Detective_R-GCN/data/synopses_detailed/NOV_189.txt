Nerve (Dick Francis, 1964) — Plot Synopsis

The novel is narrated in the first person by Rob Finn, a young English steeplechase jockey of middle social status, the only unmusical member of an otherwise celebrated musical family — his mother Olivia Finn is a concert pianist, his father (largely off-page) is a violinist, and his uncle Sir Trelawny Finn is a renowned conductor. Rob has rebelled against the family vocation and worked his way through stables in Australia and South America before returning to England to ride. He is uninvolved in any crime as a perpetrator and serves as both the protagonist-investigator and an eventual victim of attempted murder. Rob is in love with his cousin Joanna Finn, a London-based singer who is genetically a first cousin (or framed in the novel as such, with some textual ambiguity); Joanna is uninvolved in any crime and ultimately becomes Rob's investigative partner and romantic partner.

The novel opens with one of the most famous first sentences in English crime fiction: Art Mathews, an established English steeplechase jockey of middle social status, married, walks into the parade ring at Dunstable racecourse and shoots himself in the head in front of Rob Finn and the assembled crowd. Art Mathews is a victim — specifically a victim driven to suicide rather than directly murdered — uninvolved in any crime as a perpetrator. He had been the target of a slow, undermining campaign of rumors that he had become unreliable, was in financial trouble, and had argued with his trainer Corin Kellar (a real but flawed trainer of middle social status, uninvolved in the central crime as a perpetrator but complicit through gullibility in spreading the rumors). Art Mathews has no motive against anyone; his motive in killing himself is despair at his ruined reputation and unexplained loss of mounts. He has no alibi to assess, since he is not a suspect. He was physically present at the racecourse at the time of his suicide.

As Rob continues to ride, he notices a pattern. Other jockeys are being similarly destroyed. Peter Cloony, a young jockey who lives in a remote area, has acquired a reputation for arriving late to race meetings and is consequently dropped by trainers; investigation reveals that someone has been deliberately blocking the single road to his cottage on race mornings. Grant Oldfield, a once-promising jockey, has been ruined by rumors that he sells racing tips to bookmakers, has suffered a mental breakdown, and has been reduced to manual labor; the rumors prove to be false. Tick-Tock Ingersoll, a cheerful young jockey and Rob's friend, is one of the few who survives the campaign. All these jockeys are victims, uninvolved in any crime as perpetrators, with no motives against anyone, and serve as a pattern of attack pointing back to a single source.

Rob himself, after a successful early run including a dramatic win for the well-respected trainer James Axminster (a high-status English racehorse trainer with a stable of sixty horses, uninvolved in any crime as a perpetrator), is appearing on a popular racing television programme hosted by Maurice Kemp-Lore. Kemp-Lore is the novel's villain. He is a charming, photogenic, well-connected English television presenter of high social status, the son of a famous racing family, married, with an apparently impeccable public reputation. He is uninvolved in any racing as a rider himself for a reason that is the key to his psychology: Maurice Kemp-Lore is severely allergic to horses. He cannot ride, cannot be near them for long, and has therefore been excluded from the family vocation he most envied. His motive is a combination of jealousy, revenge against a sport that physically rejected him, and psychopathic tendencies — he derives pleasure from systematically destroying the careers of the jockeys he is publicly admired for celebrating. He has a public motive (none) and a concealed motive (envy and the desire to ruin those who can do what he cannot).

Maurice Kemp-Lore's alibi for each individual incident appears unbreakable because he uses his television persona and social access as cover. He is invariably visible elsewhere when individual rumors take hold, since rumors do not require physical presence. He is concealing the fact that he is the source of every malicious story about Art Mathews, Peter Cloony, Grant Oldfield, and ultimately Rob Finn. He is concealing the fact that he has personally administered a long-acting tranquilizer (a doping agent) to every horse Rob has ridden during Rob's catastrophic losing streak — having the privileged access of a television personality to enter the stables and the parade ring under the guise of pre-race interviews and behind-the-scenes filming, where he can administer a sweet that the horses readily accept and that contains the drug. The doping makes the horses lethargic on the track, ensuring Rob loses race after race. He is concealing the fact that on the morning of one of Art Mathews's last rides, he similarly tampered with Art's mount. He is concealing the fact that he himself, by virtue of his allergy, cannot dope a horse without leaving traces of his own physical reaction; this becomes one of the clues Rob uses against him.

The hidden relationship at the heart of the novel is the gap between Maurice Kemp-Lore's public identity (the friendly, knowledgeable face of British racing) and his private identity (a methodical, malicious saboteur). There is no secret marriage or undisclosed family tie in the conventional sense; the concealment is psychological and operational. Maurice Kemp-Lore pretends, in each public encounter, to be a sympathetic professional ally to the jockeys he is privately destroying. He uses his interview slots — including the one in which Rob is invited onto his programme — to plant seeds of public doubt about each target's competence.

Rob Finn, supported by Joanna Finn, conducts the investigation. He has no formal investigative role and works alongside, rather than within, racing's regulatory structures. He visits Peter Cloony and discovers the truth about the blocked road; he visits Grant Oldfield and confirms the rumor about bookmaker tips was a fabrication; he traces the pattern back to the one figure with access to every affected jockey and every affected horse — Maurice Kemp-Lore. Rob also notices that Kemp-Lore reflexively avoids close contact with horses despite his profession, which leads him to the allergy and the underlying motive.

Once Maurice Kemp-Lore realizes Rob is closing in on him, he kidnaps Rob, ties him up in a remote, freezing tack room, brutally tortures him by shackling him to a chain and leaving him without food, water, or warmth, and abandons him to die of exposure. Rob — drawing on the eponymous nerve, an unusually high pain tolerance, and ingenuity — escapes. He has no time to recover before the next major race. Concealing his injuries, he forces himself to ride and wins decisively on a horse that, this time, has not been doped — providing the public proof that his earlier "loss of nerve" was nothing of the kind.

The resolution: Rob does not deliver Maurice Kemp-Lore directly to the police; instead, he sets a trap that mirrors and inverts everything Kemp-Lore did to him. Rob arranges, with the help of allies inside the racing world, for Kemp-Lore to be publicly exposed during a live broadcast or a comparable public setting. He ensures that Kemp-Lore is forced into close, prolonged proximity to a horse — exploiting the very allergy that motivated the entire campaign — so that Kemp-Lore suffers a humiliating, visible allergic collapse in front of the racing world he had manipulated. Simultaneously, Rob feeds the same kind of carefully calibrated rumor-mill that Kemp-Lore had used against the jockeys back against Kemp-Lore himself, destroying his public reputation and his standing with the broadcast network and the racing establishment. The doping evidence, the kidnapping, and the witness testimony Rob has assembled with Joanna's help are turned over to the authorities. Maurice Kemp-Lore is identified as the perpetrator behind the suicide of Art Mathews, the ruin of Peter Cloony, the destruction of Grant Oldfield, and the attempted murder of Rob Finn. His career and reputation are destroyed, he faces criminal charges for the kidnapping and assault of Rob, and the systemic doping campaign is exposed.

Rob and Joanna's relationship, complicated throughout by their first-cousin status (a recurring point of unease in the novel), resolves with Joanna agreeing to be with Rob despite the family connection. The other surviving jockeys — Peter Cloony, Grant Oldfield, Tick-Tock Ingersoll — are vindicated and return to or recover from their damaged careers. Rob retains his place at James Axminster's stable, his reputation restored, his nerve never having been the thing in question.
