The Red-Headed League (Arthur Conan Doyle, 1891) — Plot Synopsis

Note on framing: "The Red-Headed League" is a Sherlock Holmes short story by Sir Arthur Conan Doyle, first published in The Strand Magazine in August 1891 and later collected as the second story in The Adventures of Sherlock Holmes (1892). It is unusual among the items in this dataset because the central crime is an attempted bank robbery rather than a murder — no one is killed in the story. Doyle himself ranked it second among his twelve favorite Holmes stories. The crime is solved within the story; the perpetrator is named, captured, and the gold is preserved. Note that some later television adaptations (notably the 1985 Granada series with Jeremy Brett) retroactively insert Professor Moriarty as the mastermind, but Moriarty does not appear in Doyle's original 1891 text — Conan Doyle had not yet invented him. Synopsis below treats the original short story.

The story is narrated in the first person by Dr. John H. Watson, a London general practitioner, war veteran (Afghanistan), of middle social status, married, friend and chronicler of Sherlock Holmes. Watson is uninvolved in the crime as a perpetrator and serves as the viewpoint character and as a minor participant in the climactic capture. He has no motive against anyone in the case. Sherlock Holmes is the world's first consulting detective, an unmarried man of middle social status, residing at 221B Baker Street, eccentric, brilliant, given to bouts of cocaine use during periods of intellectual idleness. Holmes is uninvolved in the crime as a perpetrator and is the lead investigator. The story opens with Holmes already in conversation with the prospective client when Watson arrives and is invited to remain.

The client and unwitting dupe (rather than victim in the traditional sense, since no harm comes to him personally) is Mr. Jabez Wilson, a London pawnbroker of low-to-middle social status, an elderly man described as "very stout, florid-faced," with a notable shock of fiery red hair. Wilson is a bachelor (a recluse with settled habits, formerly a ship's carpenter, now a small-shop proprietor in shabby Saxe-Coburg Square in the City of London). He is uninvolved in any crime as a perpetrator. He has no motive against anyone. He has no concealment behavior; he comes to Holmes openly and tells the entire story without dissembling. He has an alibi for any crime he might have been suspected of (he is not a suspect). His role in the story is that of the inadvertent target of an elaborate distraction. He has no idea that a crime is being committed using him.

Wilson's pawnshop is staffed by Wilson himself, his fourteen-year-old housemaid (a brief presence in the story, uninvolved in any crime), and a young assistant who calls himself Vincent Spaulding. Spaulding is the central villain of the story under a false identity. His real name is John Clay, a notorious London criminal described by Holmes as "a murderer, thief, smasher, and forger" — a man whose grandfather was a royal duke, who attended Eton and Oxford, and who has descended through clever, predatory crime into being one of the most-wanted villains in London. John Clay is of high social-status birth concealed behind a low social-status disguise (he poses as a willing-to-work-for-half-wages pawnshop assistant). He is unmarried, in his thirties or younger, with an acid splash mark on his forehead — the physical clue that lets Holmes identify him from Wilson's description. John Clay's motive is financial gain: specifically, the theft of a shipment of 30,000 French Napoléon gold coins (about 5,600 troy ounces of pure gold) recently borrowed by the City and Suburban Bank from a French bank and stored in the bank's cellar vault. Clay has no alibi for the time of the attempted crime because he is physically present in the bank vault committing it. He is concealing his real identity behind the alias Vincent Spaulding throughout his employment with Wilson; he is concealing his prior criminal record; he is concealing the fact that he has been digging a tunnel from Wilson's pawnshop cellar across the street and under the pavement to the bank vault next door; and he is concealing the fact that the entire "Red-Headed League" pretext was his own invention to keep Wilson out of the shop for four hours every day so that Clay could dig undisturbed.

John Clay's accomplice in the story is a co-conspirator who introduces himself to Wilson as Duncan Ross (and at another point gives the name William Morris) but whom Holmes later identifies as Archie, a red-headed criminal collaborator. Archie is of unclear social status, unmarried in any documented sense, and his motive is also financial — a share of the gold. Archie is concealing his real identity behind two pseudonyms, posing as the manager of the fictitious Red-Headed League at a small office in Pope's Court, Fleet Street, where he hires Wilson under the cover story of a bequest left by an eccentric American millionaire named "Ezekiah Hopkins" of Lebanon, Pennsylvania (Hopkins is also fictitious). Archie has no alibi for the time of the attempted bank robbery — he is the second figure who emerges through the tunnel into the bank vault and is seized by the police at the rear of the building. He is physically present at the crime scene during the climax. The hidden relationship at the heart of the story is the secret partnership between John Clay and Archie, masquerading as the unrelated figures "Vincent Spaulding" (in the pawnshop) and "Duncan Ross" (in the League office), whose coordinated false identities are designed to manipulate Wilson into vacating his shop on a regular schedule.

The "crime scene" is twofold and runs across several London locations. The pawnshop in shabby Saxe-Coburg Square is the entry point of the tunnel; the City and Suburban Bank vault on the opposite side of the block is the destination. The Pope's Court League office is a stage-managed location used purely to manipulate Wilson. At the time of the attempted robbery — late at night, after the League has been "dissolved" and Wilson has come to Holmes — Clay and Archie are physically present in the bank vault having broken upward through the cellar floor; Holmes, Watson, Inspector Peter Jones of Scotland Yard, and Mr. Merryweather (the bank's chairman, of high social status, uninvolved in any crime, who has been summoned for the trap) are concealed in the darkened vault waiting. Wilson is at home and uninvolved in the climax. Inspector Peter Jones of Scotland Yard is uninvolved in any crime, has the professional motive of capturing a wanted criminal, and is physically present at the bank for the trap. Mr. Merryweather is uninvolved in any crime, has the personal motive of protecting his bank's assets, and is physically present at the bank.

Concealment behaviors in the story are essentially all on the side of John Clay and Archie: false identities, false employment, the false "League" (with its preposterous Encyclopaedia-Britannica copying task), and the tunnel itself, which Clay has been excavating in Wilson's cellar under the cover of "developing photographs" — a plausible pretext that keeps Wilson out of the cellar. Clay is concealing the physical evidence of the digging on his own person — the worn and dirty knees of his trousers, which Holmes specifically deduces from afar and confirms by ringing the pawnshop doorbell and asking for false directions in order to inspect Clay's knees up close.

The Red-Headed League itself, as an organization, is entirely a fiction invented by Clay and Archie. There is no Ezekiah Hopkins, no bequest, no pension fund for red-headed men, no real League. The advertisement is a fake; the Pope's Court office is a rented sham; the eight weeks of "purely nominal services" copying the Encyclopaedia Britannica from A onward are pure busywork. The League dissolves abruptly on October 9, 1890 — a date posted on the office door — because by that point the tunnel under the street is completed and Clay no longer needs Wilson out of the shop. The dissolution itself is the trigger that sends Wilson to Holmes, and the suddenness of the dissolution is what tells Holmes that the long-planned crime is now imminent — likely that very night, Saturday, when the bank will be closed for two days and the gold shipment will sit unattended.

The resolution: Sherlock Holmes solves the case in a single afternoon and prevents the robbery. From Wilson's narrative he deduces several key points. The Red-Headed League's premise is too preposterous to be genuine; therefore it must be a pretext to remove Wilson from his shop on a fixed schedule. The fact that Spaulding had specifically pointed Wilson at the advertisement and had taken Wilson's place at half wages identifies Spaulding as the architect of the manipulation. Spaulding's hours alone in the cellar "developing photographs" suggest illicit activity in the cellar. The acid splash on Spaulding's forehead and Wilson's description match the description of John Clay, a known criminal. Holmes visits Saxe-Coburg Square, taps the pavement in front of the pawnshop with his stick (it sounds hollow, indicating a tunnel beneath), walks around the corner to inspect the buildings on the parallel street, and identifies the City and Suburban Bank as the obvious target. Holmes then rings Wilson's doorbell and asks Spaulding for directions to a fictitious address — purely to inspect Spaulding's trouser knees, which are worn and stained from extensive kneeling and digging. The combination — hollow pavement, adjacent bank, worn knees, dissolved League — confirms that the robbery is imminent that night. Holmes assembles his trap: he, Watson, Inspector Peter Jones of Scotland Yard, and bank chairman Mr. Merryweather conceal themselves in the darkened bank vault with the gold; police are also stationed at the rear of the pawnshop. After more than an hour's wait, a flagstone in the vault floor lifts and a hand reaches up; John Clay emerges, followed by Archie. Holmes and Jones spring on Clay; Watson holds a pistol on him; the police at the back of the pawnshop apprehend Archie. Clay, on being captured, demands to be addressed with deference befitting his royal-ducal lineage — a mannered touch that closes out his characterization. The 30,000 Napoléons are saved untouched. Back at Baker Street, Holmes explains his deductive chain to Watson and remarks, in the story's famous closing lines paraphrased here, that the case was "a little out of the common" and that such problems are what save him from the boredom of ordinary existence. The villain is John Clay; the accomplice is Archie; the motive is purely financial; the crime is prevented; no one is harmed; Jabez Wilson is left out four pounds a week from a vanished sinecure but otherwise unscathed; and Holmes returns to Baker Street to await his next case.
