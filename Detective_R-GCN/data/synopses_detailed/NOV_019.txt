The Big Four (Agatha Christie, 1927) — Plot Synopsis
Note on framing: The Big Four is unusual in the Poirot canon. It is a novelization of twelve short stories that Christie cobbled together with help from her brother-in-law during the worst period of her life (her mother's death and her divorce from Archie Christie). It is therefore an episodic spy/conspiracy thriller rather than a single-crime whodunit. There is no single "the murder" with one perpetrator and a circle of suspects; instead there are multiple murders and incidents committed by an international criminal cabal of four individuals plus their agents, and the "mystery" is the identification of the cabal's members — particularly the elusive Number Four. The synopsis below treats the cabal collectively as the villains and identifies each member, their roles, motives, and how they are unmasked.
The novel opens in London with Captain Arthur Hastings, an English former military officer of middle social status, returning from his ranch in Argentina to visit his old friend Hercule Poirot, the celebrated Belgian private detective. Hastings is the narrator and is uninvolved in any crime; he is the Watson figure throughout. Poirot is likewise uninvolved in any crime and is the lead investigator. Inspector (later Chief Inspector) James Japp of Scotland Yard, a middle-aged English policeman of middle social status, assists Poirot at intervals and is uninvolved in any crime. Miss Felicity Lemon does not feature in this novel (she was added in the 2013 television adaptation); the principal supporting figures around Poirot are Hastings and Japp.
Almost immediately upon Hastings's arrival, an unknown gaunt man stumbles into Poirot's flat covered in dust, scrawls the figure "4" repeatedly on a sheet of paper, and is shortly afterward found dead, poisoned with hydrocyanic acid (the smell of bitter almonds gives Poirot the clue). This man is a victim, uninvolved in any crime, killed by an agent of the Big Four to silence him before he could identify members of the cabal to Poirot. From this opening, Poirot reveals to Hastings that he has been investigating an international conspiracy known as the Big Four — a cabal of four criminal masterminds plotting nothing less than world domination through orchestrated political destabilization, scientific manipulation, financial control, and assassination. The villains of the novel are these four individuals collectively, supported by a network of thousands of agents. The cabal's collective motive is power on a global scale — they intend to issue orders by wireless from a hidden headquarters to their followers in every country and emerge as the world's dictators.
Number One is Li Chang Yen, a Chinese political mastermind and the overall leader of the cabal, residing in China and never leaving it during the events of the novel. Li Chang Yen has a motive of power; he represents the brain of the organization. He is concealing the entire conspiracy and his role as its leader. He has no alibi tested by Poirot because he is geographically inaccessible, but he is physically not present at any of the European crime scenes since his agents act on his orders. He is a high-status figure within Chinese politics, unmarried in the text, and is the strategic architect. His existence is concealed even from most of the organization's lower agents.
Number Two is Abe Ryland, an American multimillionaire industrialist known as the "American Soap King," reputedly richer than John D. Rockefeller. Abe Ryland is a high-status white American male, his marital status incidental, and he represents the financial wing of the cabal. His motive is a combination of power and greed/financial ambition — providing the wealth that funds the conspiracy. Early in the novel he attempts to lure Poirot to Rio de Janeiro on a fabricated pretext, ostensibly to investigate corporate fraud but in reality, Poirot deduces, to recruit or eliminate him; this is a concealment behavior identifying Ryland as a threat. Ryland has no genuine alibi for the conspiracy's activities since his public persona is itself the cover. He is concealing the fact that he is Number Two of the Big Four. He owns, through a front company, a quarry in the Italian Dolomites that conceals the cabal's underground headquarters, the Felsenlabyrinth.
Number Three is Madame Régine Olivier, a Frenchwoman, a celebrated nuclear physicist and analytical chemist whose public reputation is said to exceed even that of Pierre and Marie Curie. Madame Olivier is a high-status, internationally honored female scientist of middle age, presented as widowed or unmarried. Her motive combines power and a scientific/ideological devotion to using her research for the cabal's ends rather than for legitimate science. She represents the scientific arm of the Big Four. She is concealing the true scope of her atomic and chemical research, which she is conducting for the conspiracy. She has no firm alibi for the conspiracy's activities; her laboratory work and prestigious public position serve as her cover. She is implicated in the "Yellow Jasmine" episode involving the death of a man named Paynter, where her chemistry contributes to a poisoning.
Number Four, called the Destroyer, is the chief assassin and the most active threat throughout the novel. Number Four is eventually identified as Claude Darrell, an obscure English actor with no fixed identity, a master of disguise capable of completely altering his face, voice, and persona. Claude Darrell is a male English citizen of indeterminate social status, unmarried, and his motive is psychopathic tendencies coupled with the power and glory of being the cabal's executioner. Number Four is the perpetrator of the on-page murders and physical attacks throughout the novel: he kills the dust-coated messenger in Poirot's flat with cyanide; he is responsible for a string of further killings of witnesses, agents, and obstacles across the episodes; he commits explosions, abductions, and ambushes. Number Four has no alibi for any of these crimes because he is physically present at each, but his alibi as a person — as Claude Darrell — is that "Claude Darrell" never appears as himself; he is always disguised as someone else. He is concealing his real identity behind a rotating series of aliases and impersonations. The hidden relationship at the heart of the novel is therefore that several seemingly unrelated minor characters — a chess opponent, a manservant, a mental patient, an asylum doctor named Quentin, even at one point an assistant pretending to deliver telegrams — are in fact all the same man, Claude Darrell, in different disguises. The novel's titular "Number Four remains a mystery almost to the end" tagline describes precisely this slow unmasking. A clue Poirot uses to identify Darrell is a peculiar habit of dabbing his bread in his soup at dinner, a mannerism Darrell cannot suppress across disguises.
Several supporting figures appear in episodic chapters and are largely uninvolved in the central conspiracy except as victims or witnesses. Mr. Paynter, an elderly English gentleman, is a victim murdered in the "Yellow Jasmine" episode by agents of the Big Four to suppress what he had learned of Li Chang Yen's activities; he has no motive against anyone and was killed for what he knew. Mr. Whalley is another victim killed for similar reasons in another episode. A chess grandmaster named Dr. Savaronoff and his opponent Gilmour Wilson appear in "The Chess Problem"; Wilson is a victim murdered by an electrified chess piece arranged by Number Four in disguise, and Savaronoff is, in one of the novel's twists, himself a Big Four impersonation by Number Four — i.e., the real Savaronoff has been replaced. Countess Vera Rosakoff, a glamorous Russian jewel thief and recurring Christie character, is a complicated figure here: she is allied with or at least useful to the Big Four during portions of the novel and provides a romantic-adversarial foil for Poirot, but her loyalty is ambiguous and Poirot believes there is good in her. Her motive is financial gain and self-preservation. Her hidden relationship to the cabal is that she serves as one of their socialite agents while pretending to be merely an aristocratic refugee. She has no genuine alibi for the conspiracy's actions but is not the primary murderer.
A key narrative device is Poirot's invention of a fictitious twin brother, Achille Poirot. There is no Achille Poirot in reality; Poirot fakes his own death by a Big Four bomb attack midway through the novel and then re-emerges disguised as his nonexistent twin to deceive the cabal into believing the real Poirot is dead. This is a concealment behavior on Poirot's part, used as a counter-deception against the villains rather than for criminal purposes.
The resolution: Hastings is captured by the Big Four and used as bait to lure Poirot to the cabal's hidden underground headquarters in the Felsenlabyrinth quarry in the Italian Dolomites. Hastings, on being marched out of Poirot's flat, leaves a coded clue — four books pulled from a shelf and four lumps of coal near the grate — which Poirot recognizes. Poirot, in his Achille disguise and accompanied by police backup, follows the trap deliberately. Inside the mountain base, Poirot confronts the cabal. Number Four / Claude Darrell is unmasked, his identity confirmed by the soup-dabbing mannerism among other clues. The headquarters is destroyed in a climactic explosion: Abe Ryland, Madame Olivier, and Claude Darrell / Number Four all die in the blast inside the Felsenlabyrinth. Li Chang Yen / Number One, the mastermind, remains in China and is reported in the novel's coda to have committed suicide upon learning that the European arm of the conspiracy has been destroyed. Poirot and Hastings escape. The Big Four cabal is therefore wholly destroyed: the perpetrators have all been killed rather than tried and convicted. Hastings returns to Argentina, and Poirot, having dismantled the conspiracy, is left contemplating retirement.
